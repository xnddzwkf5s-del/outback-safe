# Preparation Methods

Choosing the right preparation method is as important as choosing the right plant. Different plant parts release their medicinal compounds under different conditions. Using the wrong method can mean the difference between effective medicine and useless tea.

---

## Infusion

**Best for:** Leaves, flowers, soft stems, aromatic plants  
**Not suitable for:** Roots, bark, seeds, tough stems

An infusion is simply steeping plant material in hot water — like making tea. It extracts volatile oils and water-soluble compounds from delicate plant parts.

### Method
1. Bring clean water to a rolling boil
2. Remove from heat briefly (30 seconds)
3. Pour over plant material in a clean vessel
4. Cover immediately (volatile oils escape with steam)
5. Steep for 5–15 minutes
6. Strain through clean cloth
7. Drink while warm

### Dosage Guidelines
- **Standard:** 1 teaspoon dried herb (or 2 teaspoons fresh) per 250 mL water
- **Frequency:** 3–4 cups daily for acute conditions, 1–2 cups for maintenance
- **Storage:** Drink immediately — infusions spoil within 24 hours without refrigeration

### Plants Best as Infusion
- Lemon myrtle (Backhousia citriodora)
- Mountain pepper (Tasmannia lanceolata)
- Eucalyptus leaves (young, fresh)
- Tea tree leaves
- Native ginger (Alpinia caerulea)

## Decoction

**Best for:** Roots, bark, seeds, tough stems  
**Not suitable for:** Aromatic leaves, flowers (volatile oils destroyed by prolonged heat)

A decoction involves simmering plant material in water. The sustained heat breaks down tough cell walls to extract compounds that won't release with simple steeping.

### Method
1. Place plant material in cold water in a pot
2. Bring to a boil, then reduce to a gentle simmer
3. Simmer covered for 15–30 minutes (longer for very tough material like bark)
4. Strain through clean cloth while hot
5. Allow to cool to drinking temperature

### Dosage Guidelines
- **Standard:** 1–2 teaspoons dried root/bark per 250 mL water
- **Frequency:** 2–3 cups daily
- **Reduction:** Simmer longer to concentrate — reduce to half volume for stronger dose

### Plants Best as Decoction
- Black wattle bark (Acacia mearnsii)
- Eucalyptus bark
- Corkwood (Duboisia myoporoides — CAUTION: TOXIC, precise dosing required)

## Poultice

**Best for:** External wounds, inflammation, insect bites, splinters, boils  
**Not for:** Deep puncture wounds, severe burns, internal use

A poultice is plant material applied directly to the skin, held in place with a cloth or bandage. It delivers medicinal compounds locally and can draw out infection, reduce inflammation, or soothe pain.

### Method — Fresh Plant
1. Harvest clean plant material
2. Crush or chew to a pulp (chewing adds enzymes that can activate some compounds)
3. Apply directly to affected area
4. Cover with a clean cloth or large leaf (lilly pilly leaves work well)
5. Secure with strip of cloth or vine
6. Leave for 20 minutes to 2 hours
7. Replace with fresh poultice as needed

### Method — Dried Plant
1. Grind dried plant to powder
2. Add small amount of warm water to form a paste
3. Apply as above

### Plants Best as Poultice
- Tea tree leaves — antiseptic
- Tick bush (Kunzea ambigua) — anti-inflammatory
- Plantain (Plantago spp.) — drawing, wound healing

### Safety
- Wash wound before applying poultice
- Do not apply to deep wounds where plant material could be trapped
- Discontinue if irritation occurs
- Cover open wounds with a thin clean cloth between poultice and wound

## Tincture

**Best for:** Long-term storage, concentrated doses, plants with alcohol-soluble compounds  
**Not suitable for:** Where alcohol is unavailable or contraindicated

A tincture extracts medicinal compounds into alcohol, which acts as both solvent and preservative. Tinctures can last for years when properly made and stored, making them the best option for building a survival medicine stockpile.

### Method
1. Chop fresh plant material finely (or grind dried material)
2. Fill a clean glass jar 1/3 to 1/2 full with plant material
3. Cover completely with alcohol (see percentages below)
4. Seal tightly
5. Store in a dark place
6. Shake daily for the first week, then weekly
7. Steep for 2–6 weeks (4 weeks standard)
8. Strain through clean cloth, squeeze out all liquid
9. Bottle in dark glass, label with plant name and date

### Alcohol Selection
- **40% (80 proof) vodka** — general purpose, good for most herbs
- **60% (120 proof)** — better for resins and gums (eucalyptus, tea tree)
- **45–50%** — ideal all-purpose strength
- **Glycerine alternative** — if alcohol is unavailable, vegetable glycerine can be used (weaker extraction, shorter shelf life)

### Dosage
- **Standard:** 2–4 mL (40–80 drops) in small amount of water, 2–3 times daily
- **Acute:** up to 5 mL every 2–4 hours for short periods
- **Children:** Reduce proportionally by body weight (see Safety section)

### Plants Best as Tincture
- Eucalyptus leaves
- Tea tree leaves
- Tick bush (Kunzea ambigua)

## Salve

**Best for:** Skin conditions, wounds, burns, chapped skin, muscle aches  
**Not for:** Internal use

A salve combines oil-infused herbs with beeswax to create a semi-solid, spreadable preparation that stays on the skin. Excellent for wound care and skin protection.

### Method
1. **Infuse oil:** Place dried herbs in a clean jar, cover with oil (olive, coconut, or rendered animal fat in survival context)
2. Warm gently if possible (solar infusion: place in sun for 2–4 weeks; or warm water bath for 2–4 hours)
3. Strain oil through cloth
4. **Make salve:** Gently warm infused oil, add beeswax (ratio: 1 part beeswax to 4–5 parts oil)
5. Stir until beeswax melts completely
6. Test consistency: drip a drop onto a cold surface — it should set firm but spreadable
7. Pour into clean containers while still liquid
8. Allow to cool and set before using

### Storage
- Store in cool, dark place
- Shelf life: 6–12 months with plant-based oils
- Rendered animal fat salves: 3–6 months

### Plants Best for Salve
- Tea tree — antiseptic
- Tick bush (Kunzea ambigua) — anti-inflammatory
- Eucalyptus — chest rub, decongestant

## Syrup

**Best for:** Cough, sore throat, children's medicine (palatable)  
**Not for:** Diabetic individuals without careful consideration

Syrup preserves a decoction or infusion in sugar or honey, making it palatable and longer-lasting. The sugar acts as a preservative and the thick consistency coats the throat.

### Method
1. Prepare a strong decoction or infusion (double strength)
2. Strain thoroughly
3. Measure the liquid
4. Add equal amount of sugar or honey (1:1 ratio by volume)
5. Heat gently, stirring until dissolved — do NOT boil after adding honey (destroys beneficial compounds)
6. Simmer gently to reduce slightly (optional, for thicker syrup)
7. Pour into clean, dry bottles while still warm
8. Seal and store in a cool, dark place

### Dosage
- **Adults:** 1–2 teaspoons as needed, up to 6 times daily
- **Children over 2:** 1/2–1 teaspoon, up to 4 times daily
- **Children under 2:** Avoid honey-based syrups (botulism risk in survival contexts)

### Shelf Life
- **Honey-based:** 3–6 months
- **Sugar-based:** 2–4 months
- Store in cool, dark place
- Discard if mould appears or smell changes

### Plants Best as Syrup
- Eucalyptus — cough, chest congestion

## Equipment in Survival Contexts

You won't have a kitchen. Improvise:

| Modern Tool | Survival Substitute |
|-------------|---------------------|
| Measuring spoons | Pinch (1/4 tsp), palmful (1 tbsp) |
| Saucepan | Any metal container, large shell, bamboo section |
| Strainer | Clean cloth, fine-weave basket |
| Glass jar | Clay pot, bamboo tube, gourd |
| Mortar & pestle | Two flat rocks |
| Labels | Charcoal on wood, bark tags |

## General Principles

1. **Cleanliness is non-negotiable** — boil all water, wash all vessels, keep hands clean
2. **Start small** — test a small dose before full therapeutic use
3. **Fresh is best** — use fresh plants whenever possible; medicinal compounds degrade with time
4. **One plant at a time** — don't combine remedies unless you know the interaction
5. **Document everything** — what plant, what part, what preparation, what dose, what result
6. **When in doubt, infusion** — the gentlest, safest method for most plants
