# Duboisia myoporoides

![Corkwood](images/plants/duboisia-myoporoides.jpg)
*Duboisia myoporoides — Corkwood. Caution: extremely toxic if misused. Image: Wikimedia Commons (CC BY-SA).*

## Common Names
- English: Corkwood, poison corkwood, duboisia
- Chinese: 澳洲毒茄 (Àozhōu dú qié — "Australian poison nightshade")
- Aboriginal: Pituri-related names (but note: true pituri is *Duboisia hopwoodii*, a different species also toxic)

## Identification
- **Habitat:** Rainforest margins, moist eucalypt forest along the east coast of Australia from southern NSW to far north Queensland. Often found on the edges of clearings and along watercourses.
- **Leaves:** Glabrous (smooth, hairless), lanceolate to elliptic, 5–15 cm long, 2–5 cm wide, alternate, with a distinct midrib. Light to mid green, slightly glossy. Crushed leaves have an unpleasant, acrid smell.
- **Flowers:** Small (6–8 mm), white, bell-shaped, in terminal panicles. Five petals, slightly reflexed. Appear in spring and summer. Resemble small potato or tomato flowers (same family, Solanaceae).
- **Bark:** Thick, corky, deeply fissured, grey-brown — this is the source of the common name "corkwood." Very distinctive once you know it.
- **Roots:** Not medicinally used (contain alkaloids).
- **Fruit:** Small (4–6 mm), round, purple-black berries. Extremely toxic — do not eat.
- **Look-alikes:** Other rainforest margin trees. The combination of corky bark, Solanaceae-type flowers (bell-shaped, white), smooth lanceolate leaves, and purple-black berries is distinctive. Do not confuse edible Solanaceae (tomato, capsicum, native *Solanum* species) — Duboisia berries are always toxic.

## Distribution
Native to the east coast of Australia from southern NSW (Batemans Bay) to far north Queensland (Cairns region). Also occurs in New Caledonia. Not naturally occurring in China or SEA. Commercial plantations exist in Queensland for pharmaceutical extraction.

## Medicinal Uses

- **Primary (commercial, NOT survival):** Source of scopolamine (hyoscine) — antispasmodic, antiemetic, used in motion sickness patches and as pre-operative medication. Source of hyoscyamine and atropine.
- **Secondary (commercial):** Used in ophthalmology for pupil dilation. Used in treatment of Parkinson's disease.
- **Traditional (Aboriginal):** Some groups used pituri (*D. hopwoodii*) as a stimulant and hunting aid, but this was a distinct species with different alkaloid profiles, and its use required specialised preparation knowledge passed down through generations. Do not attempt to replicate.

## Safety — READ THIS

The lethal dose of corkwood alkaloids is extremely small. Symptoms of poisoning include:
- Dry mouth, dilated pupils, blurred vision
- Rapid heartbeat, flushing, fever
- Confusion, hallucinations, delirium
- Seizures, coma, respiratory failure

**There is no safe dose of crude corkwood preparation. If someone ingests any part of this plant, seek emergency medical care immediately. Activated charcoal may help if administered promptly. Do NOT induce vomiting.**

## Cultivation
Commercially grown in plantations in Queensland for pharmaceutical extraction. Not recommended for home cultivation given extreme toxicity. If you encounter this plant, leave it alone and ensure children cannot access the berries.
