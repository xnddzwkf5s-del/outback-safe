# Melaleuca alternifolia

![Tea Tree](images/plants/melaleuca-alternifolia.jpg)
*Melaleuca alternifolia — Tea tree. Potent antiseptic, antifungal, antibacterial. Image: Wikimedia Commons (CC BY-SA).*

## Common Names
- English: Tea tree, narrow-leaved paperbark, tea tree oil tree
- Chinese: 茶树 (chá shù — "tea tree", though not related to the tea plant *Camellia sinensis*)
- Bundjalung (NSW north coast): Various traditional names

## Identification
- **Habitat:** Swampy, low-lying areas near watercourses in coastal northeastern NSW and southeastern Queensland. Grows on sandy or clay soils with high moisture. Often grows in dense stands.
- **Leaves:** Narrow, linear, 10–35 mm long, 1 mm wide, alternate, soft and needle-like. Strong aromatic scent when crushed — medicinal, sharp, slightly camphorous. Bright green.
- **Flowers:** White to cream, cylindrical spikes 3–5 cm long, appearing in spring and summer. Individual flowers are small with numerous stamens. Spikes appear like fluffy white bottlebrushes.
- **Bark:** Papery, peeling in thin flakes — characteristic of the paperbark group (Melaleuca). White to pale brown.
- **Roots:** Shallow, spreading root system adapted to wet soil — not medicinally used.
- **Look-alikes:** Other Melaleuca species are common. Most are safe but less medicinally potent. Key ID for *M. alternifolia*: needle-like leaves (not broad like *M. quinquenervia*), swampy habitat, papery bark, and the distinctive strong medicinal scent when leaves are crushed.

## Distribution
Native to a small area of northeastern NSW (Clarence and Richmond River catchments) and southeastern Queensland. Now cultivated commercially in plantations throughout coastal NSW and Queensland. Not naturally occurring in China or SEA.

## Medicinal Uses
- **Primary:** Topical antiseptic, antifungal, and antibacterial. Effective against *Staphylococcus aureus* (including MRSA), *Candida albicans*, and many common skin pathogens. Use for wounds, cuts, abrasions, fungal infections (tinea, athlete's foot), and acne.
- **Secondary:** Anti-inflammatory for insect bites and stings. Antiviral activity against herpes simplex (cold sores). Antiseptic mouthwash (heavily diluted). Head lice treatment. Deodorant.
- **Traditional:** Bundjalung people used crushed leaves as a poultice for wounds and skin infections. Leaf infusions were used for sore throats and coughs. The healing properties of water where tea tree leaves had fallen were well known.

## Preparation
- **Infusion:** 1 teaspoon fresh or dried leaves per 250 mL boiling water, steep covered 10 minutes. Drink 1–2 cups daily for sore throat or cough. Also use cooled as a wound wash.
- **Poultice:** Crush a handful of fresh leaves, apply directly to wounds, cover with cloth. Change every 2–3 hours. Excellent field dressing when nothing else is available.
- **Tincture:** Fresh leaves in 60% alcohol, steep 4 weeks. For topical use — dab on skin infections or add to water as a wash. Internal dose: 1–2 mL in water, 2× daily (for respiratory or digestive infections).
- **Oil:** Steam-distilled essential oil. Dilute with a carrier oil (olive, coconut) — 5–10% for skin application, 1–2% for sensitive areas. Never apply undiluted to large areas or broken skin.
- **Steam Inhalation:** Add 3–5 drops of oil or a handful of leaves to hot water, inhale for respiratory congestion.

## Safety
- **Contraindications:** Do not ingest undiluted essential oil — toxic when swallowed. Rare allergic reactions (patch test before first use). Avoid in pregnancy in medicinal doses (limited safety data). Do not use on deep puncture wounds. Do not use in eyes.
- **Side effects:** Skin irritation in ~5% of people (especially if used undiluted). Contact dermatitis with repeated use. Hormonal effects reported with high-dose/long-term topical use in some studies — stick to short courses.
- **Interactions:** No significant known interactions, but separate from other topical treatments.
- **Toxicity:** Essential oil is toxic if ingested. As little as 10 mL can cause confusion, ataxia, and respiratory distress. Keep oil away from children. No known toxicity from leaf preparations.

## Cultivation
- **Propagation:** From seed (very fine, sow on surface, keep moist) or semi-hardwood cuttings (higher success rate). Cuttings root in 4–6 weeks in warm, humid conditions.
- **Growing conditions:** Full sun, moist to wet soil, tolerates poor drainage. Frost-sensitive when young — protect for first 2 winters in cooler areas. Responds well to regular pruning (encourages bushy growth, more leaf yield).
- **Harvest:** Leaves and terminal branchlets harvested year-round. Highest oil content in spring and early summer. Dry in shade. Essential oil extraction requires steam distillation equipment. In survival context, use fresh leaves for poultices and infusions.
