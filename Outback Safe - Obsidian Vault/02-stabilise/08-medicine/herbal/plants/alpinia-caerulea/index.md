# Alpinia caerulea

![Native Ginger](images/plants/alpinia-caerulea.jpg)
*Alpinia caerulea — Native ginger. Edible root and digestive aid. Image: Wikimedia Commons (CC BY-SA).*

## Common Names
- English: Native ginger, blue ginger, Australian ginger
- Chinese: 澳洲山姜 (Àozhōu shān jiāng)
- Aboriginal: Various names including "jabbar" in some Cape York language groups

## Identification
- **Habitat:** Rainforest understory and moist gullies along the east coast of Australia from northern NSW to Cape York in far north Queensland. Prefers deep shade, high moisture, and rich soil. Often found along creek banks.
- **Leaves:** Large (up to 40 cm long), lanceolate, dark glossy green, alternate, arranged in two ranks along the stem. Crushed leaves have a mild gingery fragrance. Leaf sheaths clasp the stem.
- **Flowers:** White, small, borne in erect terminal racemes (10–30 cm long). Individual flowers are delicate with a broad lip. Appear in summer.
- **Fruit:** Most distinctive feature — bright blue, globular berries (10–15 mm diameter), containing numerous small seeds. The blue colour is unusual and comes from aluminium accumulation. Fruit appears in autumn-winter.
- **Roots:** Rhizomes are the key medicinal part. Aromatic, crisp, white inside, with a mild ginger flavour that is less pungent than true ginger (*Zingiber officinale*). Roots spread laterally near the soil surface.
- **Stem:** Upright, reed-like, to 2–3 m tall, formed by leaf sheaths.
- **Look-alikes:** Other Alpinia species (e.g., *A. arundelliana*) look similar but have red fruit (not blue). The blue fruit is the definitive ID. Also similar to other ginger-family plants (Zingiberaceae) — all have aromatic rhizomes and similar leaf structure, but A. caerulea is distinguished by the blue berries.

## Distribution
East coast of Australia from northern NSW (Grafton area) to Cape York, Queensland. Also occurs in New Guinea. Not naturally occurring in China or SEA (but other Alpinia species are common in SEA).

## Medicinal Uses
- **Primary:** Digestive aid. The rhizome contains aromatic compounds similar to culinary ginger — stimulates digestion, reduces bloating, relieves mild nausea, and has carminative (anti-gas) properties. Chew a small piece of fresh root or prepare as infusion.
- **Secondary:** Anti-inflammatory (contains compounds related to gingerols). Mild antimicrobial properties. The fruit pulp is edible and mildly gingery — can be used as a spice or breath freshener.
- **Traditional:** Aboriginal peoples across coastal Queensland used the rhizome chewed for digestive complaints and as a general tonic. Leaves were used to wrap food for cooking (imparting a mild gingery flavour). The fruit was eaten.

## Preparation
- **Infusion:** Slice 1–2 teaspoons fresh rhizome thinly, pour 250 mL boiling water over, steep covered 10 minutes. Drink 2–3 cups daily for digestive support. Pleasant mild gingery flavour.
- **Fresh:** Chew a small slice (1 cm) of fresh root directly for nausea or indigestion. One of the simplest field remedies available.
- **Decoction:** 1 tablespoon chopped rhizome per 500 mL water, simmer 15 minutes. Stronger than infusion. Drink 1/2 cup 2–3× daily.
- **Poultice:** Crush fresh rhizome, apply to mild skin inflammation. Mild warming effect. Cover, leave 20–30 minutes.
- **Food:** Slice young rhizome into stir-fries, soups, stews. Young shoots and leaves are also edible. Blue fruit can be eaten fresh or dried as a spice.

## Safety
- **Contraindications:** Generally very safe. May not be suitable for people with known ginger allergy. Avoid medicinal doses in pregnancy (though food quantities are fine).
- **Side effects:** Rare — heartburn in sensitive individuals if large quantities eaten. Much milder than culinary ginger in this respect.
- **Interactions:** Theoretically additive with blood-thinning medications (similar to culinary ginger, but weaker).
- **Toxicity:** None known. This is one of the safest Australian bush foods. The blue fruit colour is from aluminium but it's bound in a non-toxic form.

## Cultivation
- **Propagation:** Division of rhizomes is easiest — dig up a section with at least 2–3 growing points, replant immediately. Also from fresh seed (sow immediately, viability drops with drying). Seedlings are slow initially.
- **Growing conditions:** Shade to semi-shade (essential — full sun burns leaves). Rich, moist, well-drained soil. Requires consistent moisture — suffers in drought. Protect from frost (subtropical plant). Excellent in pots or as understory planting.
- **Harvest:** Rhizomes harvested year-round. Dig from the edge of the clump — don't take more than 1/3 of the plant at once. Fruit harvested when fully blue in autumn-winter. Young shoots harvested in spring-summer.
