# Tasmannia lanceolata

![Mountain Pepper](images/plants/tasmannia-lanceolata.jpg)
*Tasmannia lanceolata — Mountain pepper. Antimicrobial spice, digestive stimulant. Image: Wikimedia Commons (CC BY-SA).*

## Common Names
- English: Mountain pepper, Tasmanian pepperberry, native pepper
- Chinese: 山胡椒 (shān hújiāo — "mountain pepper", though this name is shared with other pepper-like plants)
- Aboriginal: Various names across Tasmanian Aboriginal language groups

## Identification
- **Habitat:** Cool temperate rainforest, wet sclerophyll forest, and subalpine woodland of Tasmania and the high country of Victoria and southern NSW. Prefers moist, well-drained soils in partial shade. Often found in gullies and along streams above 300 m elevation.
- **Leaves:** Lanceolate to oblanceolate, 5–12 cm long, dark glossy green above, paler underneath. Young leaves and stems are distinctively red. Aromatic when crushed — peppery, spicy, with a hint of eucalyptus. Leaf margins are entire (smooth).
- **Flowers:** Small (1–1.5 cm), creamy white to pale yellow, with 4–8 narrow petals. Male and female flowers on separate plants (dioecious). Appear in spring to early summer.
- **Fruit:** The key ID feature. Dark purple to black, globular berries (5–8 mm), on female plants. Appear in late summer to autumn. Intensely peppery and spicy when tasted (the "pepper" part of the name). Each berry contains multiple small seeds.
- **Bark:** Smooth, reddish-brown on young branches, becoming darker with age. Not medicinally used.
- **Look-alikes:** Other Tasmannia species (e.g., *T. stipitata*, Dorrigo pepper from northern NSW) have similar leaves but different fruit and geographic range. Do not confuse with toxic plants that have similar-shaped leaves — crush and smell: the distinctive HOT peppery aroma is unmistakeable. Also similar in appearance to some *Euonymus* species (toxic) — but Tasmannia has the unmistakable peppery taste/smell.

## Distribution
Tasmania, Victoria (Otways, high country), and southern NSW (Snowy Mountains, Brindabella Range). Isolated populations at higher elevations. Not naturally occurring in China or SEA, but similar species exist elsewhere.

## Medicinal Uses
- **Primary:** Antimicrobial — the essential oil (polygodial) has demonstrated strong activity against bacteria, fungi, and yeast. Use as a food preservative and antimicrobial spice. Also effective topically for skin infections.
- **Secondary:** Digestive stimulant. The spicy compounds stimulate gastric secretions, aiding digestion of protein-rich foods. Antioxidant (berries are particularly high in anthocyanins). Warming circulatory stimulant.
- **Traditional:** Aboriginal Tasmanians used the leaves and berries as a medicinal spice, particularly for stomach complaints and as a general health tonic. Leaves were also used externally as a counter-irritant for arthritis and muscle pain. The berries were a valued food and medicine.

## Preparation
- **Infusion:** 1/2 teaspoon dried leaves (it's very strong) per 250 mL boiling water, steep 5–8 minutes. Drink 1 cup after meals for sluggish digestion. Can also be used as an antimicrobial gargle for sore throat.
- **Spice (internal):** Crush dried leaves or berries and add to food as you would black pepper. The antimicrobial properties make it doubly valuable — flavour AND food safety.
- **Poultice:** Crush fresh leaves, apply to localised pain or fungal skin infections. The warming sensation is therapeutic. Leave 15–30 minutes.
- **Tincture:** Dried leaves/berries in 50% alcohol, steep 4 weeks. Dose: 1–2 mL in water, 2–3× daily for digestive support or as antimicrobial.
- **Infused Oil:** Dried leaves in olive oil for 2–4 weeks, strain — use as warming massage oil for muscle aches (not on broken skin).

## Safety
- **Contraindications:** May irritate gastric ulcers or severe reflux — the spiciness can aggravate these. Avoid in pregnancy in medicinal quantities (as with most hot spices). Do not get in eyes.
- **Side effects:** The pepperiness is INTENSE — overdose means burning mouth and throat, not toxicity. The sensation fades in 10–15 minutes. Water helps. Skin irritation if applied undiluted.
- **Interactions:** Theoretically additive with blood-thinning medication (spicy herbs generally have mild antiplatelet effects).
- **Toxicity:** None known. Safe in food quantities. Very high doses of polygodial (the active compound) can be irritating but not systemically toxic.

## Cultivation
- **Propagation:** Fresh seed (requires cold stratification — sow in autumn and leave through winter). Germination is slow and irregular (3–12 months). Also from semi-hardwood cuttings with hormone treatment. Difficult from cuttings.
- **Growing conditions:** Part shade to full sun (in cooler climates). Moist, well-drained, slightly acidic soil. Prefers cool temperate climates — struggles in heat and humidity. Protect from hot afternoon sun and drying winds. Mulch well.
- **Harvest:** Leaves harvested year-round. Berries harvested when fully black in late summer/autumn. Prune to maintain bushy shape and increase leaf yield. Dry leaves in shade at low temperature.
