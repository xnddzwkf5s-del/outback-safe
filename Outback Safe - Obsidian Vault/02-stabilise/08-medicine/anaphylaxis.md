# Anaphylaxis & Severe Allergic Reactions

Anaphylaxis is a severe, life-threatening allergic reaction that can kill within minutes. In remote Australia, where help is hours away, correct management in the first few minutes determines survival. There is no "bush fix" for anaphylaxis — but there are critical steps you can take that buy enough time for evacuation.

---

## 1. What Is Anaphylaxis?

Anaphylaxis is more than just a bad allergic reaction. It involves **two or more body systems** and can progress to complete airway obstruction or cardiovascular collapse.

### Common Triggers
- **Food:** Peanuts, tree nuts, shellfish, fish, eggs, milk, soy, sesame, wheat
- **Insect stings:** Bees, wasps, European wasps, bulldog ants, jack jumper ants
- **Medications:** Antibiotics (especially penicillins), NSAIDs (aspirin, ibuprofen), anaesthetic drugs
- **Other:** Latex, exercise (rare — exercise-induced anaphylaxis), idiopathic (no identified trigger)

### Body Systems Involved
| System | Signs |
|--------|-------|
| **AIRWAY** | Swelling of tongue/throat, difficulty breathing, hoarse voice, stridor (noisy breathing) |
| **BREATHING** | Wheezing, coughing, chest tightness, rapid breathing |
| **CIRCULATION** | Pale, cold skin, rapid weak pulse, low blood pressure, collapse, unconsciousness |
| **SKIN** | Hives, swelling (angioedema), flushing, itching — can be **ABSENT** in severe anaphylaxis |

---

## 2. Recognition — Any One of These = Anaphylaxis Until Proven Otherwise

If someone has been exposed to a known or possible trigger and has **ANY** of these symptoms, treat as anaphylaxis:

- Difficulty breathing or noisy breathing
- Swelling of the tongue or throat
- Hoarse voice or difficulty talking
- Persistent cough or wheeze
- Difficulty swallowing
- Persistent dizziness, feeling faint, or collapse
- Pale and floppy (in young children)
- Abdominal pain, vomiting (can be the only sign in some cases)

{% warning() %}
**Do not wait for skin symptoms.** Hives may not appear, especially in severe anaphylaxis where circulation is shutting down. If the airway or breathing is affected, it is anaphylaxis.
{% end %}

## 3. Adrenaline (Epinephrine) — How to Use an EpiPen

Adrenaline is the **only first-line treatment** for anaphylaxis. It works within minutes — it tightens blood vessels (raising blood pressure), relaxes airway muscles, and reduces swelling. Antihistamines and steroids are **adjuncts**, not replacements.

### EpiPen (Auto-Injector)
- **Adult EpiPen:** 300 micrograms (0.3mg) adrenaline
- **EpiPen Jr (child under 20kg):** 150 micrograms (0.15mg) adrenaline
- **AnaPen:** Australian alternative brand, same drug (adrenaline)
- **Always carry TWO devices** — one dose may not be enough

### Step-by-Step EpiPen Use

1. **Form a fist** around the auto-injector — thumb and fingers completely around the body. Never put your thumb over either end — the needle deploys from the orange tip

2. **Remove BLUE safety cap** (blue = sky, remember: "blue to the sky, orange to the thigh")

3. **Place ORANGE tip** against the **OUTER MID-THIGH** — can go through clothing (do not remove pants — it is fast and sharp enough to penetrate most fabric)

4. **Push firmly** until you hear/feel a **CLICK** — this means the needle has deployed

5. **Hold in place for 3 seconds** to ensure the full dose is delivered

6. **Remove** and massage the injection site for 10 seconds

7. **Call 000 immediately** (or activate PLB if in remote area)

8. **Monitor** — if no improvement after **5 minutes**, give the **second EpiPen**

9. **If breathing stops:** start CPR immediately

---

## 4. Adrenaline from an Ampoule (If EpiPen Not Available)

In remote areas with medical kits, adrenaline may be available in ampoules. This is an **advanced skill** — only attempt if an EpiPen is not available and the person is deteriorating.

### Preparation
- Adrenaline 1:1000 (1mg/mL) — commonly found in comprehensive remote medical kits
- Draw up into a syringe using aseptic technique

### Dosage
| Patient | Dose (IM) |
|---------|-----------|
| Adult | 0.5mL (0.5mg) |
| Child > 20kg | 0.3mL (0.3mg) |
| Child 10-20kg | 0.15mL (0.15mg) |
| Child < 10kg | 0.1mL (0.1mg) |
| Approximate formula | 0.01mL per kg body weight |

### Administration
- **Injection site:** Outer mid-thigh (vastus lateralis muscle) — same as EpiPen
- **Needle:** Use the shortest needle available (25mm or less)
- **Route:** Intramuscular (IM) only — do NOT inject IV unless trained to do so (IV can cause dangerous arrhythmias)

## 5. Position — Critical Distinction

Positioning depends on the PRIMARY symptom:

**If the person has BREATHING DIFFICULTY (swollen tongue/throat, wheezing, stridor):**
- SIT THEM UPRIGHT. Gravity helps keep the airway open.
- Do NOT lie them flat — this can worsen airway obstruction.

**If the person is FAINT/DIZZY/HYPOTENSIVE (pale, cold, weak pulse, about to collapse):**
- LIE FLAT WITH LEGS ELEVATED (Trendelenburg position). This returns blood from the legs to the central circulation.
- If they become unconscious: RECOVERY POSITION.

**If the person has BOTH breathing difficulty AND signs of shock:**
- Semi-reclined position — sitting but leaning back at ~45 degrees. This is a compromise that helps both airway AND circulation.
- Monitor continuously — if breathing worsens, sit them more upright. If they lose consciousness despite this, lie them flat.

**Pregnant woman:**
- Lie on the LEFT side. The uterus can compress the inferior vena cava when lying flat, reducing blood return to the heart. Left side avoids this.

**Unconscious person:**
- RECOVERY POSITION (on their side). This protects the airway if they vomit.
- Continue to monitor breathing — anaphylaxis can cause delayed airway swelling.

**DO NOT:**
- Let the person stand or walk. Walking pumps allergen through the system and causes rapid decompensation.
- Let the person go to the toilet alone. Vasovagal response on the toilet (bearing down) can trigger cardiovascular collapse in a person already hypotensive.

## 6. In Remote Areas Without Help

Anaphylaxis is one of the few conditions in the outback where you **must** evacuate. There is no bush remedy for a swollen airway.

### What to Do When Help Is Hours Away

1. **Give adrenaline immediately** — the first dose
2. **Call 000** or activate PLB
3. **Give antihistamines** — Zyrtec (cetirizine), Telfast (fexofenadine), Claratyne (loratadine). Give AFTER adrenaline, NOT instead of it. Antihistamines work too slowly (peak effect 1-2 hours) to treat acute anaphylaxis
4. **Give steroids** (prednisolone or hydrocortisone) — if available in your medical kit. Give AFTER adrenaline. Takes 4-6 hours to work. Reduces the late-phase reaction
5. **Position appropriately** (see table above)
6. **Monitor continuously** — watch for signs of biphasic reaction

### Biphasic Reaction (Important!)
- About **20% of anaphylaxis cases** have a second reaction
- Occurs **4-12 hours** after the initial reaction
- Can be as severe as the first reaction — or worse
- **Anyone who has had anaphylaxis must be observed for 24 hours**
- This is why hospital observation is mandatory even if the person seems better

---

## 7. Prevention

- If you have known severe allergies: **ALWAYS carry 2 EpiPens.** Always. Without exception
- **Check expiry dates** before every trip. An expired EpiPen is better than no EpiPen (reduced potency but still contains active adrenaline)
- **Tell travel companions** where your EpiPen is kept and how to use it — you may be unconscious when it is needed
- **Medical alert bracelet** in outback areas — critical if you are found unconscious
- **Avoidance:**
  - Food allergies: read ingredients carefully, ask about cooking methods, carry safe snacks
  - Insect sting allergy: cover skin, avoid perfumes and bright colours, check campsites for wasp nests, do not walk barefoot near flowering plants
  - Medication allergy: carry a written list of allergies in your wallet and on your phone

## 8. Anaphylaxis Action Plan (Print This)

| Step | Action |
|------|--------|
| **1** | **Recognise:** difficulty breathing, swelling, collapse, or any one sign after allergen exposure |
| **2** | **Give adrenaline (EpiPen)** — outer mid-thigh, through clothing |
| **3** | **Call 000** or activate PLB — say "anaphylaxis" |
| **4** | **Position appropriately** — sit up if breathing difficulty, lie flat if faint |
| **5** | **Give second EpiPen** if no improvement after 5 minutes |
| **6** | **Give antihistamines and steroids** if available (after adrenaline) |
| **7** | **Monitor continuously** — be prepared to start CPR |
| **8** | **Observe for 24 hours** even if improved — biphasic reaction risk |
