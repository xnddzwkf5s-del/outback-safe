# Black Powder

Black powder is the original propellant. For over 800 years it was the only practical explosive and firearm propellant known to humanity. It remained military-standard until the 1890s and is still used today in historical re-enactment, fireworks, and certain specialised applications.

## History

### Origins: Chinese Alchemy (9th Century)

The discovery was accidental. Tang Dynasty alchemists mixing saltpetre, sulfur, and charcoal in search of an immortality elixir noticed their mixture "flew up and burnt their hands and faces, and even the whole house where they were working burned down." By 1044 CE, the formula appears in an official military manual with three distinct recipes for bombs, smoke screens, and incendiary arrows.

### The Formula Travels West

| Century | Development |
|---------|-------------|
| 11th | Chinese fire lances — bamboo tubes packed with powder and projectiles |
| 13th | Mongols use gunpowder weapons in their conquests, transmitting knowledge to the Islamic world |
| 1267 | Roger Bacon records an encrypted formula in *Opus Majus* |
| 1326 | First European cannon depicted (Walter de Milemete manuscript) |
| 1346 | English use cannons at the Battle of Crécy — more psychological than effective |
| 1420s | "Corning" invented — wet incorporation and granulation revolutionises powder consistency |
| 1453 | Ottoman bombards breach the walls of Constantinople — the era of castle walls ends |

### The Corning Revolution

Before corning (pre-1420), gunpowder was "serpentine powder" — a dry-mixed fine dust. It was terrible. The ingredients separated during transport (saltpetre sinks, charcoal floats). It absorbed moisture instantly. Burn rate was unpredictable.

Corning — wetting the powder, pressing it into cakes, then breaking and sieving into grains — solved all these problems. It was arguably the most important advance in powder technology, more significant than any ratio tweak. Corned powder:
- Burns consistently from grain to grain
- Does not separate during transport
- Absorbs moisture far more slowly
- Can be graded by size for different applications

## Chemistry of Combustion

### The Reaction

Black powder does not detonate — it **deflagrates** (burns rapidly at subsonic speed). This is critical to understand: black powder is a low explosive, not a high explosive. In a confined space (gun barrel), the rapid gas generation creates pressure that propels a projectile. Unconfined, it burns with a satisfying "whoof" but does not shatter.

The idealised reaction:

```
10 KNO₃ + 3 S + 8 C → 2 K₂CO₃ + 3 K₂SO₄ + 6 CO₂ + 5 N₂
```

### Why the 75:15:10 Ratio?

The ratio is stoichiometrically close to optimal — it provides enough oxygen (from KNO₃) to oxidise all the carbon to CO₂ and all the sulfur to K₂SO₄, while leaving potassium to form stable carbonate and sulfate salts.

**Deviations and consequences:**

| Ratio (KNO₃:S:C) | Result |
|-----------------|--------|
| 75:15:10 | Standard — balanced power, fouling, and ignition |
| 75:10:15 | More charcoal — slower burn, more gas volume, more smoke. Used historically for blasting. |
| 78:10:12 | More saltpetre — faster, hotter burn. Barrel wear increases. Used in some 19th c. sporting powders. |
| 70:15:15 | Less saltpetre — incomplete combustion, heavy fouling. Used only in very low-quality emergency powder. |
| 80:20:0 | No sulfur — "sulfurless powder." Requires high ignition temp. Used only where sulfur is unavailable. |

### Gas Generation

1 g of black powder produces approximately:
- 270–330 cm³ of gas (at STP)
- 3,000 J of energy
- Solid residue: approximately 0.56 g (white smoke — potassium carbonate and sulfate particles)

The solids-to-gas ratio (~56:44) means more than half the powder mass becomes barrel-fouling solids. This is why black powder firearms require frequent cleaning — modern smokeless powder produces almost entirely gaseous products.

## Manufacturing Process

A consolidated reference for the complete process. The linked gunpowder pages provide detailed instructions for each ingredient.

### Phase 1: Ingredient Preparation

| Step | Ingredient | Key Quality Metric |
|------|-----------|-------------------|
| Source saltpetre | KNO₃ | Purity > 95% |
| Purify by re-crystallisation | KNO₃ | Clear needle crystals, violet flame test |
| Source sulfur | Natural S | Bright yellow, melts at 115°C |
| Purify by melting/filtration | S | No visible impurities, no arsenic smell |
| Select wood | Willow, alder, bamboo | Low-density, fast-growing species |
| Char in retort | Charcoal | ~70% fixed carbon, brownish-black, "pings" when struck |
| Grind and sieve | Charcoal | Impalpable powder, < 2% ash |

### Phase 2: Incorporation

1. Weigh ingredients: 75 g KNO₃, 15 g charcoal, 10 g sulfur (adjusting for total batch)
2. Sieve each dry ingredient through fine mesh
3. Mix charcoal and sulfur dry (no explosion risk)
4. Add water to charcoal-sulfur mix (approximately 30 mL per 100 g)
5. Gradually add saltpetre while stirring continuously
6. Work into uniform paste (15–20 minutes of kneading/folding)
7. The paste should be uniform dark grey with no streaks

### Phase 3: Granulation

1. Press paste into a flat cake approximately 1 cm thick
2. Air-dry until leather-hard (2–4 hours in shade)
3. Break into chunks and push through sieves to grade
4. Separate grain sizes by stacked sieves
5. Dry granules completely (24–48 hours in warm, ventilated shade)
6. Turn granules gently every 4–6 hours

### Phase 4: Testing and Storage

1. Conduct burn line test with 1 g sample
2. Conduct flash test for ignition sensitivity
3. If available, conduct firing comparison test
4. Log batch data: date, ingredient sources, ratios, grain size, test results
5. Package in copper or wood containers
6. Store in proper magazine at safe distance

## Grain Size Reference Guide

| Grain | Mesh Range | Burn Characteristic | Primary Use |
|-------|-----------|-------------------|-------------|
| Meal | Passes 100-mesh | Instant flash | Priming, flash powder, fireworks |
| FFFFg | 0.5–0.8 mm | Very fast | Priming pans (flintlocks) |
| FFFg | 0.8–1.2 mm | Fast | Pistols, small-bore rifles (< .40 cal) |
| FFg | 1.2–1.7 mm | Medium | Muskets, shotguns, rifles (.40–.60 cal) |
| Fg | 1.7–2.4 mm | Slow-medium | Large muskets, blunderbusses |
| Cannon #1 | 2.4–4.0 mm | Slow | Field cannons, mines, blasting |
| Cannon #2 | 4.0–8.0 mm | Very slow | Siege cannons, large blasting |
| Cannon #3 | 8.0–15 mm | Slowest | Largest bombards, quarrying |

{% info() %}
**Grain size is not optional.** Using FFFFg in a musket-load can burst the barrel — it burns too fast, pressure spikes before the ball moves. Using cannon powder for priming will cause a hangfire (slow ignition). Match grain size to application.

## Uses Beyond Firearms

### Blasting

Black powder was the primary industrial explosive until dynamite (1867). For blasting:

- Drill a hole in the rock
- Charge with coarse powder (cannon grade or larger)
- Tamp with dry clay (not sand — sand becomes shrapnel)
- Fuse with a length of match cord or slow fuse
- **Clear the area to at least 200 m**

### Signaling

A small charge of FFFg powder, confined in a tube with one end open, produces a loud report useful for:
- Emergency signaling (three shots = distress, universal signal)
- Communication over distance (pre-arranged patterns)
- Scaring predators from livestock

### Fire Starting

A pinch of meal powder in tinder catches a spark from flint and steel reliably, even in damp conditions. Carry a small amount in a waterproof container specifically for fire starting.

### Trade Good

In a long-term survival scenario, gunpowder is an extremely valuable trade commodity. A competent powder maker can trade for food, tools, and protection. Factor this into your group's economic planning — see Tier 3 Trade & Economics.

## The End of the Black Powder Era

Black powder was fully superseded by smokeless powder in military use by the 1890s. Smokeless powder (nitrocellulose-based) offers:
- Three times the energy per gram
- Almost no smoke (critical for not revealing position)
- Far less fouling
- Consistent performance regardless of humidity

However, **smokeless powder requires an industrial chemical plant** — concentrated nitric and sulfuric acids, precise temperature control, and stabilisers. It is not manufacturable in a post-collapse environment. Black powder, requiring only naturally-available minerals and charcoal, remains the only viable propellant for sustained post-collapse firearm use.
