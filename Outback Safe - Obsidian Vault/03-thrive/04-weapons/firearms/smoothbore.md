# Smoothbore Muskets

The smoothbore musket was the standard infantry weapon worldwide from approximately 1600 to 1850. It is a deceptively simple machine: a tube, a lock, a stock. But each component must be made correctly or the result is a pipe bomb held against your face.

## Barrel Making

The barrel is the most difficult component to manufacture. Medieval and early-modern gunsmiths spent years mastering barrel forging.

### Method 1: Forged and Welded (Traditional)

A flat iron bar (skelp) is heated and hammered around a mandrel, with the edges forge-welded to form a tube.

```
Step 1: Flat iron bar (skelp)
    ════════════════════════════

Step 2: Hammered into U-channel around mandrel
    ╭───────────────────────────╮
    │                           │
    ╰──╮                     ╭──╯
       └─────────────────────┘

Step 3: Forge-welded seam, then bored true
    ╭─────────────────────────╮
    │                         │
    ╰─────────────────────────╯
```

{% steps() %}
Forge a flat iron bar (skelp) — The bar length equals barrel length. The bar width equals barrel circumference. Thickness: 6–8 mm for small-bore, 8–12 mm for musket-bore.;
Heat the skelp to welding heat (white-hot, ~1200°C) — Work under a smithing hammer.;
Bend the skelp around a tapered mandrel — The mandrel is slightly smaller in diameter than the desired bore. Use a swage block with a half-round groove.;
Forge-weld the seam — Overlap the edges of the skelp and hammer at welding heat until they fuse. Flux with clean quartz sand (silica) to prevent oxidation at the weld.;
Repeat welding passes — 3–5 passes, reheating between each pass. The weld must be perfect — any gap becomes a burst point.;
Ream the bore — Using a long steel boring bit (similar to a gun drill). The reamer must run true to produce a straight, smooth bore.;
Proof the barrel — Double-charge with powder and two balls, fire remotely (see testing below).;
Polish the exterior — File and polish to final dimensions.;
Fit the breech plug — A threaded plug (or forge-welded plug) seals the rear of the barrel.
{% end %}

### Method 2: Seamless Tube (If Available)

If you have access to seamless steel tubing (salvaged from modern machinery, hydraulic cylinders, etc.), barrel making becomes dramatically easier. The tube must be:

- **Seamless** (not welded pipe — welded pipe splits along the seam under pressure)
- **Bore is already smooth** (or can be honed)
- **Wall thickness at least 3 mm** for small-bore (under .50 cal), 4–5 mm for musket-bore
- **Material is mild steel or better** (not cast iron, which shatters)

### Method 3: Bamboo (Emergency/SEA Only)

Historically used in China and Southeast Asia for early firearms ("fire lances"). A thick-walled bamboo pole (at least 4 cm outer diameter, 1 cm wall thickness) can fire a few shots with reduced charges.

Construction: Select aged bamboo at least 3 years old. Remove nodes internally with a long rod. Reinforce the breech end by wrapping tightly with wet rawhide, which shrinks as it dries. Bind with copper wire. Use half-size powder charges only.

## Bore Dimensions

Common historical calibres for smoothbore muskets:

| Designation | Bore Diameter | Ball Diameter | Typical Charge | Use |
|-------------|---------------|---------------|----------------|-----|
| .69 cal (French) | 17.5 mm | 16.5 mm | 5–7 g | Military musket |
| .75 cal (Brown Bess) | 19.1 mm | 17.5 mm | 6–8 g | British military |
| .62 cal (Fowler) | 15.7 mm | 14.7 mm | 4–6 g | Hunting/sporting |
| .50 cal (Small-bore) | 12.7 mm | 11.9 mm | 3–5 g | Small game |
| 12-gauge | 18.5 mm | — | 4–6 g | Shotgun equivalent |

The ball is always undersized relative to the bore — typically 1.0–1.5 mm smaller. The patch (cloth or paper wrapped around the ball) fills the gap and engages the bore.

### Choosing Your Calibre

- **Small game (rabbits, birds):** .50 cal or 12-gauge with shot
- **Medium game (deer, wallabies):** .62–.69 cal
- **Large game (cattle, buffalo):** .69–.75 cal
- **Defense:** .69–.75 cal (psychological effect, stopping power)
- **Ammunition economy:** Smaller calibre uses less lead and less powder per shot

**AU Note:** For typical Australian game (kangaroo, wallaby, feral pig, goat), a .62 cal smoothbore is a good all-rounder.

## Stock Construction

The stock holds the barrel and lock in alignment with the shooter's body.

    └──────┬──────┘                    └──────────────┘

    ┌──────▼──────────────────────────────────────────┐
    │                                                 │

    │   └───┘                                         │

### Suitable Woods

| Wood | Region | Notes |
|------|--------|-------|
| **Walnut** (European/American) | Temperate | The gold standard. Strong, stable, carves well. |
| **Maple** (sugar/rock) | Temperate | Excellent. Very hard, takes fine detail. |
| **Beech** | Europe, Asia | Common military stock wood. Adequate. |
| **Acacia** (blackwood, mulga) | **AU** | Excellent dense hardwoods. Australian acacias are superb stock woods. |
| **Jarrah** | **AU (WA)** | Very hard, stable. Difficult to work but extremely durable. |
| **Teak** | **SEA** | Excellent weather resistance. Hard on tools (silica content). |
| **Bamboo laminate** | **SEA, CN** | Split, glue, and press bamboo strips into a composite. |

### Stock Inletting

The barrel channel and lock mortise must be cut precisely. The barrel should sit in its channel with full-length contact (no high spots that create pressure points). The breech must seat firmly against solid wood — this is where all the recoil force is transmitted.

{% steps() %}
Plane the stock blank to approximate shape — Leave 3–5 mm excess on all surfaces.;
Cut the barrel channel — Using a round-bottomed plane or gouge matching the barrel profile. Work slowly, checking fit frequently with lampblack (soot the barrel, press into channel, cut away black marks).;
Inlet the lock mortise — The lock plate must fit flush with the stock surface. The internal cavity must accommodate the mechanism without binding.;
Inlet the trigger and trigger guard — Trigger pivot should be smooth with minimal play.;
Fit the breech plug tang — The rearmost extension of the barrel that screws into the wrist of the stock.;
Shape the exterior — Rasp and file to final profile. Sand smooth.;
Finish — Boiled linseed oil, tung oil, or a traditional varnish. Multiple thin coats, rubbed in.
{% end %}

## Loading Procedure

The standard drill, unchanged for 200 years. Practise this until it is automatic — and always follow the safety checks.

{% steps() %}
**Prime or half-cock** — Place the lock on half-cock (safety position). The cock is held back from the pan, but the trigger is locked.;
**Load powder** — Pour a measured charge of powder down the barrel. Use a pre-measured charger — NEVER pour from a powder horn or flask directly into the barrel. A glowing ember can ignite the flask.;
**Seat the charge** — Tap the butt on the ground once to settle powder into the breech.;
**Insert ball and patch** — Place a lubricated cloth patch over the muzzle, place the ball on top, and start it with the short starter. The patch should be lubricated (spit, tallow, beeswax-lard mix).;
**Ram the ball home** — Using the ramrod, push the ball down in steady strokes until it seats firmly on the powder. Do NOT bounce the ramrod — this can deform the ball or crush powder grains.;
**Mark the ramrod** — When the ball is seated, the ramrod should protrude a specific distance. Mark this on the rod so you can verify seat by sight.;
**Prime the pan** — For flintlock: pour a small amount of FFFFg or meal powder into the priming pan (approximately 0.2–0.3 g). Close the frizzen.;
**Full cock** — Bring the cock to full-cock position. The weapon is now ready to fire.;
**FIRE** — Aim, squeeze trigger. After firing, note if the shot felt normal. Weak report = possible squib. Wait 30 seconds before investigating.
{% end %}

## Cleaning

Black powder fouling is hygroscopic and corrosive — it absorbs moisture from the air and rusts steel within hours.

{% steps() %}
Remove the barrel from the stock if possible — If pinned, remove pins. If fixed, clean barrel in-situ but protect the stock from water.;
Plug the touch hole — A wooden toothpick or matchstick.;
Pour warm (not boiling) water into the barrel — Fill to about half. Cover muzzle with thumb.;
Slosh vigorously — Tip barrel up and down to wash the breech area.;
Pour out — The water will be black. Repeat until water runs clear.;
Scrub with a bronze bore brush — Wrapped with cleaning patch. Never use steel brushes — they scratch the bore.;
Dry thoroughly — Dry patches until they come out clean. Then oil patch.;
Oil the bore — Animal fat (lard), vegetable oil (linseed), or mineral oil. A rust-preventative film is essential.;
Clean the lock — Remove, brush out fouling, oil all moving parts.;
Clean the stock channel — Fouling blows back into the lock area and under the barrel. If neglected, it rots the wood and rusts the barrel from underneath.
{% end %}

After the first cleaning, check the barrel 24 hours later — a surprising amount of additional fouling will have loosened. Run an oiled patch through again.

## Proof Testing

Every newly-made barrel must be proofed before anyone fires it from the shoulder.

{% steps() %}
Secure the barrel in a test stand — A heavy timber frame, barrel pointed downrange. The stand should absorb recoil and contain fragments.;
Double-charge the barrel — Twice the normal service charge of powder.;
Load with two balls — Double projectile weight for maximum pressure.;
Prime/flintlock as normal.;
Retreat to safe distance (at least 20 m) and fire by pulling a string attached to the trigger — Or by touching a long fuse to a primed pan.;
Examine the barrel — Look for bulges, cracks, split seams, or the breech plug shifting.;
If it passes, mark the barrel — A proof mark (a stamped symbol or engraved mark) indicates the barrel has been proofed. This is your barrel's certification.
{% end %}

A barrel that passes double-charge proof is safe for normal service loads. A barrel that bulges but does not burst should be scrapped — a bulge is a future burst point.

{% info() %}
**The "Brown Bess" British musket proof load:** The Tower of London proof house used a charge of approximately 20 g of powder behind two balls. The standard service charge for a .75 cal Bess was approximately 8 g. This 2.5× proof factor was considered adequate for a service life of thousands of rounds.
{% end %}
