# Walls

Walls are the foundation of fortification. They define your perimeter, slow attackers, and provide a fighting platform for defenders.

## Wall Types (Ranked by Construction Difficulty)

| Type | Construction Time | Materials | Defense Rating | Lifespan |
|------|------------------|-----------|---------------|----------|
| **Earth rampart** | 1–2 weeks (20 m length, 10 people) | Earth, turf | ★★★ | Decades (with maintenance) |
| **Timber palisade** | 2–4 weeks (20 m length, 10 people) | Logs, earth | ★★★★ | 10–20 years |
| **Earth + timber combination** | 3–6 weeks | Earth, logs, stone facing | ★★★★★ | 20–50 years |
| **Dry stone wall** | 4–8 weeks | Stone (abundant on site) | ★★★★★ | Centuries |
| **Mortared stone** | 12+ weeks | Stone, lime mortar | ★★★★★ | Centuries |

## Earth Rampart

An earth rampart is the fastest substantial wall to build. It consists of excavated earth piled into a continuous mound with a ditch on the outside.

```
    OUTSIDE                                  INSIDE
    
    ┌───────┐                    ╔══════════╗
    │ DITCH │                    ║ RAMPART  ║
    │       │                    ║          ║
    │  ~2 m │                    ║  ~3 m    ║
    │  deep │                    ║  above   ║
    │       │                    ║  ground  ║
    └───┬───┘                    ║  level   ║
        │                        ╚══════════╝
        └────────── 4-6 m ──────────────────┘
```

### Construction

{% steps() %}
Mark the wall line — Outer face of the ditch is the forward edge.;
Excavate the ditch — 2–3 m deep, 3–4 m wide at the top, sloping to 1 m wide at the bottom. The spoil (excavated earth) is piled on the inside to form the rampart.;
Build the rampart — Pile earth 3–4 m above original ground level. Slope the outer face at 60° (steeper = harder to climb). Slope the inner face at 45° (gentle = easier for defenders to access).;
Compact the earth in layers — Each 30 cm layer should be tamped firmly before adding the next. Poor compaction leads to slumping.;
Face the outer slope — Turf (grass-side down), stone, or timber revetment to prevent erosion.;
Plant the rampart — Grass or fast-growing ground cover on the slopes prevents rain erosion.;
Build a fighting step (banquette) — A step on the inside face, approximately 1.5 m below the top, where defenders stand. This allows defenders to fire over the parapet while remaining partially protected.
{% end %}
```

### Dimensions

| Parameter | Minimum | Recommended |
|-----------|---------|-------------|
| Ditch depth | 1.5 m | 2.5–3.0 m |
| Ditch width (top) | 3 m | 4–5 m |
| Rampart height (above ground) | 2 m | 3–4 m |
| Rampart width (top) | 1.5 m | 2–3 m |
| Outer slope | 60° | 60–70° |
| Inner slope | 45° | 45° |

### The Ditch

The ditch serves multiple purposes:
- Provides earth for the rampart
- Creates an additional obstacle
- Attackers in the ditch are below your line of sight from the rampart — but also trapped and vulnerable to dropped objects
- A dry ditch can be filled with obstacles (sharpened stakes, caltrops)
- A wet ditch (moat) is even better — see Moats page

{% info() %}
**The "V" profile ditch** — a ditch with steep sides and a narrow bottom — is more effective than a wide, shallow ditch. An attacker who falls in cannot easily climb out, especially with the rampart towering above them on the other side.
{% end %}

## Timber Palisade

A palisade is a fence of vertical logs set into the ground. It is faster to build than a stone wall and provides better close-in defense than a bare earth rampart.

```
        OUTSIDE                        INSIDE
    
    ┌────────────────────────────────────────┐
    │                                        │
    │  ┃┃┃┃┃┃┃┃┃┃┃┃┃┃┃┃┃┃┃┃┃┃┃┃┃┃┃┃┃┃┃  │
    │  ┃                                        ┃  │
    │  ┃    LOG PALISADE                         ┃  │  ← Fighting
    │  ┃    3-4 m high                           ┃  │     platform
    │  ┃                                        ┃  │     (inside)
    │  ┃                                        ┃  │
    │  ┃                                        ┃  │
    └──┴────────────────────────────────────────┴──┘
       ↑
    Set 1 m into ground
```

### Materials

- **Logs:** Straight, de-barked, 3–4 m long, 15–25 cm diameter
- **Best woods:** Rot-resistant species — oak, cedar, cypress, jarrah, ironbark, teak
- **Pointed tops:** Sharpened points deter climbing. Can be fire-hardened.
- **Quantity:** Approximately 5–7 logs per metre of palisade (for 15 cm logs with small gaps)

### Construction

{% steps() %}
Prepare logs — De-bark all logs. Sharpen the top end to a point (axe or adze). The bottom end is cut flat.;
Dig a trench — 1 m deep × 40 cm wide along the wall line. Deeper is better — 1.0–1.2 m prevents attackers from digging under or levering out logs.;
Char the buried portion — Scorch the bottom 1.2 m of each log in a fire. Charring dramatically improves rot resistance in the ground.;
Set the logs — Drop each log into the trench, point upward. Butted tightly together, or with small gaps (2–3 cm) for arrow loops.;
Backfill the trench — Earth and stone packed tightly around the base of each log. Tamp firmly.;
Level the tops — Cut or trim to a consistent height (optional — uneven tops are harder to climb but may interfere with fighting platform).;
Bind the top — A horizontal timber (wall plate) pegged or lashed along the inside face at the top ties the palisade together.;
Build a fighting platform — A walkway 1.5–2 m above ground on the inside. Defenders stand on this to shoot over the palisade.;
Add a parapet — If logs are tall enough (4+ m), the section above the fighting platform serves as a parapet, protecting defenders from incoming missiles.
{% end %}
```

### Arrow Loops

If logs are spaced with 2–3 cm gaps, these gaps serve as vertical arrow loops. For better coverage, cut or drill dedicated arrow loops:

```
    ┌─────────────────────────────────────────┐
    │ ┃                                          ┃│
    │ ┃    ┌──┐     ┌──┐     ┌──┐              ┃│
    │ ┃    │  │     │  │     │  │   ← Arrow    ┃│
    │ ┃    │  │     │  │     │  │      loops   ┃│
    │ ┃    └──┘     └──┘     └──┘              ┃│
    │ ┃                                          ┃│
    └─────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

Each loop should be 10–15 cm wide (externally) narrowing to 3–5 cm wide (internally). The external opening is wide to allow a field of view; the internal opening is narrow to protect the defender.

## Earth and Timber Combination (Best for Survival)

Combine an earth rampart with a timber palisade on top for the best of both:

```
    OUTSIDE                                  INSIDE
    
        │                                    ┃┃┃┃┃
        │  DITCH                             ┃ LOG ┃ ← Palisade on top
    ────┘                                    ┃PALI-┃    of rampart
    ═══════════                              ┃SADE ┃
    ═══════════                              ┃┃┃┃┃┃
        ╲                                    ╱
         ╲        EARTH RAMPART            ╱
          ╲      ╔══════════════╗        ╱
           ╲_____╚══════════════╝______╱
```

The earth rampart raises the palisade to 5–6 m above the surrounding ground. The palisade provides a covered fighting position. The earth absorbs projectiles that miss the palisade.

## Stone Walls

### Dry Stone (No Mortar)

Dry stone walls rely on careful fitting and gravity. They are enormously labour-intensive but essentially permanent.

{% steps() %}
Excavate a foundation trench — 30–50 cm deep, wider than the wall.;
Lay the foundation course — The largest, flattest stones. The foundation must be level and stable.;
Build courses upward — Each stone should span the joint of the two stones below (like bricklaying). "One over two, two over one.";
Through-stones — Every 1–1.5 m of height, lay stones that span the full width of the wall. These tie the two faces together.;
Batter (inward slope) — Both faces of the wall slope inward. A wall 2 m wide at the base may be only 1 m wide at 4 m height. This is essential for stability.;
Fill the core — The centre of the wall is filled with smaller stones and rubble. This core must be compact and stable — a shifting core will bulge the faces outward.;
Coping stones — Large, flat stones on top to cap the wall and shed water.
{% end %}

### Mortared Stone

If you can produce lime mortar (limestone heated to 900°C to make quicklime, slaked with water, mixed with sand), mortared stone walls are far more durable than dry stone. The mortar fills gaps, locks stones together, and weatherproofs.

See the Construction section (Tier 3) for lime mortar production.

## Defensive Enhancements

### Chevaux-de-Frise

A horizontal beam with projecting spikes, placed on top of a wall or in a breach:

```
    ────┬─────┬─────┬─────┬───
        │     │     │     │
        │     │     │     │      ← Sharpened stakes projecting
    ────┴─────┴─────┴─────┴───      in all directions
    ════════════════════════════  ← Horizontal beam
```

### Abatis

Trees felled with branches facing outward, sharpened, and laid in a continuous line as an outer obstacle:

```
       ╲  │╱   ╲│╱    ╲│╱
        ╲ │ ╱  ╲│╱   ╲ │ ╱
         ╲│╱    ╲│╱    ╲│╱
    ════════════════════════  ← Trunks toward defenders
```

Abatis is difficult to cross, provides cover for neither side (you can't hide behind a spiky tangle), and is made from materials at hand — the trees you cleared for your field of fire.

### Thorn Hedge

Planting dense, thorny vegetation along your wall line creates a living barrier:

- **AU options:** Prickly acacia (*Acacia victoriae*, *A. paradoxa*), blackthorn (*Bursaria spinosa*), prickly pear (invasive — but effective)
- **SEA options:** Rattan (viciously thorny), bougainvillea, citrus (thorny varieties)
- **Temperate options:** Hawthorn, blackthorn, holly, rose briar

A mature thorn hedge 2–3 m wide is effectively impassable without cutting tools and time — during which defenders are shooting at you.

## Maintenance

Walls require constant maintenance:

- **Earth ramparts:** Check for erosion after heavy rain. Repair slumps immediately. Maintain grass cover.
- **Timber palisade:** Check for rot at ground level annually. Replace rotting logs immediately — a single gap invites escalade. Re-char bases every 3–5 years.
- **Stone walls:** Check for bulging faces (core settlement). Check for frost damage in cold climates. Repoint mortar every 10–20 years.
- **Ditches:** Clear silt and debris annually. Maintain the steep profile — a slumping ditch is an easy crossing.
