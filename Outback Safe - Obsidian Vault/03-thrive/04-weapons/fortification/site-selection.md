# Site Selection

A good site makes fortification easy. A bad site makes it impossible. No amount of wall-building can compensate for a location that lacks water, is overlooked by higher ground, or has no escape route.

## The Five Essential Criteria

### 1. Water (Non-Negotiable)

You must have a water source inside or immediately adjacent to your defensive perimeter. A siege that cuts off water will defeat you in 3 days — no walls will save you.

**Ideal:** A spring or well inside the walls.
**Acceptable:** A stream running through the compound (must be controllable — can it be dammed or poisoned upstream?).
**Unacceptable:** The nearest water is outside the perimeter. You will lose any siege within days.

### 2. High Ground

```
    GOOD                               BAD
    
    ┌─────┐                            ┌─────┐
    │ YOU │                            │THEM │
    │     │ ← Overlooks approach       │     │ ← Overlooks your position
    └──┬──┘                            └──┬──┘
       │                                  │
       │  Slope (attackers climb up)      │  Slope (attackers come down)
       │                                  │
    ┌──▼──┐                            ┌──▼──┐
    │THEM │                            │ YOU │
    │     │                            │     │
    └─────┘                            └─────┘
```

Being above your attackers gives you:
- Longer range for missiles (arrows, thrown spears)
- Gravity assists your projectiles, resists theirs
- Better visibility of approaches
- Attackers are exhausted from climbing before they reach your walls

**Avoid:** Any position overlooked by higher ground within 500 m (crossbow range) or 1000 m (firearm range).

### 3. Fields of Fire

Every approach to your position must be visible from your walls. "Dead ground" — areas an attacker can approach unseen — must be eliminated.

```
    Dead ground (bad):
    
    ┌─────┐
    │ YOU │
    └──┬──┘
       │
       │  ← You can't see this slope
    ┌──▼──┐
    │THEM │  ← Attackers can approach to
    │HERE │      within 20 m without being seen
    └─────┘
    
    Cleared field of fire (good):
    
    ┌─────┐
    │ YOU │
    └──┬──┘
       │
       │  ← Clear view, 200+ m
       │
       │  ← Attackers visible for entire approach
       │
    ┌──▼──┐
    │THEM │
    │HERE │
    └─────┘
```

Clear all vegetation, rocks, and terrain features that provide cover within 100 m of your walls. This is labour-intensive but essential.

### 4. Escape Route

Every fortress is a potential tomb. You must have a way out.

- **Sally port** — A hidden gate not visible from the main approaches
- **Tunnel** — From inside the compound to a concealed exit outside the walls
- **Rope descent** — A section of wall with prepared ropes, overlooking terrain attackers cannot easily reach
- **Water escape** — If adjacent to a river or coast, a boat hidden on your side

The escape route is for two scenarios: (1) the walls are breached and the position is lost, or (2) you need to sortie out to break a siege (attack the attackers).

### 5. Defensible Terrain

Natural barriers reduce the length of wall you must build:

| Terrain Feature | Wall Saved | Notes |
|-----------------|-----------|-------|
| **Cliff face** (vertical, 5+ m) | 100% of cliff width | Essentially impassable |
| **Steep slope** (> 45°) | 50–75% of slope width | Attackers exhausted climbing |
| **River** (fast, deep) | 50–70% of river width | Defensible, but bridges must be controlled |
| **Swamp / marsh** | 80–100% of marsh width | Impassable to heavily-laden attackers |
| **Dense thorn scrub** | 30–50% of scrub width | Natural barbed wire; can be enhanced by planting more |

## Site Assessment Checklist

When evaluating a potential site, score each criterion:

| Criterion | Weight | Score (1–5) |
|-----------|--------|-------------|
| Water inside perimeter | 5× | |
| High ground (not overlooked) | 4× | |
| Clear fields of fire (100+ m) | 3× | |
| Escape route | 3× | |
| Natural barriers (reduces wall length) | 2× | |
| Good soil (for earthworks, agriculture) | 1× | |
| Building materials nearby (timber, stone) | 1× | |
| Concealment from distant observation | 1× | |

A site scoring under 60 (out of 100, weighted) should be rejected.

## Site Preparation

Once a site is selected, prepare it before building walls:

{% steps() %}
Clear vegetation within 100 m — All trees, shrubs, tall grass. Burn or haul away. Nothing should provide cover for an attacker closer than 100 m.;
Survey and mark wall lines — String lines for straight walls; follow contour lines for walls on slopes.;
Mark gate locations — Primary gate faces the approach path (unavoidable). Secondary/sally gate faces concealed direction.;
Mark tower locations — At wall corners and every 30–50 m along straight sections.;
Dig drainage — Before building, ensure the site drains. A walled compound that floods in rain becomes a muddy, disease-ridden pit.;
Stockpile building materials — Timber, stone, earth for ramparts. Have materials on site before starting construction. An incomplete wall is worse than no wall (it advertises your position without providing protection).;
Establish lookouts — Before any construction begins, post lookouts to watch approaches while you work.
{% end %}

## Camouflage vs. Prominence

A philosophical choice:

**Camouflaged position:** Hidden from casual observation. Earth-and-timber walls blend into surroundings. No smoke visible from distance. Difficult to find — but once found, may not look like a strong position.

**Prominent position:** Visible. Stone walls, towers, flags. Declares "this is a defended settlement." Deters casual raiders — but ambitious attackers know exactly where you are.

In a survival context, **camouflage is usually preferable.** You are not a castle — you are a hidden community. Let your defenses be discovered only when someone is already close enough to be within your effective range.
