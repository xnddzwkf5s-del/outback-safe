# Charcoal for Gunpowder

Charcoal is the fuel in gunpowder — but not all charcoal is equal. The wood species, carbonisation temperature, and final form dramatically affect burn rate. A powder made with poor charcoal will be weak, inconsistent, and foul your barrel.

## The Role of Charcoal in Gunpowder

Charcoal provides the carbon that reacts with oxygen released by the saltpetre. However, it is not just carbon — gunpowder-grade charcoal retains volatile organic compounds (tars, creosote) that dramatically accelerate the burn. **Fully carbonised charcoal (pure carbon) makes terrible gunpowder** — it burns too slowly.

The ideal gunpowder charcoal is:
- **Approximately 70% fixed carbon**, 30% volatiles
- **Very low ash content** (under 2% by weight)
- **Light, porous, and easily friable**
- **Black with a slight brownish tint** (not grey — grey means over-charred)

## Best Wood Species

The finest gunpowder charcoals come from fast-growing, low-density woods. Historically, the English government mandated specific species for powder mills.

### Top Tier (Excellent)

| Wood | Region | Notes |
|------|--------|-------|
| **Willow** (*Salix* spp.) | Global temperate | The gold standard. Fast, clean burn. |
| **Alder** (*Alnus* spp.) | Global temperate | Nearly equal to willow. Preferred in continental Europe. |
| **Buckthorn** (*Rhamnus* spp.) | Europe, Asia | Traditional English powder charcoal. |

### Good Tier (Very Usable)

| Wood | Region | Notes |
|------|--------|-------|
| **Hazel** (*Corylus* spp.) | Europe, Asia | Good substitute, slightly more ash. |
| **Dogwood** (*Cornus* spp.) | Global | Good density, reasonable burn. |
| **Poplar / Aspen** (*Populus* spp.) | Global temperate | Light and fast-burning. |
| **Lime / Linden** (*Tilia* spp.) | Europe, Asia | Soft, even-burning. |
| **Bamboo** (young shoots) | Asia, Pacific, AU-North | **Excellent AU/SEA option.** Fast-growing, very low ash. Use young culms under 2 years. |

### Adequate Tier (Works But Inferior)

| Wood | Notes |
|------|-------|
| Pine, fir, spruce | Too resinous — unpredictable burn, excessive fouling |
| Oak, beech, maple | Too dense — slow burn, hard to incorporate |
| Fruit woods (apple, cherry) | Adequate but ash content typically higher |

### Avoid Entirely

- Any resinous/coniferous wood (pine, cedar, cypress)
- Any wood with high silica content (teak, some tropical hardwoods)
- Treated, painted, or glued wood
- Bark — high in minerals, produces excessive ash

{% info() %}
**AU/SEA Note:** *Grevillea robusta* (silky oak) and *Melaleuca* (paperbark) species produce lightweight, low-ash charcoal. Both are abundant in northern Australia. Bamboo (*Bambusa* and *Dendrocalamus* species) from Southeast Asia and northern Queensland is excellent — split young culms lengthwise, remove outer waxy layer, and char as usual.

## Charring Process

### Method: Retort Charring (Recommended)

Retort charring gives the most consistent results by heating the wood in the absence of air, allowing volatiles to escape while the carbon structure remains.

{% steps() %}
Cut wood into uniform pieces approximately 20–30 cm long × 2–3 cm diameter — Remove all bark. Season for 3–6 months to reduce moisture content.;
Build or obtain a metal retort — A steel drum with a tight-fitting lid and a 2 cm vent pipe works well. The vent pipe should extend at least 1.5 m from the retort.;
Load the retort with wood — Pack loosely; air must circulate between pieces.;
Seal the lid — Use clay or mud to seal any gaps. Only the vent pipe should allow gas escape.;
Build a fire under/around the retort — Heat steadily. Do not rush.;
Watch the vent pipe — White smoke appears first (steam + light volatiles), then yellow-brown smoke (tars and heavier volatiles). When the smoke thins and turns bluish-transparent, the process is nearly complete.;
Test for completion — Hold a small flame to the vent gas. If it ignites, volatiles are still evolving. When the gas no longer ignites (or burns with a weak blue flame), charring is complete (typically 2–4 hours for a drum-sized batch).;
Seal the vent and allow to cool completely — **Do not open while hot.** Air ingress into a hot retort will cause the charcoal to ignite. Cool for at least 12 hours.;
Open and inspect — Good charcoal is black with a slightly brownish cast, rings when tapped, and snaps cleanly (not crumbles). It should feel light in the hand.
{% end %}

### Method: Earth Pit (Lower Tech)

If a metal retort is unavailable:

{% steps() %}
Dig a pit approximately 1 m diameter × 1 m deep — Sloping sides.;
Build a central fire in the pit — Let it burn down to hot coals.;
Stack wood vertically around the central coals — Tightly packed, tallest pieces in the middle.;
Cover with a layer of green leaves or damp straw — Then cover with earth, leaving a small vent hole at the top and bottom.;
The wood smoulders for 1–3 days — Monitor the vent smoke as above.;
When smoke stops, seal all vents and let cool for 24 hours — Dig out carefully.
{% end %}

The earth pit method is less consistent but requires no special equipment. Expect approximately 30–40% yield by weight (1 kg dry wood yields 300–400 g charcoal).

## Grinding Charcoal

{% steps() %}
Break charcoal into small chunks with a wooden mallet — Not metal.;
Grind in a wooden mortar with a wooden pestle — or between two flat stones. Bronze mortar is acceptable if wood is unavailable — never iron or steel.;
Sieve through fine cloth or a 100-mesh screen — The resulting powder should be impalpable (no grit when rubbed between fingers).;
Store in a sealed container — Charcoal absorbs moisture from the air, which ruins powder consistency.;
Grind only what you need — Freshly ground charcoal performs better than stored.
{% end %}

## Quality Tests

| Test | Good Charcoal | Poor Charcoal |
|------|---------------|---------------|
| Appearance | Black with brownish sheen | Grey, shiny-black, or still wood-coloured |
| Sound when struck | Metallic "ping" | Dull thud (undercooked) or shatters (overcooked) |
| Fracture | Clean, conchoidal | Splintery or crumbly |
| Ash content | White ash, under 2% | Grey or coloured ash, over 3% |
| Burn test | Burns without smoke or flame, even glow | Smokes, flames, or sputters |
| Feel | Light, slightly oily to touch | Heavy (undercooked) or harsh (overcooked) |

### Ash Content Test

Weigh exactly 1 g of ground charcoal. Burn it completely on a clean metal dish (use a blowpipe or bellows). Weigh the remaining ash. If it exceeds 0.03 g (3%), the charcoal is too impure for gunpowder.

## Storage

Store charcoal in airtight containers away from moisture. Wooden barrels with tight lids are traditional. Charcoal stored properly lasts indefinitely — archaeological charcoal from Roman forges 2000 years old would still make acceptable powder.
