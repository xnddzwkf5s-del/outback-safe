# Testing Gunpowder

**Never assume your powder is good.** Test every batch before relying on it for hunting or defense. The difference between good and poor powder can be the difference between a successful hunt and a stuck ball in your barrel — or worse, a burst barrel.

## Pre-Testing Safety

{% steps() %}
Test outdoors only — Clear area, no dry vegetation within 5 m.;
Test in small quantities — Start with 1 g for burn tests, never more than 5 g for initial strength tests.;
Have sand bucket ready — Water spreads burning powder.;
Stand upwind — Sulfur dioxide smoke is an irritant.;
Wear eye protection — Always.;
Never lean over a powder sample — Keep your face above and behind, not over the test.;
Ignite with a long match, fuse, or taper — Never a lighter held close. Extend your arm fully.
{% end %}

## Test 1: Visual Inspection

Before any ignition test, inspect the powder:

| What to Check | Good Powder | Problem | Fix |
|---------------|-------------|---------|-----|
| Colour | Dark grey to black | Brownish = impure saltpetre; Yellowish = excess/unmixed sulfur | Re-purify ingredients |
| Uniformity | Consistent grain colour | Speckled/mottled = poor mixing | Re-incorporate batch |
| Surface | Matte, dry feel | Glossy/shiny = moisture absorption or excess sulfur | Dry further; check sulfur ratio |
| Grain integrity | Grains hold shape when rubbed between fingers | Crumbles to dust = under-bound | Slightly more water during incorporation |
| Dust content | Minimal fine dust | Cloud of dust when poured = poorly polished/over-handled | Re-sieve to remove dust |
| Smell | Faint SO₂ (match smell) | Sharp ammonia smell = impure saltpetre; No smell (unusual) | Saltpetre needs further purification |

## Test 2: Burn Rate Test

This is the most important quality test. It tells you how fast and how completely your powder burns.

{% steps() %}
Place exactly 1 g of powder on a clean, flat, non-flammable surface — A ceramic tile, smooth stone, or metal plate.;
Form into a line approximately 5 cm long × 5 mm wide — Not a pile. A line gives visible burn progression.;
Touch a lit fuse or long match to one end — Stand back immediately.;
Observe the burn — Time it if possible. Good powder burns in a bright flash, under 0.5 seconds for 1 g.;
Examine the residue — The surface should show grey-white ash, not black unburnt particles.
{% end %}

**Burn time benchmarks (1 g line, FFG grain size):**

| Result | Time | Diagnosis |
|--------|------|-----------|
| Excellent | < 0.5 s, bright flash, dense white smoke | Ready for use |
| Good | 0.5–1.0 s, steady burn, white-grey smoke | Usable; may be slightly under-incorporated |
| Fair | 1.0–2.0 s, sputters or hisses | Check charcoal quality; possibly over-charred |
| Poor | > 2.0 s, leaves black residue | Impure ingredients or wrong ratio; re-make |
| Danger | Burn stops mid-line | Too wet or severely impure — discard batch |

### Interpreting the Residue

```
White/grey powder, no black specks  →  Complete combustion — excellent
Grey with scattered black specks    →  Adequate — incomplete charcoal burn
Black residue, powdery              →  Poor — charcoal not fine enough or bad saltpetre
Yellow residue                      →  Excess sulfur not consumed
Sticky, tarry residue               →  Charcoal over-charred (too many volatiles driven off)
Wet-looking spots                   →  Moisture in powder — dry further
```

## Test 3: The Flash Test

Tests ignition sensitivity — important for flintlock reliability.

{% steps() %}
Place a very small amount of FFFFg or meal powder (approximately 0.1 g, about a pea-sized pinch) on a ceramic tile.;
Touch with a glowing ember or red-hot wire (not a flame — simulate flintlock spark temperature).;
Good powder ignites instantly with a "poof" — No delay, no sputtering.;
Poor powder smoulders or fails to ignite — Insufficient sulfur or poor incorporation.
{% end %}

## Test 4: Strength Test (Provisional)

A simple comparative test using a weighted projectile:

{% steps() %}
Construct a simple mortar — A sturdy metal tube closed at one end, mounted at 45° angle. **NB: This is a test device. Do not use as a weapon.**;
Load with exactly 5 g of your test powder and a standard projectile — A lead ball of known weight (e.g., 20 g), tightly patched.;
Fire at 45° elevation over level ground — Measure the distance from muzzle to first impact.;
Compare against a known-good powder baseline — If available, compare with commercial black powder of the same grain size.;
Repeat 3 times and average — Consistency matters as much as absolute distance.
{% end %}

**Acceptable performance:** Within 15% of baseline range. If your powder throws 80 m and known-good throws 100 m, check charcoal quality and incorporation time.

## Test 5: Fouling Test

Powder that burns dirty fouls barrels quickly, reducing accuracy and potentially causing dangerous pressure buildup.

{% steps() %}
Fire 3 shots with a clean barrel using your test powder;
Inspect the bore — Use a cleaning patch on a rod;
Scrape a patch and inspect the fouling

**Acceptable:** Dark grey, dry, flaky residue that patches out easily
**Problem:** Hard, black, tarry residue that resists cleaning — charcoal quality issue
**Problem:** Wet, sticky residue — moisture in powder or sodium contamination in saltpetre
{% end %}

## Calibration: The Standard Reference

If you have access to commercial black powder (e.g., GOEX, Swiss, Wano), keep a small quantity as reference. Test your powder side-by-side: burn line test, flash test, and firing test. The commercial standard tells you what to aim for.

## Common Problems and Solutions

| Problem | Likely Cause | Solution |
|---------|-------------|----------|
| Slow burn, lots of smoke | Charcoal over-charred (too little volatiles) | Reduce charring time/temperature; try different wood |
| Fast, violent burn, bright flash | Too much saltpetre, or grains too fine | Re-check ratio; use coarser grain size |
| Won't ignite in flintlock | Insufficient sulfur, or grains too coarse for priming | Use FFFFg/meal for priming; increase sulfur to 10% |
| Hisses and sputters | Moisture in powder | Dry thoroughly; store with desiccant |
| Brown fumes | Sodium contamination (sodium nitrate, not potassium) | Re-purify saltpetre by re-crystallisation |
| Corrodes barrel rapidly | Sulfur excess or impure saltpetre (chlorides) | Re-purify both ingredients; clean barrel immediately after use |
| Grains crumble to dust in storage | Under-bound during incorporation | Use slightly more water and longer incorporation time |
| Fine dust accumulation (dangerous) | Poor granulation or excessive handling | Re-sieve to remove dust immediately. Do not store dusty powder. |

{% info() %}
**Keep a test log.** Record every batch: ingredient sources, incorporation time, grain size, burn test result, and firing test result. Over 10–20 batches, patterns emerge that tell you exactly how to adjust your process. Without records, you are guessing each time.
{% end %}
