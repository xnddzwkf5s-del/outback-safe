# Swords

The sword is a specialised weapon — exclusively for fighting other humans. It is not a hunting tool. A sword has no utility purpose — it cannot chop wood (it will break), it cannot butcher game efficiently (a knife is better), and it cannot be thrown (it will break). A sword is a sidearm for personal defense and close combat.

In a survival context, make spears, bows, and axes before attempting a sword. Once basic needs are met and you have forging capability, a sword may be worthwhile for defense and trade.

## Sword Types and Priorities

| Type | Length | Weight | Difficulty | Utility |
|------|--------|--------|------------|---------|
| **Long knife / short sword** (40–60 cm) | Single-handed | 0.5–1.0 kg | ★★★ | Good — multi-purpose |
| **Machete / bolo** (50–70 cm) | Single-handed | 0.6–1.2 kg | ★★★ | Excellent — tool and weapon |
| **One-handed sword** (70–90 cm) | Single-handed | 1.0–1.5 kg | ★★★★ | Poor — weapon only |
| **Two-handed sword** (110–140 cm) | Two-handed | 2.0–3.5 kg | ★★★★★ | None — dedicated weapon |

For survival, the **long knife / machete form** is ideal — it serves as both a tool (clearing brush, light chopping) and a weapon. A full-length sword is a luxury project for when basic forging is well-established.

{% info() %}
**SEA Note:** The *bolo* (Philippines), *parang* (Malaysia/Indonesia), and *golok* are traditional blade forms that bridge the gap between tool and weapon. These designs have been refined over centuries for jungle use — clearing vegetation, harvesting crops, and personal defense. They are probably the single most practical blade form for survival in Southeast Asian environments.
{% end %}

## Materials

### Blade Steel

| Source | Carbon Content | Use | Notes |
|--------|---------------|-----|-------|
| **Leaf spring** (vehicle) | 0.5–0.7% C | ★★★★★ Excellent | Already is spring steel. Do not overheat — preserve existing temper. |
| **Coil spring** (vehicle) | 0.5–0.7% C | ★★★★★ Excellent | Straighten while hot, then re-treat. |
| **Old files** | 0.9–1.2% C | ★★★★ Very good | High carbon — brittle if over-hardened. Temper carefully. |
| **Railroad spike** (high-carbon) | 0.3–0.4% C | ★★ Borderline | Not enough carbon for a good edge. Adequate for machete. |
| **Mild steel** (structural) | < 0.2% C | ★ Not suitable | Cannot be hardened. Will not hold an edge. |
| **Wrought iron** | < 0.1% C | ✗ Useless | Cannot be hardened at all. Not a sword material. |

### Handle Materials

- **Hardwood:** Oak, ash, walnut, jarrah, ironbark, mulga, teak
- **Antler:** Deer, elk (not available wild in AU; feral deer in some areas)
- **Bone:** Large animal leg bones (cattle, buffalo)
- **Horn:** Water buffalo horn (SEA) — excellent, traditional
- **Leather washers:** Stacked leather discs, compressed and shaped

### Key Dimensions

| Parameter | Long Knife/Machete | One-Handed Sword |
|-----------|-------------------|-----------------|
| Overall length | 50–70 cm | 80–100 cm |
| Blade length | 35–50 cm | 65–80 cm |
| Blade width (at base) | 3–5 cm | 4–6 cm |
| Blade thickness (at base) | 4–6 mm | 5–7 mm |
| Blade thickness (at tip) | 2–3 mm | 2–3 mm |
| Tang length | Full length of handle | Full length of handle |
| Tang width | At least 1/2 blade width | At least 1/2 blade width |
| Point of balance | 5–10 cm from guard | 8–15 cm from guard |

### Tang Design

The tang is the single most important structural element. A blade that snaps at the tang during use becomes a flying piece of sharp steel.

**Full tang** (recommended): The tang extends the full length and width of the handle. Handle scales (wood, bone, antler) are riveted to each side. This is the strongest design.

```
    ┌──────────────────────────────────┐
    │  BLADE                           │
    │                                  │
    ├──────┬─────────────┬─────────────┤
    │      │    TANG     │             │
    │      │             │             │
    └──────┴─────────────┴─────────────┘
           ↑
        Guard
        (optional)
```

**Stick tang** (traditional, adequate): A narrower tang that runs through the handle and is peened (riveted over) at the pommel. Adequate if properly sized, but more prone to breakage than a full tang in a poorly-made blade.

## Forging Process

{% steps() %}
Select and prepare the steel — If using a leaf spring, cold-cut to rough shape. If using scrap, forge-weld pieces together to achieve the needed mass.;
Forge the profile — Heat to bright orange (1000–1100°C). Hammer to rough shape: taper from guard to tip, taper from centre line to edges. Leave the edges 2–3 mm thick — grinding will bring them to final dimension.;
Forge the tang — Draw out the handle section to full tang dimensions. Do not cut the tang too narrow — keep it at least 1/2 the blade width.;
Forge the bevels — Hammer the edge bevels in, working from the spine toward the edge. Keep the centre line straight.;
Normalise — Heat to critical temperature (non-magnetic, ~770°C) and air-cool three times. This relieves forging stresses and prepares the steel for hardening.;
Straighten while normalising — Blades almost always warp during forging. Straighten at each normalising heat.;
Grind to final shape — File or grind the profile, bevels, and tang. Leave the edge approximately 0.5–1.0 mm thick before heat treatment (thinner edges may crack during quench).;
Clay coat (optional — for differential hardening) — A thin layer of clay along the spine, leaving the edge exposed. The clay slows cooling of the spine during quench, producing a hard edge with a softer, tougher spine. This is the traditional Japanese *hamon* technique.;
Harden — Heat to critical (non-magnetic, ~770°C). Hold at temperature for 2–3 minutes. Quench in warm oil (vegetable oil at ~50°C). The blade should be file-hard (a file skates without biting).;
Temper IMMEDIATELY — A hardened, un-tempered blade is glass-hard and will shatter. Temper at 200–250°C for 1–2 hours. The edge should be straw to bronze coloured.;
Straighten during tempering — Blades often warp during quench. Can be straightened while at tempering temperature (use a straightening jig).;
Final grind and polish — Bring the edges to sharp. Finish the blade surface (satin, polished, or left with forge scale for a rustic finish).;
Fit the handle — Attach scales (full tang) or fit a through-tang handle. Rivet or peen in place.;
Sharpen — Final edge honing with fine stones.
{% end %}

## Heat Treatment Reference

| Stage | Temperature | Colour | Duration | Purpose |
|-------|------------|--------|----------|---------|
| Normalising | 770–800°C | Bright red | Heat + air cool × 3 | Stress relief, grain refinement |
| Hardening | 770–800°C | Bright red | 2–3 min soak, oil quench | Form martensite (hard phase) |
| Tempering (machete) | 230–270°C | Straw to purple | 1 hour | Toughness for chopping |
| Tempering (sword) | 200–230°C | Light straw | 1–2 hours | Edge retention + reasonable toughness |

## Guard (Crossguard)

A guard prevents your hand from sliding onto the blade when thrusting into a target.

- **Mild steel bar**, slotted to fit over the tang
- **Brass or bronze** — non-rusting, traditional
- **Hardwood** — simplest, least durable

The guard should be at least 2 cm longer than the blade width on each side (so 4 cm total wider than the blade).

## Sharpening

### Edge Geometry

- **Machete / chopping blade:** Convex edge, approximately 25–30° per side. Durable, resists chipping.
- **General-purpose sword:** Flat grind to edge, approximately 20–25° per side. Balanced cutting and durability.
- **Razor-sharp sword (cutting competition):** Approximately 15–20° per side. Cuts beautifully, dulls quickly.

### Sharpening Process

{% steps() %}
Establish the edge angle with a coarse stone — File or 100–220 grit stone. Remove steel until a burr forms along the entire edge. A burr is a very thin wire edge that you can feel catch your fingernail — it indicates the two bevels have met.;
Refine with medium stone — 400–600 grit. Remove the coarse scratches. A smaller burr should form.;
Refine with fine stone — 1000–2000 grit. The edge should now be shiny along the bevel.;
Strop — On leather charged with fine abrasive (jeweller's rouge, chromium oxide, or even wood ash paste). Stropping removes the final microscopic burr and aligns the edge.;
Test — The blade should shave arm hair easily for a sharp edge. For a machete or heavy chopper, it should cleanly slice paper.
{% end %}

## Scabbard (Sheath)

A scabbard protects the blade (and the user) when not in use.

### Wooden Scabbard

{% steps() %}
Select two thin boards of lightweight wood — Approximately 5 mm thick each. Willow, poplar, cedar, or bamboo.;
Carve the blade cavity into each half — The cavity should be a snug fit for the blade.;
Glue the two halves together — Hide glue or modern wood glue.;
Shape the exterior — Rounded edges, tapered to match blade profile.;
Wrap with leather, rawhide, or fabric — Or leave as finished wood.;
Add a belt loop or suspension system — Leather straps, metal rings.
{% end %}

### Leather Scabbard

- Wet-form leather around the blade (wrap blade in plastic to prevent rust during forming)
- Stitch along one edge
- Treat with oil or wax for water resistance
