# Bows

The bow is humanity's most successful hunting weapon — used on every inhabited continent for at least 10,000 years. A well-made bow is quiet, reliable, and made entirely from renewable materials.

## Bow Types

| Type | Construction | Draw Weight Range | Difficulty | Best Use |
|------|-------------|-------------------|------------|----------|
| **Self bow** | Single piece of wood | 20–80 lb (90–360 N) | ★★ | First bow, learning |
| **Backed bow** | Two layers (wood + sinew/rawhide) | 30–100 lb (130–450 N) | ★★★ | Better performance |
| **Composite bow** | Wood + horn + sinew, glued | 40–160 lb (180–710 N) | ★★★★★ | Maximum power |
| **Bamboo bow** | Multiple bamboo strips laminated | 20–60 lb (90–270 N) | ★★★ | Best AU/SEA option |

{% info() %}
**Draw weight is measured at 28 inches (71 cm) of draw.** A 40 lb (180 N) bow at 28" means it takes 40 lb of force to hold the string at 28 inches from the bow's belly. This is the standard reference point. Your personal draw length may differ.
{% end %}

## Bow Woods

### Ideal Bow Woods (★★★★★)

| Wood | Region | Density (kg/m³) | Notes |
|------|--------|-----------------|-------|
| **Yew** (*Taxus baccata*) | Europe, W. Asia | 670 | The legendary bow wood. Natural laminate — heartwood resists compression, sapwood resists tension. |
| **Osage orange** (*Maclura pomifera*) | N. America (naturalised AU) | 860 | Outstanding. Takes extreme compression. Yellow-orange, darkens with age. |
| **Lemonwood** (*Calycophyllum candidissimum*) | C/S. America | 800 | Excellent. Used for professional target bows. |

### Very Good Bow Woods (★★★★)

| Wood | Region | Notes |
|------|--------|-------|
| **Black locust** (*Robinia pseudoacacia*) | Naturalised AU, global | Near-equal to osage. Abundant in AU as introduced species. |
| **Hickory** (*Carya* spp.) | N. America (planted elsewhere) | Outstanding tension strength. Popular for backed bows. |
| **Mulberry** (*Morus* spp.) | Global | Related to osage. Good bow wood. |
| **Elm** (*Ulmus* spp.) | Europe, Asia | Tough, resilient. English longbows sometimes made from elm when yew unavailable. |

### AU/SEA Bow Woods (★★★ — ★★★★)

| Wood | Region | Notes |
|------|--------|-------|
| **Spotted gum** (*Corymbia maculata*) | Eastern AU | Hard, dense (~950 kg/m³). Must be carefully tillered to avoid chrysals (compression fractures). |
| **Ironbark** (*Eucalyptus* spp.) | Eastern AU | Very hard and dense. Makes powerful but unforgiving bows. |
| **Red ash** (*Alphitonia excelsa*) | Northern AU, SEA | Traditional Aboriginal bow wood. Lighter than eucalypts, easier to work. |
| **Acacia** (mulga, gidgee) | Arid AU | Very dense. Short bows only — tends to chrysal. |
| **Bamboo** (*Bambusa*, *Dendrocalamus*) | SEA, N. AU | See bamboo bows below. |
| **Palmyra palm** (*Borassus*) | SEA, India | Outer wood of trunk makes serviceable bow. |
| **Teak** (*Tectona grandis*) | SEA | Better as a backing material than a self-bow. |

### Wood Selection

Select a stave from a straight-grained section of trunk or large branch:
- **Length:** Your height plus 30 cm (for a longbow). Minimum: 150 cm for an adult bow.
- **Diameter:** At least 10–15 cm (for splitting — you want one radial section)
- **Straight grain:** Absolutely essential. Run-out (grain that angles across the bow) will cause breakage.
- **No knots, branches, or defects in the working section**
- **Seasoned:** Air-dried for at least 6 months (1 year ideal). Green wood takes set (permanent bend) and shoots weakly.

## Self-Bow Construction

### The Stave

```
          ┌──────────────────────────────────┐
          │            BACK                  │  ← Outer ring (one growth ring)
          │  ═══════════════════════════════  │     must be unbroken along
          │                                  │     entire length
          │             BELLY                │
          └──────────────────────────────────┘
          ↑              ↑                  ↑
        Upper          Handle            Lower
        limb                            limb
```

{% steps() %}
Split or saw the stave — Follow the grain. The stave should be approximately 5 cm wide × 5 cm deep × bow length + 30 cm.;
Identify the back — The outside of the tree (under the bark) becomes the back of the bow (faces away from archer). Follow ONE growth ring for the entire back — do not cut through rings. This is the single most important rule of self-bow making.;
Rough out the profile — Mark the centre, then the handle section (10–15 cm, centred). Taper from handle to tips. The tips should be approximately 1.5 cm wide.;
Floor tillering — Hold the bow by the handle, place the tip on the floor, and push gently. The bow should bend evenly along both limbs. If one spot bends more than the rest, that's a "hinge" — remove wood from elsewhere to catch up.;
String the bow (low brace) — When it bends evenly at floor tiller, string it at low brace (string just barely taut, maybe 5 cm from the belly).;
Tillering — This is the art of bow making. Place the bow on a tillering stick or tree. Pull the string to progressively greater distances, observing the bend. The goal is an even, elliptical bend — more bend in the outer limbs, less near the handle. Remove wood ONLY where the bow is bending too little.;
Work to desired draw weight and length — Check frequently with a scale. Once you reach the desired weight at your draw length, STOP removing wood. You cannot put wood back.;
Final brace height — The string should be approximately 15–18 cm from the belly at the handle when braced (for a longbow).;
Shoot in — Shoot 50–100 arrows. The bow will settle. Check tiller and adjust if needed.;
Finish — Seal with several coats of linseed oil, tung oil, or traditional fat/wax mixture. A bow left unsealed will absorb moisture and take set.
{% end %}

## Bamboo Bows (AU/SEA)

Bamboo is an excellent bow material and abundant in Southeast Asia and northern Australia. It grows fast, seasons quickly, and requires less precision tillering than wood.

### Method: Laminated Bamboo Bow

{% steps() %}
Harvest mature bamboo culms — At least 3 years old. The culm should be 10–15 cm diameter.;
Split the culm into strips — Strips 3–4 cm wide × bow length.;
Plane or scrape the inner surface flat — Remove the pithy inner layer. The outer surface (with the hard, silica-rich skin) becomes the back.;
Season the strips — Dry slowly in shade for 2–3 months. Forced drying causes splitting.;
Heat-treat the belly strips — Hold over a fire or heat gun until the surface turns golden-brown. This hardens the bamboo and improves compression resistance.;
Laminate 2–3 strips together — Using hide glue, fish glue, or modern epoxy. The outer strip has the hard skin facing out (back of bow). Clamp firmly and cure for 1 week.;
Shape and tiller as a self-bow — Bamboo laminate is more forgiving than wood but still needs tillering.;
Finish — Seal all cut edges to prevent moisture ingress between lamination layers.
{% end %}

## Bowstrings

### Materials

| Material | Source | Pros | Cons |
|----------|--------|------|------|
| **Linen/flax** | Flax plant | Traditional, strong | Stretches when wet |
| **Hemp** | Hemp plant | Very strong, traditional | Harder to process than flax |
| **Sinew** | Animal tendons (deer, kangaroo, cattle) | Excellent elasticity | Stretches when wet; time-consuming to process |
| **Rawhide** | Animal hide, cut into strips | Strong, durable | Stretches when wet; permanent stretch |
| **Nettle** (*Urtica* spp.) | Wild plant | Good fibre, traditional | Labour-intensive processing |
| **Silk** | Silkworm cocoons | Light, strong, water-resistant | Requires silk production capability |
| **Cotton** | Cotton plant | Easy to work | Weak for draw weight — only light bows |
| **Modern Dacron/B-50** | Synthetic (salvaged) | Excellent, waterproof | Finite supply in collapse scenario |

### Making a Linen Bowstring

{% steps() %}
Obtain long-fibre flax/linen — Or process flax stalks into fibres.;
Form a Flemish loop at one end — The upper loop that goes over the top nock.;
Measure the string length — The string should be approximately 8–10 cm shorter than the unstrung bow length (for a longbow).;
Twist the fibres into a 2-ply or 3-ply cord — Even tension on all plies.;
Serve the middle section (approximately 20 cm) — Wrap tightly with fine thread to protect against arrow nock wear and finger abrasion.;
Form the lower loop — With a bowyer's knot (timber hitch) for adjustability, or a fixed loop.;
Wax the string — Rub with beeswax to waterproof and reduce friction.
{% end %}

### String Care

- Wax regularly — beeswax rubbed into the string with friction (use a leather scrap)
- Keep dry — a wet string stretches, changing brace height and cast
- Carry a spare string always
- Replace when strands begin to fray in the serving area

## Arrow Construction

### Shaft Materials

| Material | Pros | Cons | Region |
|----------|------|------|--------|
| **Port Orford cedar** | Straight, light, traditional | Not available wild outside NW America | Commercial only |
| **Sitka spruce** | Light, stiff, excellent | Not available wild outside NW America | Commercial only |
| **Bamboo** | Natural shaft (already hollow), tough, straight | Needs node smoothing; varies in spine | **SEA, CN, N. AU** |
| **River cane / reed** (*Arundinaria*, *Phragmites*) | Natural shaft, easy to straighten | Weaker spine than bamboo | Global wetlands |
| **Hardwood shoots** (hazel, dogwood, viburnum) | Can harvest straight shoots | Must be seasoned; labour to straighten | Global temperate |
| **Pine dowels** | Fast to make if you have a dowel plate | Heavy, inconsistent spine | Anywhere with pine |
| **Aluminium tubing** (salvaged) | Perfectly straight, consistent | Heavy, bends permanently on hard impact | Salvaged modern materials |

### Arrow Spine

Spine is the arrow shaft's stiffness — how much it flexes when a weight is hung from its centre. This is the single most important arrow property.

**The Archer's Paradox:** An arrow must flex around the bow handle when released. Too stiff (high spine) and it bounces off the bow, flying left (for a right-handed archer). Too flexible (low spine) and it over-flexes, flying right. Correct spine allows the arrow to "paradox" around the bow and fly straight.

**Rule of thumb:** Heavier draw weight → stiffer arrow required. Longer arrow → more flex (lower effective spine).

| Draw Weight | Approximate Arrow Spine |
|-------------|------------------------|
| 20–30 lb | 0.500–0.600" deflection (low spine) |
| 30–40 lb | 0.450–0.500" |
| 40–50 lb | 0.350–0.450" |
| 50–60 lb | 0.300–0.350" |
| 60–80 lb | 0.250–0.300" (high spine — very stiff) |
| 80+ lb | < 0.250" (extra stiff) |

### Fletching

Feathers stabilise the arrow in flight. Three feathers is standard — one "cock" feather (odd colour, perpendicular to nock) and two "hen" feathers.

**Best feathers:** Turkey, goose, swan (large birds). In AU: emu, magpie goose, brolga. In SEA: various waterfowl.

{% steps() %}
Split the feather along the quill — Use a sharp knife. You want one half-feather per vane.;
Trim to shape — Traditional parabolic or shield-cut profile, approximately 10–15 cm long × 1–2 cm high.;
Glue to shaft — Hide glue or pine pitch. Space 120° apart. The cock feather is oriented perpendicular to the nock (faces away from the bow when nocked).;
Bind the leading edge — Wrap fine thread around the front 5 mm of each feather to prevent it from lifting during flight.;
Waterproof the quill base — A dab of glue or wax seals the cut quill.
{% end %}

### Arrowheads

| Type | Material | Use |
|------|----------|-----|
| **Field point** (bullet shape) | Steel, iron, bone | Target practice, small game |
| **Broadhead** (two or three cutting edges) | Steel, iron | Large game — cuts arteries, causes bleeding |
| **Blunt** (flat or rounded) | Hardwood, horn, steel | Small game — stuns without penetrating |
| **Bodkin** (narrow, square-section) | Steel, iron | Armour penetration (military) |
| **Fire-hardened point** | Hardwood, sharpened and charred | Emergency/training arrows |
| **Bone point** | Bone, ground to shape | Fish, small game |

**Forging a simple broadhead:**

{% steps() %}
Forge from a piece of mild steel (old file, spring, scrap) — approximately 3 mm thick.;
Shape a triangular blade with a tang — The tang inserts into the arrow shaft.;
Grind or file the edges sharp — Single bevel is traditional for arrows (the bevel imparts a slight spin).;
Harden and temper — Heat to red, quench in oil, temper to purple (270°C) — hard enough to hold an edge, tough enough not to shatter on bone.;
Insert tang into shaft — Drill or split a slot.;
Bind tightly with sinew or strong thread — The binding prevents the shaft from splitting on impact. A dab of pine pitch or hide glue seals the binding.
{% end %}

## Shooting Technique (Basic)

**Stance:** Side-on to target, feet shoulder-width apart. Weight evenly distributed.

**Grip:** The bow handle rests in the web between thumb and forefinger. Do not grip tightly — a "loose" grip allows the bow to pivot naturally on release.

**Draw:** Three-finger draw (Mediterranean release) — index finger above arrow, middle and ring below. Draw to a consistent anchor point (e.g., index finger to corner of mouth, thumb under jawbone).

**Release:** Relax the fingers — do not pluck the string away from your face. The string pushes your fingers aside. A clean release is silent.

**Follow-through:** Hold your position until the arrow hits the target.
