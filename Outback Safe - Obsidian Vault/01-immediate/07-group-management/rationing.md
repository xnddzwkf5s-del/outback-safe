# Rationing — Fair Distribution

Resource distribution is the most common source of group conflict. A clear, transparent system established early prevents hoarding, accusations of favouritism, and morale-destroying resentment.

---

## Distribution Principles

1. **Transparency** — everyone sees the total available and what each person receives
2. **Predictability** — same time, same place, same process every day
3. **Proportionality** — those who need more get more; those who contribute more get more
4. **No private stashes** — all food/water is communal. Hidden stashes destroy trust.
5. **Leader eats last** — the leader ensures everyone is fed before eating. This is symbolic AND practical — if there is not enough, the leader takes the shortage.

---

## Water Rationing

Water is distributed on a strict schedule:

| Climate | Per person per day | Distribution schedule |
|---------|-------------------|----------------------|
| Cool (< 20°C) | 2 L | 250 mL every 2 hours (8 doses) |
| Warm (20–30°C) | 3 L | 375 mL every 2 hours (8 doses) |
| Hot (> 30°C) | 4 L | 500 mL every 2 hours (8 doses) |
| Extreme heat (> 35°C) | 5–6 L | 500–750 mL every 2 hours |

**Additional allocation:**
- Heavy workers: +1–2 L
- Pregnant/lactating women: +1 L
- Injured/sick (with fever or diarrhoea): +1–2 L
- Children: proportional to body weight (~75% of adult ration for a 10-year-old)

---

## Food Rationing

→ See [Emergency Rationing](01-immediate/05-food-emergency/emergency-rationing) for full nutritional details.

**Key group rules:**
- Serve food in identical portions. Use a standard measure (same cup, same-sized pieces).
- One person does all serving — the quartermaster
- If portions are not identical, the quartermaster takes the last/smallest portion
- No trading ("I will give you my fish for your nuts") — it creates inequality and resentment

---

## The Quartermaster Role

The quartermaster controls and distributes all group resources:
- Maintains the inventory (mental or written)
- Determines daily rations based on total supply and group needs
- Distributes food and water at set times
- Is accountable to the leader
- Cannot give themselves extra — this is a trust position

## 🇦🇺 Australian Outback Resource Sharing Context

### Water — Pooled and Tracked Centrally

In the Australian outback, **water is the single most critical shared resource.** Unlike food or fuel, there is no substitute for water, and you cannot carry enough for an extended unplanned delay.

**Convoy water protocol:**

- **Each vehicle carries their OWN water** for normal consumption (minimum 5 L per person per day, for the planned trip duration plus 3 days buffer)
- **Shared emergency water** — one designated vehicle carries a communal emergency supply (20 L minimum) for the whole group, not to be touched except in genuine emergency
- **Track centrally:** At every stop, the lead vehicle asks: "Everyone OK for water?" If any group is running low, the convoy stops and redistributes at the next town/roadhouse
- **At roadhouses:** Top up ALL water containers, not just yours. Fill jerry cans, camelbacks, and bottles

**Water sources in the outback:**

| Source | Can you drink it? | Notes |
|--------|------------------|-------|
| Town water / roadhouse | Yes | Safe. No treatment needed. |
| Artesian bore | Usually yes | May be hot (50–70°C) — let it cool. High mineral content but generally safe. |
| River / creek (seasonal) | No — treat first | High sediment, livestock contamination. Boil, filter, or treat. |
| Billabong / waterhole | No — treat first | Animal dung, algae, bacteria. High risk of gastroenteritis. |
| Rainwater (from vehicle recovery) | Yes — best option | Rain on a tarp or bonnet is the purest source. Collect in clean containers. |

### Fuel — Individual Vehicle Responsibility

**Fuel is NOT pooled** in standard Australian convoy travel. Each vehicle is responsible for their own fuel.

**Before the trip:**

- Each driver calculates their fuel range based on their vehicle's consumption
- Add a 25–30% buffer for detours, soft sand (doubles consumption), headwinds
- Agree on fuel stops and refuel as a group — nobody leaves a fuel stop without the entire convoy
- **Diesel vehicles** — carry extra diesel in jerry cans (10–20 L on extreme tracks)
- **Petrol vehicles** — same logic, but petrol is more volatile. Store safely.

**Diesel is the preferred fuel for remote Australian travel.** It is more energy-dense, less flammable, and more efficient for heavy loads. If all vehicles in a convoy are diesel, fuel logistics are simpler.

### Food — Shared in Emergency

Each vehicle generally carries their own food. But for long convoys (2+ weeks on extreme tracks):

- **Evening meals are often shared** — one vehicle provides meat, another provides vegetables, another provides drinks
- **Emergency food cache** — one vehicle carries a sealed box of long-life food (dehydrated meals, rice, pasta) for the whole group, only used if the trip is extended
- **Fresh food first** — eat highly perishable items (fresh meat, dairy) in the first 3–5 days. Then tinned/dehydrated.

### "Never Leave a Vehicle Behind"

This is an unwritten rule of Australian outback travel: **the convoy does not leave a broken-down vehicle behind.**

**If a vehicle breaks down:**

1. All vehicles stop. Assess the problem.
2. If repairable: repair on site (spare parts, tools shared across the convoy)
3. If not repairable: **passengers are redistributed** across other vehicles
4. **No one travels alone** — the driver of the broken vehicle travels with another vehicle
5. The abandoned vehicle is recovered later (tagged with GPS location, reported to authorities)

**Why this rule exists:** Leaving someone stranded in the Australian outback with no vehicle can be fatal. Temperatures exceed 45°C, the next town may be 500 km away, and water runs out fast. The convoy is responsible for every person who started the trip.
