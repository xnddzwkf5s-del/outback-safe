# Leadership — Who Leads & How

Survival leadership is not about rank or authority. It is about keeping people alive. The best survival leaders are not always the loudest or most confident — they are the ones who make good decisions, own their mistakes, and keep the group functioning under pressure.

---

## Qualities of a Survival Leader

| Quality | Why it matters |
|---------|---------------|
| **Calm** | Panic is contagious; calm is equally contagious. A leader who stays calm under pressure anchors the group. |
| **Decisive** | Inaction is a decision — usually the worst one. A wrong decision made quickly is often better than a perfect decision made too late. |
| **Fair** | Perceived unfairness destroys group cohesion faster than any external threat. |
| **Competent** | At least one survival skill the group respects (medical, fire, navigation). |
| **Humble** | Admits mistakes. "I was wrong. Here is what we do now." This builds trust. |
| **Enduring** | Leadership is exhausting. The leader must manage their own energy, sleep, and food to remain effective. |

---

## The Decision-Making Framework

When facing a group decision under pressure:

{% steps() %}
**Define the problem** — in one sentence, what are we deciding?; **List options** — what are the possible courses of action? (Do this quickly — do not list 20 options. 2–4 is enough.); **Assess each option** — what do we know? What do we NOT know? What are the risks?; **Make the decision** — the leader decides. If time allows, consult the group. If it does not, decide unilaterally and explain afterward.; **Communicate clearly** — everyone must understand what was decided and why.; **Execute** — assign who does what, by when; **Review** — after the action, ask: did it work? What would we do differently?
{% end %}

## Decision Speed vs. Decision Quality

```
    Decision Matrix

    HIGH STAKES  |  HIGH STAKES
    TIME CRITICAL |  TIME AVAILABLE
    -------------+---------------
    Leader       |  Group
    decides      |  consensus
    alone        |
    -------------+---------------
    Anyone       |  Leader
    decides;     |  with group
    defer        |  input
    LOW STAKES   |  LOW STAKES
    TIME CRITICAL |  TIME AVAILABLE
```

| Situation | Decision process |
|-----------|-----------------|
| Immediate life threat (fire, flood, attack) | Leader decides alone. No consultation. |
| Urgent but not immediate (shelter location, rationing) | Leader with brief group input |
| Non-urgent (camp layout, daily schedule) | Group discussion, leader confirms |
| Routine (what to cook, who gathers firewood) | Delegate to the person responsible |

## Leadership Rotation

In prolonged survival situations (weeks+), rotate leadership:

- **Prevents burnout** — leadership is mentally exhausting
- **Prevents power consolidation** — no one becomes a dictator
- **Shares the burden** — everyone understands how hard leadership is
- **Builds redundancy** — if the leader is injured, others have practiced leading

**Rotation format:** Every 3–7 days, a new leader assumes command. The outgoing leader remains as advisor for the first day.

---

## The Burden of Command

The leader makes decisions that may cause harm or death. This is the burden. Acknowledge it but do not let it paralyse you.

**Principles for hard decisions:**
- **Life over limb** — saving a life justifies risking injury
- **Group over individual** — the survival of many outweighs the survival of one (triage principle)
- **Transparent reasoning** — explain WHY a hard decision was made. "I chose this because..." is better than silence.
- **Own the outcome** — if a decision leads to harm, the leader owns it. "I made that call. It was wrong. I am sorry."

---

## Common Leadership Failures

| Failure | Why it happens | How to avoid |
|---------|---------------|-------------|
| **Decision paralysis** | Fear of being wrong | A wrong decision is usually fixable; no decision kills. Decide. |
| **Micromanagement** | Insecurity | Delegate. Trust the people you assigned. |
| **Favouritism** | Personal relationships | Apply rules equally. If you give your friend extra food, the group will fracture. |
| **Martyrdom** | "I will do everything myself" | You will burn out. Delegate. Your primary job is to lead, not to do. |
| **Refusal to admit error** | Ego | Admitting a mistake builds trust. Doubling down on error destroys it. |
| **Isolation** | "The leader must be alone" | The leader needs one trusted confidant to discuss decisions with. Isolation leads to poor judgement. |

---

## The Leader's Self-Care

A leader who collapses cannot lead. Prioritise:

- **Sleep** — delegate night watch. The leader needs at least 6 hours of sleep.
- **Food** — the leader eats. A leader running on empty makes bad decisions.
- **Water** — the leader drinks. Dehydration impairs cognition before it impairs the body.
- **Mental breaks** — 15 minutes alone, away from the group, once per day. Process. Breathe. Reset.

## 🇦🇺 Australian Convoy Leadership Context

### Convoy Leadership — Lead and Tail Vehicles

In Australian outback convoy travel, leadership is split between two key roles:

| Role | Who | Responsibilities |
|------|-----|-----------------|
| **Lead vehicle** | Most experienced person in the group | Sets pace, navigates, calls hazards, manages stops, makes route decisions |
| **Tail vehicle** | Second most experienced person | Ensures nobody is left behind, watches for mechanical issues, closes gates, last through river crossings |

**This dual-leadership model is standard Australian 4WD practice.** No single vehicle can manage convoy safety alone — the lead driver must focus on the road ahead, and the tail driver must focus on the convoy behind.

### Setting the Pace

The lead vehicle sets the pace based on road conditions and the LEAST capable vehicle in the convoy:

| Surface | Pace | Notes |
|---------|------|-------|
| Sealed road | Up to speed limit | Standard highway driving |
| Gravel / corrugations | 60–80 km/h | Reduce speed for the worst corrugations — shaking damages vehicles and people |
| Deep sand | 15–30 km/h | Momentum is critical — DO NOT stop unnecessarily |
| Rocky track | 10–20 km/h | Tyre-destroying terrain — pick lines carefully |
| River crossing | Walk it first | Lead vehicle walks the crossing. Then drives it slowly. Tail vehicle watches. |
| Mud | Variable | Slow, steady. Do not spin tyres — dig in. |

**The convoy is only as fast as the slowest vehicle.** If the lead driver constantly pulls ahead, the tail vehicle spends all day catching up — exhausting for everyone.

### Calling Hazards on UHF

The lead vehicle calls hazards to following vehicles:

> "Cattle on the road, 500 metres ahead — all vehicles slow down"
> "Washout on the left, take the right line"
> "Soft sand coming up — drop tyre pressure now"
> "Oncoming vehicle — slow down, single lane ahead"

**Standard UHF protocol:**

- Lead calls the hazard first
- Each vehicle in turn acknowledges ("Received, vehicle 2 slowing")
- Tail confirms: "Vehicle 4 acknowledges, last through"

### Managing Stops

The lead vehicle calls stops based on time and fuel:

- **Every 2 hours minimum** — get everyone out, stretch, check fluids
- **Every fuel stop** — reset the odometer, reconfirm route and fuel strategy
- **At every major junction** — wait for all vehicles. No exceptions.
- **When anyone needs** — medical, toilet, rest

**Stop protocol:**

1. Lead calls: "Stopping in 2 km at the creek crossing, all vehicles"
2. Each vehicle acknowledges
3. Lead pulls over in a safe, visible location
4. Tail pulls in behind and confirms all vehicles are present
