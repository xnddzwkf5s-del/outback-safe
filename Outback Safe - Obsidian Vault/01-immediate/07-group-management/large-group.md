# Large Group (50–200 People)

At this scale, the group becomes a small village. Not everyone knows everyone. Communication requires structure. Resources demand central management. Specialisation begins in earnest.

For context on how groups grow, see [Scaling Transitions](01-immediate/07-group-management/scaling-transitions).

## Leadership

- Single-leader model breaks down above ~30 people — a **council or committee** is required
- Council of 3–7 members representing different functions (water, food, security, health, tools)
- Council elects a spokesperson or head for coordination
- Delegated authority: sub-leaders manage specific domains
- Day-to-day decisions handled by domain leads, not the full council

## Water

- Central water source required — a single stream, well, or spring
- Distribution system: buckets, jerry cans, or improvised piping
- Storage tanks: 200 L drums or larger, multiple units
- Bulk storage target: **3 days' supply minimum**
- Boiling or filtration becomes a dedicated operation

## Food

- Foraging alone is insufficient for 50+ people — **hunting parties and foraging teams** are needed
- Rotating teams: 4–6 hunters, 4–6 foragers, 2–4 fishers
- Preservation becomes critical: smoking, drying, salting, fermenting
- Central food store with controlled access

## Shelter

- Village layout: family or friendship clusters of 4–8 people per structure
- Separate structures for: sleeping, cooking, storage, meetings, infirmary
- Communal spaces: central fire, eating area, workbench
- Paths between structures — keep them clear for movement and firebreaks

## Security

- Perimeter defined and patrolled
- 3–5 sentries per shift, 2-hour rotations
- Early warning system: tripwires, noise makers, lookout posts
- A dedicated security lead coordinates shifts and threat assessment

## Labour

**Specialisation begins.** People should not rotate through all tasks daily — assign based on aptitude.

| Role | Approx. % of Group | Responsibilities |
|---|---|---|
| Water & sanitation | 10 % | Collection, transport, treatment, waste management |
| Food & foraging | 20 % | Hunting, fishing, gathering, preservation |
| Construction & tools | 15 % | Shelter building, repairs, tool maintenance |
| Security | 15 % | Sentries, patrols, early warning |
| Health & medicine | 5 % | Wound care, illness, hygiene enforcement |
| Leadership & admin | 5 % | Council, record keeping, rationing |
| General labour | 30 % | Flexible pool for daily tasks |

**Total:** 100 % — everyone works.

## Decision-Making

- **Representative model** — domain leads handle their area; the council handles cross-domain issues (e.g. "do we use labour on shelter expansion or food storage?")
- Group-wide meetings once per week (or less frequently as systems stabilise)
- Council meetings daily or every second day

## Conflict

- Personal disputes become more common — not everyone will get along
- A designated mediator (council member) handles disputes
- Formal rules are written down (see Record Keeping)
- Consequences: extra labour shifts, loss of privileges, temporary exclusion
- The group must have a process — resentment builds quickly at this scale

## Mobility

- **Semi-permanent** — you can move seasonally, but pack-up takes 1–2 days
- Heavy items (storage tanks, tools) may need to be cached or left behind
- Plan for seasonal moves: summer camp vs. winter camp

## Resource Math

| Resource | Requirement per Person per Day | Group of 50 (Daily) | Group of 200 (Daily) |
|---|---|---|---|
| Water (drinking) | 1 L | 50 L | 200 L |
| Water (cooking/hygiene) | 2–3 L | 100–150 L | 400–600 L |
| Food energy | 2 000 kcal | 100 000 kcal | 400 000 kcal |
| Storage water (3-day buffer) | 3–4 L × 3 days | 450–600 L | 1 800–2 400 L |
| Shelter space (sleeping) | 2–3 m² | 100–150 m² | 400–600 m² |

### Daily Food Target in Real Terms

| Source | Approx. Yield | People Fed (at 2 000 kcal/day) |
|---|---|---|
| Medium deer | 30 kg meat (~60 000 kcal) | 30 people for 1 day |
| 10 kg fish | ~12 000 kcal | 6 people for 1 day |
| 5 kg edible plants/berries | ~3 000 kcal | 1.5 people for 1 day |
| 20 eggs | ~1 400 kcal | 0.7 people for 1 day |

**Takeaway:** A group of 50 needs roughly 2 medium deer (or equivalent) **per day**, plus foraging and fishing. Without agriculture, a group of 200 requires hunting on an industrial scale that is rarely sustainable.

## 🇦🇺 Australian Outback Context — Tag-Along Tours and Club Trips

In the Australian outback, "large group" means **tag-along tours or large 4WD club trips** — commercial or organised groups of 10–15+ vehicles travelling together. This is fundamentally different from the post-collapse settlement model described above.

### Commercial Tag-Along Tours

These are guided tours offered by operators on popular tracks:

| Tour type | Typical size | Duration | Example tracks |
|-----------|-------------|----------|---------------|
| Cape York tag-along | 8–12 vehicles | 10–14 days | OTL (Old Telegraph Track), CREB Track |
| Canning Stock Route tour | 8–15 vehicles | 4–6 weeks | Full Canning Stock Route |
| Fraser Island tour | 6–10 vehicles | 5–7 days | Fraser Island (K'gari) eastern beach |
| Simpson Desert tour | 10–15 vehicles | 10–14 days | Madigan Line, French Line, WAA Line |

**Roles in a tag-along tour:**

| Role | Responsibility |
|------|--------------|
| **Lead vehicle** (tour guide/driver) | Navigation, pace, hazard communication, meal coordination |
| **Tail vehicle** (tour guide/driver) | Ensures no one left behind, sweeper, mechanical support |
| **Intermediate guides** (on large tours) | Vehicle groups of 3–5, each with an experienced driver |
| **Tour participants** | Follow protocol, maintain radio discipline, assist with camp duties |

**Tag-along protocols:**

- **Morning briefing every day** — route plan, fuel stops, hazards, radio check
- **Radio check** — every vehicle calls in at the start: "Vehicle 1 good, Vehicle 2 good..."
- **Head count at every turn** — lead vehicle calls the turn, waits for tail to confirm all vehicles through
- **Fuel coordination** — tour operator arranges fuel drops or advises on fuel stops
- **Accommodation** — if catered, lead vehicle provides breakfast and dinner. Participants provide lunch.
- **No-one deviates from the convoy** without notifying the lead via radio

### Large 4WD Club Trips

4WD clubs (e.g., Land Rover Club, Toyota 4WD Club, Outback Adventurers) run trips but the structure is different from commercial tours:

| Aspect | Commercial tag-along | Club trip |
|--------|---------------------|-----------|
| **Organiser** | Paid tour operator | Club member (volunteer) |
| **Food** | Usually catered (dinner + breakfast) | Self-catered |
| **Fuel** | Operator coordinates | Individual responsibility |
| **Safety** | Operator carries sat phone/PLB, first-aid kit, recovery gear | Shared across members |
| **Pace** | Fixed itinerary | Flexible, consensus-based |

**Club trip size:** Typically 6–12 vehicles. At 15+ vehicles, the convoy becomes unwieldy — communication delay between front and back means the lead vehicle is 2+ km ahead by the time the tail reports an issue.

### The Convoy Communication Challenge

Large convoys (10+ vehicles) have a fundamental problem: **the lead vehicle cannot see the tail vehicle.**

- On a straight track, the lead may be 1–2 km ahead of the tail
- UHF range is 5–8 km — good enough, but the message must be relayed through all vehicles
- In dust, the lead disappears from view after 100 m

**Solution: Vehicle relay.** Each vehicle repeats the lead's call:

> Lead: "Convoy, 3 km to turn-off, reduce speed"
> Vehicle 2: "Convoy, turn-off in 3 km, reduce speed"
> Vehicle 3: "Turn-off in 3 km"
> ...
> Tail: "Received, tail slowing for turn-off"

### When Large Groups Work

Large convoys work well when:

- All vehicles have **reliable UHF radios** (handheld is acceptable but vehicle-mount is better)
- All drivers have **basic 4WD experience** (the group is only as strong as the weakest driver)
- The **route is well-defined** (Turn left at the windmill, turn right at the big gum tree)
- The tour operator or club leader has **done the track before**
- **Fuel range** of all vehicles is known and compatible

### When Large Groups Are a Problem

| Problem | Why | Mitigation |
|---------|-----|------------|
| **Different capabilities** | Stock SUV + heavily modified 4WD cannot travel at the same pace | Split into sub-groups (A-group = modified, B-group = stock) with separate leads |
| **Different experience** | Beginners + experts = frustration for both | Beginner vehicles stay near the front; experienced drivers at the back |
| **Pace expectations** | "Get there" vs. "stop at every creek" | Agree on philosophy before departure |
| **Long water/fuel stops** | 15 vehicles refuelling takes 45+ minutes | Self-serve: each driver manages their own fuel while others shower/buy food |
| **Camp space** | A large convoy needs a BIG flat area | Scout the camp before bringing the convoy in. Send Tail + one other to find space. |
