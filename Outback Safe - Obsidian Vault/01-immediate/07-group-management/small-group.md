# Small Group (5–20 People)

At this scale, the group functions like an extended household or a close-knit expedition team. Everyone knows everyone. Decisions are fast. Specialisation is minimal.

For context on how groups evolve, see [Scaling Transitions](01-immediate/07-group-management/scaling-transitions).

## Leadership

- A single leader or a pair works best — democratic votes for every decision waste time
- Leader chosen by demonstrated competence, not popularity
- Second-in-command ensures continuity if the leader is injured

## Water

- Individuals carry and manage their own water
- Small containers (1–5 L canteens, bottles, hydration bladders)
- Bulk storage is minimal — fill as needed from source
- See **[Water — Finding, Collecting, Purifying](01-immediate/02-water/)** for collection methods

## Food

- Foraging, trapping, and fishing can sustain the group day-to-day
- Limited food storage — a week's worth at most
- No need for large-scale preservation systems
- See **[Food (Emergency)](01-immediate/05-food-emergency/)** for emergency procurement

## Shelter

- 2–4 structures sufficient — a central communal shelter plus sleeping shelters
- Sleep in shared quarters for warmth and security
- Structures can be improvised; no need for permanent construction yet

## Security

- 1 sentry on duty at a time
- Rotating shifts of 2–4 hours
- Everyone participates — no dedicated guard role

## Labour

- Everyone does everything — there is no "cook" or "water carrier" role
- Tasks assigned daily by the leader based on immediate need
- Cross-training is organic: everyone learns basic skills by doing

## Decision-Making

- Fast and informal — leader decides, group can overrule by consensus
- Full-group discussions held once per day (morning briefing)
- Emergency decisions by the leader alone

## Conflict

- Personal — disputes are between individuals, not factions
- Resolved directly: talk it out or leader mediates
- No formal punishment system — ostracism is the ultimate sanction
- Small groups cannot afford prolonged internal conflict

## Mobility

- Can relocate quickly — pack and move in under an hour
- No heavy infrastructure to leave behind
- Advantage: outrun threats, follow resources seasonally

## Resource Math

| Resource | Requirement per Person per Day | Group of 10 (Daily) | Group of 20 (Daily) |
|---|---|---|---|
| Water (drinking) | 1 L minimum | 10 L | 20 L |
| Water (cooking/hygiene) | 2–3 L | 20–30 L | 40–60 L |
| Food energy | 2 000 kcal minimum | 20 000 kcal | 40 000 kcal |
| Shelter space | 2–3 m² sleeping | 20–30 m² | 40–60 m² |

**Note:** Water requirements double in hot climates or with heavy labour. Food requirements increase by 30–50 % during cold exposure.

## 🇦🇺 Australian Outback Context — Small Convoy (2–5 Vehicles)

**2–5 vehicles is the standard Australian outback trip.** This is the most common configuration for remote travel and aligns well with the small group dynamics described above.

### Typical Outback Small Convoy

| Vehicle count | Typical group size | Best for |
|--------------|-------------------|----------|
| 2 vehicles | 3–6 people | Close friends, couples. One breakdown = everyone affected. |
| 3 vehicles | 6–10 people | Ideal — one breakdown means 2 vehicles remain. Most resilient small convoy. |
| 4–5 vehicles | 10–16 people | Larger groups for big trips. Requires UHF communication discipline. |

**Single vehicle travel** (solo or family) is only recommended on well-travelled tracks:

| Track | Single vehicle OK? | Reason |
|-------|-------------------|--------|
| Tanami Track | Usually yes | Moderate traffic. Roadhouses spaced 200–300 km apart. |
| Gibb River Road (dry season) | Usually yes | Moderate traffic in winter. Roadhouses every 150–200 km. |
| Oodnadatta Track | Usually yes | Town network, moderate traffic. |
| Birdsville Track | Caution | Remote, limited traffic. Carry a PLB. |
| **Simpson Desert** | **No** | Extreme conditions, soft sand, no services. Minimum 2 vehicles. |
| **Canning Stock Route** | **No** | One of the most remote tracks on Earth. Minimum 3 vehicles. |
| **Anne Beadell Highway** | **No** | Extreme remoteness. Minimum 3 vehicles + sat phone + PLB. |

**Key rule:** Never take a single vehicle on extreme desert tracks (Simpson, Canning, Anne Beadell) without a sat phone and PLB, even with back-up. If your single vehicle breaks, you are stranded.

### Convoy Communication

- **UHF radio** for all convoy communication
- Channel 40 (highway) for passing vehicles or Channel 35/5 on remote tracks
- Each vehicle assigned a number (Vehicle 1 = lead, Vehicle 2, Vehicle 3 etc.)
- Call signs: "Vehicle 1 to convoy, stopping in 2 km"

### Daily Routine

| Time | Activity |
|------|----------|
| 06:00–07:00 | Wake, pack camp, breakfast |
| 07:00–07:15 | Morning briefing — route, fuel, hazards, radio check |
| 07:15–12:00 | Driving (morning block — cooler hours) |
| 12:00–13:00 | Lunch stop — fuel check, stretch, shade |
| 13:00–16:00 | Driving (afternoon block — hottest hours) |
| 16:00+ | Find camp — set up, cook, relax, debrief |
