# Conflict Resolution

Conflict in a survival group is inevitable. People are stressed, hungry, tired, and afraid. The question is not whether conflict will arise but how it is handled when it does. Unresolved conflict destroys groups; well-managed conflict can even strengthen them.

---

## Why Survival Groups Fight

| Trigger | Why it happens |
|---------|---------------|
| **Perceived unfairness** | "She got more food than me" — the most common trigger |
| **Workload imbalance** | "I have been gathering firewood all day while he sits there" |
| **Decision disagreement** | "We should stay here" vs. "We should move" |
| **Personality clashes** | Some people just do not get along, and stress magnifies this |
| **Panic contagion** | One person's fear infects others, leading to rash decisions |
| **Resource scarcity** | The less there is, the more people fight over it |

---

## De-escalation Techniques

When tempers flare, the goal is to reduce emotional intensity so rational discussion can occur.

{% steps %}
**Stay calm yourself** — your calm is the anchor. Do not match their anger.; **Lower your voice** — shouting at a shouting person escalates. A quiet, steady voice forces them to quiet down to hear you.; **Use their name** — "James, I hear you. Tell me what is wrong." Names are grounding.; **Acknowledge the emotion** — "I can see you are angry. That is understandable." Validation without agreement.; **Do not argue facts yet** — an angry person cannot process logic. Address emotion first, facts later.; **Create physical space** — step back. Invite them to sit down. Physical de-escalation precedes verbal.; **Offer a break** — "Let us both take 10 minutes and come back to this." Time reduces adrenaline.; **Reframe the problem** — "We both want the group to survive. We just disagree on how. Let us work on that together."
{% end %}

---

## Mediation Framework

When two group members are in conflict, a neutral third party (the leader or another trusted person) mediates:

{% steps %}
**Separate the parties** — talk to each one individually first. Let each tell their side without interruption.; **Bring them together** — in a neutral space, not in front of the whole group.; **Set ground rules** — no interrupting, no personal attacks, focus on the problem not the person.; **Each person states their view** — what happened, how they feel, what they need.; **Identify common ground** — "You both want what is best for the group."; **Brainstorm solutions** — what could resolve this?; **Agree on a solution** — both parties must accept it. If they cannot agree, the mediator decides.; **Follow up** — check in the next day. Is the solution holding?
{% end %}

---

## When to Separate

Some conflicts cannot be resolved. When conflict threatens group safety:

| Situation | Action |
|-----------|--------|
| Physical violence or credible threat | Immediate separation. The aggressor is removed from the group or restrained. |
| Persistent sabotage (hoarding, refusing work, undermining leadership) | Private warning → public accountability → separation |
| Irreconcilable personality conflict (constant fighting) | One party moves to a different sub-group or camp area |
| Mental health crisis (psychosis, suicidal ideation) | Constant supervision. This is a medical issue, not a discipline issue. |

**Separation is a last resort but sometimes the only option.** A group of 4 functional people survives better than a group of 5 with one person destroying cohesion.

## 🇦🇺 Australian Convoy Conflict Context

### Common Sources of Conflict in Outback Travel

Outback convoy conflict is rarely about personality — it is usually about **pressure, fatigue, and differing expectations.**

| Trigger | Why it happens | AU example |
|---------|---------------|------------|
| **Route choice** | "We should take the shorter track" vs. "The longer track is safer" | Directly common: Oodnadatta Track vs. Stuart Highway; Birdsville Track vs. the paved road |
| **Pace** | "I want to make distance" vs. "I want to enjoy the scenery" | The fault line between "get there" drivers and "enjoy the journey" drivers |
| **Risk tolerance** | Different comfort levels with river crossings, rough tracks, or night driving | "I'll drive through that creek" vs. "No way — I'm walking it first" |
| **Fatigue** | Long days of corrugations and heat make everyone irritable | Day 3 on the Canning Stock Route — tempers fray |
| **Resource disagreement** | Fuel, water, or time budgets | "We need to refuel now" vs. "We have enough to reach Halls Creek" |
| **Breakdown frustration** | Someone's vehicle causes an unplanned delay | Tyre punctures, mechanical failures, bogging in sand |

### Decision Protocol — The Most Experienced Makes the Call

In Australian outback travel, there is a clearly established hierarchy for decision-making:

1. **The person with the most relevant experience** makes the final call after consulting the group
2. If there is a debate, everyone states their position, the lead driver decides, and the group accepts it
3. **Decisions are not up for renegotiation after they are made** — once the convoy commits to a route or action, everyone follows

The lead vehicle / most experienced person carries the weight of this decision-making role. Respect it.

### The 4WD Convention — Any One Person Can Veto

This is a respected convention in Australian 4WD travel:

**Any one person in the convoy can veto a river crossing, a dune attempt, or a risky track.**

- If someone says "I am not comfortable crossing that creek", the crossing is abandoned
- If someone says "That dune looks too soft — I do not want to attempt it", the attempt is called off
- **No pressure, no shaming, no negotiating** — the veto is absolute

**Why:** The person who is uncomfortable makes mistakes under pressure. A forced crossing by an unwilling driver is the most dangerous moment in outback travel. If even one person feels unsafe, the group finds an alternate route or waits for conditions to improve.

### If Major Disagreement — Split the Convoy

Sometimes a disagreement cannot be resolved. The convoy splits:

| Rule | Detail |
|------|--------|
| **Minimum 2 vehicles per group** | No solo vehicle — if a group splits, each sub-group must have at least 2 vehicles |
| **PLB/sat phone allocation** | If the group has one PLB or sat phone, the less experienced group carries it |
| **Agreed rendezvous point** | Both groups agree on the next meeting point (a town, a roadhouse, a named track junction) |
| **UHF channel** | Both groups agree on a monitoring channel and check-in schedule (e.g., every evening at 6 PM on Channel 40) |
| **No hard feelings** | Split is a practical decision, not a breakup. "We will catch up at the Mitchell Falls turnoff in 3 days." |

### De-escalation in the Outback Context

When a disagreement arises in the convoy:

{% steps %}
**Stop the vehicles** — do not argue while driving. Pull over at a safe spot.; **Get everyone out** — sitting in a hot vehicle makes everything worse. Stand in the shade.; **One person speaks at a time** — the lead driver (or tail driver if the lead is involved) facilitates.; **State the problem clearly** — "We disagree on whether to take the Tanami or stay on the highway. What are everyone's concerns?"; **Find the common ground** — everyone wants to get home safely. The disagreement is about HOW, not WHY.; **Decide** — if consensus cannot be reached, refer to the veto rule or use the decision protocol.; **Move on** — once decided, drop the topic. Do not re-litigate at the campfire.
{% end %}
