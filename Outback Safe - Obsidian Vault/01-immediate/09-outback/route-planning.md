## Before You Leave — The Safety Triangle

The most important safety tool on any outback trip is a properly briefed person who is **not going** with you.

### Trip Intention Form

Prepare a written trip intention form and leave it with a responsible contact. Include every detail — no such thing as too much.

| Field | Details to Include |
|---|---|
| **Route** | List every waypoint, track name, and planned camp. Include dates and estimated arrival times. |
| **Vehicle** | Make, model, colour, registration number. Attach a photo if possible. |
| **People** | Full names, ages, mobile numbers, known medical conditions/allergies. |
| **Equipment** | Water carried (litres per person), comms gear, recovery kit, first aid. |
| **Return** | Expected return date and time. Include a **"Raise alarm by"** deadline — the time at which your contact should stop waiting and call emergency services. |

### Who to Leave It With

- Local police station (preferred — they hold formal Job Sheets)
- Park ranger (for national park trips)
- Responsible family member or friend who will act on it

**Critical rule:** If you change your route — even a single detour to a different track — notify your contact immediately. An alarm raised at the wrong location can be fatal.

## Route Planning

### Paper Maps (Your Primary Navigation)

Digital devices fail. Paper maps do not. Carry them and know how to read them.

| Brand | Best For |
|---|---|
| **Hema** | Best all-rounder for outback. Their Desert Series and Great Desert Tracks atlases are the gold standard. |
| **Westprint** | Specialist maps for SA, NT and WA deserts. |
| **State Government Topographic** | Free or low-cost. Best detail for walking/emergency navigation. |

### GPS & Digital Navigation

- **Garmin** — with offline topo maps. Most reliable dedicated device.
- **Hema Explorer** — mobile app, works well, download maps before you go.
- **Avenza Maps** — georeferenced PDF maps on your phone. Download from state mapping agencies.
- **Gaia GPS** — good for topo, satellite overlay, and route tracking.

Carry a secondary device with offline maps as backup. Phones lose signal. Devices run out of battery.

### Map Reading Basics

- **Grid references:** Learn MGRS or GDA94/MGA — 6-digit gives 100 m accuracy, 8-digit gives 10 m.
- **Contour lines:** 20 m intervals on most outback maps. Closer lines = steeper terrain.
- **Track classifications:** Understand the difference between a sealed road (Class 1), a maintained gravel road (Class 2-3), and a formed earth track (Class 4-5).

### Track Research Before You Go

Check current conditions — not old forum posts, not last year's trip report:

- **Facebook groups** for the area (e.g. Flinders Ranges 4WD, Simpson Desert tours)
- **National park websites** for closures and fire danger ratings
- **BOM weather** for flood warnings and severe heat
- **Track condition reports** from Parks Victoria, NPWS, QPWS — many publish weekly updates in season

### Seasonal Considerations

| Season | Restriction |
|---|---|
| **Wet season (Nov–Apr)** — Northern Australia | Most tracks impassable. Flooding closes roads for days. Do not travel during this period in remote NT, QLD, or northern WA. |
| **Summer (Dec–Feb)** — Southern Australia | Extreme fire danger. Many national parks close entirely on Total Fire Ban days. Heatstroke risk above 40°C. |
| **Autumn/Spring** | Prime travel window for most of Australia. |
| **Winter (Jun–Aug)** | Excellent in the north. Very cold nights (below 0°C) in inland SA, QLD, and central Australia. |

### Fuel Planning

Run the numbers before you leave. Do not rely on "I'll find a servo."

1. Calculate total trip distance (include detours and exploring)
2. Multiply by 1.15 for rough terrain consumption increase
3. Identify your last guaranteed fuel stop
4. Determine if you need to carry jerry cans

**Typical consumption estimates:**

| Terrain | Consumption (L/100 km) | Range from 80 L tank |
|---|---|---|
| Sealed highway | 11–14 | 570–730 km |
| Gravel road | 14–18 | 440–570 km |
| Rough/soft tracks | 18–25 | 320–440 km |
| Deep sand | 22–30 | 260–360 km |

**Jerry can rule:** Carry 20 L per jerry and strap securely. Never mount on bonnet (blocks vision, hazards in crash). Roof-rack only if rated and low-profile.

### Daily Distance Limits

| Terrain | Daily Maximum |
|---|---|
| Sealed roads (highway) | 300–500 km |
| Gravel roads | 200–300 km |
| Rough tracks | 100–200 km |
| Deep sand/dune fields | 50–100 km |

These are **including** breaks, recovery time, and photograph stops. Outback driving is exhausting. Plan for fewer km than you think.

### Camp Location Planning

Pre-plan your camp each day. Look for:

- Near a reliable water source (where known — never assume)
- Shade (trees, cliff faces, vehicle shadow)
- Away from floodways and creek beds — flash flooding kills
- High ground if rain is forecast
- Clear of heavy fuel load (grass, scrub) in fire season
- Good UHF reception if possible (hilltop/clearing)

## Communications Schedule

### Daily Check-In Protocol

Establish a specific check-in time with your contact. Every day, without fail, at the same time.

1. **Call or sat-text** your contact at the scheduled time
2. **Report:** current GPS coordinates (MGRS or lat/lon), next waypoint, crew condition
3. **State:** next day's planned route and campsite
4. **Confirm:** next check-in time

### Missed Check-In Procedure

Your contact must know exactly when to escalate:

| Time Since Missed Check-In | Action |
|---|---|
| +0 hours | Check-in was due. Contact waits. |
| +1 hour | Contact tries to reach you — phone, text, email. |
| +3 hours | Contact calls the local police station with your trip intention form. |
| Raise alarm | Police initiate search using your route card. |

### UHF Radio Protocol

| Channel | Use |
|---------|-----|
| Ch 5 | **EMERGENCY ONLY** (repeater-enabled, legal restriction) — do not use for general chat |
| Ch 10 | 4WD clubs and convoys |
| Ch 11 | **CALLING channel** — make contact, then move to another channel |
| Ch 18 | Caravan and camper convoys |
| Ch 29 | Pacific Highway (NSW/QLD) |
| Ch 40 | **HIGHWAY** — trucks, road trains, general highway use. Monitor this when driving. |
| Ch 35 (Deprecated) | Historically the emergency channel. Some legacy repeaters may still operate, but Ch 5 is the official emergency channel. Do not rely on Ch 35. |

If travelling in convoy, agree on a dedicated convoy channel (typically 4WD clubs use channels 12–16, 24–30, or 34–40 depending on your radio — check the manual for repeater/official allocations in your state).

### Satellite Communications

Only reliable way to communicate from deep outback. Options:

| Device | Type | Pros | Cons |
|---|---|---|---|
| Garmin inReach | Two-way satellite texting + SOS | Reliable, map sharing, weather forecasts | Subscription required |
| Zoleo | Two-way satellite texting + SOS | Lower cost, pairs with phone app | Phone battery dependent |
| Iridium GO | Satellite hotspot (voice + text) | Full voice calls over satellite | Bulky, expensive, redundant for most trips |
| PLB (EPIRB) | One-way emergency beacon | No subscription, 406 MHz + homing | No messaging — only SOS |

Carry a PLB **in addition to** a satellite messenger. PLBs have no subscription, never fail to connect, and work in the worst conditions.

## Permits & Legal

### National Park Permits

| State | Agency | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **NSW** | NPWS | Most parks require vehicle entry and camping permits. Book via their website. |
| **QLD** | QPWS | Camping permits required for nearly all parks. Online booking system. |
| **SA** | DEWNR / National Parks SA | Desert parks (Flinders, Lake Eyre, etc.) need permits. Book online. |
| **WA** | DBCA | Remote parks (Karijini, Cape Range, etc.) need entry and camping permits. |
| **NT** | NT Parks | Central desert parks (Finke Gorge, Watarrka, etc.) require permits. |
| **VIC** | Parks Victoria | Mostly free entry, but camping must be booked, especially in Alpine areas. |
| **TAS** | Parks and Wildlife Service | Parks Pass required for vehicle entry to most parks. |
| **ACT** | ACT Parks | Limited remote travel but camping permits required in Namadgi. |

### Aboriginal Land Permits

Large areas of remote Australia are Aboriginal land. **You must have a permit to enter.**

- **NT** — Northern Land Council (north) or Central Land Council (south). Apply 2–4 weeks in advance. Covers Arnhem Land, Simpson Desert access, and many remote areas.
- **SA** — APY Lands — strict permits required. Apply via the APY Lands office.
- **WA** — Various PBCs (Prescribed Body Corporates). Check individual areas.
- **QLD** — Cape York — many communities require permits. Check before you go.

Aboriginal land permits are enforced. Fines apply. Some areas also have cultural fees.

### Station / Private Land

Never enter a cattle station or private property without permission.

- **Ring ahead** — most station homesteads are visible from access roads but may be unoccupied.
- **Leave gates as you find them** — open to open, closed to closed. This is not optional.
- **Do not camp** without asking. Some stations offer paid camp sites.
- **Do not approach stock** — do not harass cattle, sheep, or horses.

### Camping Permits

Required in most national parks and some state forests. Book online before you leave — many remote camp sites have limited capacity.

- **Some national parks have self-registration** (envelope system at the park entrance). Carry cash.
- **Most popular parks** require online booking weeks in advance.

### Fire Regulations

- **Total Fire Ban days** — no fires whatsoever. Gas stoves with an on/off switch are usually permitted. Solid fuel stoves are not. Check the local fire authority website daily.
- **Permit fires** — required in most parks and on some private land outside fire season.
- **Never leave a fire unattended** — it's illegal, dangerous, and can become a bushfire within minutes.
- **Use established fire pits** where available. Do not build new rings.

### Rubbish — Leave No Trace

**All rubbish goes out with you.** Nothing gets buried. Nothing gets burned (including paper and food scraps in arid areas).

- Carry extra rubbish bags
- Separate food waste and pack out
- If you find someone else's rubbish, carry it out too

## Vehicle Preparation Checklist

### Mechanical Check (Full Service)

Before any outback trip, have the vehicle serviced by a mechanic who knows it's going remote:

| System | Check |
|---|---|
| Engine oil & filter | Change before trip. Carry 5 L spare. |
| Air filter | Replace if dusty. Carry a spare. |
| Fuel filter | Replace. Carry a spare. |
| Oil filter | Carry a spare. |
| Belts | Check for cracks, tension. Carry a spare set. |
| Hoses | Check for cracks, bulges, soft spots. Carry a spare set. |
| Brakes | Pads, rotors, fluid level and condition. |
| Coolant | Flush and pressure test radiator. Carry 50/50 premix. |

### Tyres

- **All-terrain** or **mud-terrain** tyres — not highway (HT) tyres for remote tracks.
- **2 spares minimum** for remote travel (Simpson Desert, Canning Stock Route, etc.).
- Check spare: is it the same size/brand? Is it inflated correctly? Does it match your road tyres?
- Carry: tyre repair kit, two 12 V compressors (one backup), pressure gauge, tyre deflator.

### Suspension & Steering

- **Shock absorbers** — outback corrugations destroy worn shocks in hours. Replace if close to end of life.
- **Springs** — sagged springs reduce ground clearance and load capacity.
- **Bushes** — worn suspension bushes accelerate tyre wear and degrade handling at speed.

### Cooling System

Overheating at 45°C in the middle of nowhere is a life-threatening situation.

- Flush cooling system
- Pressure test radiator cap and hoses
- Check radiator for debris blockage (bugs, mud between fins)
- Carry spare: radiator cap, thermostat, hose repair kit, coolant

### Fuel System

- Check for leaks at all connections
- Carry a spare fuel filter
- If driving diesel, carry a spare lift pump and know the bleed procedure
- If diesel with a water separator, empty it before departure

### Electrical

- **Dual battery system** highly recommended — fridge, lights, and devices on auxiliary battery. Cranking battery stays separate.
- Check alternator output
- Carry spare fuses (all types used in your vehicle), spare bulbs
- Carry a portable jump starter (Lithium-ion type) as a backup
- Clean all battery terminals and apply anti-corrosion spray

### Accessories

- **Bullbar** — highly recommended for animal strikes and recovery points.
- **Driving lights** — not essential but very useful for outback night driving (minimise night driving though).
- **Snorkel** — only useful if you plan deep water crossings. Not needed for dust (modern air boxes are already baffled).

### Load Distribution

| Rule | Why |
|---|---|
| Heavy items low and forward | Keeps centre of gravity stable, improves handling. |
| Strap everything | A 20 L jerry can becomes a lethal projectile in a sudden stop or rollover. |
| Roof-rack as last resort | Raises centre of gravity significantly. Only carry light, bulky items. |
| Check GVM before loading | Gross Vehicle Mass includes passengers, fuel, water, gear. **Do not exceed it.** |

If you're unsure about weight, find a public weighbridge before you leave. Overloaded vehicles handle dangerously and are illegal.

## Convoy Procedures

### Ideal Vehicle Count

- **Minimum:** 2 vehicles for any remote travel
- **Ideal:** 2–3 vehicles
- **Maximum (for a single group):** 4–5 (track dust makes larger groups dangerous)

A single vehicle in remote Australia is a survival scenario waiting to happen. If you must travel solo, carry a PLB, sat messenger, and be extremely conservative.

### Lead Vehicle Responsibilities

- **Navigates** — holds the route card and GPS.
- **Sets the pace** — drives at the slowest vehicle's comfortable speed.
- **Calls hazards** on UHF — oncoming traffic, washouts, stock, sharp rocks, soft sand patches. Call early.
- **Reports turns** — "Turning left onto Middle Earth Track in 500 metres."

### Tail Vehicle Responsibilities

- **Counts** — ensures all vehicles make each turn.
- **Keeps visual contact** — drops back if necessary to see the vehicle behind.
- **Reports lost vehicles** — "Convoy, tail. Vehicle 3 has stopped. All stop."

### Spacing

| Condition | Distance Between Vehicles |
|---|---|
| Sealed road | 200–500 m |
| Gravel road | 200–300 m (reduce at speed) |
| Dusty track | 100–200 m (reduce to keep visual only) |
| Deep sand | Stopped — only one vehicle per dune at a time |

### Dust Protocol

- **Lights ON** at all times in convoy (day and night)
- **Windows UP** and **recirculate air** when dust is thick
- If you cannot see through the dust, slow down until you can
- Use UHF to maintain contact — "Vehicle 2, can you see my lights?"

### Lost Vehicle Protocol

1. Stop at the last known turn or landmark
2. Wait 15 minutes
3. Send a vehicle (never the whole convoy) back to retrace the route
4. Maintain UHF contact with the missing vehicle
5. If still no contact after 30 minutes, call the trip intention contact

### River Crossings

- **Walk first** — check depth, current, bottom condition
- **Enter in low range, 2nd gear** — steady throttle, don't change gear
- **Bow wave** — maintain speed to create a bow wave that keeps water out of the engine bay
- **One vehicle at a time** — never cross with multiple vehicles
- **After crossing** — check diff breathers, brake function
- **Know your wading depth** — standard 4WD: ~600 mm. With snorkel: 700–900 mm or more. Winch air intake: depends on setup.

## Emergency Response Plan

### The Five Steps

**Step 1 — STOP and STAY CALM**

Do not make decisions from panic. Sit down. Take three deep breaths. The vehicle is your shelter and your best chance of being found.

**Step 2 — ASSESS**

| Question | Answer Determines |
|---|---|
| Is anyone injured? | First aid priority |
| How much water do we have? | Rationing protocol |
| Can we communicate? | Which Step 3 method to try first |
| Where exactly are we? | Whether walking out is feasible |

**Step 3 — COMMUNICATE**

Attempt in this order:

1. **PLB/EPIRB** — activate immediately if life-threatening or no comms. No subscription required. A 406 MHz PLB with GPS sends your exact coordinates to the Australian Rescue Coordination Centre.
2. **Satellite text** (inReach, Zoleo, Iridium) — send SOS or text your contact a location update.
3. **UHF Radio** — call on channel 5 (emergency repeater) or channel 40 (call for general traffic to relay).
4. **Mobile phone** — only works in range of a tower. Try a high point. If no signal after 5 minutes, conserve battery.

**Step 4 — MAKE VEHICLE VISIBLE**

- Raise bonnet (distress signal)
- Tie reflective material (towel, foil blanket, mirror) to roof or antenna
- Stamp/arrange ground signals in clearing: **X** (need medical), **SOS** (3 large letters), arrow pointing to your location
- If safe, light a smoky fire (green leaves on base of fire) in daylight; clear flame at night
- Use your headlights and hazard lights if you have battery power

**Step 5 — CONSERVE AND WAIT**

- **Ration water immediately:** 3–4 L per person per day in hot conditions. Do not wait until you're thirsty.
- **Create shade** — rig tarp from side of vehicle. Stay in the shade of the vehicle at minimum.
- **Rest during the day** — travel/walk only at dawn and dusk if necessary.
- **Signal check:** every 30 minutes do a visual and audible signal sweep (mirror flash, horn blast).

### When Walking Out Is Justified

Walking out is **almost always the wrong decision**. Only consider it if:

- You know there is help within 20 km
- There is a visible track or road leading to civilisation
- You have at least 4 L of water per person for the walk
- The injured party can be moved safely
- A clear, detailed note is left in the vehicle stating your direction of travel, time of departure, and condition

If you walk out and miss the search party coming from the other direction, you die. **Stay with the vehicle.**
