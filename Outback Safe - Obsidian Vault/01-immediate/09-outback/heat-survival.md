# Extreme Heat Survival

The Australian outback regularly exceeds 45°C in summer. Ground temperatures can reach 70°C+ on dark sand or bitumen. In these conditions, the difference between comfort, injury, and death is measured in hours. Understanding how heat kills you — and how to stop it — is the most critical survival skill for outback travellers.

---

## Heat Illness Progression

| Stage | Core Temp | Symptoms | Action |
|-------|-----------|----------|--------|
| **Heat Cramps** | 37°C–38.5°C | Painful muscle spasms (often legs/abdomen), heavy sweating | Rest in shade, drink electrolyte solution, stretch gently |
| **Heat Exhaustion** | 38.5°C–40°C | Heavy sweating, cold/clammy skin, weak pulse, nausea, headache, dizziness, pale skin | Move to shade immediately, lie down, elevate feet, drink cool water (sips), remove excess clothing, apply cool wet cloths |
| **Heat Stroke** | > 40°C | **Hot dry skin** (no sweating), red/flushed skin, **confusion/agitation**, rapid strong pulse, body temp rising, **loss of consciousness** | **This is a medical emergency.** Cool the body by any means: cold water immersion if available, wet cloths on neck/armpits/groin, fan aggressively. Call 000. Without treatment, death occurs within minutes. |

**Heat stroke kills.** Unlike heat exhaustion, the body has stopped sweating — it can no longer cool itself. Once core temperature exceeds 41°C, organ damage begins. Permanent brain damage or death follows rapidly.

---

## Heat Stroke Recognition — Quick Test

If someone in your group collapses or seems confused in extreme heat, check:

1. **Skin: is it dry?** If they have stopped sweating and skin is hot and dry, this is heat stroke
2. **Mental state: are they confused, aggressive, or unconscious?** Confusion is a late sign
3. **Pulse: is it rapid and strong?** (vs. heat exhaustion where pulse is weak)
4. **Temperature: is their skin hot to touch?** If it hurts to hold your hand on their skin for more than 2 seconds, they are dangerously hot

**Heat stroke is a life-threatening emergency.** A person with heat stroke must be cooled before anything else — before water, before calling for help. Cool first, treat second.

---

## Heat Stroke — Emergency Treatment (Golden Window: 30 Minutes)

Heat stroke is a MEDICAL EMERGENCY. Mortality increases rapidly without treatment. The goal is to reduce core body temperature to below 39°C within 30 minutes.

**Cold water immersion (GOLD STANDARD):**
If you have access to any body of water (creek, billabong, water-filled tarp, even a cattle trough):
1. Immerse the person up to their neck in the coldest water available
2. Support their head — they may lose consciousness
3. Monitor continuously — do NOT leave them alone in water
4. Remove when shivering begins or when temperature is below 39°C (if you can measure it)

**If immersion is not possible:**
1. Move to shade
2. Remove clothing
3. Pour water over the entire body — any water, any temperature (even warm water cools through evaporation)
4. Fan the person — create airflow (vehicle fan, manual fanning, anything)
5. Apply ice packs or cold wet cloths to neck, armpits, and groin (where major blood vessels are close to skin)
6. Continue until person is alert and oriented

**What NOT to do:**
- Do NOT give fluids by mouth if the person is confused or unconscious (aspiration risk)
- Do NOT use alcohol or ice baths (can cause vasoconstriction, slowing heat loss)
- Do NOT delay cooling while waiting for help — cool first, transport second

**After cooling:**
- The person can RE-HEAT after initial cooling (temperature instability). Monitor continuously for 24 hours.
- Watch for dark urine (rhabdomyolysis — muscle breakdown releasing myoglobin that can cause kidney failure). This requires hospital treatment.
- Even if the person seems recovered, seek medical assessment. Heat stroke causes organ damage that may not be immediately apparent.

**Exertional vs Classic Heat Stroke:**
- **Exertional:** from physical activity in heat (younger, fitter people pushing too hard). Body is HOT and SWEATING. Rapid onset.
- **Classic:** from environmental heat exposure (older, medically vulnerable, no activity). Body is HOT and DRY (sweating mechanism failed). Gradual onset over days.

Both are treated identically — COOL FAST.

## Shade Creation

Shade is your most urgent priority — more important than water, more important than fire. In 45°C, you have roughly 3 hours without shade before heat stroke risk becomes life-threatening.

### Vehicle Shade

- Park so the vehicle casts maximum shade at midday
- The **southern side** of the vehicle gets the longest shade in Australia (sun tracks north)
- Open all doors on the southern side to create a larger shaded area
- Lie under the vehicle chassis — the body blocks direct sun and the ground underneath stays cooler

### Tarp and Blanket Shade

- Tie a tarp or emergency blanket between the vehicle roof and the ground on the southern side
- Angle it so the vehicle is one wall and the tarp is the roof
- Leave space underneath for airflow — do not box yourself in

### Digging In

- Sand can be dug to create a shaded pit
- Dig a trench on the southern side of the vehicle, wide enough to lie in
- Cover the trench with the tarp, weighted down with rocks or sand
- The ground below 30 cm stays significantly cooler than the surface

### Natural Shade

- Mulga, coolabah, and desert oaks provide shade — but check for dead branches
- Creek beds with overhanging banks provide excellent shade
- Under rock overhangs and cliff faces

## Activity Timing

| Time | Activity |
|------|----------|
| 05:00–09:00 | Work: set up camp, gather firewood, explore, signal |
| 09:00–10:00 | Transition: finish all physical work, enter shade |
| 10:00–16:00 | **Rest. No physical activity.** Hydrate. Sleep. Stay in shade. |
| 16:00–18:00 | Work: improve shelter, collect more fuel, check signals |
| 18:00–20:00 | Transition to evening: eat, plan next day, UHF/phone checks |
| 20:00–05:00 | Rest, sleep, overnight watch rotation |

**The 10:00–16:00 rest window is not optional.** Working through the middle of the day in outback heat is how people die from heat stroke.

---

## Daily Rhythm in Extreme Heat

The structured day below mirrors the Activity Timing table above, with added context on why each window matters.

- **0500:** Wake, hydrate, pack camp, travel
- **1000:** Stop for the day. Find or make shade. No more driving.
- **1000–1600:** Rest in maximum shade. No physical activity. Sip water every 30 minutes.
- **1600:** If water is sufficient and vehicle is working, drive 1–2 hours to reposition
- **1800:** Set camp, cook if safe, prepare for night

**Why the midday shutdown matters:** The human body cannot cool itself once ambient temperature exceeds 37°C without effective evaporation. When the wet-bulb temperature (combined heat and humidity) exceeds survivability limits, the body's cooling system fails entirely — sweating no longer works. At 40°C+ air temperature, heat stroke risk spikes dramatically with any physical exertion. This is why **no physical activity between 1000 and 1600** is a rule, not a recommendation.

---

## Wet Bulb Temperature — When Sweating Stops Working

Your body cools itself by evaporating sweat. When humidity is high, sweat doesn't evaporate — it drips off uselessly. The **wet bulb temperature** measures the combined effect of heat AND humidity on the human body's ability to cool itself.

**The 35°C wet bulb threshold:**
At a wet bulb temperature of 35°C, a healthy person at complete rest in shade with unlimited drinking water will eventually die from overheating. The body simply cannot shed heat fast enough.

**How to estimate wet bulb (no instruments):**
- If you are sweating and it IS evaporating (you feel cooler, skin is drying): you are below wet bulb limits. You can cool yourself.
- If you are sweating and it is NOT evaporating (sweat dripping continuously, skin stays wet, no cooling sensation): you are approaching wet bulb limits. STOP ALL ACTIVITY.
- If the air feels "heavy," "thick," or "oppressive" and even in shade you can't cool down: wet bulb is dangerously high.

**Wet bulb danger zone (rough guide):**

| Dry Temp | Humidity | Wet Bulb (approx) | Danger |
|----------|----------|-------------------|--------|
| 45°C | 10% (desert) | ~22°C | Low — sweat works well |
| 40°C | 50% (coastal) | ~30°C | High — limit activity |
| 38°C | 70% (tropical) | ~32°C | Very High — rest in shade |
| 36°C | 85% (monsoon buildup) | ~34°C | Extreme — stop everything |
| 35°C | 100% | ~35°C | Lethal — body cannot cool itself |

**What to do when wet bulb is high:**
1. Reduce activity to absolute minimum — do not exert yourself
2. Maximise airflow — fan yourself, find wind, create breeze
3. Use water for evaporative cooling — wet your skin and clothing
4. Seek the coolest microclimate — deep shade, creek bed, air-conditioned vehicle if running
5. Monitor for heat illness — wet bulb conditions can cause heat stroke without physical exertion

**Regional note:**
- **Desert (Simpson, Central Australia):** Wet bulb rarely dangerous — humidity is very low. Dry heat is survivable with shade and water.
- **Tropical coast (Darwin, Cairns, Broome):** Wet bulb can reach dangerous levels during wet season buildup (October–December). This is when healthy people die from heat exposure without ever exercising.

## Hydration Strategy

| Condition | Water Requirement |
|-----------|-------------------|
| Rest, 30°C shade | 500 mL per hour |
| Rest, 40°C shade | 750 mL per hour |
| Rest, 45°C+ shade | 1 L per hour |
| Walking, 40°C | 1.5 L per hour |
| Walking, 45°C | 2 L per hour |

### How to Hydrate

- **Sip, don't chug.** 500 mL every 20–30 minutes. Chugging water triggers urination and wastes water.
- **Drink before you're thirsty.** Thirst means you are already dehydrated.
- **Electrolyte replacement.** Add a pinch of salt to each litre of water. Without salt, you can develop hyponatraemia (water poisoning) from drinking plain water in excess.
- **Never ration water by reducing intake below 2 L per day.** Rationing water causes dehydration, which impairs judgement and kidney function.
- **Do not drink alcohol or caffeine.** Both are diuretics and accelerate dehydration.

### Signs of Dehydration

| Stage | Signs |
|-------|-------|
| Mild | Thirst, dry mouth, dark urine |
| Moderate | Fatigue, headache, dizziness, no urination for 6+ hours |
| Severe | Confusion, rapid breathing, inability to sweat, no urination for 12+ hours |
| Critical | Unconsciousness, organ failure |

### Improvised Electrolyte Replacement (No Commercial Products)

If you have no electrolyte sachets, no salt, and no commercial products, you can still replace electrolytes from natural sources.

**Warning:** If you have salt and sugar, use the standard oral rehydration formula: 1L water + 6 level teaspoons sugar + 0.5 level teaspoon salt. This is MORE EFFECTIVE than any improvised method. Only use the methods below if you have nothing else.

**Bush electrolyte sources:**
- **Animal blood** (cooked): Rich in sodium, potassium, and iron. Cook thoroughly before consuming. Collect from freshly killed game or butchering. A small amount of cooked blood mixed with water provides electrolytes. Do NOT drink raw blood — parasite risk.
- **Bone broth:** Boil animal bones (kangaroo, goanna, any available) for 1+ hours. The broth contains sodium, potassium, calcium, and magnesium. Drink the liquid.
- **Saltbush (Atriplex species):** Widespread across arid Australia. Chew fresh leaves to extract salty liquid, spit out the fibrous leaf. Leaves contain 20-30% sodium chloride by dry weight. DO NOT eat large quantities — oxalate content can cause kidney stress.
- **Samphire (Sarcocornia/Salicornia):** Coastal and inland salt lakes. Succulent stems, naturally salty. Eat raw in small amounts. Contains sodium, magnesium, potassium.
- **Charcoal/ash:** Wood ash (from clean hardwood, NOT treated timber or painted wood) mixed into water provides potassium and calcium. Use VERY small amount — about 1/4 teaspoon per litre. Too much causes gastrointestinal distress. Filter through cloth before drinking.

**Signs you need electrolytes (not just water):**
- Muscle cramps (especially legs, hands)
- Headache that doesn't improve with water alone
- Confusion, irritability
- Dark urine despite drinking adequate water
- Swollen fingers or ankles (water retention — body holding fluid because sodium is too low)

**Hyponatraemia (water intoxication):**
Drinking large amounts of plain water without electrolyte replacement dilutes blood sodium. Symptoms: headache, confusion, nausea, seizures. This can be fatal and has killed endurance athletes and outback travellers.

**Prevention:** For every 3-4 litres of water consumed in heat, consume some electrolyte source (bone broth, saltbush, ash water, or a pinch of salt if available). Do not drink more than 1 litre of plain water per hour for extended periods without electrolyte intake.

## Clothing

| Item | Requirement |
|------|-------------|
| **Shirt** | Light-coloured, long-sleeved, loose-fit cotton or linen. Not synthetic — synthetic traps heat. |
| **Pants** | Light-coloured, long pants. Protects legs from sun and ground heat. |
| **Hat** | Wide-brimmed — no caps. The brim must shade your neck and ears. |
| **Neck cover** | A flap or cloth covering the back of the neck. Critical for preventing heat stroke. |
| **Footwear** | Closed shoes. The ground is hot enough to cause burns through thin soles. Do not go barefoot. |
| **Sunglasses** | Protect eyes from UV and glare. Improvise: cut slits in fabric. |

### Wet Clothing Trick

Wetting your shirt and hat adds evaporative cooling. In dry heat (low humidity), this can drop your skin temperature significantly. Re-wet every 30–60 minutes as it dries. **Only do this if you have surplus water** — evaporation uses water you may need for drinking.

## Vehicle Interior Safety

- **Never sit in a parked car with the engine off in summer.** Interior temperatures reach 60°C–80°C within 15 minutes.
- Crack windows 1–2 cm to let hot air escape — but not enough for flies to swarm.
- Open both doors wide for 30 seconds before getting in.
- Do not leave water bottles in the vehicle — heat degrades plastic and leaches chemicals.
- Do not store lighters or aerosol cans in the vehicle — they can explode.

---

## Vehicle-Specific Heat Management

### Aircon vs No Aircon

- If aircon is working, use it — it is not a luxury, it is a survival tool for preventing heat stress on long drives
- If aircon is broken, roll windows fully down, place a wet cloth on your neck (evaporative cooling on the carotid artery), and increase water intake
- On long drives in extreme heat, rotate who drives every 60–90 minutes to reduce fatigue and heat exposure

### Engine Heat

- The engine bay and bonnet radiate 70°C–80°C of heat for hours after stopping — do not sit on the bonnet or near the radiator after a long drive
- When setting up camp, orient the vehicle so the engine bay faces away from where you will rest
- Let the engine cool for at least 30 minutes before checking fluids or performing any mechanical work

### Vehicle Colour and Heat

- White vehicles reflect up to 80% of solar radiation — interior stays measurably cooler
- Dark vehicles absorb more heat: interior can be 10°C–15°C hotter than a white vehicle in the same conditions
- If you have a dark-coloured vehicle, extra shade and ventilation are critical

### Creating Vehicle Shade

- Park **facing the sun** so the vehicle body (bonnet and windscreen) blocks the direct solar load
- Run a tarp from the roof rack to the ground on the **southern side** of the vehicle
- Weight the tarp with rocks, sandbags, or full water containers
- Leave 30–50 cm gap at the bottom for airflow under the tarp — stagnant air under a tarp becomes a furnace
- If no tarp is available, open all doors on the shaded side and drape blankets or clothing between door frames

## Ground Temperature Awareness

| Surface | Typical Temperature (45°C air) |
|---------|-------------------------------|
| Light sand | 55°C–60°C |
| Dark sand / red soil | 60°C–70°C |
| Bitumen / asphalt | 60°C–75°C |
| Rock (dark) | 65°C+ |
| Vegetated ground | 35°C–45°C |
| Under vehicle shade | 35°C–45°C |
| 30 cm below surface (any) | 25°C–30°C |

Burns from brief contact with 60°C+ surfaces occur in under 5 seconds. **Do not sit, lie, or kneel directly on bare sand or rock without insulation** — use a floor mat, seat cushion, or folded clothing.

## Heat Survival Timeline

The table below shows how quickly conditions progress in extreme heat (45°C air temperature) depending on what resources you have available.

| Time at 45°C | Without Shade | With Shade + Water | With Shade + Water + Wet Cloth |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 hour | Heat exhaustion begins | OK | OK |
| 3 hours | Heat stroke possible | Heat exhaustion risk | OK |
| 6 hours | Fatal risk | Serious risk | Managing |
| 12 hours | Likely fatal | Heat stroke risk | Heat exhaustion |
| 24 hours | Fatal | Critical risk | Serious |
| 48 hours | — | Probable fatal | Critical without electrolyte |

**Key takeaway:** Shade buys you time. Shade plus water doubles it. Shade plus water plus evaporative cooling (wet cloth) gives you the best chance. But none of these are a substitute for rescue.

## Night Time

The Australian desert has some of the most extreme temperature swings on Earth. A 40°C day can drop to 5°C or below by dawn, particularly in winter and in elevated inland areas.

### Cold Exposure Risk

- **Hypothermia is a real risk** in the outback, especially if you are already dehydrated and exhausted
- A person in wet or sweaty clothing at 5°C with wind can develop hypothermia within hours
- You must carry a warm layer (jumper, hoodie, jacket, or thermal) even in summer
- A sleeping bag (at least a summer-weight bag) should be in your vehicle kit year-round

### Sleeping Off the Ground

- Desert ground conducts heat away from your body rapidly at night, accelerating cold stress
- Sleep on a swag, stretcher, camp bed, sleeping mat, or an improvised bed of branches and grass
- Do not sleep directly on the bare ground — you will lose body heat and wake cold or dangerously chilled

### Wind Protection

- Use the vehicle as a windbreak: sleep on the lee side
- If wind is persistent, rig a tarp as a windbreak wall
- A tent or swag with a fly provides critical wind protection in open desert

### Night as a Resource

- Night is the best time to travel if you are walking out — cooler temperatures mean less water loss
- Night is also the best time to signal: fires, torches, and reflective signals are visible from kilometres away in the dark
- Use daylight hours for rest and planning; use darkness for movement and signalling
