# Signal Fire

Fire is your most versatile signalling tool. It works day and night, is visible for kilometres, and requires only materials from your environment.

---

## The Three-Fire Triangle

```
    Three-Fire Triangle (Overhead)

         /\  fire
        /  \
       /    \
      /      \
     /        \
    /   clear  \
   /    area    \
  /              \
 /\      /\      /\
/__\____/__\____/__\
fire              fire
```

Three fires arranged in a triangle (each 15–30 m apart) is the international distress signal. It is unmistakably human-made. A single fire could be a camper. Three fires in formation = someone needs help.

If you cannot sustain three fires, even one large, smoky fire is better than nothing.

---

## Day Signalling — Smoke

Smoke is visible for 5–15 km in clear conditions and is the best daytime signal.

### Black Smoke (Best for Light Backgrounds — Snow, Sand, Pale Sky)

{% steps() %}
Build a hot, established fire; Add materials that produce thick black smoke: rubber (tyre pieces if scavenged), oil-soaked rags, plastic, green eucalyptus leaves; Eucalyptus leaves are one of the best natural black smoke producers (the oils burn dark); Damp (not wet) green vegetation also works
{% end %}

### White Smoke (Best for Dark Backgrounds — Forest, Night)

{% steps() %}
Add green vegetation, damp leaves, or moss to a hot fire; Steam + incomplete combustion = thick white smoke; Do not completely smother the fire — you need heat under the green material
{% end %}

### Smoke Column Maintenance

{% steps() %}
Keep a ready pile of smoke-producing material near each fire; When you hear or see aircraft: dump the material on the fire immediately; You have 30–60 seconds to produce smoke before an aircraft passes out of sight; After the aircraft passes, let the fire burn back to flames for heat/fuel efficiency; Replenish the smoke pile for next time
{% end %}

---

## Night Signalling — Flame

Flame is visible for 10–20 km at night. A single fire on a ridge top can be seen from tremendous distances.

### Building a Signal Fire for Night

{% steps() %}
Build a raised fire platform (logs or rocks) to elevate flames above ground-level obstructions; Use light, fast-burning fuel for tall flames — dry kindling, dried palm fronds, dry grass; Have a stockpile ready to feed the fire rapidly; When you see or hear an aircraft: feed the fire aggressively for maximum flame height; Do not use smoke-producing material at night — maximise flame visibility
{% end %}

---

## Torch Tree

A torch tree is a standing dead tree (or large bush) that you prepare to ignite as an instant signal:

{% steps() %}
Select an isolated dead tree or large bush in a visible location; Pile dry kindling and tinder at the base; When you need the signal, ignite the base; The tree will burn as a massive torch visible for 30+ km at night; Have an extinguishing plan — shovel, sand, water ready
{% end %}

---

## Maintaining Signal Readiness

A signal fire that takes 10 minutes to produce smoke is useless — aircraft pass in seconds.

**Signal fire readiness checklist:**
- Three fire lays built and ready (if you can sustain three)
- Dry kindling and tinder at each, protected from weather
- Smoke-producing material piled beside each fire (daytime)
- Fast-burning fuel piled beside each fire (nighttime)
- Ignition source at each fire (not one lighter shared between three)
- Fire maintained (banked or small flame) vs. built from scratch each time

## 🇦🇺 Australian Signal Fire Context

### Green Gum Leaves for White Smoke

In the Australian outback, **green gum (eucalyptus) leaves** are the single best natural material for producing thick, visible **white smoke**. The eucalyptus oils burn with a dense white column that contrasts perfectly against red outback dirt — making it visible from aircraft at altitude.

- **How:** Build a hot fire base. Pile fresh green gum leaves on top. The leaves smoulder and produce a thick white smoke column.
- **Why it works in AU:** Most Australian ground is red/brown — white smoke is the highest-contrast daytime signal.
- **Availability:** Eucalyptus grows everywhere across Australia. Even in the arid centre, river red gums line dry creek beds.

### The Three-Fire Triangle — International Distress

**3 fires in a triangle** is the recognised international distress signal. In the outback:

- Space fires 15–30 m apart in a triangle
- Use natural fuel (dead branches, spinifex, mulga) — no additional supplies needed
- For maximum aerial visibility: burn green vegetation on each fire for smoke columns
- If you cannot sustain three fires, **one large smoky fire** is still far better than nothing

### Fire Bans — Know Before You Light

**Check for fire bans before lighting any signal fire.** Total fire bans (TFB) or days of catastrophic fire danger are common in the Australian summer.

| Fire danger rating | Can you light a signal fire? |
|-------------------|------------------------------|
| Low / Moderate | Yes — with firebreak and attended at all times |
| High | Yes — extreme caution, full firebreak |
| Very High | Only in an absolute life-or-death emergency |
| Severe / Extreme / Catastrophic | **No — do not light any fire.** Use other signals instead. |

**Alternatives during fire bans:** signal mirror, ground signals, electronic/PLB, audio signals. A wildfire started by your signal fire will kill you faster than dehydration.

The Australian **Fire Danger Rating** is issued daily by the Bureau of Meteorology. Check at any roadhouse, visitor centre, or on the BOM website before your trip.

### Black Smoke from Tyres — Last Resort Only

Burning a vehicle tyre produces a massive column of **black smoke** visible for 30+ km in clear conditions. However:

- **Massive fire risk** — tyre fires burn extremely hot and are difficult to extinguish
- **Environmental impact** — toxic fumes (benzene, sulphur compounds, heavy metals)
- **Only use in a genuine life-or-death emergency** when you have sighted an aircraft
- **Single use** — once lit, a tyre fire cannot be extinguished with available resources
- **Alternatives:** Thick green gum leaves produce a large smoke column without the risks of tyre fires
