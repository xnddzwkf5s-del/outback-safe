# Audio Signals — Whistle, Shout & Pattern

Sound-based signaling works when visual signals cannot — in dense forest, at night, in fog, or when you are injured and cannot move to a visible location. A whistle carries further than a human voice and uses far less energy.

---

## The Universal Distress Pattern

**Three blasts / three shots / three shouts = distress.**

- Three whistle blasts, pause, repeat
- Three gunshots, pause, repeat
- Three shouts, pause, repeat

**Response from searchers:** Two blasts/shots = "I hear you, stay where you are, I am coming."

**Never use three of anything unless you need help.** False alarms tie up search resources and — in some jurisdictions — are criminal offences.

---

## Whistle Signaling

A whistle should be part of every survival kit. It requires minimal effort, carries well, and cuts through wind and rain better than a voice.

### Whistle Patterns

| Pattern | Meaning |
|---------|---------|
| **3 blasts** | Distress — I need help |
| **2 blasts** | Acknowledged / I am here |
| **1 blast** | Where are you? / I am here (less standard) |
| **Continuous blasts** | Emergency — immediate assistance |

### Whistle Range

| Terrain | Audible range (decent whistle) |
|---------|-------------------------------|
| Open, still air | 1–2 km |
| Open, windy | 500 m – 1 km |
| Dense forest | 200–500 m |
| Mountains with echo | Variable — echoes can confuse direction |
| Over water | 500 m – 1.5 km |

**Blow at regular intervals.** Searchers work in grids. A blast every 60 seconds gives them continuous bearing information.

---

## Voice Signaling

Shouting is the least efficient signaling method — it expends energy, dehydrates, damages vocal cords with prolonged use, and carries the shortest distance. But it costs nothing and is available instantly.

**Shout "HELP" not just noise.** Searchers are listening for words, not generic sounds.

**Voice conservation:**
- Do not shout continuously — you will lose your voice within hours
- Blast pattern: 3 shouts, rest 1 minute, repeat
- At night, sound carries further (cooler, denser air)
- Face the direction you think searchers are — sound is directional

---

## Improvised Noisemakers

| Improvised tool | How to make | Range |
|----------------|-------------|-------|
| **Two rocks struck together** | Find hard, resonant rocks (not crumbly) | 200–500 m |
| **Stick against hollow log** | Drum on a hollow tree trunk or log | 500 m – 1 km |
| **Gunshots** | Standard distress: 3 shots, pause, repeat | 2–5 km |
| **Car horn** | If vehicle battery still works — 3 honks | 1–2 km |
| **Metal on metal** | Strike a pot, pan, vehicle body, or metal sheet | 500 m – 1.5 km |
| **Bamboo clapper** | Split bamboo, strike together | 200–500 m |

---

## Gunshot Signaling

If you have a firearm:

- **3 shots in succession, spaced 2–3 seconds apart, repeated every 15–30 minutes**
- **Do not fire randomly** — searchers listen for patterns. Random shots could be hunting.
- **Conserve ammunition** — you may need it for defence
- **Aim into a safe backstop** — into soft ground or thick tree trunk. Bullets travel kilometres.

---

## Sound Discipline

In some situations, you may NOT want to signal:

| Situation | Action |
|-----------|--------|
| Hostile humans in the area | Stay silent. Sound reveals your position. |
| Predator nearby | Make noise to scare it off (predators generally avoid loud humans), then be quiet. |
| Avalanche terrain | Do not make loud noises — sound can trigger avalanches. |
| Searchers are very close | Escalate: whistle → voice → physical signals |

---

## Night Signaling with Sound

At night, sound is your best signal (unless you have fire/flame). The cooler, denser air at night carries sound further, and searchers often conduct night listening stops — periods of silence to listen for survivors.

**During search listening stops:** Blow the whistle at regular intervals. Do not shout continuously — it is harder to locate a continuous sound than an intermittent one.

## 🇦🇺 Australian Audio Signal Context

### Vehicle Horn — 3 Blasts = Distress

Your vehicle horn is the most powerful audio signal available to outback travellers:

- **Three honks** (long, 2–3 seconds each), pause, repeat = international distress
- **Range:** 1–3 km in open outback (sound carries further over flat terrain)
- **Battery preservation:** Only use the horn when you can see or hear a potential rescuer. DO NOT honk endlessly — you will drain the battery.
- **Use at dawn/dusk:** Sound carries furthest in the cool, dense air of early morning and late evening.
- **If vehicle is broken down:** Protect the battery. Only honk when you see an aircraft or vehicle approaching. Reserve battery for UHF radio or satellite device.

### UHF Radio — Emergency Channel 5

UHF CB radio is the standard communication tool for Australian outback travellers:

| Channel | Purpose |
|---------|---------|
| **Channel 5** | Emergency — monitored by volunteers and travellers |
| **Channel 40** | Highway channel — passing vehicles, general communication |
| **Channel 35** | Emergency repeater input (where repeaters are available) |

**Emergency call format:**

> "BREAK BREAK BREAK — This is [your name/vehicle] on channel 5 — [Your location — road name, distance from nearest town, GPS if known] — [Nature of emergency — medical, mechanical, lost] — [Number of people] — Over"

**Distress call:**

> "MAYDAY MAYDAY MAYDAY — [your name/vehicle] — [location] — require immediate assistance — [emergency details] — Over"

**Response format for other travellers:**

> "[Caller name], this is [responder name] on channel 5. I acknowledge your emergency. I am [location]. Am relaying to [next person/town]. Standing by on channel 5."

### Whistle Blasts — SOS Pattern

A whistle is standard gear for remote Australian travel. The universal 3-3-3 pattern is recognised by all Australian SAR services:

| Pattern | Meaning |
|---------|---------|
| **3 short, 3 long, 3 short** (SOS) | Distress — I need help |
| **2 blasts** | Acknowledged / I hear you |
| **1 blast** | Where are you? / I am here |
| **Continuous blowing** | Immediate emergency |

**Performance in AU terrain:**

| Terrain | Whistle range |
|---------|--------------|
| Open outback (flat, no vegetation) | 1.5–3 km |
| Open outback (light wind) | 1–2 km |
| Scrub / mulga country | 500 m – 1 km |
| Dense bush (coastal ranges) | 200–500 m |
| Rocky terrain / gorges | Variable — echoes can confuse direction |

**Always carry a whistle on your person** (not in your pack). It costs $5 and weighs nothing. In a situation where you are injured and cannot shout, a whistle can save your life.

### Gunshot Signals — Australian Context

**3 shots spaced 5 seconds apart** is the recognised distress signal in Australian survival tradition.

**However — Australian firearm laws are strict.** Most outback travellers do not carry firearms:

- Handguns are effectively impossible for civilians to own
- Rifles and shotguns require a license (Category A/B), a genuine reason (hunting/sport), and safe storage
- Carrying a firearm in national parks is prohibited in most states
- Many remote travellers (backpackers, 4WD tourers) do not carry firearms at all

**If you do not have a firearm, do not worry.** Most Australian travellers do not. Your other signaling options (mirror, whistle, PLB, UHF radio, satellite device) are equally effective.

**If you DO have a firearm** (e.g., you are on a hunting trip):

- 3 shots, pause, 3 shots, pause — NOT consecutive
- Do not fire randomly — searchers listen for patterns
- Conserve ammunition
- Aim into a safe backstop — never fire into the air (bullets come down kilometres away)

**Important:** False distress calls using firearms are a criminal offence in all Australian states. Never use the distress signal unless you genuinely need help.
