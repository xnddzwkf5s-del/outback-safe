# Emergency Rationing

In a survival situation, food is finite. Whether you have a backpack of supplies, scavenged items, or foraged resources, rationing extends your survival window. But rationing incorrectly — eating too little when you should eat more, or vice versa — can accelerate your decline.

---

## How Little Can You Survive On?

### Minimum Caloric Requirements

| State | Daily calories needed | What happens below this |
|-------|----------------------|------------------------|
| **Complete rest, warm conditions** | 1,200–1,500 | Body burns fat and muscle. Sustainable for 2–4 weeks with water only. |
| **Light activity (foraging, cooking)** | 1,800–2,200 | Below this, activity performance degrades. You slow down, make mistakes. |
| **Moderate work (shelter building)** | 2,500–3,000 | Calorie deficit leads to rapid muscle loss and fatigue. |
| **Heavy work (hiking, construction)** | 3,500–5,000 | Unsustainable from wild food alone in first week. |

**The hard truth:** In most survival scenarios, you will operate at a significant calorie deficit. Your body can handle this — human metabolism evolved for feast-and-famine cycles. The danger is not the calorie deficit itself but the micronutrient deficiencies that accompany it and the impaired judgement that hunger causes.

---

## The Starvation Timeline

```
Week 1: Hunger, irritability, weakness. Body burns glycogen, then fat.
Week 2: Ketosis. Hunger decreases. Mental clarity may improve ("fasting high").
Week 3: Significant weight loss (5–10 kg). Muscle wasting begins.
Week 4: Severe weakness. Immune system compromised. Injuries do not heal.
Week 5–8: Organ damage begins. Heart muscle weakens. Death risk increases.
Week 8+: Death from organ failure or infection becomes likely.
```

**These timelines assume adequate water intake.** Without water, death from dehydration comes in days 3–7, well before starvation.

**Refeeding syndrome risk:** If someone has been starving for more than 5 days, reintroducing food too quickly can cause fatal electrolyte shifts (refeeding syndrome). Start with small amounts of easy-to-digest food (broth, soft vegetables, insects). Gradually increase over 3–5 days.

## Rationing Strategy — 3-Tier System

### Tier 1: Minimum Survival Ration (Days 1–7)

- **Calories:** 500–800 per day
- **What this looks like:** One handful of insects (100 g), two handfuls of edible greens, and 1–2 starchy roots or nuts per day
- **Who it is for:** Everyone, until water and shelter are secured and food sources are identified
- **Physiological note:** You will feel hungry. You will lose weight. You will be weak. But you will survive.

### Tier 2: Sustaining Ration (Week 2+)

- **Calories:** 1,200–1,800 per day
- **What this looks like:** Expanded foraging/gathering, fish catches, larger insect harvests
- **Who it is for:** Once food gathering systems are functional
- **Goal:** Stabilise weight loss and maintain basic activity capacity

### Tier 3: Work Ration (When Available)

- **Calories:** 2,000+
- **What this looks like:** Surplus food — eat to satiety
- **Who it is for:** People doing heavy work (construction, long-range exploration)
- **Principle:** The people burning the most calories need the most food. Fairness is not equality — it is proportional allocation.

## Nutrient Priorities in Rationing

When food is extremely limited, prioritise these nutrients:

| Priority | Nutrient | Why | Best sources |
|----------|----------|-----|-------------|
| **Critical** | Protein | Prevents muscle wasting; immune function | Insects, fish, nuts |
| **Critical** | Sodium + Potassium | Electrolyte balance; prevents cramping and cardiac issues | Salt (if available), blood (from game), bone broth, leafy greens |
| **High** | Vitamin C | Prevents scurvy (2–3 month onset) | Lemon myrtle tea, green tree ants, fresh greens, rosella |
| **High** | Iron | Prevents anaemia; oxygen transport | Insects, blood, dark leafy greens (nettle, dandelion) |
| **High** | B12 | Nerve function; only in animal sources | Insects, fish, liver, shellfish |
| **Moderate** | Calcium | Bone health; muscle function | Bone broth, small fish bones, some greens |
| **Moderate** | Vitamin A | Vision; immune function | Liver, dark greens, orange fruits |

**People doing heavy physical work need more sodium.** If someone develops muscle cramps, headache, or fatigue disproportionate to the work, they need salt. A pinch of salt (if available) in water can resolve this quickly.

## Group Rationing — Fairness vs. Equality

Group food distribution is one of the most common sources of conflict. A clear, transparent system established BEFORE the crisis is essential.

### Rationing Principles

{% steps() %}
**Assess total available food** — inventory everything. Calculate total days of food at minimum ration.; **Assess individual needs** — children need fewer calories but more protein per kg. Pregnant/lactating women need extra. Heavy workers need extra.; **Establish a rationing standard** — e.g., 1,200 cal/day/person base + 500 cal/day for heavy workers; **Distribute at set times** — twice daily. Predictability reduces anxiety.; **Distribute transparently** — everyone sees what everyone else gets. No secret stashes.; **One person controls distribution** — the leader or a designated quartermaster. This prevents hoarding and conflict.; **No private food stashes** — communal food allocation. Private stashes breed distrust and hoarding.
{% end %}

### Who Gets Extra?

| Person | Additional allocation | Reason |
|--------|----------------------|--------|
| Heavy workers (builders, wood gatherers) | +30–50% | They are burning the calories for the group's benefit |
| Pregnant or breastfeeding women | +500 cal/day | Producing milk requires enormous energy; foetal development |
| Growing children (4–14) | +10–20% (per kg, protein-heavy) | Growth cannot be deferred. Protein essential. |
| Injured/sick recovering | +10–20% protein | Healing requires amino acids. The immune system is protein-hungry. |

### Who Gets Less (Temporarily)?

| Person | Reduced allocation | Reason |
|--------|-------------------|--------|
| Sedentary adults not contributing | Minimum ration | Fairness is proportional to contribution |
| Healthy adults with higher body fat | Can survive on minimum ration longer | Fat reserves = stored calories |

{% info() %}
**Children under 4**: Young children have smaller stomachs and cannot eat large volumes. Their food must be nutrient-dense (insects, fish, nut pastes). Do not fill them with low-calorie greens — they need concentrated calories.
{% end %}

## Water Rationing — A Special Note

Water should **never** be rationed below the minimum daily requirement (2 L per person in cool conditions, 3–4 L in hot). Unlike food, water deficit causes rapid, irreversible decline.

**Water rationing rules:**
- **Sip, do not gulp.** Small amounts frequently retain more hydration than large amounts occasionally.
- **Distribute at regular intervals** — every 2 hours, a measured amount.
- **The leader drinks last** — this ensures fair distribution and prevents "I'm thirstier than you" disputes.
- **Do not withhold water from anyone showing signs of heat stress or dehydration** — the damage is being done. Give water.

## Managing Hunger — Psychological Techniques

Hunger is partly physiological, partly psychological. The psychological component can be managed:

- **Routine** — eat at the same times each day. Your body adjusts to a schedule (ghrelin, the hunger hormone, entrains to meal timing)
- **Warm fluids** — hot water (no tea, just hot water) creates the sensation of having consumed something. The warmth triggers satiety signals
- **Chewing** — chewing on something (a twig, a piece of leather, gum if you have it) triggers digestive preparation and reduces hunger sensation temporarily
- **Distraction** — busy hands and minds think less about food. Assign tasks
- **Sleep** — you cannot eat while sleeping, and sleep conserves energy. 8+ hours if possible
- **Small, frequent portions** — 50 calories every 2 hours feels more satisfying than 200 calories twice a day (even if the total is the same)

## When NOT to Ration

There are circumstances where eating MORE now is the right decision:

| Situation | Action |
|-----------|--------|
| You have found a large, perishable food source | Eat what you can, preserve the rest |
| Someone is doing critical heavy work (shelter, rescue) | Feed them NOW — the work saves the group |
| A child is crying from hunger | Feed the child. Children's nutritional needs are not deferrable. |
| Food is spoiling and cannot be preserved | Eat it. Spoiled food is wasted food. |
| You are about to travel a long distance | Eat a larger meal before departure. Energy for movement. |
| Morale is collapsing | A shared meal lifts group spirit more than any speech. |

## Preserving Surplus Food

If you have more food than you can eat immediately:

| Method | Best for | How |
|--------|----------|-----|
| **Drying (sun/wind)** | Insects, fish, lean meat, fruit | Slice thin. Hang in sun and wind. Cover with mesh to exclude flies. |
| **Smoking** | Fish, meat | Hang over cool, smoky fire (not flames). 6–12 hours. |
| **Burying in cool earth** | Roots, tubers | Dig hole 30+ cm deep. Line with leaves. Cover. Keeps for weeks in temperate climates. |
| **Salt preservation** | Fish, meat | If salt is available. Pack in salt. (Salt may be scarce in the wild.) |
| **Fat preservation (confit)** | Meat | Cook meat slowly in its own fat. Submerged in fat, it keeps for weeks. |

## Refeeding Syndrome — The Danger of Eating After Starvation

If someone has been in severe calorie deficit for more than 5 days, the sudden reintroduction of food can trigger a fatal electrolyte shift. The body has adapted to starvation; carbohydrates trigger an insulin surge that pulls phosphate, potassium, and magnesium from the blood into cells. This causes cardiac arrhythmia, respiratory failure, and death.

**Signs of refeeding syndrome:**
- Confusion, weakness, seizures (within 1–3 days of eating)
- Irregular heartbeat, fluid retention, respiratory difficulty

**Prevention:**

{% steps() %}
Start with 200–300 calories on Day 1 of refeeding — predominantly protein and fat (not carbohydrates); Day 2: 400–600 calories; Day 3: 600–900 calories; Day 4: 900–1,200 calories; Day 5+: gradual increase to full ration; If you have salt or electrolytes, provide them; Monitor for confusion, weakness, or irregular heartbeat
{% end %}
