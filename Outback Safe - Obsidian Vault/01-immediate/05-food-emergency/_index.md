# Food (Emergency) — Foraging & Rationing

**Food is your lowest priority in the first week.** You can survive 3 weeks without food. You cannot survive 3 days without water or 3 hours without shelter. Do not divert energy from water, shelter, or fire to find food until those basics are secured.

This section exists for when you are ready — typically Day 3 or later, once water, shelter, and fire are established.

---

## The Food Hierarchy — First Week

1. **Ration what you have** — stretch existing food supplies
2. **Scavenge processed food** — post-disaster buildings, vehicles, abandoned camps
3. **Insects** — safest wild protein; abundant everywhere
4. **Common known edibles** — only plants you can identify with 100% certainty
5. **Fishing** — passive (set lines), energy-efficient
6. **Universal edibility test** — for unknown plants (last resort)

**Do not hunt large game in Week 1.** It expends enormous calories, requires tools you may not have, and carries injury risk in a situation where any injury is dangerous.

---

## Calorie Reality Check

| Activity level | Daily calorie need | What that looks like in wild food |
|---------------|-------------------|----------------------------------|
| Resting survivor | 1,200–1,500 kcal | ~1.5 kg of insects, or ~3 kg of most wild greens |
| Active (building shelter, gathering) | 2,500–3,500 kcal | ~3 kg of insects, or ~6 kg of wild greens, or ~1 kg of fatty fish |
| Heavy work (construction, long travel) | 3,500–5,000 kcal | Difficult to sustain from wild food alone in Week 1 |

**You will be in calorie deficit.** Accept it. Prioritise protein and micronutrients over calories. Your body has fat reserves; it does not have unlimited reserves of potassium, magnesium, or vitamin C.

---

## Food Safety — The Golden Rules

1. **If you cannot identify it with 100% certainty, do not eat it.**
2. **Cook everything.** Heat kills parasites, bacteria, and some plant toxins.
3. **One new food at a time.** Wait 8 hours before trying another new food — if you have a reaction, you know the cause.
4. **Do not eat mushrooms unless you are a trained mycologist.** The difference between edible and deadly can be microscopic.
5. **Do not eat anything that smells of almonds/peach pits** (cyanide compounds), is milky-sapped (often toxic), or has three-leaved growth pattern (poison ivy relatives).

## What NOT to Eat — The Universal Avoid List

These rules apply everywhere. Do not eat:

- **Mushrooms** — unless you are a trained mycologist with local knowledge
- **Plants with milky or discoloured sap** — most are toxic (exceptions: dandelion, wild lettuce — but if you are not 100% sure, avoid)
- **Plants with three-leaved growth pattern** — poison ivy, poison oak relatives
- **Plants with umbrella-shaped flower clusters** — hemlock family (includes some of the deadliest plants on Earth)
- **Beans, bulbs, or seeds inside pods** — many contain toxic alkaloids
- **Plants with almond scent in leaves or stems** — indicates cyanogenic glycosides
- **Grain heads with purple/black spurs** (ergot) — fungal infection causes ergotism (convulsions, gangrene)
- **Anything growing in contaminated water** — absorbs heavy metals and pathogens
- **Brightly coloured insects** (red, orange, yellow combined with black) — aposematic colouration = "I am toxic"
- **Hairy caterpillars** — many have urticating hairs that cause severe reactions
- **Any animal acting strangely** (docile when it should be aggressive, uncoordinated, circling) — possible disease (rabies, chronic wasting disease)

## 🇦🇺 Australian Emergency Foods

> **Australia Note:** This page has been adapted for Australian conditions.

### Australian Insects — High-Protein Survival Food

| Insect | Where to find | How to eat | Nutrition |
|--------|---------------|------------|-----------|
| **Witchetty grubs** (*Endoxyla leucomochla*) | Roots of Acacia trees (especially mulga). Dig at the base of wattle/acacia trees. | Eat raw (nutty, buttery taste) or roasted in coals. | Extremely high protein (~15 g per 100 g). High fat — good survival food. |
| **Bardi grubs** (larger, *Trogus* spp.) | Roots of eucalypts and wattle. Look for trees with swollen roots. | Roast on hot coals. Larger than witchetty — more calories per grub. | High protein, high fat. |
| **Bogong moths** (*Agrotis infusa*) | Seasonal in alpine areas (Snowy Mountains, Brindabellas). They aestivate (summer-sleep) in rock crevices on high peaks. | Traditional Aboriginal food. Roast, grind into paste. | High fat. Traditional source of seasonal protein. |
| **Green ants** (*Oecophylla smaragdina*) | Tropical northern AU. Their nests are leaf structures woven together in trees. | Abdomen has a citrus-like flavour. Eat raw — just the green abdomen. | Vitamin C from the citrus-tasting abdomen. |
| **Honey ants** (*Camponotus inflatus*) | Desert AU (central). Nests are underground chambers with swollen "honeypot" ants. | The repletes (swollen ants) are filled with nectar. Pluck and eat raw. | Sugary nectar — quick energy. Highly prized traditional food. |
| **Grasshoppers** (various spp.) | Everywhere in AU | Remove legs and wings. Roast or fry. | High protein (~20 g per 100 g dry). |

**Insect gathering tips:** The best time for grubs is late dry season to early wet season (Oct–Dec). Look for trees with dried sap or sawdust at the base — signs of grub activity.

### Deadliest Australian Plant Look-Alikes — Quick Warning

| Edible plant | Deadly look-alike | How to distinguish |
|-------------|------------------|-------------------|
| **Rosella** (*Hibiscus sabdariffa* — edible flowers) | **Wild tobacco** (*Solanum mauritianum*) | Rosella has red fleshy calyces (not a typical fruit shape). Wild tobacco has small orange berries. |
| **Wild passionfruit** (*Passiflora foetida* — edible when ripe) | **Strychnine tree** fruit | Passionfruit has distinctive lattice-pattern shell when ripe. Strychnine fruit is orange-like in appearance. If the fruit looks like a small orange and the tree is not a citrus, DO NOT eat it. |
| **Native currant / muntries** | **White cedar** fruit (*Melia azedarach*) | White cedar berries look like small olives/yellow-brown dates. Muntries look like small green-red apples. Learn to tell them apart. |

### Australian Animal Hazards — Respect the Wildlife

| Animal | Danger | Where | What to do |
|--------|--------|-------|------------|
| **Cassowary** | Can kill with kicks — sharp claws on inner toe | Rainforest Qld | Give wide berth. Do not approach. If it charges, protect your abdomen and face. Run in zigzag — cassowaries are fast but turn slowly. |
| **Wild pigs** | Aggressive in groups, sharp tusks | All states, especially NSW, Qld, NT | Climb a tree. Pigs cannot climb. If caught, protect your groin and legs. |
| **Feral camels** | Unpredictable, can kick and bite | Simpson Desert, central Australia | Give wide berth. Do not approach. An adult camel can kill a person with a single kick. |
| **Crocodiles** | Ambush predator | Northern waterways | See Crocodile Safety section below. |
| **Snakes** | Venomous (brown snake, taipan, tiger, death adder) | Everywhere | See Animal Threats page. |
