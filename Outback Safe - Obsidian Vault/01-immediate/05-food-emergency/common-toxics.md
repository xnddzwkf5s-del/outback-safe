# Common Toxics — 10 Deadliest Australian Plants

> **🇦🇺 Australia Note:** This page has been rewritten for Australian conditions — covering the most dangerous plants found across Australia. Always cross-reference the plant you are considering with this list. If you cannot identify it with 100% certainty, do not eat it. Full stop.

---

## The Rule of Thumb for Australia

Some of the world's most toxic plants grow in Australian gardens and bushland. **Do not assume a plant is safe because it looks like a known edible or because birds are eating the fruit.** Many birds can eat fruit that would kill a human.

**If you cannot identify a plant with 100% certainty, do not eat it. Full stop.**

---

## Top 10 Deadly Australian Plants

### 1. Gympie-Gympie / Stinging Tree (*Dendrocnide moroides*)

- **Where:** Rainforest Qld, northern NSW. Common in disturbed areas, along tracks.
- **Toxin:** Moroidin — a neurotoxin peptide.
- **Lethal potential:** Rarely lethal to humans but can cause anaphylactic shock. The sting is described as **being burned with acid and electrocuted simultaneously**. Pain lasts months and recurs when the area is exposed to cold water. Can cause severe psychological trauma.
- **Look-alike:** None edible — but you need to know it to avoid touching it. The leaves are large, heart-shaped with serrated edges, covered in fine silica hairs.
- **What to do if stung:** Apply **dilute hydrochloric acid** (not available in the wild) or **hot wax depilation** to remove hairs. Do NOT rub with water — that makes it worse. Remove visible hairs with tweezers. Apply adhesive tape to remove fine hairs. Pain management is the main treatment.
- **Note:** Aboriginal people used Gympie-Gympie sting as a punishment (the pain is that bad). You will not eat it — but you might touch a leaf in the dark, and the consequences are severe.

### 2. Strychnine Tree (*Strychnos nux-vomica*)

- **Where:** Tropical Qld, NT, northern WA. Also common in .
- **Toxin:** Strychnine.
- **Lethal dose:** A few seeds.
- **Look-alike:** The fruit looks like a small orange — about the size of a golf ball, orange when ripe. It can be mistaken for a wild citrus.
- **Action:** Do NOT eat any fruit that looks like a small orange unless you are certain it is a citrus. The strychnine tree has orange fruit but is NOT a citrus.
- **Symptoms:** Violent muscle spasms, arching of the back (opisthotonos), respiratory failure. The person is conscious throughout. Death is agonising.

### 3. Castor Oil Plant (*Ricinus communis*)

- **Where:** Widespread weed throughout AU — wastelands, roadsides, disturbed areas, backyards.
- **Toxin:** Ricin — one of the most potent natural toxins known.
- **Lethal dose:** 4–8 seeds for an adult (if chewed). 1 seed can kill a child.
- **Look-alike:** The seeds are distinctive — mottled brown pattern like an engorged tick. No common edible look-alike, but children and uninformed adults may eat the seeds.
- **Key ID features:** Large (2–3 m), palmate (hand-shaped) leaves with 5–11 lobes. Flower spikes produce spiny seed pods containing 3 seeds each.
- **Symptoms:** Delayed 2–36 hours. Severe abdominal pain, vomiting, profuse diarrhoea (often bloody), multi-organ failure.
- **Note:** The outer seed coat must be broken. Swallowing whole seeds may pass through undigested. Do not risk it.

### 4. Deadly Nightshade (*Atropa belladonna*)

- **Where:** Naturalised in southern AU (especially VIC, TAS, SA) — waste areas, old gardens.
- **Toxin:** Tropane alkaloids (atropine, scopolamine, hyoscyamine).
- **Lethal dose:** 2–5 berries can kill a child. 10–20 berries can kill an adult.
- **Look-alike:** Shiny black berries that look superficially like edible berries (blackberries, native currants).
- **Key ID features:** Bushy shrub with green/purple bell-shaped flowers. Shiny black berries with a star-shaped calyx at the base.
- **Symptoms:** Blurred vision, dry mouth, dilated pupils, hot dry skin, confusion, hallucinations, seizures, coma, death. The person becomes delirious and hyperthermic.
- **Note:** *Atropa belladonna* is NOT the same as *Solanum nigrum* (black nightshade — which is sometimes edible when cooked). Do not confuse them. If in doubt, do not eat any black berry growing on a bush with bell-shaped flowers.

### 5. Angel's Trumpet (*Brugmansia* spp.)

- **Where:** Common garden ornamental throughout AU. Frequently naturalised in warm areas (QLD, NSW, WA, NT).
- **Toxin:** Tropane alkaloids (scopolamine, atropine, hyoscyamine).
- **Lethal dose:** Small amounts can be fatal.
- **Look-alike:** Large trumpet-shaped flowers hanging down. NOT a food plant — but occasionally people mistake the flowers or leaves for edible things. Some people deliberately ingest it for hallucinations (a common cause of poisoning).
- **Key ID features:** Large (10–30 cm) hanging trumpet flowers — white, yellow, pink, or orange.
- **Symptoms:** Delirium, visual hallucinations, hyperthermia, paralysis, respiratory failure, death. Victims are completely unresponsive to reality. Do not touch your eyes after handling.
- **Note:** All parts are toxic. Even handling the plant can be dangerous if you then touch your mouth or eyes.

### 6. Milkwort / Bitter Leaf (*Polygala* spp.)

- **Where:** Various *Polygala* species across AU — including native species and introduced weeds. Some species contain cyanogenic glycosides.
- **Toxin:** Cyanogenic glycosides (release cyanide when the plant is damaged).
- **Look-alike:** Some species have small, pea-like flowers and can be confused with edible legumes.
- **Note:** Not all Polygala species are dangerously toxic, but the presence of cyanogenic compounds in some means you should avoid the entire genus in a survival situation.

### 7. Green Cestrum (*Cestrum parqui*)

- **Where:** Widespread weed in NSW, Qld, VIC, SA — along waterways, disturbed areas, old gardens. Originally from South America.
- **Toxin:** Parquine and other unidentified toxins (neurotoxic and hepatotoxic).
- **Lethal dose:** A few leaves can kill a cow. Proportionally little for humans.
- **Look-alike:** The small dark purple-black berries can look like edible berries. The leaves have an unpleasant smell when crushed.
- **Key ID features:** Shrub (1–3 m) with greenish-yellow tubular flowers. Black berries (poisonous). Leaves smell bad when crushed.

### 8. Mother of Millions / Mother of Thousands (*Bryophyllum* spp.)

- **Where:** Widespread weed across AU — especially Qld, NSW, coastal areas. Common in gardens and naturalised on coastlines.
- **Toxin:** Bufadienolides (cardenolides — cardiac glycosides, similar to those in cane toads).
- **Lethal potential:** Highly toxic to livestock (especially cattle). Human poisonings are rare but serious.
- **Look-alike:** It is a succulent — the distinctive plantlets on leaf margins make it unmistakable. No common edible look-alike.
- **Symptoms:** Cardiac arrhythmias, weakness, vomiting. Can be fatal in large quantities.
- **Note:** The toxicity is well-known to Australian cattle farmers — cattle deaths are common. The plant is becoming more widespread.

### 9. Oleander (*Nerium oleander*)

- **Where:** Planted as ornamental throughout AU — common in suburban gardens, along roadsides, in parks. Naturalised in many areas.
- **Toxin:** Cardiac glycosides (oleandrin, neriin).
- **Lethal dose:** **ONE leaf can kill an adult.** The smoke from burning oleander is toxic.
- **Look-alike:** NOT an edible plant — but people have used oleander sticks as skewers or cooking sticks, which transfers the toxin to food.
- **Key ID features:** Evergreen shrub (2–6 m) with showy pink, white, or red flowers. Narrow, dark green leaves in whorls.
- **Symptoms:** Nausea, vomiting, abdominal pain, irregular heartbeat, cardiac arrest (within hours).
- **Rule:** Never use oleander wood for cooking fires, skewers, or stirring sticks. This is a common cause of oleander poisoning in Australia because the plant is everywhere.

### 10. Mushrooms — Death Cap (*Amanita phalloides*) and Yellow Stainer (*Agaricus xanthodermus*)

**🇦🇺 Death Cap (*Amanita phalloides*)**

- **Where:** Under exotic trees (oak, chestnut, pine) in the ACT, VIC, SA, TAS, southern NSW. Especially Canberra, Melbourne, Adelaide. Introduced with tree plantings.
- **Toxin:** Amatoxins (alpha-amanitin).
- **Lethal dose:** Half a cap can kill an adult.
- **Look-alike:** Edible field mushrooms (Agaricus) and edible puffballs. The death cap is responsible for 90%+ of mushroom poisoning deaths worldwide.
- **Key ID features:** White stalk with a "cup" (volva) at the base. Greenish-yellow cap. White gills. White spore print.
- **Symptoms:** **Delayed onset 6–24 hours:** severe vomiting, diarrhoea, abdominal cramps. Then a deceptive "recovery" period (2–3 days) where the person feels better — while their liver is being destroyed. Death from liver failure 5–10 days later.
- **Canberra/Melbourne/VIC specific:** These areas have the highest concentration of death caps in Australia. If you are in these areas, do NOT eat any wild mushroom with white gills.

**🇦🇺 Yellow Stainer (*Agaricus xanthodermus*)**

- **Where:** Throughout AU — common in urban lawns, parks, gardens. One of the most common suburban mushrooms.
- **Toxin:** Phenol compounds.
- **Look-alike:** Edible field mushrooms (Agaricus campestris, Agaricus arvensis). They look very similar.
- **Key ID features:** The base of the stem stains **bright yellow** when cut or bruised (field mushroom stains pinkish). It also smells of **phenol (ink)** — a strong chemical smell that becomes more noticeable when cooking.
- **Symptoms:** Severe vomiting, diarrhoea, abdominal pain (usually non-fatal but dangerous because of dehydration in a survival situation).

**The Mushroom Rule: Do not eat any wild mushroom unless you are a trained mycologist.** This is not negotiable. The difference between edible and deadly can be microscopic.

## Universal Edibility Test — Essential for Australia

If you have exhausted all other options and must try an unknown plant, the Universal Edibility Test is your last resort. **This is NOT a substitute for positive identification.**

{% info %}
**The Universal Edibility Test is a step-by-step protocol for testing an unknown plant for toxicity.** It takes approximately 24 hours and involves testing the plant on your skin, lips, tongue, and finally eating a small amount. If any step produces a negative reaction (burning, numbness, nausea), stop and discard the plant.

→ Full protocol: [Universal Edibility Test](01-immediate/05-food-emergency/universal-edibility-test)

**Australia-specific note:** The test is particularly important in Australia because many Australian plants contain toxins (cyanogenic glycosides, fluoroacetate, solanine) that have no immediate taste warning. The test's 24-hour timeline accounts for delayed-onset toxins.

## Australian Plants with Cyanogenic Glycosides (Cyanide-Releasing)

Be aware of these groups when foraging:

| Plant group | Cyanide compounds? | Notes |
|-------------|-------------------|-------|
| **Acacia seeds** (wattle) | Yes — some species. Also fluoroacetate (1080 poison) in *A. georginae* (Georgina gidgee) | Always roast before eating. **Do NOT eat seeds from unknown wattle species** — *A. georginae* contains 1080 poison and has killed people and livestock. Positive species identification is required before consuming any wattle seed. |
| **Bamboo shoots** | Yes — all species | Must be boiled in multiple changes of water |
| **Cassava** | Yes | Not wild in AU, but be aware |
| **Bracken fern** (*Pteridium esculentum*) | Yes | Contains carcinogens AND thiaminase. Do not eat in quantity. |
| **Almond-scented leaves** (cyanide warning sign) | Likely | If leaves or stems smell like almonds, they likely contain cyanogenic compounds. Do not eat. |

**The almond scent test:** Crush a leaf or stem. If it smells like almonds (marzipan), it contains cyanogenic glycosides. Do not eat it.
