# Severe Bleeding — Haemorrhage Control

Uncontrolled bleeding kills within minutes. It is the number one cause of preventable death in trauma. If you learn nothing else from this wiki, learn this page.

---

## Recognising Life-Threatening Bleeding

| Type | Appearance | Severity |
|------|-----------|----------|
| **Arterial** | Bright red, spurting with heartbeat | Critical — can exsanguinate in under 2 minutes |
| **Venous** | Dark red, steady flow | Serious — slower but can still be fatal |
| **Capillary** | Oozing, red | Minor — clots on its own |

If blood is pooling on the ground, spurting, or soaking through clothing in seconds, treat it as life-threatening. Do not wait.

---

## Treatment Ladder

Work through these in order. If one step fails to control bleeding, escalate to the next.

### Step 1: Direct Pressure

{% steps() %}
Expose the wound — cut or tear clothing away; do not pull fabric out of a wound; Apply firm, continuous pressure directly on the wound with a clean cloth, gauze, or your hand; Hold pressure for at least 3 minutes without lifting to check — every time you release, you break the forming clot; If blood soaks through, add more material on top; do not remove the soaked layer; Maintain pressure until bleeding stops or you need to escalate to the next step
{% end %}

**Improvised pressure dressings:** T-shirt, bandana, sock, towel. Anything clean. If nothing is clean, use whatever you have — infection is survivable; exsanguination is not.

### Step 2: Wound Packing (Junctional Wounds)

For deep wounds in the groin, armpit, or neck where a tourniquet cannot be applied:

{% steps() %}
Pack gauze or clean cloth deep into the wound cavity — not on top, inside; Use your fingers to push material as deep as possible into the source of bleeding; Continue packing until the cavity is full and tight; Hold direct pressure on top of the packed wound for 3 minutes; If bleeding continues, remove packing and re-pack more tightly
{% end %}

**Junctional wounds** are particularly dangerous because you cannot tourniquet them. Pack aggressively.

### Step 3: Tourniquet (Limbs Only)

**When to use:** Bleeding from an arm or leg that direct pressure cannot control. Also: amputation, or any wound where blood is spurting uncontrollably.

#### Commercial Tourniquet (CAT or SOFT-T)

{% steps() %}
Place the tourniquet 5–8 cm above the wound (or as high on the limb as possible if the wound site is unclear); Pull the strap as tight as possible before turning the windlass; Turn the windlass rod until bleeding stops completely; Secure the windlass in the clip; Write the time of application on the tourniquet or on the patient's forehead; Do not loosen or remove — this is now a medical decision
{% end %}

#### Improvised Tourniquet

If you do not have a commercial tourniquet:

{% materials() %}
A strip of strong fabric at least 5 cm wide and 60 cm long (belt, shirt strip, backpack strap); A rigid windlass (stick, pen, screwdriver, spoon handle — anything strong that will not snap)
{% end %}

{% steps() %}
Wrap the fabric strip around the limb, 5–8 cm above the wound; Tie a half-knot; Place the rigid object on the knot; Tie a full square knot over it; Twist the windlass until bleeding stops; Secure the windlass in place (tie another strip around the limb and windlass to lock it); Note the time
{% end %}

### Step 4: Haemostatic Agents

If your kit contains haemostatic gauze (QuikClot, Celox):

- Pack the wound with haemostatic gauze instead of plain gauze
- The kaolin or chitosan coating accelerates clotting
- Hold pressure for 3 minutes as normal

## After Bleeding is Controlled

1. **Keep the patient warm** — blood loss causes hypothermia. Cover with blankets, emergency blanket, or clothing.
2. **Do not give food or drink** if the wound is severe — surgery may be needed.
3. **Monitor for shock** — pale, cold, clammy skin, rapid weak pulse, confusion. → [Shock Management](01-immediate/01-first-aid/shock)
4. **Elevate legs** (if no spinal injury) to help blood flow to vital organs.
5. **Reassess every 5 minutes** — bleeding can restart.

## Special Cases

### Scalp Wounds

Scalp wounds bleed profusely but are rarely life-threatening. Direct pressure is effective. If the skull is visible or depressed, do not apply pressure — cover loosely and seek advanced care.

### Neck Wounds

Do not apply circumferential pressure (choking risk). Use direct pressure with a gloved hand directly over the bleeding site. Pack if possible. **Cover the wound with an occlusive dressing** (plastic, credit card, anything that seals) if you suspect a vein has been opened — air can enter the bloodstream (air embolism), which is rapidly fatal.

### Abdominal Evisceration

If organs are visible:
- Do not push them back in
- Cover with moist, clean cloth (sterile saline if available, clean water if not)
- Cover moist layer with plastic to prevent drying
- Do not give anything by mouth

### Impaled Objects

**Do not remove the object.** It may be plugging the wound. Stabilise the object with bulky dressings on either side and bandage around it to hold in place.

---

## Common Mistakes

- **Peeking** — Lifting the dressing to "check" breaks the clot. Don't peek.
- **Too low** — Tourniquet placed just above the wound instead of high on the limb. Go high and tight.
- **Not tight enough** — If there's still a distal pulse, the tourniquet is too loose. Tighten until bleeding stops.
- **Removing soaked dressings** — Add more on top. Don't remove the ones already there.
- **Delaying the tourniquet** — If direct pressure isn't working after 30 seconds, escalate. Do not wait.
