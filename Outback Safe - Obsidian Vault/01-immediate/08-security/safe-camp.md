# Safe Camp — Layout, Fire & Escape Routes

A well-laid-out camp is safer, more efficient, and easier to defend. The layout determines sight lines, fire safety, escape routes, and how effectively the group can respond to threats.

---

## Zone 1: Sleeping Area

- **Furthest from approaches** — sleep at the "back" of the camp, protected by fire and open ground
- **Sleep head toward the fire** — you wake facing the fire and can see the camp
- **Sleeping positions staggered** — if a threat enters, they cannot attack a neat row of sleeping people
- **Each person has an assigned sleeping spot** — in an emergency, you know where everyone should be and who is missing
- **Weapons/tools within arm's reach** — a stout stick, knife, or tool beside each bed
- **Pack under head** — your pack serves as a pillow AND is immediately available if you need to flee

---

## Zone 2: Fire Area

```
    Fire Placement

    Reflector wall (behind fire toward sleeping area)
    |||||||||||||||
    |||||||||||||||
          /\
         /  \  fire
        /    \
       /      \
      / SLEEP  \
     /  AREA    \
    /____________\
```

- **Fire is central** — warmth radiates to sleeping area; light illuminates the camp
- **Reflector wall between fire and sleeping area** — directs MORE heat toward sleepers AND provides a visual screen (you are not silhouetted against flames)
- **Fire at least 3 m from any shelter** — sparks, embers, and heat
- **Fire placed so it does NOT illuminate sentries** — sentry positions are outside the firelight, looking in
- **Keep a fire-starting kit at the fire, not at your bed** — if you need fire urgently, you need it at the fire site

---

## Zone 3: Gear & Food Storage

- **Food stored 50+ m from sleeping area** — hung from a tree branch (3+ m high), or in a sealed container buried in cool earth
- **Gear organised and covered** — tarps or bark covers keep tools dry and prevent them being visible from a distance
- **Tools returned to the SAME place every time** — "where is the axe?" should never be a question in an emergency
- **Water containers grouped but separated from food** — water needs its own storage area

---

## Zone 4: Work Area

- **Downwind of sleeping area** — smoke, food smells, and work noise go away from the sleeping area
- **Cooking done here** — cooking at the work fire, not the sleeping fire, keeps food smells away from your bed
- **Flat ground** — trip hazards in the dark are a common cause of survival injuries
- **Tool maintenance here** — sharpening, repairing, making

## Escape Routes

Every camp needs at least two escape routes in opposite directions.

```
    Escape Route Planning

              ESCAPE ROUTE A
                   ^
                   |
    =====================================
    |              CAMP                |
    |                                   |
    =====================================
                   |
                   v
              ESCAPE ROUTE B
```

Mark each escape route with something visible at night — a white rock, a strip of cloth, reflective material; Walk each escape route during daylight so everyone knows it; Each route should lead to a rally point — a pre-agreed meeting location 100–300 m from camp; The rally point should have cover (rocks, dense vegetation) and a secondary escape route; Practice: "Escape drill — Route A. Go." Everyone moves to the rally point. Time how long it takes.; Everyone knows the rally protocol: go to the rally point, wait 5 minutes for others, if no one arrives, proceed to the secondary rally point

## Latrine Area

- **At least 50 m from camp**
- **Downhill/downstream from water source and camp**
- **Downwind of camp**
- **Marked path** — no one should wander into the latrine area by accident in the dark
- **Hygiene** — ash from the fire sprinkled in the latrine hole reduces odour and flies
- **Cover waste** — each use: cover with soil. Dig a new hole when the old one is full.

---

## Fire Safety in Camp Layout

- **Clear a 3 m radius around the fire** — bare earth, no fuel
- **Fire is not under overhanging branches**
- **Fire is not on peat or duff soil** (organic soil that can smoulder underground for weeks and reignite)
- **Have an extinguishing method always ready** — bucket of water, sand, shovel
- **Fire is positioned so prevailing wind does not blow sparks into shelters or dry vegetation**
- **In extreme fire danger conditions (hot, dry, windy), consider NO FIRE** — cold camps are sometimes safer

---

## Campsite Discipline

| Rule | Why |
|------|-----|
| **No food in sleeping area** | Attracts animals and insects to where you sleep |
| **No rubbish left around camp** | Attracts animals; degrades hygiene; looks occupied |
| **Paths between zones kept clear** | Trip hazards cause injuries |
| **Wash hands after latrine, before food** | Prevents disease. Even without soap, water + ash scrubbing is effective. |
| **Quiet after designated hours** | Allows sleep. Noise discipline at night. |
| **Fire is attended at all times** | Unattended fires spread or die. |

**A camp is a work in progress.** It gets safer and more efficient every day. Do not try to perfect it on Day 1.

## 🇦🇺 Australian Outback Camp Safety Checklist

Before setting up camp in the Australian outback, run through this checklist:

### 1. Stay Away from Water (50 m+)

- **Crocodile risk** — in tropical northern Australia (north of Rockhampton, across the NT top end, and Broome to Kununurra), camp at least 50 m from any water source. Crocodiles hunt at night and can move surprisingly fast on land.
- **Flood risk** — outback creeks and rivers can flood without warning, even if it isn't raining where you are. A storm 100 km upstream can send a flash flood down a dry creek bed. Never camp in a dry creek bed or low-lying area near a creek.
- **Mosquito risk** — camping near water attracts mosquitoes. Ross River and Barmah Forest viruses are transmitted by mosquitoes in the tropics.

### 2. Stay Away from Gum Trees (Widow-Makers)

**Gum trees (eucalyptus) are notorious for dropping branches without warning.** This is called "sudden limb drop" and is common in hot, still weather:

- Do not camp directly under a gum tree
- Do not park your vehicle under a gum tree
- Do not pitch your tent under a gum tree
- **Look up before you set up** — check directly above your tent, dining area, and fire pit for dead branches

**The dead branch above your tent is called a "widow-maker"** by Australian campers — for good reason. A falling branch can kill or seriously injure a sleeping person.

### 3. Check Above for Dead Branches

| What to look for | Danger level |
|-----------------|-------------|
| Large dead branch overhead | Critical — move camp immediately |
| Dead branch with green leaves further out | Moderate — the branch may be dying. Consider moving. |
| Loose bark, peeling sections | Low — check for insects (termite damage) |
| Healthy, full canopy | Safe — establish camp |

### 4. Level Ground — Check for Bull Ant Nests

Before finalising your camp site:

- **Look for bull ant nests** — small mounds of loose dirt, often with a single entrance hole. Bull ants are aggressive and deliver an extremely painful sting.
- **Check the ground** — if the ground is covered with leaves or debris, clear a patch and check underneath before pitching a tent
- **Level ground** — sleeping on a slope is uncomfortable and reduces blood circulation. Find the flattest area available.

### 5. Firebreak Around Fire

| Fire requirement | Detail |
|-----------------|--------|
| **Clear radius** | Minimum 3 m of bare earth around the fire — no leaves, no grass, no debris |
| **Overhead clearance** | No branches within 5 m above the fire |
| **Wind direction** | Check the prevailing wind. Position fire so sparks blow away from tents, vehicles, and vegetation. |
| **Extinguishing method** | Bucket of water, sand, or a shovel always beside the fire |
| **Fire danger rating** | Check the daily Fire Danger Rating. If Severe or above, consider no fire at all. |

### 6. Food Sealed and Stored Away from Tent

- All food in sealed plastic containers or metal bins
- Store 50+ m from sleeping area
- Hang from a tree branch (3+ m high) or seal in a locked vehicle
- No food in the tent at any time — not even a single muesli bar
- Clean dishes and cooking utensils immediately after eating
- Food scraps go in a sealed rubbish bag, not left in the fire

### 7. Vehicles Parked Facing Exit Route

- **Park vehicles pointing toward the exit** — this is critical for bushfire escape. If a fire front approaches, you need to leave instantly, not reverse and turn around.
- Do not block other vehicles — park in a row or circle, but leave a clear exit lane
- Park on firm ground — do not park on soft sand or boggy ground that you might get stuck in

### 8. Know the Next Town and Fuel Distance

Before settling in for the night:

- What is the **next town with fuel**? How many kilometres?
- What is your **fuel range**? Can you make it to the next fuel stop?
- What is the **next available water**? (town, roadhouse, bore)
- If you had to evacuate at 2 AM in an emergency (fire, injury, flood), which direction would you drive?

### 9. UHF Radio on Channel 40 Overnight

- Leave the UHF radio on Channel 40 (highway channel) overnight, powered by your vehicle battery (or a portable if you have one)
- Channel 40 is monitored by road trains, truckies, and other travellers
- If someone needs to contact you (or you need to call for help), Channel 40 is the standard channel
- Road trains and truckies often relay emergency messages across vast distances
- **Power management:** If your vehicle battery is marginal, turn the radio off at night and check in at dawn. Better to have a charged battery for driving out than a radio for a call that may never come.

### 10. Satellite Device Accessible

- Keep your **PLB or satellite messenger** within arm's reach at night, not buried in luggage
- Store it in a pocket of your tent, in your sleeping bag, or beside your pillow
- In an emergency at night, you do not want to search through a packed vehicle for your PLB
