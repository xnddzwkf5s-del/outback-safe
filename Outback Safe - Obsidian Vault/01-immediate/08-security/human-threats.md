# Human Threats — Avoidance & De-escalation

In a collapse or post-disaster scenario, other humans are simultaneously your greatest potential resource and your greatest potential threat. The majority of people in crisis will cooperate and help. A minority will exploit the situation. Your challenge is distinguishing between them — ideally before they are close enough to harm you.

---

## The Spectrum of Human Intent

| Type | Behaviour | How to respond |
|------|-----------|---------------|
| **Rescuers** | Uniformed, organised, calling out, vehicles/aircraft | Signal immediately. Cooperate fully. |
| **Fellow survivors (cooperative)** | Cautious, signalling, seeking to join or trade | Approach cautiously. Establish communication from a distance. |
| **Fellow survivors (desperate)** | May be irrational, aggressive from fear/hunger. Not malicious — just desperate. | De-escalate. Share information. Share resources if you can afford to. Desperate people become allies if treated with dignity. |
| **Opportunistic (looters, thieves)** | Looking for easy targets. Not violent unless confronted or prevented. | Deterrence — show that you are alert, organised, and not an easy target. Do not engage unless necessary. |
| **Predatory (violent, organised)** | Intent to harm, control, or take by force. | Avoid entirely. Conceal camp. If unavoidable, group defence. |

---

## Decision Matrix — To Engage or Not

| Situation | Engage | Avoid |
|-----------|--------|-------|
| Uniformed rescuers calling your name | ✅ Engage | |
| Lone person, unarmed, signalling distress | ✅ Cautious engagement | |
| Group of strangers approaching camp unannounced | | ❌ Avoid. Observe from concealment. |
| Armed individuals (not uniformed rescuers) | | ❌ Avoid. Do not reveal position. |
| Someone demanding entry/resources at night | | ❌ Do not open. Communicate from behind cover. |
| Injured person genuinely needing help | ✅ Help cautiously | |

## Communication at a Distance

When encountering strangers, communicate before they reach your camp:

{% steps() %}
Stop them at a distance — 30+ m. "That's close enough. Who are you and what do you need?"; Assess their response — calm, reasonable answers are reassuring. Agitation, evasiveness, or demands are red flags.; Keep your group behind you — do not reveal how many of you there are, your supplies, your camp location; Offer help from a distance — "We can leave water/food at this spot for you. You can collect it when we have moved back." This tests intent: a genuinely needy person accepts help. A predator wants access to YOU.; Do not invite strangers into your camp on first contact — establish trust over hours/days, not minutes
{% end %}

## De-escalation Techniques

If a situation is tense but not yet violent:

| Technique | How |
|-----------|-----|
| **Acknowledge their needs** | "I understand you are hungry/thirsty. That is a terrible situation." Validation without concession. |
| **Offer a limited, no-strings exchange** | "We have some water we can share. Here." Small generosity tests and builds trust. |
| **Create a shared problem** | "We are all trying to survive this. How can we help each other?" Reframes from "us vs. them" to "us vs. the situation." |
| **Use calm, slow body language** | Hands visible. No sudden movements. No aggressive postures. |
| **Maintain physical distance** | At least 2 arm-lengths. Closer = threatening. |
| **Speak slowly and quietly** | Loud = aggressive. Quiet = controlled. |
| **Give them a dignified exit** | "Let us both think about this and meet again tomorrow." Even if you have no intention of meeting, offering an exit without humiliation prevents escalation. |

## Deterrence — Looking Like a Hard Target

Predators (human or animal) select easy targets. Make your group look like a hard one:

- **Visible organisation** — sentry visible, tasks being performed, ordered camp
- **Multiple people visible** — lone survivors are easy targets; groups are not
- **Obvious alertness** — sentry makes eye contact, waves acknowledgment
- **Visible tools** — a person carrying an axe or machete (for firewood, shelter building) looks capable, not aggressive
- **Fire** — a maintained fire signals occupancy and capability. A cold camp looks abandoned and easy to investigate.

---

## Campsite Security Against Humans

- **Do not silhouette yourself against firelight** — sentries should be outside the firelight, looking in
- **Conceal supplies** — do not leave food, water containers, tools, or weapons visible from a distance
- **Vary routines** — do not send the same person to the same water source at the same time every day. Predictable = ambushable.
- **Have a rally point** — if the camp is compromised, everyone knows where to regroup
- **Cache supplies** — bury or hide backup food, water, and tools away from camp. If the camp is looted, you still have resources.

---

## When All Else Fails — Last Resort Defence

This wiki does not cover weapons or combat. Those are Tier 3 topics. But in immediate self-defence:

- **Group defence is exponentially more effective than solo defence.** A group of 4 shouting, throwing rocks, and brandishing tools deters most attackers.
- **Fire** — a flaming branch is an effective deterrent. Few people will advance into a swinging torch.
- **Noise** — shouting, whistles, banging metal. Noise attracts attention, which attackers do not want.
- **Escape is victory.** If you can flee safely without casualties, that is a win. Survival is the objective, not "winning" a fight.
- **If escape is impossible and attack is imminent**, fight with everything available. Sticks, rocks, fire, numbers. There are no rules in self-defence.

---

## After an Encounter

- **Debrief the group** — what went well? What would we do differently?
- **Adjust security** — did the encounter expose a weakness in perimeter, concealment, or routine?
- **Document injuries** — even minor injuries need monitoring for infection
- **Do not assume the threat is gone** — if someone approached once, they may return with more people

## 🇦🇺 Australian Context — Human Threats in the Outback

### The Reality: Remote Area Travellers Are Overwhelmingly Helpful

In Australian outback travel, **the threat from other people is extremely low.** The outback culture is one of mutual support and assistance. If you break down, someone will stop to help. If you pass another convoy on a remote track, you stop for a chat, share information about road conditions, and exchange contact details.

- **Most encounters are positive** — people offer help, share fuel, give directions
- **The "mateship" culture is real** — Australians in remote areas look out for each other
- **The threat profile is very different** from post-collapse or urban environments

### Real Risks — Opportunity Theft

While violent crime is rare, **opportunity theft does occur** in popular tourist areas:

| Location | Risk type | Prevention |
|----------|-----------|-----------|
| **Popular campsites** (Uluru, Kakadu, Fraser Island, coastal camps) | Theft from tents/vehicles while occupants are away | Lock your vehicle when away from camp. Do not leave valuables visible. |
| **Roadhouses / fuel stops** | Theft from unattended vehicles | Lock doors. Do not leave fuel, water, or expensive gear on the roof or tray visible. |
| **Caravan parks** | Theft from tents and caravans | Lock tent zippers from inside. Lock bicycle with a cable lock. |
| **Town parking** (especially Darwin, Alice Springs) | Smash-and-grab from vehicle | Leave nothing visible. Park in well-lit areas. |

**Never leave fuel or water unattended** at a campsite you leave unoccupied. Fuel is the most valuable resource in the outback. Water is even more critical — someone tampering with your water supply could be fatal.

### If Confronted at a Campsite

Violent confrontation with other travellers in the outback is extremely rare. If it happens:

{% steps() %}
**De-escalate immediately** — "We do not want any trouble. We can share what we have." Acknowledging their needs disarms most situations.; **Offer limited help** — "We have some water and food we can share. We will leave it here and step back." This tests intent: genuinely needy people accept help. Aggressors want access to YOU.; **Do not escalate** — no shouting, no posturing, no threatening. You are not defending territory. You are managing a rare, unusual situation.; **Move on** — if you feel unsafe, pack up and leave. It is not cowardice. It is survival.; **If you cannot leave** — retreat to your vehicle, lock doors, and call 000 (if there is phone signal) or use your PLB.
{% end %}

### Emergency Numbers for the Outback

| Service | Number | When to use |
|---------|--------|------------|
| **Police, Fire, Ambulance** | 000 | Any genuine emergency — life threatening, fire, medical, crime |
| **SES** (State Emergency Service) | 132 500 | Non-life-threatening emergencies: storm damage, flood, vehicle stuck |
| **Police Assistance Line** | 131 444 | Non-urgent police matters: theft report (no immediate danger), minor property crime |
| **Crime Stoppers** | 1800 333 000 | Report suspicious activity anonymously |
| **PLB activation** | Via beacon | Life-threatening emergency only. Once activated, do not turn off. |

**For genuine threats in remote areas:** If you have 000 coverage (rare in remote areas), call. Otherwise, **activate your PLB.** The police will respond.
