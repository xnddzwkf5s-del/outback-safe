# Shelter — Protection from the Elements

**Exposure is the most underrated killer in survival situations.** In extreme heat or cold, you have hours, not days. A well-built shelter extends that window indefinitely.

---

## Why Shelter Matters More Than You Think

Your body maintains its core temperature within a narrow band (36.5–37.5°C). Outside that range:

| Core temperature | Condition |
|-----------------|-----------|
| 35°C | Hypothermia begins — shivering, confusion |
| 32°C | Shivering stops — severe confusion, risk of cardiac arrest |
| 28°C | Profound unconsciousness — death likely |
| 39°C | Heat exhaustion — headache, nausea, weakness |
| 41°C | Heat stroke — organ failure, brain damage, death |

**Shelter is what keeps you within the survivable band.**

---

## The Five Functions of Shelter

1. **Blocks wind** — wind chill can drop effective temperature by 10–20°C
2. **Sheds rain** — wet clothing conducts heat 25 times faster than dry clothing
3. **Traps body heat** — a properly enclosed shelter can be 10–15°C warmer than outside
4. **Provides shade** — in hot climates, shade can be 10–15°C cooler than direct sun
5. **Psychological security** — a shelter = a home base; morale matters

---

## Shelter Decision Matrix

| Scenario | Priority shelter | Why |
|----------|-----------------|-----|
| **Minutes until dark, cold rain** | Immediate shelter (vehicle/cave/overhang) | Wet = dead. Fastest possible coverage. |
| **Hours until dark, cold, no tarp** | Debris hut | Gold standard — waterproof, insulated, survives sub-zero |
| **Hours until dark, have tarp** | Lean-to (single-slope) | 15-minute build; fire-friendly |
| **Dry cold, strong wind** | Windbreak + fire | Convection is the enemy. Block the wind. |
| **Hot, dry (desert)** | Shade + ventilation | Sun exposure kills. Airflow cools. |
| **Hot, humid (tropical)** | Elevated platform + rain roof | Ground is wet, insect-ridden. Ventilation critical. |
| **Snow/winter** | Snow shelter (quinzhee/cave) or insulated debris hut | Snow is an excellent insulator. Use it. |
| **Coastal** | Windbreak + rain shelter | Coastal winds are relentless. Salt corrosion on gear. |
| **Nothing at all, NOW** | 10-minute debris pile | Survival cocoon. Not comfortable, but you live. |

## The Shelter Timeline

Build in this order:

1. **First 30 minutes** — Immediate shelter from wind and rain using what exists (clothing, natural overhangs, vehicle) → [Immediate Shelter](01-immediate/03-shelter/immediate-shelter)
2. **First 2 hours** — Quick shelter (lean-to, debris pile) that keeps you dry through the first night → [Lean-To](01-immediate/03-shelter/lean-to)
3. **Day 2** — Improve: add insulation, waterproofing, proper bedding → [Debris Hut](01-immediate/03-shelter/debris-hut)
4. **Day 3+** — Refine: proper debris hut or permanent structure, fire reflector, storage

## Universal Shelter Principles

- **Smaller is warmer.** Your body heat warms the air. A smaller enclosed space warms faster. A shelter should be just large enough for you to lie down.
- **Insulation underneath you matters more than above you.** You compress insulation beneath you, reducing its effectiveness. Make your ground bed twice as thick as your roof insulation.
- **The entrance faces away from the wind.** Ideally, also facing east (morning sun) in cold climates.
- **Ventilation prevents condensation.** A fully sealed shelter in cold weather will fill with condensation from your breath. A small vent at the highest point allows moisture to escape.
- **A fire reflector changes everything.** A simple wall of logs or rocks behind your fire reflects heat into your shelter instead of losing it to the open air.

## Regional Shelter Considerations

### Australia

- **Northern (tropical):** Cyclone-proof; elevated for monsoon flooding; maximum ventilation; mosquito netting critical
- **Central (desert):** Shade primary; windbreak secondary; insulation from hot ground; winter nights can drop below freezing — be prepared
- **Southern (temperate):** Four-season preparation; cold wet winters; bushfire-aware site selection in summer; abundant timber for construction

## Bedding — The Forgotten Half of Shelter

A shelter without proper bedding is half a shelter. You lose more heat to the ground than to the air.

{% materials %}
Thick layer of dry leaves, grass, or pine needles (30 cm minimum compressed); Ferns or bracken; Bark sheets (paperbark in Australia is excellent); Cardboard (scavenged — surprisingly good insulation); Spruce/pine boughs (springy, insulate well); Sleeping mat or inflatable pad (if in kit)
{% end %}

**The compression test:** Lie on your bedding. If you can feel the ground through it, add more. Your body weight compresses loose material to 1/3 of its lofted thickness.

{% info %}
**Australian specific:** Paperbark (Melaleuca) is one of the best natural bedding materials on Earth. It is soft, insulating, water-resistant, and peels in large sheets. Found across northern and eastern Australia. Aboriginal Australians have used it for shelter and bedding for tens of thousands of years.

## 🇦🇺 Australian Shelter Priorities & Materials

### Shelter Priorities by Australian Climate Zone

| Climate zone | Primary threats | Shelter priority | Key features needed |
|-------------|----------------|-----------------|---------------------|
| **Tropical north** (Darwin, Kimberley, Cape York) | Monsoon rain, flooding, mosquitoes, crocodiles | Rain protection + ventilation + insect barrier | Elevated floor, waterproof roof, mosquito netting, good airflow |
| **Arid centre** (Alice Springs, SA, WA desert) | Extreme heat (45°C+ day), extreme cold (0°C night), wind, sun | Shade + wind protection + overnight cold insulation | Thick shade roof, windbreak wall, insulated ground bed, small sleeping enclosure for warmth at night |
| **Southern coast** (Melbourne, Adelaide, Perth, Tasmania) | Cold rain, strong winds, coastal storms | Wind + rain + cold protection | Waterproof roof, windbreak on prevailing side, insulated ground bed, fire reflector |
| **East coast** (Sydney, Brisbane) | Mild winters, hot humid summers, storms | Temperate — adaptable | Good drainage, ventilation, weatherproof roof |
| **Alpine** (Snowy Mountains, TAS highlands) | Snow, frost, hypothermia | Insulation + snow load + wind | Enclosed shelter, thick insulation, snow-shedding roof, windproof walls |

### Natural Shelter Materials by Australian Region

| Material | Region | Use | Quality |
|----------|--------|-----|---------|
| **Paperbark sheets** (*Melaleuca*) | Northern and eastern AU | Roofing, walling, bedding | Excellent — waterproof, flexible, lightweight. Peel in large sheets. Lasts weeks in rain. |
| **Spinifex thatching** (*Triodia*) | Arid interior | Roofing, wall thatch | Good — spiky, interlocking, sheds rain. Wear gloves to harvest. Burns easily — keep fire away. |
| **Palm fronds** | Tropical north (Qld, NT) | Roofing thatch | Excellent in wet season. Lay like shingles, overlap generously. |
| **Eucalypt branches** (leafy) | Everywhere | Shade structure, debris covering | Good for shade — breaks down fast (2–3 days. Replace regularly.) |
| **Wattle / mulga branches** | Throughout AU | Structural framework | Good — strong, straight branches for framing. |
| **Grass tree leaves** (*Xanthorrhoea*) | Coastal heath, granite belts | Thatching, bedding | Adequate — prickly but sheds water. Not as good as paperbark. |
| **Kurrajong bark** (*Brachychiton*) | Eastern and northern AU | Cordage, tying, binding | Good — fibrous bark, strip and use as rope. |
| **Cordage from native hibiscus** | Northern AU | Tying, lashing | Excellent — the inner bark strips into strong cord. |

### Australian Shelter Hazards — What to Check For

| Hazard | Region | What to do |
|--------|--------|------------|
| **Snakes seeking shelter** | Everywhere in AU (especially warm weather) | Check under your bedding, inside your shelter, BEFORE occupying. Shake out boots and clothing every morning. |
| **Bull ants** | Everywhere — common under logs, leaf litter | **Painful bite** — burning sensation that lasts hours. Do not build shelter near ant mounds or fallen logs. |
| **Crocodiles** | Northern waterways — camp 50 m+ from water | Do not camp on riverbanks in croc country. Do not wash dishes or collect water at night. |
| **Mosquitoes** | Everywhere — east coast, tropics, inland after rain | Ross River virus, Barmah Forest virus, Kunjin virus, Japanese encephalitis (Torres Strait). Sleep under mosquito netting or smoke smudge fire. |
| **Bush flies** | Outback (central AU — insane densities in summer) | Camp where there is breeze. Wind keeps flies down. Smoke helps. Wear a fly net if available. |
| **Ticks** | East coast (QLD, NSW, VIC), bushland | Paralysis ticks (east coast), scrub ticks. Check yourself thoroughly every evening. Remove with tweezers — pull straight out, don't twist. |
| **Stinging tree** (*Dendrocnide moroides*) | Rainforest Qld, northern NSW | Do not brush against or handle. The silica hairs inject a neurotoxin. Pain lasts months. Learn to identify the heart-shaped leaves with serrated edges. |
| **Limbs falling (widow-makers)** | Any eucalypt (especially River Red Gums) | Eucalypts shed branches without warning, especially in hot weather. Do NOT camp under them. Check for hanging dead branches. |

### Traditional Aboriginal Shelter Types

- **Breakwind (wurley / gunyah):** A simple curved windbreak made of branches and bark. Common across Australia. Quick to build (30 min), provides wind protection but not full rain cover.
- **Paperbark humpy:** Dome frame of branches covered with overlapping paperbark sheets. Waterproof. Typical of northern and eastern Australia. Warm, dry, relatively comfortable.
- **Spinifex shelter:** A grass dome of spinifex thatch over a branch frame. Arid regions. Surprisingly waterproof. Takes 2–3 hours.
- **Rock shelter:** Natural overhangs used seasonally across Australia. Often near permanent water. Check for snakes before using.
