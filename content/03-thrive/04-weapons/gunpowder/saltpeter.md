# Saltpetre (Potassium Nitrate)

Saltpetre (potassium nitrate, KNO₃) is the oxidiser in black powder and the hardest ingredient to obtain. Without it, gunpowder is impossible. It supplies the oxygen that allows the charcoal and sulfur to burn violently even in a sealed chamber.

## Natural Sources

Saltpetre forms naturally wherever nitrogen-rich organic matter decomposes in the presence of potassium-bearing soil or ash, under alkaline conditions with adequate oxygen and moisture.

### Nitre Beds (Manure Heaps)

The most accessible source. Urine and manure, allowed to decompose in contact with soil, wood ash, and limestone over 1–2 years, develop saltpetre crystals as a white efflorescence.

**Construction:**

```
    Layer of straw (aeration)
   ═══════════════════════════
    Manure + urine + soil mix
          (1 metre thick)
   ───────────────────────────
    Layer of wood ash
   ───────────────────────────
    Limestone rubble base
   ═══════════════════════════
    Earth (drainage)
```

{% steps() %}
Select a location with partial shade and good drainage — Full sun dries the bed; full shade slows decomposition.;
Dig a pit approximately 1 m × 2 m × 0.5 m deep — Line sides with limestone or crushed shells if available.;
Layer the base — 5 cm of crushed limestone, then 5 cm of wood ash.;
Add organic material — Pile manure, urine-soaked straw, and nitrogen-rich plant waste (legume leaves, nettles) to 1 metre height.;
Inoculate with old nitre soil — If you have existing nitre-rich earth, scatter a handful into the pile.;
Cover with a thatched roof — Keep rain off but allow airflow. The pile must remain damp, not wet.;
Turn every 3–4 weeks — Aerate with a wooden fork, adding small amounts of urine if drying out.;
Harvest after 12–18 months — White or yellowish crystals on the surface indicate nitre is ready.
{% end %}

### Cave and Rock Shelter Deposits

Bat guano accumulated over centuries in limestone caves undergoes nitrification. Look for:

- **White crystalline crusts** on cave walls and floors
- **Ammonia smell** — indicates active decomposition
- **Damp, alkaline conditions** — nitre forms best at pH 8–10

**AU/SEA locations:** Limestone caves in karst regions. Notable: Gomantong Caves (Borneo), Batu Caves (Malaysia), Chillagoe caves (Queensland), Nullarbor caves (South Australia). Many contain massive bat colonies and centuries of guano accumulation.

### Soil Extraction

In some regions, nitre forms naturally in the topsoil after long dry seasons:

1. Collect surface soil (top 5 cm) from animal pens, under old buildings, or near dried watercourses
2. Look for the characteristic salty-white efflorescence after rain followed by drought
3. Test soil by touching a sample to your tongue — nitre has a sharp, cooling, bitter taste (unlike table salt)

## Extraction Process

### Method: Hot Water Leaching

{% steps() %}
Crush nitre-bearing earth or guano into small pieces — Not powder, just break up clods.;
Build a leaching barrel — A wooden barrel with a layer of coarse gravel at the bottom, then a layer of straw, then fill with nitre earth. Place a collection vessel beneath.;
Pour hot (not boiling) water through — The water dissolves potassium nitrate but leaves most organic matter behind.;
Collect the leachate — It should be a brownish-yellow liquid.;
Repeat with fresh hot water — Run through 2–3 times to maximise extraction.;
Test the leachate — Place a drop on a cool metal surface. If crystals form quickly as it cools, the concentration is good.
{% end %}

### Method: Ash Leaching (Potassium Extraction)

If you have sodium nitrate (Chile saltpetre) but need potassium nitrate:

{% steps() %}
Dissolve the impure nitrate in minimum hot water;
Add hardwood ash — The potassium carbonate in ash exchanges potassium for sodium;
Filter and crystallise — Potassium nitrate crystallises out on cooling, sodium carbonate stays in solution
{% end %}

## Purification (Refining)

Raw nitre leachate contains sodium chloride, calcium salts, and organic compounds. These must be removed for quality gunpowder.

{% steps() %}
Boil the leachate to reduce volume by half — Impurities begin precipitating as scum. Skim this off.;
Filter through cloth while hot — Removes suspended solids.;
Cool slowly — Potassium nitrate crystallises as long needle-like crystals (prisms) at around 30°C. Sodium chloride and other salts stay in solution.;
Harvest the first crop of crystals — These are the purest. The remaining liquid (mother liquor) can be re-boiled for a second, less pure crop.;
Wash crystals with minimum ice-cold distilled water — Removes surface impurities without dissolving too much KNO₃.;
Re-dissolve and re-crystallise — For weapons-grade powder, repeat the process 2–3 times. Each re-crystallisation improves purity.

**Purity test:** Pure KNO₃ crystals should be white or clear, not yellowish. When a small pinch is placed on a clean knife blade and heated, it should melt and burn with a violet flame (the potassium flame test). Yellow flame indicates sodium contamination.
{% end %}

## Field Testing Purity

| Test | Pure KNO₃ Result | Impure Result |
|------|-----------------|---------------|
| Flame colour | Violet (potassium) | Yellow = sodium contamination |
| Crystal appearance | Long, clear needles | Cubic crystals = NaCl; yellow = organics |
| Taste | Sharp, cooling, bitter | Plain salty = NaCl contamination |
| Burn test | Deflagrates with hiss, white smoke | Sputters, pops = moisture; yellow smoke = sulfur contamination |

## Yield Expectations

- **Nitre bed (1 m², 18 months):** 200–500 g of refined KNO₃
- **Bat guano (10 kg dry):** 300–800 g of refined KNO₃
- **Nitre soil (10 kg):** 50–200 g of refined KNO₃

{% info() %}
**Scale matters.** A single shot from a 12-gauge smoothbore uses approximately 5 g of powder (3.75 g saltpetre). A nitre bed yielding 500 g of KNO₃ therefore provides enough saltpetre for roughly 130 shots. Plan your production accordingly — hunting and defense will consume powder continuously.
{% end %}

## Sodium Nitrate Alternative

Chile saltpetre (sodium nitrate, NaNO₃) occurs naturally in large deposits in the Atacama Desert. It was commercially important in the 19th century. However, **sodium nitrate is hygroscopic** — it absorbs moisture from the air, making powder unreliable in humid conditions.

If sodium nitrate is your only source, you must convert it to potassium nitrate using the ash-leaching method above. Even then, the resulting powder will be more moisture-sensitive than true KNO₃ powder.
