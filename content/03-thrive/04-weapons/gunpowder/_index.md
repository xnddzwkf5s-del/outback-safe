# Gunpowder

Gunpowder — specifically **black powder** — is the foundation of all pre-modern firearms, blasting, and signaling. It is a mechanical mixture of three ingredients, not a chemical compound. This means it can be manufactured with relatively simple equipment, unlike modern smokeless powders which require an industrial chemical plant.

## Historical Context

The earliest confirmed formula for gunpowder appears in the Chinese *Wujing Zongyao* (武经总要, "Complete Essentials for the Military Classics") of 1044 CE, though it was likely known decades earlier. Chinese alchemists searching for an elixir of immortality discovered that mixing saltpetre, sulfur, and charcoal produced a substance that burned with alarming speed.

The formula spread along the Silk Road:
- **China (9th–11th century)** — Fire lances, bombs, rockets
- **Islamic world (13th century)** — Refined purification, first cannons
- **Europe (13th–14th century)** — Roger Bacon records the formula; cannons at Crécy (1346)
- **Global (15th–17th century)** — Standardisation, corning, military adoption

## The Chemistry

Black powder combustion is a self-oxidising reaction. The saltpetre (potassium nitrate, KNO₃) provides the oxygen, the charcoal (carbon, C) provides the fuel, and the sulfur (S) lowers the ignition temperature and accelerates the reaction.

**Simplified reaction:**
```
10 KNO₃ + 3 S + 8 C → 2 K₂CO₃ + 3 K₂SO₄ + 6 CO₂ + 5 N₂
```

In reality, the combustion products are a complex mix of solids (potassium carbonate, potassium sulfate, potassium sulfide) and gases (carbon dioxide, nitrogen, carbon monoxide). The solid products create the characteristic white smoke — roughly 56% of the powder mass becomes solid residue.

## The Three Ingredients

| Ingredient | Role | Ideal Ratio | Source Difficulty |
|------------|------|-------------|-------------------|
| Saltpetre (KNO₃) | Oxidiser | 75% | ★★★ Hardest |
| Charcoal (C) | Fuel | 15% | ★ Easiest |
| Sulfur (S) | Accelerant | 10% | ★★ Moderate |

## Why the Ratio Matters

The classic ratio is **75:15:10** (saltpetre:charcoal:sulfur) by weight. This is not arbitrary:

- **Too much saltpetre (>78%)** — Burns too fast, high pressure spikes, barrel damage
- **Too little saltpetre (<72%)** — Incomplete combustion, weak propellant, excessive fouling
- **Too much charcoal** — Slow burn, low pressure, projectile barely leaves barrel
- **Too much sulfur** — Corrosive residue, foul smell, no velocity gain
- **Missing sulfur entirely** — Powder still burns but ignites at much higher temperature, unreliable in flintlocks

## Manufacturing Overview

The full process, detailed in individual pages:

1. **Source** raw materials — See saltpetre, sulfur, and charcoal pages
2. **Purify** each ingredient to highest practical quality
3. **Weigh** precisely using the 75:15:10 ratio
4. **Grind** each ingredient separately to fine powder
5. **Mix** thoroughly while dampened (wet incorporation)
6. **Press** into cakes under moderate pressure
7. **Granulate** by breaking cakes and sieving to desired grain size
8. **Dry** completely in a warm, ventilated area
9. **Test** small batches before scaling up
10. **Store** in safe, dry, separated containers

{% info() %}
**Process order matters.** Never grind the mixed powder dry — this is how you create a dust explosion. Always incorporate ingredients while damp. Read the Mixing page completely before attempting manufacture.
