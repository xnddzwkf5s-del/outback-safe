# Armour

Armour protects the body from weapons. In a survival context, the primary threat is not battle but accidents, animal attacks, and the occasional hostile encounter. A practical approach to armour prioritises what you can make and what protects the most vital areas.

## The Armour Hierarchy

| Type | Protection Level | Weight (full suit) | Manufacturing Difficulty | Best Use |
|------|-----------------|-------------------|--------------------------|----------|
| **Padded (gambeson)** | ★★ vs. cuts, ★ vs. thrusts | 2–4 kg | ★★ | Everyday wear, under other armour |
| **Leather (hardened)** | ★★★ vs. cuts, ★★ vs. thrusts | 3–6 kg | ★★★ | Light defense, hunting |
| **Chainmail** | ★★★★ vs. cuts, ★★ vs. thrusts | 8–15 kg | ★★★★★ | Serious defense |
| **Scale / lamellar** | ★★★★ vs. cuts, ★★★ vs. thrusts | 6–12 kg | ★★★★ | Good compromise |
| **Plate (steel)** | ★★★★★ vs. everything | 15–25 kg | ★★★★★ | Best protection, heaviest |
| **Brigandine** | ★★★★ vs. cuts, ★★★ vs. thrusts | 5–8 kg | ★★★★ | Good all-rounder |
| **Improvised (plastic, tyre, etc.)** | ★★–★★★ | 3–6 kg | ★ | Emergency use only |

## Padded Armour (Gambeson)

A gambeson is a quilted jacket stuffed with padding. It was worn as standalone armour by poorer soldiers and as padding under mail or plate by everyone else. It is surprisingly effective — a well-made gambeson will stop slashing cuts from most edged weapons.

### Construction

{% materials() %}
Outer shell: Heavy linen, canvas, or strong cotton — approximately 2 m × 1.5 m; Inner lining: Lighter linen or cotton; Padding: Raw wool, cotton batting, scrap fabric, or multiple layers of heavy cloth (15–25 layers); Heavy thread for quilting
{% end %}

{% steps() %}
Cut the pattern — A simple T-tunic shape, slightly oversized to accommodate padding. Front and back pieces, with sleeves.;
Layer the padding — Spread the padding material evenly between outer shell and inner lining. For layered cloth padding, simply stack 15–25 layers of heavy linen or canvas.;
Pin or baste the layers together — Keep everything aligned.;
Quilt in vertical lines — Sew through all layers in parallel lines approximately 5–8 cm apart. The quilting prevents the padding from shifting and bunching.;
Quilt additional patterns — Diamond or cross-hatch quilting provides more stability than simple vertical lines.;
Sew the pieces together — Shoulder seams, side seams, sleeve attachment. Reinforcement stitching at stress points.;
Add closures — Toggles and loops (buttons may break), or ties. Front opening is practical.;
Try on and adjust — The gambeson should be snug but not restrictive. It will compress slightly with wear.
{% end %}

**Thickness:** Minimum 10 mm of compressed padding. Ideal: 15–20 mm.

A 15 mm padded gambeson will stop a slashing cut from a knife or sword. It will significantly reduce the penetration of a thrust. It will NOT stop an arrow or crossbow bolt.

## Hardened Leather Armour

Raw leather offers moderate protection. Hardened leather (*cuir bouilli* — "boiled leather") is significantly better.

### Hardening Methods

**Method 1: Hot Water Hardening**

{% steps() %}
Cut and shape the leather piece — Vegetable-tanned leather, 3–5 mm thick. Cut larger than needed (leather shrinks during hardening).;
Immerse in hot water at 80°C (not boiling — boiling makes leather brittle) — Hold submerged for 30–60 seconds until the leather curls and thickens.;
Remove from water — The leather will be soft and pliable.;
Shape immediately — Form to the desired curve (over a wooden form, or directly on the body part with padding underneath). Work fast — the leather sets in 1–2 minutes.;
Allow to cool and dry — The leather hardens as it dries. Full hardness develops over 24 hours.;
Seal — Apply beeswax or oil to prevent re-softening from moisture.
{% end %}

**Method 2: Wax Hardening**

{% steps() %}
Heat beeswax or paraffin to 90–100°C — Not smoking (fire hazard).;
Heat the leather piece in a low oven or near a fire to warm it — Warm leather absorbs wax better.;
Immerse in the hot wax — Hold until bubbles stop rising (1–5 minutes depending on thickness). The wax displaces moisture and fills the leather's fibre structure.;
Remove and wipe excess — The leather will be stiff and dark.;
Shape while cooling — As the wax solidifies, the leather hardens.;
Polish — Buff with a cloth to remove surface wax.
{% end %}

Wax-hardened leather is stiffer and more water-resistant than hot-water-hardened leather. It provides better protection but is heavier and more brittle in cold weather.

### Leather Armour Pieces

**Vambraces (forearm guards):**
- Simple tube with a gap on the inside of the forearm
- Lace or buckle closure
- Protects from string-slap (archery) and defensive cuts

**Cuirass (chest piece):**
- Shaped front plate covering chest and abdomen
- Shoulder straps
- The hardened leather should be at least 4–5 mm thick

**Greaves (shin guards):**
- Curved plates covering the front of the shins
- Strapped behind the calf

## Chainmail (Mail)

Chainmail is the most labour-intensive armour to make, but it is incredibly effective against cuts and provides flexible, comfortable protection.

### Wire Making

{% steps() %}
Draw iron or mild steel wire — Wire approximately 1.5–2.0 mm diameter. If you have access to drawn wire (salvaged fencing wire, etc.), use it. If not, forge wire by drawing a hot iron rod through successively smaller holes in a draw plate.;
Anneal the wire — Heat to red and cool slowly. Soft wire is easier to coil and cut.
{% end %}

### Ring Making

{% steps() %}
Coil the wire around a mandrel — A steel rod of the desired inner ring diameter (typically 8–10 mm). Wind tightly into a long coil.;
Cut the coil into individual rings — Using wire cutters or a chisel. Each cut produces one ring. The cut ends should meet cleanly with no gap.;
Flatten the overlap area (optional — for riveted mail) — Hammer the overlapping ends flat.;
Punch or drill a small hole for the rivet — Through both overlapped ends.;
Close each ring around its neighbours and rivet — Or, for butted mail (simpler, weaker), simply close the ring by butting the ends together.
{% end %}

**Butted mail** (ends just pressed together, no rivet) is much faster to make but will open under stress. A strong thrust from a spear will pop butted rings apart. **Riveted mail** (each ring secured with a tiny rivet) is the historical standard and dramatically stronger.

### Weave Pattern

The standard European 4-in-1 pattern:

```
    Each ring passes through four others:
    
    ┌─┐ ┌─┐ ┌─┐
    │ │ │ │ │ │    ← Ring passes through four neighbouring rings
    └┬┘ └┬┘ └┬┘
     │   │   │
    ┌┼─┐┌┼─┐┌┼─┐
    ││ │││ │││ │    ← Each ring linked to 4 others
    └┼─┘└┼─┘└┼─┘
     │   │   │
```

This pattern hangs correctly, drapes well, and provides natural stretch around curves.

## Helmet Construction

A helmet is the single most important piece of armour. Head wounds are disproportionately fatal.

### Minimum Viable Helmet

**Hardened leather skull-cap:**
- Vegetable-tanned leather, 5–6 mm thick
- Hot-water hardened as a deep bowl shape
- Suspension liner inside (leather straps or padded cloth) — creates air gap between head and helmet critical for impact absorption
- Chin strap

A hardened leather helmet will stop a glancing blow from a club, deflect a knife cut, and protect from falling debris. It will not stop an arrow or direct blow from an axe.

### Helmet Liner

The liner is as important as the shell. A helmet without a liner transfers impact directly to the skull.

```
        ┌─────────────────────┐
        │   HELMET SHELL      │
        │                     │
        │  ┌───────────────┐  │
        │  │ Suspension    │  │  ← Leather straps or woven cord
        │  │ webbing       │  │      creating ~2 cm air gap
        │  │               │  │
        │  └───────┬───────┘  │
        │          │          │
        │    ┌─────▼─────┐    │
        │    │  HEAD     │    │
        └────┴───────────┴────┘
```

{% steps() %}
Create a suspension cradle — Leather straps or strong cord arranged in a star pattern.;
Attach to the helmet rim — Rivets or lacing holes around the inner rim.;
Adjust — The head should rest in the cradle with a 2 cm air gap between skull and helmet shell.;
Add padding — A padded cloth cap worn underneath provides comfort and additional protection.;
Chin strap — Essential. A helmet that falls off is worse than no helmet (it's a distraction).
{% end %}

## Scale / Lamellar Armour

Scales or lamellae are small plates laced together into a flexible sheet. This is a good compromise between leather (light but weak) and mail (strong but labour-intensive).

### Materials

- **Leather scales** — Quickest to make. Punch from hardened leather.
- **Horn scales** — Water buffalo horn, boiled and flattened. Traditional in Asia.
- **Bone scales** — Large animal bones, cut and shaped.
- **Steel scales** — Best protection. Cut from 1 mm sheet steel or salvaged metal.

### Construction

{% steps() %}
Cut identical scales — Rectangular with rounded bottom, approximately 4 cm × 6 cm.;
Punch lacing holes — 4–6 holes per scale in a standard pattern.;
Lace rows together — Using strong cord (sinew, linen, or leather thong). Each scale overlaps the ones below it (like roof tiles).;
Lace rows to each other — Building from bottom to top, so each row overlaps the row below.;
Attach to a cloth or leather backing — Optional but adds structural integrity.;
Shape to body — Lamellar sheets can be assembled into a vest, shoulder guards, or skirt.
{% end %}

## Practical Priorities

For survival, acquire armour in this order:

1. **Helmet** (head wounds are fatal)
2. **Gloves/gauntlets** (hands are exposed in every activity)
3. **Forearm guards** (defensive wounds — forearms take cuts when you raise them to protect face/body)
4. **Torso protection** (chest and abdomen contain the vital organs)
5. **Shin guards** (legs are hard to protect in a fight; shin strikes are common)

{% info() %}
**Animal defence consideration:** If your primary threat is animal attack (dingoes, feral dogs, boar), focus on forearm guards and shin guards. Predators typically go for extremities first. A thick leather vambrace can be the difference between a bad bite and a severed artery.
{% end %}
