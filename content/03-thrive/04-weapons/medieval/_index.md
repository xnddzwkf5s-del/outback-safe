# Medieval Weapons

Firearms are powerful, but they depend on a supply chain — gunpowder, lead, flints. Medieval weapons have a different advantage: **they are infinitely renewable.** A bow made from local wood, an arrow from local saplings, a spearhead forged from scrap iron — these can be made, used, repaired, and replaced without any external inputs.

## Why Medieval Technology Matters

In a prolonged survival scenario, ammunition runs out. Gunpowder ingredients become scarce. Mechanical parts break with no spares. At that point, the group that can make and use bows, crossbows, and spears survives while the group that depended on firearms starves.

### Advantages Over Firearms

- **No ammunition supply chain** — Arrows and bolts are renewable
- **Silent** — No gunshot to alert game (or hostiles) for kilometres
- **No smoke** — Doesn't give away your position
- **Lighter logistics** — A bow and 20 arrows weigh less than a musket and ammunition
- **Repairable in the field** — No precision parts to fail
- **Legal almost everywhere** — Archery is rarely regulated
- **Stealth hunting** — Can take multiple animals from a group without scattering them

### Disadvantages vs. Firearms

- **Lower projectile energy** — An arrow delivers 50–100 J; a musket ball delivers 1000–2000 J
- **Skill-intensive** — A competent archer requires years of practice; a musketeer can be trained in weeks
- **Less armour penetration** — Arrows struggle against metal armour; musket balls do not
- **Weather-sensitive** — Bowstrings and feather fletching are affected by rain
- **Physical strength required** — War bows (80–150 lb draw weight) require specific physique

## The Medieval Weapons Path

The recommended order of development:

1. **Spears** — Easiest to make. Immediate hunting and defense. No advanced materials needed.
2. **Bows** — Takes time to learn but infinitely renewable. Self-bows first, then composite bows.
3. **Shields and armour** — Protection before you engage in combat.
4. **Crossbows** — Easier to train on than bows. Mechanical advantage means anyone can use them.
5. **Swords** — The most difficult to forge. Last priority — a spear does 80% of what a sword does for 10% of the effort.

## Historical Context

This section draws primarily on:
- **European medieval tradition** (10th–15th centuries): Longbows, crossbows, plate armour
- **Chinese military technology** (Warring States through Ming): Crossbows, composite bows, lamellar armour
- **Southeast Asian traditions**: Bamboo bows, rattan shields, blowpipes (not covered here — see Hunting section)
- **Australian Aboriginal technology**: Spear throwers (woomera), hardwood spears, shields
