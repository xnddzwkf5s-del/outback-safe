# Shields

A shield is your first line of defense — the thing that stops a weapon before it reaches your armour or body. In a survival context, a shield is much easier to make than armour and arguably more important. A well-made shield can stop arrows, deflect spear thrusts, and provide cover from thrown rocks.

## Shield Types

| Type | Shape | Diameter/Size | Weight | Best Use |
|------|-------|---------------|--------|----------|
| **Round shield** (centre-grip) | Circular | 60–90 cm | 3–6 kg | All-purpose, dynamic defense |
| **Kite shield** | Tear-drop | 100–120 cm tall | 5–8 kg | Full body coverage, static defense |
| **Heater shield** | Inverted triangle | 50–70 cm tall | 3–5 kg | Personal combat, lightweight |
| **Buckler** | Small round | 20–40 cm | 1–2 kg | Paired with sword, active parrying |
| **Pavise** | Large rectangle | 120–150 cm tall | 8–15 kg | Archer cover, static position |
| **Wicker/rattan shield** | Any | 60–90 cm | 2–4 kg | Lightweight, SEA/AU traditional |

## Materials

### Shield Body

| Material | Pros | Cons | Difficulty |
|----------|------|------|------------|
| **Plywood** (salvaged) | Strong, stable, readily available (modern scrap) | Degrades when wet if not sealed | ★ (easiest) |
| **Planks** (edge-glued) | Traditional, can be curved | Heavy; glue joint failure | ★★ |
| **Plywood (self-made)** | The historical method — thin wood veneers glued cross-grain | Time-consuming to make the plywood itself | ★★★ |
| **Rawhide over wood** | Adds tremendous toughness | Rawhide stretches when wet | ★★★ |
| **Wicker / rattan** | Lightweight, durable, traditional SEA/AU | Penetrable by thrusts | ★★ |
| **Leather (thick, 8+ mm)** | Flexible, won't splinter | Heavy, penetrable by arrows | ★★ |
| **Steel** (2–3 mm) | Near-indestructible | Very heavy (10+ kg for full size) | ★★★★ |

### Shield Covering

A wood shield needs a facing to prevent it from splitting along the grain when struck:

- **Rawhide** (best) — Stretched wet over the shield face and edges, shrinks as it dries. Creates a tough, unified surface. Edge binding is especially critical.
- **Canvas / heavy linen** — Glued to the face with hide glue or modern adhesive. Not as tough as rawhide but easier.
- **Leather** — Thin leather glued and stitched over the face.

### Edge Protection

The edge of a shield takes the most abuse — weapons are deflected here. Reinforce it:

- **Rawhide binding** — A strip of wet rawhide stitched around the entire edge. Shrinks as it dries, gripping the rim tightly.
- **Metal rim** — A thin iron or brass strip nailed or riveted around the edge. Heavy but very durable.
- **Leather binding** — Thick leather strip stitched on. Adequate, easier to make than rawhide.

## Round Shield Construction (Centre-Grip)

The most practical shield for survival — dynamic, all-purpose, and straightforward to construct.

```
            ┌─────────────────┐
            │                 │
            │    ┌───────┐    │
            │    │ BOSS  │    │  ← Steel or iron bowl covering
            │    │ (grip │    │      hand hole
            │    │ inside)│   │
            │    └───────┘    │
            │                 │
            └─────────────────┘
            ↑                 ↑
        Rawhide or       Wood planks
        canvas facing    (butt-joined or
                          plywood)
```

{% steps() %}
Build or obtain the shield blank — Three layers of 3 mm plywood glued cross-grain, or edge-glued planks approximately 10 mm thick.;
Cut to circle — 60–80 cm diameter is practical for survival use. Larger is heavier; smaller offers less protection.;
Shape the curve (optional) — Soak the blank in water or steam, then clamp over a curved form. A gentle curve (5 cm deep at centre) deflects blows better than a flat shield.;
Cut the hand hole — Centred. Approximately 10 cm × 15 cm oval.;
Install the boss — A steel or iron bowl (the shield boss) is riveted over the hand hole. The hand grips a wooden bar across the hole, protected by the boss. If you cannot make a metal boss, a thick hardwood boss carved to shape does the job.;
Face the shield — Stretch wet rawhide over the entire face and edges. Stitch or nail in place. As the rawhide dries over 2–3 days, it shrinks tightly. Or glue canvas.;
Paint — Linseed oil paint or milk paint. Painting seals the surface and extends shield life.;
Install the grip — A hardwood bar (approximately 3 cm diameter) across the hand hole. Shaped to fit the hand. The grip should be aligned so that when you hold it, your knuckles are at 10 o'clock and 4 o'clock (not horizontal — this is more natural for the wrist).;
Install a guige (carrying strap) — A long leather strap riveted to the back. Allows the shield to be slung over the shoulder when not in use.
{% end %}

## Kite Shield Construction (Strap-Grip)

A kite shield provides full-body coverage and is strapped to the forearm rather than centre-gripped.

```
        ┌─────────┐
        │         │
        │         │  ← Top rounded
        │  ┌───┐  │
        │  │   │  │  ← Enarmes (arm straps)
        │  └───┘  │
        │         │
        │         │
        │         │
        │         │
        └────┬────┘
             │
             ▼  ← Bottom point
```

### Strapping (Enarmes)

{% steps() %}
Install a forearm pad — A padded leather cushion on the inside of the shield where the forearm rests.;
Install the primary arm strap — A wide leather strap (approximately 5 cm) near the elbow. The forearm passes through this strap.;
Install the hand grip — A second strap or a rigid handle near the shield's centre. The hand grips this.;
Install the guige — The long carrying strap.;
Adjustment: When strapped correctly, the shield should cover from shoulder to knee when standing. The bottom point should nearly touch the ground when kneeling behind it.
{% end %}

## Wicker / Rattan Shield (SEA/AU)

Traditional throughout Southeast Asia and the Pacific Islands. Lightweight, surprisingly tough, and uses renewable materials.

{% materials() %}
Rattan vines (Calamus spp.) — abundant in SEA rainforests — or flexible willow/pliable saplings for frame; Split rattan or thin bamboo strips for weaving; Wooden handle (hardwood)
{% end %}

{% steps() %}
Build the frame — Bent rattan or green saplings formed into the desired shape (round, oblong, or rectangular). Bind joints with rattan strips or cordage.;
Weave the body — Split rattan or thin bamboo strips woven in an over-under pattern. Tight weave.;
Double-layer for strength — Two layers of weaving, crossed at 90° to each other.;
Install the grip — A carved wooden handle lashed to the centre (for centre-grip) or leather straps (for forearm-strap grip).;
Bind the edge — Rattan binding around the entire rim.;
Optional — Face with rawhide or thick leather for additional puncture resistance.
{% end %}

A well-made rattan shield 70 cm in diameter weighs only 2–3 kg. It will turn a spear thrust (the point may penetrate the weave but the shield absorbs the energy) and stop arrows from low-poundage bows. It will not stop a crossbow bolt or a high-poundage war arrow.

{% info() %}
**AU Note:** Aboriginal Australian shields were traditionally made from hardwood (mulga, ironbark) carved from a single piece, or from bark (e.g., stringybark) folded and shaped. Hardwood parrying shields (*murnong*, *mulga wood*) are narrow (10–20 cm wide) and used actively to deflect spears and clubs rather than to hide behind. They are not passive shields — they are wielded dynamically.
{% end %}

## Shield Maintenance

- **Keep dry when not in use** — Wet wood warps, glued joints separate, rawhide softens
- **Re-paint annually** — The paint is more than decoration; it's weatherproofing
- **Check edge binding regularly** — Rewet and tighten rawhide if it stretches
- **Check grip and straps** — Leather rots, rivets work loose. Check before any serious use
- **Store flat or hung by the guige** — Leaning a shield on its edge causes warping

## Using a Shield (Basic Principles)

### Centre-Grip Round Shield

- **Don't hide behind it** — The shield is an active tool. Move it to intercept attacks, not a static barrier.
- **Keep it extended** — A shield held close to the body protects little. Extend it toward the threat — this closes distance the weapon must travel and exposes the opponent's hand to counter-strike.
- **Edge-forward can be offensive** — The rim of a shield can strike, push, and trap an opponent's weapon.

### Strap-Grip Kite/Heater Shield

- **Cover the body in a fighting stance** — The shield should cover from the left shoulder to left knee (for a right-handed fighter).
- **"Present" the shield** — Extend slightly forward. This protects the forward leg and creates a barrier the opponent must work around.
- **Keep it high enough to see over** — The top rim should be at or just below eye level.

### Against Arrows

- **Close the distance** — The shield is most effective when moving. Stand still and the archer finds gaps.
- **Kneel behind a kite shield** — Kneeling presents a smaller target. The bottom point of a kite shield touches the ground, covering the legs.
- **Arrow penetration test** — Test your shield against your own bow at expected engagement range. Know what it will and won't stop.
