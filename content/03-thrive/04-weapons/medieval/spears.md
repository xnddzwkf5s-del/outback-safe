# Spears

The spear is humanity's oldest purpose-built weapon — used by every culture on every continent. It is simple, effective, and requires almost no materials to produce a functional version. A sharpened stick is already a spear. A fire-hardened sharpened stick is a weapon that can take medium game.

## Why Spears First

In any survival situation, make a spear before attempting a bow.

- **Minutes to make** (hours for a bow)
- **No string required** (bow needs a string)
- **Multi-purpose** — hunting, defense, walking staff, fishing, shelter pole
- **Effective at close range** — a thrust to centre mass with a sharp spear is immediately lethal
- **Keeps distance** — 2 m of spear between you and a predator (or hostile human) is better than a knife at arm's reach

## Spear Types

| Type | Length | Primary Use | Construction Time |
|------|--------|------------|-------------------|
| **Thrusting spear** | 1.8–3.0 m | Close defense, large game at bay | 30 min – 2 hours |
| **Throwing spear (javelin)** | 1.2–1.8 m | Medium-range hunting | 30 min – 2 hours |
| **Heavy hunting spear** | 2.0–2.5 m | Boar, bear, large game | 2–4 hours |
| **Atlatl dart** | 1.2–2.0 m | Long-range hunting (with atlatl) | 1–2 hours + atlatl |
| **Fishing spear (multi-pronged)** | 1.5–3.0 m | Fish, frogs, small aquatic game | 1–3 hours |

## Shaft (Haft) Materials

| Material | Pros | Cons | Region |
|----------|------|------|--------|
| **Ash** (*Fraxinus* spp.) | Excellent. Straight, strong, shock-resistant. | Not native to AU/SEA. | Europe, N. America |
| **Hickory** (*Carya* spp.) | Outstanding impact resistance. | Not native to AU/SEA. | N. America |
| **Bamboo** | Hollow (light), naturally straight, tough. | Must reinforce tip area. Thin-walled varieties need filling. | **SEA, CN, N. AU** |
| **Spotted gum** (*Corymbia maculata*) | Very hard, durable. Excellent spear wood. | Heavy. Can split if not seasoned. | **Eastern AU** |
| **Mulga** (*Acacia aneura*) | Extremely hard, traditionally used for spears. | Heavy, hard to work. | **Arid AU** |
| **Red ash** (*Alphitonia excelsa*) | Light, tough. | Softer than eucalypts. | **Northern AU, SEA** |
| **Teak** (*Tectona grandis*) | Durable, weather-resistant. | Heavy, silica dulls tools. | **SEA** |
| **Willow** (*Salix* spp.) | Light, straight shoots. | Too soft for heavy use — javelins only. | Global temperate |
| **Sapling (any hardwood)** | Readily available. | Must be straightened and seasoned. | Global |

### Selecting a Spear Shaft

- **Straightness:** As straight as possible. Gentle curves can be heat-straightened.
- **Diameter:** 25–35 mm at the grip (wider for heavy thrusting spears).
- **Length:** At least 30 cm taller than you (for thrusting). Eye-level for throwing.
- **No knots or branches:** Knots are weak points — the spear will break at a knot.
- **Seasoned:** Green wood is weak and heavy. Air-dry for at least 3 months if possible.

## Heat Straightening

{% steps() %}
Identify the curve — Mark the inside of the curve.;
Heat the curved section over coals or with a torch — Rotate constantly. Heat until the wood is too hot to touch (~100–150°C) but not scorching. Do not char the surface.;
Bend against the curve — Using a forked tree or a purpose-made bending jig. Over-bend slightly (the wood will spring back).;
Hold in position until cool — This may take 10–15 minutes. The wood fibres relax in the heated state and set in the new position.;
Repeat for multiple curves — Work from the thickest end to the thinnest.;
Do not rush — Forcing cold wood will crack it.
{% end %}

## Spearheads

### Fire-Hardened Point

The simplest spearhead — no separate piece required.

{% steps() %}
Sharpen the end of the shaft to a point — A long taper, approximately 20–30 cm.;
Scorch the tapered section in hot coals — Rotate constantly. The surface should turn dark brown-black, not ash-grey (which means it's burning).;
The heat drives out moisture and partially carbonises the surface — The result is a hard, durable point that resists blunting.;
Optional: Rub with fat or oil while still warm — The oil polymerises in the heat, creating a water-resistant surface.
{% end %}

A fire-hardened spear point can penetrate the hide of medium game (deer, kangaroo, pig) and is adequate for self-defense against most predators.

### Stone Point

Stone points are sharper than fire-hardened wood but more fragile.

{% steps() %}
Knap a point from suitable stone — Flakes of chert, flint, quartzite, or obsidian. Triangular or leaf-shaped, approximately 5–10 cm long. See Tool Making section (Tier 2) for knapping technique.;
Notch the base — For hafting.;
Split the end of the spear shaft — Cut a slot approximately 5 cm deep.;
Insert the stone point — The base of the point sits in the slot.;
Bind tightly with sinew, rawhide, or strong cordage — The binding compresses the shaft around the point.;
Seal with pine pitch, tree resin, or hide glue — Waterproofs the binding and adds strength.
{% end %}

### Metal Point

If you have access to iron or steel:

```
         ┌──────────────┐
         │    BLADE     │  ← Forged to shape
         │              │
         │   ┌──────┐   │
         │   │Tang  │   │  ← Inserts into shaft
         └───┴──────┴───┘
```

{% steps() %}
Forge a spearhead from mild steel or recycled iron — Leaf-shaped or triangular, 15–25 cm long.;
Forge a long tang — At least 10 cm of tang, tapering to a point. The tang should be at least 8 mm thick at the base.;
Grind or file cutting edges — Both edges sharp (double-edged) or one edge (single-edged — easier to make).;
Harden the edges — Heat the blade to red (non-magnetic, ~770°C), quench the cutting edges in oil. Temper to purple-blue (270–290°C) for toughness. Leave the tang soft (do not harden — a hardened tang snaps on impact).;
Drill or burn a socket in the shaft — The tang inserts into a hole drilled or burned into the end of the shaft.;
Bind the shaft end tightly — Heavy cordage or rawhide wrapping around the shaft where the tang is inserted prevents splitting.
{% end %}

## Atlatl (Spear Thrower)

The atlatl (Nahuatl word) or woomera (Australian Aboriginal) is a lever that extends the thrower's arm, dramatically increasing spear velocity and range.

```
            ┌──────────────────────────┐
            │     ATLATL (thrower)     │
            │                          │
    ┌───────┴──┐               ┌───────▼──┐
    │  Hook    │               │  Handle  │
    │(engages  │               │          │
    │  dart)   │               │          │
    └──────────┘               └──────────┘
    
    Length: 40–70 cm
    Hook: Bone, antler, or hardwood spur
    
    ═══════════════════════════════════════
                DART (thrown)
    ═══════╦═══════════════════════════════
           ↑
       Nock (cup) engages hook
```

### How It Works

The atlatl acts as a lever. When you throw, the atlatl rotates forward around your grip, multiplying the speed at the hook end. At the moment of maximum velocity, the dart disengages and flies free. An experienced user can throw a dart at 40–45 m/s — comparable to a 40 lb bow's arrow velocity — but with a much heavier projectile (100–200 g vs. 30–50 g for an arrow).

### Construction

{% steps() %}
Select a hardwood board or branch — 40–70 cm long, 3–4 cm wide, 1.5–2 cm thick.;
Shape the handle end — Comfortable grip, slightly wider than the shaft.;
Carve a groove along the top — The dart rests in this groove during the throw.;
Fit the hook (spur) — A bone, antler, or hardwood peg projecting upward at the rear end. The spur engages the nock (cup) at the base of the dart.;
Optional: Add a finger loop — A leather loop at the handle for secure grip.;
Optional: Add a weight stone — Some atlatl designs include a stone weight (bannerstone) tied to the shaft. This adds mass and changes the flex characteristics. Not essential.
{% end %}

### Dart Construction

Atlatl darts are longer and more flexible than hand-thrown spears:

- **Length:** 1.5–2.5 m (longer = more flex = more energy storage during the throw)
- **Diameter:** 10–15 mm at the grip, tapering to 8–10 mm at the tip
- **Weight:** 100–200 g
- **Material:** Willow shoots, bamboo, river cane, young saplings
- **Flex:** The dart should flex noticeably during the throw — this is not a flaw, it's how the energy is stored and released

### Performance

| User Level | Dart Weight | Velocity | Kinetic Energy | Effective Range |
|------------|-------------|----------|----------------|-----------------|
| Beginner | 150 g | 20–25 m/s | 30–47 J | 15 m |
| Intermediate | 150 g | 30–35 m/s | 68–92 J | 25 m |
| Expert | 150 g | 40–45 m/s | 120–152 J | 40 m |

For comparison: a 40 lb bow arrow (~30 g) at 50 m/s = 38 J. An atlatl dart delivers 2–4× the kinetic energy of an arrow, but with a much heavier, slower projectile.

{% info() %}
**AU Note:** The woomera (Australian Aboriginal spear thrower) is one of the finest atlatl designs ever developed. Typically made from mulga or other dense hardwood, 60–80 cm long, with a distinctive leaf-shaped blade and a resin-mounted bone or hardwood hook. The design has been refined over at least 40,000 years of continuous use. If you are in Australia, study local museum examples or Aboriginal cultural resources for design details.
{% end %}

## Spear Fighting (Basic Principles)

While the focus of this wiki is survival hunting and defense, basic spear technique:

### Thrusting

- **Grip:** Rear hand at butt, front hand 1/3 from rear. Thumbs toward the point.
- **Stance:** Lead foot forward, knees slightly bent.
- **Thrust:** Drive from the rear hand, sliding the shaft through the front hand. The front hand guides; the rear hand delivers power.
- **Recovery:** Pull back immediately after thrust. A lodged spear is a gift to your opponent.

### Throwing (Hand)

- **Grip:** Hold the spear at its balance point (usually 1/3 from the front).
- **Stance:** Side-on to target, opposite foot forward.
- **Throw:** Overhand, like throwing a ball, but the spear releases from the fingers with a final wrist snap. The snap imparts spin for stability.
- **Range:** Effective to 15–20 m for an untrained thrower. Practice extends this to 30 m.

### Against Animals

- **Never throw your only spear at a predator.** A charging animal will not be stopped by a thrown spear unless it's a perfect hit. Keep the spear for thrusting defense at close range.
- **Against boar/pig:** Aim for the chest behind the shoulder. The shoulder blade is tough — go behind it.
- **Against large herbivores (cattle, buffalo):** Side-on shot behind the shoulder. Do not face them head-on — the skull and rib cage are thickest at the front.
