# Firearms

Firearms represent the apex of pre-industrial weapon technology. A competent gunsmith with access to iron, charcoal, and saltpetre can equip a community with weapons that dramatically shift the balance of power in hunting and defense.

## Smoothbore vs. Rifled

The fundamental choice in firearm design:

| Characteristic | Smoothbore | Rifled |
|---------------|-----------|--------|
| Accuracy | Poor beyond 50 m | Good to 200+ m |
| Reloading speed | 3–4 shots/minute | 1–2 shots/minute |
| Manufacturing difficulty | ★★ Moderate | ★★★★ Difficult |
| Ammunition tolerance | High (loose ball works) | Low (tight fit required) |
| Barrel fouling tolerance | High | Low (rifling fills with fouling) |
| Versatility | Shot or ball | Ball only (shot damages rifling) |
| Best use | Hunting, close defense | Precision shooting, sniping |

For survival purposes, **start with smoothbore.** The military standard for 200 years was the smoothbore musket for good reason: it works with imperfect ammunition, reloads quickly, and is far easier to manufacture. A rifled barrel requires precision tooling that is extremely difficult without machine tools.

## Ignition Systems (Chronological)

### Matchlock (1400s–1600s)

```
    ┌──────────┐
    │  Barrel  │
    └────┬─────┘
         │
    ┌────▼─────┐   ┌─────────┐
    │  Pan &   │◄──│  Slow   │
    │  Touch   │   │  Match  │
    │  Hole    │   │ (cord)  │
    └──────────┘   └────┬────┘
                        │
              ┌─────────▼─────────┐
              │  Serpentine       │
              │  (S-shaped lever) │
              └───────────────────┘
                        │
                   Trigger
```

**Pros:** Simple mechanism, easy to make. Slow match burns for hours.
**Cons:** Glowing match visible at night (giveaway). Match goes out in rain. Constant fire hazard near powder.

### Wheellock (1500s–1600s)

A spring-driven serrated wheel spins against iron pyrite, producing sparks. Like a modern lighter.

**Pros:** No glowing match, works in light rain.
**Cons:** Complex clockwork mechanism — difficult to manufacture and repair. Expensive. Never military standard.

### Flintlock (1600s–1830s)

```
              ┌──────────┐
              │  FLINT   │ ← Held in the cock (hammer)
              │ (knapped)│
              └────┬─────┘
                   │ strikes downward
    ┌──────────────▼───────────────┐
    │  FRIZZEN (steel face)        │
    │  ┌──────────────────────┐    │
    │  │  SPARKS shower into  │    │
    │  │  PAN (priming powder)│    │
    │  └──────────┬───────────┘    │
    └─────────────┼────────────────┘
                  │
    ┌─────────────▼───────────────┐
    │  TOUCH HOLE                │
    │  (flash enters barrel)     │
    └────────────────────────────┘
```

**Pros:** Reliable, no glowing match, works in reasonable weather, manageable to manufacture. Standard for ~200 years.
**Cons:** Flint wears out (20–30 shots per knapping). Vulnerable to heavy rain. Misfire rate approximately 15–20% in field conditions.

### Percussion Cap (1820s–1870s)

A copper cap containing fulminate of mercury detonates when struck, sending flame through the nipple to the main charge.

**Pros:** Nearly waterproof, misfire rate under 2%, no flint to knap.
**Cons:** Requires manufacture of percussion caps (fulminate of mercury is dangerously sensitive and toxic to produce). Caps are consumables — once your stock is gone, the weapon is useless.

## The Survival Firearm Path

The recommended progression:

1. **Start with matchlock** — Easiest to manufacture. Accepts imperfect tolerances. Good for learning barrel-making and stock-crafting.
2. **Upgrade to flintlock** — Once you have barrel-making experience, the flintlock mechanism is worth the additional complexity.
3. **Stay with flintlock** — Do not attempt percussion beyond using found caps. Flintlock is renewable: flint can be knapped from local chert, quartz, or agate.
