# Firearm Repair

Every firearm will eventually break. Regular use, corrosive powder residue, and the fundamental violence of containing an explosion against your shoulder guarantee it. The gunsmith's art is keeping guns running despite this.

## What Breaks and Why

| Component | Failure Rate | Typical Failure Mode | Criticality |
|-----------|-------------|---------------------|-------------|
| Flint | Every 20–30 shots | Edge rounds over | Low (replace in seconds) |
| Mainspring | Every 500–2000 shots | Fatigue crack, break at bend | High (weapon disabled) |
| Sear spring | Every 500–2000 shots | Weakens, fails to engage | High (unsafe — may fire unexpectedly) |
| Frizzen face | Every 1000–3000 shots | Wears smooth, stops sparking | High (weapon disabled) |
| Sear nose | Every 1000–5000 shots | Wears, slips off tumbler notch | Critical (extremely dangerous) |
| Tumbler notch | As sear nose | Wears, sear won't engage | Critical |
| Cock jaw screw | Ongoing | Works loose, lost | Low (wire replacement possible) |
| Frizzen spring | Every 500–2000 shots | Snaps | High (frizzen won't close — pan spills) |
| Stock wrist | Eventual | Cracks at narrowest point | Medium (can be repaired, see below) |
| Barrel | Rare | Bulge from obstruction, burst from overload | Critical (replace barrel) |

## Lock Repair and Tuning

### Replacing a Mainspring

The mainspring (V-spring) is the lock's muscle. It is the most common disabling failure.

{% steps() %}
Remove the lock from the stock — Typically one or two side-lock screws.;
Bring the cock to full-cock and insert a spring vise — A spring vise compresses the mainspring so it can be removed without damage. Without a vise, the spring will fly out violently when released.;
Remove the mainspring screw — The spring is now free.;
Compare the new spring to the old — The new spring should match in shape, length, and tension. A spring that is too strong makes the trigger pull heavy; too weak and ignition suffers.;
Install the new spring — Engage the stub in the lock plate hole, insert screw, tighten.;
Release spring vise carefully — The spring should seat firmly.;
Test the lock — Cock and release several times. Listen for consistent, strong action.
{% end %}

### Making a Spring (If No Spare Available)

{% steps() %}
Source spring steel — High-carbon steel rod or flat stock, approximately 2.5–4.0 mm thick depending on application. An old file (ground to shape) can work if properly tempered.;
Forge or file to shape — The classic V-spring shape.;
Harden: Heat to bright red (800–850°C), quench in oil — The spring will be glass-hard and brittle at this stage. If dropped, it will shatter.;
Temper IMMEDIATELY: Polish a section of the spring to bright steel — Heat gently and watch the oxide colours. Heat until the polished section turns deep blue (approximately 300°C). Quench in water at this point.;
The spring should now be springy — Test by compressing. It should return to its original shape.;
If it breaks during testing — Too hard. Re-heat to blue and temper slightly higher (purple-blue, ~320°C).;
If it takes a set (stays bent) — Too soft. Re-harden and temper at a slightly lower temperature.
{% end %}

### Re-hardening a Frizzen

When the frizzen face wears smooth and stops producing sparks:

{% steps() %}
Remove the frizzen from the lock — One pivot screw.;
Clean the frizzen face — Remove all dirt and oil.;
Prepare case-hardening compound — (See Flintlock page).;
Pack the frizzen, face down, in the compound inside a sealed container.;
Heat to 850–900°C, hold for 2–4 hours.;
Quench directly from the container into water.;
Temper to light straw (200–220°C) — The frizzen face should just barely resist a file.;
Test on a piece of flint — It should produce a shower of sparks.
{% end %}

**Alternative — Re-facing a worn frizzen:**

If the frizzen face is deeply grooved or eroded, it can be re-faced rather than re-hardened:

{% steps() %}
File or grind the old face flat;
Forge or cut a piece of high-carbon steel (approximately 2 mm thick) to match the frizzen shape;
Braise or silver-solder the new face onto the old frizzen body (borax flux, brass or silver solder);
File to final shape, leaving the new face slightly proud;
Quench the assembly from 800°C to harden the face;
Temper the face to light straw

**Caveat:** Braising heat may draw the temper from the frizzen pivot — re-harden or live with it if the pivot is not critical.
{% end %}

## Stock Repair

### Cracked Wrist

The wrist (the narrow section behind the lock) is the most stressed part of the stock. Cracks here are common.

{% steps() %}
Clean the crack thoroughly — Remove all oil and dirt. Acetone or alcohol if available.;
Open the crack slightly — Use wooden wedges to gently spread the crack just enough to get adhesive in.;
Apply glue — Hide glue (traditional, reversible, very strong) or modern epoxy. Work the glue deep into the crack with a thin blade or dental pick.;
Clamp firmly — Wrap with rubber strips (cut from inner tube), twine, or use padded clamps.;
Allow to cure fully — Minimum 24 hours for hide glue.;
For structural reinforcement — Drill a small hole through the wrist from top to bottom, insert a hardwood dowel with glue, trim flush.;
Alternatively — Wrap the wrist with rawhide (wet), which shrinks as it dries, forming a tight, reinforcing band.
{% end %}

### Loose Barrel Pins/Bands

Barrel pins (small metal pins through the stock that hold the barrel) can work loose:
- Fill the hole with a sliver of hardwood and glue
- Re-drill to size
- Install new pin

### Stripped Screw Holes

Wood screw holes in the stock eventually strip:
- Plug the hole with a glued hardwood dowel
- Cut flush
- Drill a new pilot hole
- Re-install the screw

## Field Repair Kit

Every gun owner should carry:

| Item | Use |
|------|-----|
| Spare flints (3 minimum) | Primary spark source |
| Flint leather/lead (3 pieces) | Cushioning flint in jaws |
| Mainspring (1 spare) | Most common disabling lock failure |
| Frizzen spring (1 spare) | Second most common spring failure |
| Sear spring (1 spare) | Essential for safe function |
| Turnscrew (screwdriver fitting lock screws) | Disassembly |
| Vent pick (wire) | Clearing blocked touch hole |
| Small file | Dressing frizzen face, fitting parts |
| Spare jaw screw | Lost screws are common |
| Spring vise | SAFELY removing springs |
| Cleaning jag and patches | Essential maintenance |
| Small oil bottle | Rust prevention |
| Copper rod (if ball gets stuck) | Removing stuck ball (see below) |

## Stuck Ball Removal

If a ball becomes stuck in the barrel (squib load, forgotten charge, loading error):

### Method 1: Pull the Ball

{% steps() %}
Remove the lock — Prevent any chance of ignition.;
Pour water or oil down the barrel — Let it soak around the ball for 10–15 minutes. This loosens fouling and lubricates the ball.;
Use a ball puller — A tapered wood screw on the end of the ramrod. Screw it firmly into the lead ball.;
Pull steadily — Not jerking. Two people: one holds the gun, one pulls the rod. A T-handle on the ramrod helps.;
If the ball won't budge, soak longer — Patience is safer than force.
{% end %}

### Method 2: Blow It Out (Last Resort)

{% steps() %}
Confirm the ball is past the touch hole — Insert ramrod and mark depth. Compare to exterior barrel measurement.;
Work FFFFg or meal powder into the touch hole — Using a vent pick or small wire. Tap the barrel to settle powder under the ball. Aim to get at least 0.3–0.5 g of powder behind the ball.;
Replace the lock and prime — Use a reduced main charge to minimise risk.;
Point in safe direction and fire — The small charge should push the ball out (possibly weakly).;
If it does not work — Try one more time with slightly more powder. If still stuck, the barrel must be unbreeched (remove the breech plug — gunsmith level repair).;
DO NOT keep adding powder and firing — Each attempt risks the barrel.
{% end %}

## Touch Hole Erosion

The touch hole erodes over time from hot gases:
- A 1.5 mm touch hole becomes 2.0 mm after approximately 500 shots
- A 1.5 mm touch hole becomes 2.5 mm after approximately 1000 shots

Once the touch hole exceeds 2.5 mm, replace it:
- **Removable vent liner:** Unscrew old, screw in new (brass or steel)
- **Integral touch hole:** Drill and tap for a vent liner, or retire the barrel

A worn touch hole vents gas sideways forcefully — a hazard to the shooter's eyes and anyone standing beside them.

## When to Retire a Barrel

- Visible bulge anywhere along the barrel (internal or external)
- Crack at the breech (hard to see — inspect with good light)
- Deep pitting that reduces wall thickness by more than 25%
- Touch hole erosion beyond repair
- Unknown history (a found barrel of unprovable origin)
