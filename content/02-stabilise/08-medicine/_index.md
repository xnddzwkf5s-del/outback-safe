# Medicine & First Aid

## Outback Medicine — A Different World

In the Australian outback, professional medical help is not minutes away — it is hours or days. The RFDS is the backbone of remote emergency response, but in many areas their response time is measured in hours, and in extreme conditions (flood cut-offs, fire, aircraft availability) it can stretch to a day or more.

This section is about what you can do in that gap — the hours between injury and help. It is not a substitute for professional medical training. It is a reference for the reality of remote Australia.

## What Kills Fastest in the Outback

| Threat | Time to Death (Untreated) | Priority |
|--------|---------------------------|----------|
| **Snake bite** (brown snake, taipan) | 30 min – 3 hours | **Highest** — correct first aid buys hours |
| **Heat stroke** | Minutes after collapse | **Highest** — cool before anything else |
| **Major bleeding** | 3–5 minutes (arterial), longer (venous) | **Highest** — compress immediately |
| **Dehydration** | 6–48 hours (environment-dependent) | **High** — preventable, daily priority |
| **Severe allergic reaction** | 5–30 minutes (airway swelling) | **High** — adrenaline saves |
| **Infection** | Days to weeks | **Medium** — treat early before systemic |
| **Fracture with blood loss** | Varies (femur/pelvis = internal bleeding) | **Medium-High** — depends on location |

**The triage reality:** In the outback, you cannot treat everything. Your job is to treat what will kill first, stabilise what you can, and evacuate what you cannot.
