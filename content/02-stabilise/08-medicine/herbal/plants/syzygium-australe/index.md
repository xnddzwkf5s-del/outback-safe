# Syzygium australe

![Lilly Pilly](/images/plants/syzygium-australe.jpg)
*Syzygium australe — Lilly pilly. Vitamin C source, edible fruit. Image: Wikimedia Commons (CC BY-SA).*

## Common Names
- English: Lilly pilly, brush cherry, scrub cherry, creek satinash
- Chinese: 澳洲蒲桃 (Àozhōu pú táo)
- Aboriginal: Various names — "midjim" in some Queensland languages (though true midjim is *Austromyrtus dulcis*)

## Identification
- **Habitat:** Rainforest margins, moist eucalypt forest, and along watercourses on the east coast of Australia from southern NSW to central Queensland. Commonly found as riparian vegetation. Also widely cultivated as a garden plant (hedge/screen).
- **Leaves:** Opposite, ovate to elliptic, 4–10 cm long, dark glossy green, with a distinct acuminate (drawn-out) tip. New growth is often bright pink to red — a key visual field ID. Crushed leaves have a faint fruity scent. Oil glands visible when leaf is held to light.
- **Flowers:** Creamy white, numerous fluffy stamens (up to 2 cm across), in terminal or axillary clusters. Resemble small bottlebrushes. Appear in late spring to summer.
- **Fruit:** Oval to pear-shaped, 1.5–3 cm long, pink to deep magenta-purple when ripe, with a crisp texture and single large seed. Flesh is tart-sweet, edible, high in vitamin C. Fruit appears in late summer to autumn.
- **Bark:** Smooth, greyish-brown, slightly flaky in older trees. Not medicinally used.
- **Look-alikes:** Many other Syzygium species (over 50 in Australia) have similar fruit, most are edible — if it looks like a lilly pilly, it generally IS a lilly pilly. The pink new growth, opposite glossy leaves with acuminate tips, fluffy cream flowers, and pink-magenta oval fruit identify *S. australe*. Do not confuse with toxic *Eugenia uniflora* (Surinam cherry, introduced) which has ribbed fruit and is not native — but even that is edible when ripe. Lilly pillies are generally a safe group.

## Distribution
East coast of Australia from southern NSW to central Queensland. Widely cultivated as a garden plant throughout Australia. Not naturally occurring in China or SEA, but genus Syzygium is common across SEA (e.g., *S. aromaticum* — cloves, *S. cumini* — jambolan).

## Medicinal Uses
- **Primary:** Vitamin C source — fresh fruit provides prevention and treatment of scurvy in survival situations where fresh food is scarce. The fruit is one of the most reliable wild vitamin C sources on the Australian east coast.
- **Secondary:** Astringent — the leaves and bark contain tannins and can be used as a mild astringent decoction for diarrhoea or as a mouthwash. Antioxidant (fruit is rich in anthocyanins, the purple pigments).
- **Traditional:** Aboriginal peoples ate the fruit fresh and used leaf decoctions for digestive complaints. The fruit was often crushed and soaked in water to make a refreshing drink.

## Preparation
- **Fresh fruit:** Eat raw when fully ripe (deep pink to purple). 10–20 fruit daily will provide significant vitamin C. Pick and eat as you find them — they don't store well fresh.
- **Decoction (leaves):** 1 tablespoon dried leaves per 250 mL water, simmer 10–15 minutes. Strain. Drink 1/2 cup 2–3× daily for mild diarrhoea. Cool and use as mouthwash.
- **Jam/Preserve:** Essential survival food preservation — cook fruit with sugar or honey (equal parts by volume), simmer until thickened. Stores for months. Vitamin C degrades somewhat with cooking but significant amounts remain.
- **Infusion:** 1 teaspoon dried leaves per 250 mL boiling water, steep 10 minutes. Milder than decoction. Drink for sore throat.

## Safety
- **Contraindications:** None significant. Generally recognised as safe. The seed is large and hard — don't swallow it (choking hazard, not toxic).
- **Side effects:** Excess consumption of raw fruit can have a mild laxative effect (common with high-vitamin C fruit eaten in quantity). Tannins from leaf decoction can cause constipation with prolonged use.
- **Interactions:** Tannins can reduce iron absorption — take leaf preparations between meals if iron status is a concern.
- **Toxicity:** None known. This is a safe and widely consumed bush food. The fruit is regularly eaten by children.

## Cultivation
- **Propagation:** Fresh seed (sow immediately — viability drops quickly). Germinates in 2–6 weeks. Also from semi-hardwood cuttings (moderate success). Many named cultivars exist in horticulture.
- **Growing conditions:** Full sun to partial shade. Adaptable to most soils but prefers moist, well-drained conditions. Tolerates light frost once established. Very hardy and low-maintenance. Excellent hedge plant — responds well to pruning.
- **Harvest:** Fruit harvested when fully coloured (pink to purple) in late summer/autumn. Fruit on the same tree ripens over several weeks. Pick individually. Prune after fruiting to maintain shape and encourage next season's growth.
