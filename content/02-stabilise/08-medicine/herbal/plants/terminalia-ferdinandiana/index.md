# Terminalia ferdinandiana

![Kakadu Plum](/images/plants/terminalia-ferdinandiana.jpg)
*Terminalia ferdinandiana — Kakadu plum. World's highest natural source of vitamin C. Image: Wikimedia Commons (CC BY-SA).*

## Common Names
- English: Kakadu plum, billygoat plum, green plum, gubinge, murunga
- Chinese: 卡卡杜李 (Kǎkǎdù lǐ)
- Aboriginal: Gubinge (Bardi, Nyul Nyul), madoorr (Yawuru), kabinyn (Worrorra), and many other names across language groups of northern Australia

## Identification
- **Habitat:** Open woodland, savanna, and monsoon forest across northern Australia — Kimberley region of WA, Top End of NT, and northern Queensland. Prefers sandy to loamy soils, often near watercourses. Deciduous in the dry season.
- **Leaves:** Broadly elliptic to ovate, 10–20 cm long, light green, alternate or spirally arranged, clustered near branch tips. Deciduous — tree loses leaves in the dry season (May–October). New leaves appear with the first rains.
- **Flowers:** Small (5–8 mm), creamy white to pale yellow, in slender axillary spikes 5–12 cm long. Fragrant. Appear during the wet season (December–March).
- **Fruit:** Ovoid drupe, 1.5–2.5 cm long, pale green to yellowish-green when ripe, with a single large hard stone. Flesh is tart and astringent when raw. The key ID: fruit appears in the late wet/early dry season (March–June). This fruit holds the Guinness World Record for highest natural vitamin C content — up to 5,300 mg per 100 g (compare to oranges at ~50 mg/100 g).
- **Bark:** Grey to brown, rough, fissured on older trees. Not the primary medicinal part.
- **Look-alikes:** Other Terminalia species have similar fruit. *T. ferdinandiana* is distinguished by: the combination of northern Australian distribution, broadly elliptic leaves clustered at branch tips, deciduous in dry season, and small green-yellow fruit. Other Terminalia fruit are generally not toxic but have different nutrient profiles.

## Distribution
Endemic to northern Australia: Kimberley (WA), Top End (NT), and northern Queensland. Not naturally occurring in China or SEA, but genus Terminalia is widespread across tropical Asia and Africa (e.g., *T. catappa*, tropical almond).

## Medicinal Uses
- **Primary:** The world's highest natural source of vitamin C. A single fruit provides more vitamin C than an entire bag of oranges. Essential for preventing and treating scurvy in survival situations. Vitamin C is also critical for wound healing, immune function, and collagen synthesis.
- **Secondary:** Potent antioxidant (contains high levels of ellagic acid and other phenolic compounds). Antiseptic — Aboriginal peoples used the inner bark and fruit as an antiseptic wash for wounds and skin infections. Anti-inflammatory.
- **Traditional:** A cornerstone of Aboriginal bush medicine and food across northern Australia. The fruit was eaten fresh, dried, or ground into a paste. Inner bark decoctions were used for skin conditions, wounds, and sores. The bark was also used for digestive complaints.

## Preparation
- **Fresh fruit:** Eat raw. The taste is tart to astringent — an acquired taste. 1–2 fruit daily is more than enough to prevent scurvy. In a survival context, this is the most potent natural scurvy preventative on Earth.
- **Dried fruit:** Harvest ripe fruit, slice or leave whole, dry in sun until leathery. Stores for months. Grind to powder and add to food or water for vitamin C on demand.
- **Decoction (inner bark):** 1 tablespoon chopped inner bark per 500 mL water, simmer 20 minutes. Strain. Use cooled as wound wash or skin antiseptic.
- **Infusion (leaves):** 1 teaspoon dried leaves per 250 mL hot water, steep 10 minutes. Mild antiseptic and vitamin C source (less potent than fruit).

## Safety
- **Contraindications:** None significant. The fruit is very tart — may irritate mouth sores or gastric ulcers. Generally considered safe and widely consumed.
- **Side effects:** Raw fruit can cause mouth and throat irritation in large quantities due to high acidity. The seed is large and hard — do not swallow (choking hazard, not toxic).
- **Interactions:** High vitamin C intake can theoretically affect warfarin efficacy (if pharmaceutical blood thinners are available), but this is unlikely to be relevant in survival contexts.
- **Toxicity:** None known. This is one of the most important and safest bush foods in Australia.

## Cultivation
- **Propagation:** Fresh seed (remove fruit pulp, sow immediately). Germination is slow (3–6 months) and irregular. Nicking the hard seed coat may improve speed. Very difficult from cuttings.
- **Growing conditions:** Full sun, well-drained sandy to loamy soil. Tropical to subtropical climate — requires distinct wet and dry seasons. Frost-sensitive (tropical plant). Moderate growth rate. Deciduous in dry season.
- **Harvest:** Fruit harvested when pale green to yellow-green and starting to soften, typically March–June. Harvest by hand or with a pole to shake branches over a ground sheet. Fruit bruises easily. Inner bark harvested in small quantities only from branches (not trunk — can kill tree).
