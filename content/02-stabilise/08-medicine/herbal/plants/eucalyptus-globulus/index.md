# Eucalyptus globulus

![Blue Gum](/images/plants/eucalyptus-globulus.jpg)
*Eucalyptus globulus — Blue gum. Antiseptic, decongestant, respiratory support. Image: Wikimedia Commons (CC BY-SA).*

## Common Names
- English: Blue gum, Tasmanian blue gum, southern blue gum
- Chinese: 蓝桉 (lán ān)
- Aboriginal: Various names across language groups

## Identification
- **Habitat:** Open forest, woodland, widespread across southern and eastern Australia. Thrives in well-drained soils, tolerates poor conditions. Introduced and naturalised in parts of China and Southeast Asia.
- **Leaves:** Adult leaves are long (15–35 cm), narrow, sickle-shaped (falcate), dark glossy green, with distinct eucalyptus scent when crushed. Juvenile leaves are opposite, round, blue-grey, and sessile (attached directly to stem without petiole).
- **Flowers:** Large (up to 5 cm across), white to cream, solitary in leaf axils. Numerous prominent stamens. Buds have a warty, blue-grey operculum (cap) that falls off as flower opens.
- **Bark:** Smooth, shedding in long strips to reveal pale grey, cream, and bluish patches. Rough, persistent bark at base of older trees.
- **Roots:** Extensive deep root system — not typically harvested medicinally.
- **Look-alikes:** Other eucalypts are generally safe but less potent medicinally. Do NOT confuse with Oleander (*Nerium oleander*) which has superficially similar long narrow leaves — oleander has showy pink/white five-petaled flowers (not fluffy eucalypt flowers), and oleander leaves lack the distinctive eucalyptus scent when crushed.

## Distribution
Native to Tasmania and southeastern Australia (Victoria, southern NSW). Widely planted throughout Australia, and introduced to China, Southeast Asia, South Africa, Mediterranean, and California. One of the most widely planted eucalypts globally.

## Medicinal Uses
- **Primary:** Respiratory decongestant and expectorant. The essential oil (1,8-cineole/eucalyptol) loosens mucus, opens airways, and has antimicrobial properties. Excellent for colds, sinus congestion, bronchitis.
- **Secondary:** Antiseptic for minor wounds and skin infections. The diluted oil or leaf decoction can clean wounds when pharmaceutical antiseptics are unavailable.
- **Traditional:** Aboriginal Australians used eucalyptus leaf poultices for wounds, and inhaled steam from crushed leaves for respiratory conditions. Leaf infusions were used for fever.

## Preparation
- **Infusion:** Use 1–2 teaspoons dried crushed leaves per 250 mL boiling water. Steep covered for 10 minutes. Drink 2–3 cups daily for respiratory conditions. Note: taste is strong and camphorous.
- **Decoction:** Simmer 1 tablespoon bark or coarser leaves per 500 mL water for 15 minutes. Strain. Use as a wound wash (cooled) or drink 1/2 cup 3× daily.
- **Poultice:** Crush fresh leaves, apply directly to minor wounds or insect bites. Cover with cloth. Change every 2 hours. The volatile oils provide topical antiseptic action.
- **Tincture:** Fresh leaves in 60% alcohol (resins need higher proof). Steep 4 weeks. Dose: 1–3 mL in water, 3× daily.
- **Steam Inhalation:** Add a handful of fresh leaves (or 5–10 drops of oil) to a bowl of just-boiled water. Lean over with towel over head. Breathe deeply for 5–10 minutes. Repeat 2–3× daily for respiratory congestion.

## Safety
- **Contraindications:** Do not use eucalyptus oil internally — it is toxic when swallowed (as little as 5 mL can be fatal, especially in children). Leaf preparations (infusion/decoction) are safe in standard doses. Avoid in pregnancy (theoretical risk — no strong data, but not worth the risk). Avoid in inflammatory gastrointestinal conditions.
- **Side effects:** Contact dermatitis in sensitive individuals (patch test before using oil on skin). Nausea if infusion is too strong. May trigger asthma in some people.
- **Interactions:** Theoretically may affect liver enzyme metabolism of pharmaceuticals. Separate from other medicines by 2 hours.
- **Toxicity:** Eucalyptus essential oil ingestion is a medical emergency. Do not ingest the oil. Keep oil and concentrated preparations away from children.

## Cultivation
- **Propagation:** From seed. Seeds are tiny — sow on surface of seed-raising mix, keep moist, germinates in 2–4 weeks. Also by cuttings from young growth (difficult).
- **Growing conditions:** Full sun, well-drained soil, drought-tolerant once established. Fast growing (1–2 m per year). Frost-tolerant once established. Does not tolerate waterlogged soil.
- **Harvest:** Leaves can be harvested year-round. Young leaves have higher oil content. Dry in shade to preserve volatile oils. Bark harvested from pruned branches (not trunk — can kill tree).
