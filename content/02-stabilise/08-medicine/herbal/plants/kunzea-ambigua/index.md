# Kunzea ambigua

![Tick Bush](/images/plants/kunzea-ambigua.jpg)
*Kunzea ambigua — Tick bush. Anti-inflammatory and analgesic topical remedy. Image: Wikimedia Commons (CC BY-SA).*

## Common Names
- English: Tick bush, white kunzea, poverty bush
- Chinese: Not established in Chinese medicine
- Aboriginal: Various names across Tasmanian and Victorian language groups

## Identification
- **Habitat:** Sandy coastal heathlands, dunes, and open forest along the coast of Tasmania, Victoria, and southern NSW. Also found on rocky headlands. Tolerates salt spray and poor sandy soils.
- **Leaves:** Small (5–12 mm), narrow, linear to oblanceolate, alternate, aromatic when crushed — fragrance is spicy, herbaceous, slightly eucalyptus-like but softer. Bright green to grey-green.
- **Flowers:** White to pale pink, small (8–12 mm), with numerous prominent stamens longer than the petals, giving a fluffy appearance. Borne in dense clusters at branch tips. Appear in spring and summer (November–February).
- **Bark:** Fibrous, stringy, grey-brown, persistent. Not medicinally used.
- **Roots:** Not medicinally used.
- **Look-alikes:** Other Kunzea species (e.g., *K. ericoides*, burgan) look similar. The combination of coastal sandy habitat, fluffy white flowers with stamens much longer than petals, small aromatic leaves, and spring/summer flowering distinguishes it. Not dangerous if confused with related Myrtaceae species, but medicinal potency varies.

## Distribution
Coastal southeastern Australia: Tasmania, Victoria, southern NSW. Not occurring naturally in China or SEA. Cultivation outside native range is limited.

## Medicinal Uses
- **Primary:** Anti-inflammatory and analgesic for muscle aches, joint pain, arthritis, and soft tissue injuries. Recent scientific interest has confirmed traditional knowledge — kunzea oil is comparable to some topical NSAIDs.
- **Secondary:** Antiseptic for minor wounds and skin infections. Soothing for insect bites (paradoxically useful in tick country). The essential oil has demonstrated antimicrobial activity. Respiratory decongestant.
- **Traditional:** Aboriginal peoples of Tasmania used kunzea leaf poultices for sore muscles, sprains, and joint pain. Leaves were also steeped for a medicinal bath to treat general aches.

## Preparation
- **Infused Oil:** Fill a jar loosely with fresh leaves and flowers, cover with olive or coconut oil. Place in a warm spot (or solar infuse) for 2–4 weeks. Strain. Use as massage oil for sore muscles and joints.
- **Poultice:** Crush fresh leaves and flowering tips, apply directly to painful area. Cover with cloth. Leave 30 minutes to 2 hours. Warming sensation is normal.
- **Salve:** Infuse oil as above, then add beeswax (1:5 ratio wax to oil) to make a spreadable salve. Excellent for arthritis and chronic pain.
- **Infusion:** 1 teaspoon dried herb per 250 mL boiling water, steep 10 minutes. Drink 1–2 cups daily for systemic anti-inflammatory effect. Pleasant spicy-herbal flavour.
- **Tincture:** Fresh leaf and flower in 50% alcohol, steep 4 weeks. Dose: 2–3 mL in water, 2–3× daily for pain and inflammation.
- **Steam Inhalation:** Handful of leaves in hot water for respiratory decongestion.

## Safety
- **Contraindications:** Avoid in pregnancy (limited safety data). Discontinue if skin irritation occurs. Do not use on open wounds without diluting.
- **Side effects:** Mild warming/tingling sensation on skin (normal). Rare skin sensitivity — patch test before use. May cause drowsiness in some individuals.
- **Interactions:** No significant known interactions. Theoretically additive with other anti-inflammatory herbs.
- **Toxicity:** Generally recognised as safe. No known toxicity at therapeutic doses.

## Cultivation
- **Propagation:** From seed (sow on surface, keep moist) or semi-hardwood cuttings (dipping in rooting hormone improves success). Seedlings are slow initially.
- **Growing conditions:** Full sun to partial shade. Well-drained sandy soil. Very drought-tolerant once established. Tolerates salt spray and coastal conditions. Frost-tolerant to about -5°C. Slow to moderate growth.
- **Harvest:** Leaves and flowering tips harvested during flowering (spring/summer) when oil content peaks. Prune regularly to keep compact and productive. Dry in shade.
