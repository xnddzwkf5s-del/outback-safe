# Herbal Database

## Overview

This database covers medicinal plants native to Australia, drawing on both traditional Aboriginal bush medicine and modern phytochemical research.

These plants have been selected for:
- **Efficacy** — documented medicinal value across multiple traditions
- **Availability** — common and widespread in their native ranges
- **Safety** — favourable risk profile when correctly identified and prepared
- **Versatility** — multiple uses from a single plant

## How to Use This Database

### Step 1: Identify Your Need
Browse the plant list below and match your condition to a plant's primary use.

### Step 2: Verify the Plant
Each plant entry includes detailed identification information. Cross-reference at least three features (leaf, flower, habitat, season) before harvesting. When in doubt, do not use.

### Step 3: Choose a Preparation
Use the appropriate method for the plant part:

| Method | Best For | How | Times |
|--------|----------|-----|-------|
| **Infusion** | Leaves, flowers | Pour boiling water, steep covered | 5–15 min |
| **Decoction** | Roots, bark, seeds | Simmer in water | 15–30 min |
| **Poultice** | External wounds, inflammation | Crush fresh or reconstitute dried, apply directly | 20 min–2 hrs |
| **Tincture** | Long-term storage, concentrated dose | Steep in 40–60% alcohol | 2–6 weeks |
| **Salve** | Skin conditions, burns | Infuse in oil, add beeswax | N/A |
| **Syrup** | Cough, sore throat, children | Decoction + sugar/honey, reduce | N/A |

### Step 4: Follow Safety Protocols
Always start with small doses. Test for allergic reactions. Consult multiple identification features.

## Plant Identification Basics

### The Multi-Feature Rule
Never identify a plant by a single feature. Confirm at least three of:

1. **Leaf shape and arrangement** — simple vs. compound, opposite vs. alternate, margin type (smooth, serrated, lobed)
2. **Flower structure** — number of petals, colour, arrangement (single, cluster, spike), season
3. **Bark texture and colour** — smooth, rough, peeling, fissured
4. **Root type** — taproot, fibrous, rhizome, tuber
5. **Habitat and location** — elevation, soil type, water proximity, sun exposure
6. **Smell and taste** — crush a leaf and smell; only taste if you're already confident (and never swallow during ID)
7. **Season** — flowering time, fruiting time, leaf-on/leaf-off

### Common ID Mistakes

| Mistake | Example | Consequence |
|---------|---------|-------------|
| ID by leaf only | Hemlock vs. wild carrot | Fatal poisoning |
| Ignoring habitat | Water hemlock in wet areas vs. edible in dry | Fatal poisoning |
| Confusing seedlings | Foxglove seedling vs. comfrey | Cardiac toxicity |
| Similar flowers | Monkshood vs. larkspur | Severe toxicity |
