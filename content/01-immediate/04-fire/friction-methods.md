# Friction Fire

Friction fire is the original human fire-starting technology — and still one of the most reliable, because it requires no manufactured tools. If you have wood, cordage, and the right technique, you have fire.

But be warned: friction fire is a **skill**, not knowledge. Reading this page will not give you a fire on your first attempt. It will give you the knowledge to practice until it is reliable. Practice before you need it.

---

## The Physics of Friction Fire

Key insight: **friction fire produces a coal (ember), not a flame.** You transfer the glowing coal to a tinder bundle and blow it into flame. Understanding this sequence prevents the most common beginner mistake — looking for flame at the friction point. There will be none.

## Method 1: Bow Drill

![Bow drill fire starting](/images/fire/bow-drill.jpg)
*Bow drill method — most reliable friction technique. Image: Wikimedia Commons (CC BY-SA).*

The bow drill uses a bow to spin a spindle against a hearth board. It is the most mechanically efficient friction method — and the one you should master first.

### Materials

{% materials %}
**Bow:** curved green branch, 60–80 cm long, finger-thick, with some flex but not floppy; **Bow string:** paracord, bootlace, strong vine, or strip of bark (paperbark or hibiscus bark in AU, bamboo fibre in CN/); **Spindle:** straight dry stick, 15–20 cm long, 2–3 cm diameter. Round is important — carve to shape if necessary.; **Hearth board:** flat piece of dry softwood, 30–40 cm long, 10 cm wide, 2–3 cm thick; **Handhold (bearing block):** piece of hardwood, stone with a depression, shell, or bottle cap. Must not generate friction at the top.; **Tinder bundle:** dry, fluffy tinder to receive the coal (see Fire Basics); **Leaf or bark:** to catch the coal dust
{% end %}

### Choosing Woods (AU/CN/)

The spindle must be harder than the hearth board. Friction concentrates on the softer wood, producing char dust while the harder spindle resists wear.

**Good hearth woods (softer):**

| Wood | Region |
|------|--------|
| Willow | , temperate AU |
| Cedar | Introduced in AU, |
| Mulga (*Acacia aneura*) | Arid Australia — good for BOTH hearth and spindle |

**Good spindle woods (harder):**

| Wood | Region |
|------|--------|
| Mulga | Arid Australia — outstanding spindle wood |
| Ironbark | Australia — very hard, excellent spindle |

**Test the woods:** Press your thumbnail into the hearth board. If it leaves a mark, it is soft enough. If it does not, choose a softer wood or harder spindle.

---

### Step 1: Assemble the Bow Drill Set

{% steps %}
**Carve the spindle:** Round, straight, 15–20 cm long. The top end (against handhold) should be pointed (conical) — this minimises friction at the handhold. The bottom end (against hearth) should be blunt-rounded — maximises friction surface.; **Prepare the hearth board:** The board must be flat on both sides. Carve or sand smooth if necessary.; **Prepare the handhold:** Carve a depression in the handhold for the top of the spindle. Lubricate with oil, grease, green leaves (crushed), or soap. This reduces friction at the top — you want friction ONLY at the bottom.; **String the bow:** The cord should be taut enough to grip the spindle but loose enough to wrap around. Tie one end of the cord to the bow, wrap the cord once around the spindle, then tie the other end. The spindle should be on the OUTSIDE of the bow (between the bow and your body).
{% end %}

### Step 2: Burn the Seating Hole

{% steps %}
Position the spindle about 2 cm from the edge of the hearth board; Spin the spindle by hand to create a dark, smooth depression that centres the spindle; This is your "seat" — it prevents the spindle from skating across the board under pressure
{% end %}

### Step 3: Cut the Notch

{% steps %}
Cut a V-shaped notch from the edge of the hearth board INTO the seating hole; The notch should be about 1/8 of the circle (a 45° pie-slice); The notch collects char dust and allows it to fall through onto your coal catcher; Place a green leaf or piece of bark under the notch to catch the coal dust
{% end %}

### Step 4: Position and Start Bowing

{% steps %}
Position: Place your left foot (if right-handed) on the hearth board to hold it steady. The notch should face away from you, with the leaf underneath.; Insert spindle: Loop the bow string once around the spindle. The spindle should be between the bow and your shin.; Position hands: Place the spindle tip in the seating hole. Place the handhold on top of the spindle. Grip the bow with your right hand near the middle.; Start slow and steady: Begin bowing slowly and steadily. The spindle should spin without wobbling. If it wobbles, your spindle is not round, your seating hole is not centred, or your bowing motion is uneven.; Build speed gradually: Once the spindle is spinning smoothly, increase speed. You are NOT trying to produce a coal yet — just getting the feel.
{% end %}

### Step 5: Build Heat Until Smoke Appears

{% steps %}
Look for smoke: After 30–60 seconds of steady bowing at decent speed, smoke should appear. Smoke means the wood is heating up and charring — it does NOT mean you have a coal.; Increase pressure and speed: As smoke builds, press down harder on the handhold AND bow faster. This increases friction heat.
{% end %}

### Step 6: Form the Coal

{% steps %}
Watch for the coal: After smoke is consistent, look at the notch. Dark char dust will accumulate on the leaf below. When the dust continues to smoke AFTER you stop bowing, you have a coal.; Remove the spindle carefully: The coal is in the notch debris, not attached to the spindle. Lift the spindle away gently without disturbing the coal pile.
{% end %}

**Signs of a successful coal:**
- Char dust continues smoking for 10+ seconds after you stop drilling
- A faint glow is visible in the dust pile (shield from light to see it)
- The dust pile is dark brown/black

### Step 7: Transfer Coal to Tinder

{% steps %}
Gently tap the hearth board to release any remaining coal dust onto the leaf; Transfer the smoking coal dust to your tinder bundle — the tinder should already be in your hand, cupped around the coal; Do not dump the coal into the tinder. Place the tinder AROUND the coal.; Close the tinder bundle gently around the coal — do not compress it (smothers the coal); Lift the bundle to mouth level — NOT directly in front of your face (fire safety); Blow gently but steadily into the bundle — long, controlled breaths, not short puffs; You are oxygenating the coal, not blowing it out. Steady airflow.; Smoke will increase dramatically. Keep blowing.; The tinder will burst into flame — usually when you least expect it; Place the burning tinder into your prepared fire lay
{% end %}

### Common Mistakes — Bow Drill

- **Spindle not round:** If it wobbles, carve it until it spins true. A wobbly spindle wastes energy and produces no coal.
- **Cord too loose:** The cord must grip the spindle. If it slips, increase tension or use a stick to twist the cord tighter (a toggle).
- **Pressing too hard, bowing too slow:** Speed generates heat more than pressure. Focus on smooth, fast bowing first.
- **Giving up at smoke:** Smoke is halfway. Beginners stop at the first sign of smoke. Keep going for another 60+ seconds.
- **Handhold burning:** Lubricate generously. Green leaves crushed between handhold and spindle work. If your palm burns, your handhold is too thin or un-lubricated.
- **Notch too wide:** If the notch is wider than 45°, the spindle slips out. If too narrow, char dust can't fall through. Re-cut if needed.

### Success Indicators — Bow Drill

- Smoke appears within 30–60 seconds of steady bowing
- Char dust is dark brown, not pale tan (pale = not hot enough)
- Dust pile continues smoking independently for 10+ seconds after stopping
- Faint red/orange glow visible when shaded from direct light
- Coal is large enough to transfer — at least a 5 mm pile

## Method 2: Hand Drill

![Hand drill fire starting](/images/fire/hand-drill.jpg)
*Hand drill method — the simplest but most physically demanding friction technique. Image: Wikimedia Commons (CC BY-SA).*

The hand drill is the simplest friction method — no bow, no cord. Just your hands spinning a spindle against a hearth. It is also the hardest. Master the bow drill first.

### Materials

{% materials %}
**Spindle:** straight, dry stick, 60–90 cm long, 1–1.5 cm diameter. Must be perfectly straight and round.; **Hearth board:** same as bow drill — flat, dry softwood with notch; **Tinder bundle:** prepared and ready; **Leaf or bark:** coal catcher
{% end %}

### Technique

{% steps %}
Use a longer, thinner spindle (60–90 cm, 1–1.5 cm diameter); Start with hands at the top of the spindle, rolling it between palms while pressing down; As hands reach the bottom, quickly move them back to the top — do not stop spinning; This "floating" technique maintains continuous rotation; Same hearth board and notch setup as bow drill; Expect this to take 3–10 times longer than a bow drill — build endurance; Suitable dry, soft woods for hand drill: mullein stalks, yucca, hibiscus, pawpaw
{% end %}

### Common Mistakes — Hand Drill

- **Stopping between strokes:** Every pause cools the friction point. Maintain continuous rotation via floating.
- **Spindle too short:** You need 60–90 cm to get enough hand travel per stroke. Shorter = more strokes per heat unit = exhaustion.
- **Palms blistered:** Build calluses through practice. In an emergency, wrap hands in cloth or use gloves.
- **Wrong wood:** The spindle must be dry and straight. Any curve causes wobble and energy loss.

### Success Indicators — Hand Drill

- Smoke within 2–3 minutes (longer than bow drill)
- Char dust accumulating in the notch
- Coal formation takes 3–5× longer than bow drill

{% info %}
**Hand drill is an advanced skill.** If you have not practiced it before your emergency, use a bow drill instead. The hand drill requires specific wood types and considerable upper-body endurance. Most beginners take 20+ attempts to get their first coal.
{% end %}

## Method 3: Fire Plow

![Fire plow technique](/images/fire/fire-plow.jpg)
*Fire plow — rubbing a hardwood stick along a softwood groove. The most physically demanding method. Image: Wikimedia Commons (CC BY-SA).*

The fire plow rubs a hardwood stick along a groove in a softwood board, pushing char dust ahead of the plow tip until it ignites. It is the simplest design — two pieces of wood and no cord — but the least mechanically efficient.

### Materials

{% materials %}
**Hearth board:** softwood, flat, 40–60 cm long, 10 cm wide. Paulownia, poplar, cottonwood are ideal.; **Plow stick:** hardwood, 20–30 cm long, 2–3 cm diameter. Blunt, rounded tip.; **Tinder bundle:** prepared and ready
{% end %}

### Technique

### Step 1: Prepare the Groove

{% steps %}
Carve a straight groove down the length of a softwood hearth board, 1 cm deep; Smooth the groove sides — the plow stick must slide freely without catching
{% end %}

### Step 2: Plow Rapidly

{% steps %}
Use a hardwood plow stick with a blunt, rounded tip; Rub the plow stick rapidly back and forth along the groove; Char dust accumulates at the far end of the groove; Continue until the dust pile is smoking and glowing; Transfer to tinder as with bow drill
{% end %}

**Best woods for fire plow:** Soft hearth (paulownia, poplar, cottonwood), hard plow stick (oak, ironbark, bamboo).

### Common Mistakes — Fire Plow

- **Groove not smooth:** Rough grooves catch the plow stick, wasting energy. Sand or carve smooth.
- **Not enough speed:** The plow requires high speed to overcome heat dissipation. Sprint, don't jog.
- **Plow tip too sharp:** A sharp tip digs into the groove. Blunt and rounded is correct — it rides the surface.
- **Running out of stamina:** This is the most physically demanding method. Take breaks every 30 seconds. The char dust retains some heat briefly.
- **Wrong wood pair:** Soft hearth, hard plow. Reversed = the plow wears down instead of the hearth.

### Success Indicators — Fire Plow

- Fine, dark char dust accumulating at the groove end
- Smoke rising from the dust pile independently
- Glowing ember visible in the dust pile

## Method 4: Pump Drill

The pump drill uses a flywheel and a crossbar to convert vertical pumping motion into sustained spindle rotation. It is more efficient than a bow drill — but requires more construction.

### Materials

{% materials %}
**Spindle:** straight stick, 50–70 cm long, 2 cm diameter; **Flywheel:** heavy disc — stone with centre hole, thick wooden disc, or clay disc. 10–15 cm diameter, heavy enough to maintain momentum.; **Crossbar:** wooden bar, 30–40 cm long, with a centre hole the spindle passes through freely; **Cord:** 2 lengths of cord, tied from crossbar ends to top of spindle; **Handhold:** same as bow drill — lubricated bearing block; **Hearth board:** same as bow drill
{% end %}

### Technique

### Step 1: Assemble the Pump Drill

{% steps %}
Fit the flywheel onto the spindle, low down (near the bottom); Slide the crossbar onto the spindle above the flywheel — it must move freely up and down; Tie one cord from left end of crossbar to the top of the spindle; Tie the second cord from right end of crossbar to the top of the spindle; The cords should wrap around the spindle shaft so that pushing the crossbar down unwinds them, spinning the spindle; The flywheel momentum carries the spindle through, rewinding the cords in reverse, ready for the next push
{% end %}

### Step 2: Operate the Pump Drill

{% steps %}
Place spindle tip in hearth board seating hole; Hold the handhold on top of the spindle; Push the crossbar down smoothly — the spindle spins as the cords unwind; At the bottom of the stroke, the flywheel continues spinning, rewinding the cords; Push down again — continuous pumping = continuous spinning; Same coal formation and transfer process as bow drill
{% end %}

### Common Mistakes — Pump Drill

- **Flywheel too light:** Insufficient momentum to rewind the cords. Use a heavier flywheel or spin it manually to start.
- **Cords uneven:** If one cord is shorter than the other, the crossbar tilts and the spindle wobbles. Measure carefully.
- **Hearth board not secured:** The pump drill generates significant downward force. Pin the hearth board firmly.
- **Overcomplicating the build:** If you don't have the materials or time, use a bow drill instead. The pump drill is an efficiency upgrade, not a first choice.

### Success Indicators — Pump Drill

- Smooth, wobble-free spindle rotation
- Sustained spin through multiple pump cycles without manual restart
- Consistent smoke and char dust production with less effort than bow drill

## Troubleshooting — All Friction Methods

| Problem | Likely cause | Fix |
|---------|-------------|-----|
| No smoke | Not enough speed or pressure | Increase both. Check wood — is it dry? |
| Smoke but no coal | Not enough sustained effort after smoke | Keep going. Smoke is halfway. Maintain speed + pressure for another 60 seconds. |
| Spindle squeaks | Wood is glazing (polishing smooth instead of producing dust) | Roughen the spindle tip and hearth seat with a knife or rough stone |
| Spindle wobbles | Spindle not round, or seating hole off-centre | Re-carve spindle round. Re-burn seating hole. |
| Cord slips | Cord not grippy enough, or too loose | Increase tension. Add pine resin or beeswax for grip. Paracord inner strands grip well. |
| Handhold friction | Handhold too soft or not lubricated | Use harder material. Lubricate. Too much downward pressure — focus on speed. |
| Coal dust blows away | Windy conditions or clumsy transfer | Shield with your body. Transfer very gently. |
| Dust is pale tan, not dark | Temperature too low | Increase speed. Pale dust = not hot enough. You want dark brown/black. |
| Dust pile forms but no glow | Not enough sustained heat after dust forms | Continue for 30–60 seconds after dust appears. The dust must reach ignition temperature. |

## Practice Advice

**Do not wait for an emergency to try this for the first time.** The bow drill has a steep learning curve. Most people take 5–20 attempts to get their first coal. Practice in controlled conditions:

- Dry weather, sheltered location
- Known good woods (soft hearth, hard spindle)
- Have backup fire-starting methods available
- Practice until you can reliably produce a coal in under 5 minutes

## 🇦🇺 Australian Friction Fire — Woods, Materials & Techniques

> **Australia Note:** This page has been adapted for Australian conditions.

### Best Australian Bow Drill Woods

Australia has excellent friction fire woods. The key is knowing which species work together.

| Hearth board (softer) | Spindle (harder) | Region | Notes |
|----------------------|------------------|--------|-------|
| **Mulga** (*Acacia aneura*) | **Mulga** | Arid central/western AU | Same-species match works well with mulga. A premium Australian combination. |
| **Banksia** (various spp.) | **Wattle** (*Acacia* spp.) | East coast, south-west WA | Banksia is soft enough to serve as hearth. Wattle provides the harder spindle. |
| **Hibiscus** (native, *Hibiscus* spp.) | **Hibiscus** | Northern AU, coastal | Same-species pair. Hibiscus wood is surprisingly good. |
| **Corkwood** (*Hakea* spp.) | **Ironbark** | Eastern AU | Corkwood is very soft — excellent hearth. Ironbark is extremely hard spindle. |
| **Currawang** (*Acacia doratoxylon*) | **Currawang** | Eastern AU | Tough, dense wood — works for both. |
| **Paperbark** (NOT for hearth) | **Bamboo** (dry) | Northern AU, introduced bamboo groves | Paperbark is too soft for a proper hearth, but bamboo from old garden plantings works as spindle. |

**Testing the pair:** Press your thumbnail into the hearth board. If it leaves a visible dent, the wood is soft enough. If the spindle wood dents your thumbnail more easily than the hearth board dents, swap them.

### Hand Drill — Best Australian Material

The **grass tree** (*Xanthorrhoea*) flower spike is the best hand drill material in Australia:

- **Where to find:** Coastal heath, sandstone ranges, open forest (east coast NSW/QLD, south-west WA). Not found in arid interior.
- **Why it works:** The flower spike is straight, pithy on the inside with a hard outer shell. The pith produces excellent char dust. The outer shell provides the structure.
- **Preparation:** Cut a dry flower spike about 60–80 cm long. Remove the outer green scales. The pithy core is your spindle.
- **Hearth board:** Use a flat piece of dry banksia, corkwood, or soft wattle.

### Fire Plow — Australian Woods

| Component | Best wood | Region |
|-----------|-----------|--------|
| **Plow stick** (hard) | Mulga, ironbark, acacia | Various AU |
| **Hearth board** (soft) | Native pine (*Callitris*), kurrajong (*Brachychiton*), corkwood | Eastern AU, central AU (Callitris) |

**Kurrajong** (*Brachychiton populneus*) is an excellent fire plow hearth — soft, fibrous, and produces good char dust. Found across eastern Australia.

**Native pine** (*Callitris* spp.) — cypress pine found across inland NSW, Qld. The wood is soft, resinous, and burns easily. Excellent hearth material.

### Where to Find Materials by Region

| Region | Bow drill pair | Tinder | Notes |
|--------|---------------|--------|-------|
| **Central desert** (Alice Springs, SA, WA inland) | Mulga spindle + mulga hearth | Kangaroo dung, spinifex | Mulga is everywhere. Best friction fire region in AU. |
| **Eastern coast** (Sydney to Cairns) | Wattle spindle + banksia hearth | Paperbark, grass tree | Abundant materials. Grass tree for hand drill. |
| **North Qld / NT tropics** | Hibiscus + hibiscus, or bamboo spindle + softwood hearth | Paperbark (best in AU) | Wet season makes everything damp. Collect materials in dry season and cache them. |
| **South-west WA** | Banksia hearth + wattle spindle | Paperbark, grass tree | Similar to east coast. Abundant banksia in the region. |
| **Temperate south** (VIC, SA, TAS) | Wattle spindle + softwood hearth (willow, poplar) | Stringybark, dry grass | European introduced trees (willow, poplar) also work well as hearth. |

### Traditional Aboriginal Fire-Making Knowledge

Aboriginal Australians have used friction fire for tens of thousands of years. Some techniques to be aware of:

- **Fire saw** — a split stick method using a vine or flexible branch sawed across a notch in a softwood board. Common in northern Australia.
- **Fire drill** — the hand drill method using grass tree flower spike was widely used across eastern Australia.
- **Fire plough** — a groove-and-plow method was used in the central and western deserts using mulga and acacia.
- **Fire thong** — a strip of possum or kangaroo skin was pulled rapidly through a notch in a softwood board. Less common but documented.

**Key traditional insight:** Char dust production is more important than heat. The goal is to create a pile of fine, dark char dust that will hold an ember. Aboriginal fire-makers would stop and check the colour of the dust regularly.
