# Common Edibles — 20 Australian Wild Foods

> **🇦🇺 Australia Note:** This page has been rewritten for Australian conditions. All plants listed are native or naturalised in Australia. Always cross-reference with [Common Toxics](01-immediate/05-food-emergency/common-toxics) to ensure you are not misidentifying a look-alike.

**The safest wild foods are those with few or no toxic look-alikes, are widespread, and require minimal preparation. Learn to identify these 20 before you are in an emergency.**

**Always cross-reference with [Common Toxics](01-immediate/05-food-emergency/common-toxics) to ensure you are not misidentifying a look-alike.**

## Fruits & Berries

### 1. Bush Tomato / Desert Raisin (*Solanum centrale*)

- **Where found:** Arid central Australia (NT, SA, WA) — sandy soils, stony plains
- **Description:** Low perennial shrub (30–50 cm), grey-green leaves, purple flowers. The fruit is a small (1–2 cm) berry that dries to a raisin-like consistency on the bush.
- **Edible parts:** Fruit — dried on the bush (when brown/tan), or fresh (green to yellow — must be fully ripe).
- **How to prepare:** Eat dried fruit as is (sweet, raisin-like). Fresh fruit: remove the outer skin. Can be ground into bush tomato powder (a staple Australian native spice).
- **Key ID features:** Dried fruit remains on the bush in a papery husk. The plant has a distinctive earthy-smoky smell when crushed.
- ⚠️ **Look-alike warning:** Other *Solanum* species in central Australia can be toxic. Only eat the fruit when it is brown and dried on the bush — the green fruit of this species itself is also toxic. This is a plant where **100% positive identification is essential**.

  **CRITICAL IDENTIFICATION:** Several other desert Solanum species ('wild tomatoes') also produce fruit that dries on the bush and is TOXIC. The "dried on the bush" rule alone is NOT sufficient to distinguish edible from toxic species. 

  **Positive identification requires:**
  1. **Flower must be purple with a yellow centre** (all safe desert raisin species have this)
  2. **The dried fruit must be enclosed in a papery calyx** (the green/brown husk that held the flower)
  3. If you did not see the plant in flower, DO NOT eat the fruit

  **Toxic look-alikes:** Solanum quadriloculatum (western nightshade) has greenish-white flowers and NO papery calyx enclosing the fruit. Solanum ellipticum has blue-purple flowers but different leaf shape and fruit that is NOT edible even when dried.

### 2. Quandong / Desert Peach (*Santalum acuminatum*)

- **Where found:** Arid and semi-arid AU — widespread in SA, NSW, Qld, WA. Grows in sandy soils, often near mulga or acacia (it is a root parasite).
- **Description:** Small tree or shrub (2–6 m), narrow grey-green leaves, inconspicuous flowers. Fruit is a bright red (sometimes yellow) drupe, 2–3 cm diameter, with a distinctive rough-skinned stone inside.
- **Edible parts:** Fruit flesh (fresh or dried). Kernel inside the stone (roasted — tastes like macadamia).
- **How to prepare:** Flesh — eat fresh (sweet-tart), or dry for long-term storage. Kernel — crack the hard stone, roast the kernel in coals for 5 minutes.
- **Key ID features:** Bright red fruit, rough-textured stone inside. The tree is often growing near or through an acacia or mulga (hemiparasitic roots).
- **Nutrition:** High in vitamin C (comparable to oranges), vitamin E, and antioxidants.

### 3. Wattle Seed (Selected Safe *Acacia* spp.)

- **Where found:** Safe species listed below are widespread in arid/semi-arid areas.
- **Description:** Trees or shrubs with distinctive yellow or cream ball-shaped flowers. Seed pods are long, narrow, and often coiled or twisted.
- **Edible parts:** Mature seeds from dry seed pods.
- **How to prepare:** Collect dry pods. Shell seeds. Roast seeds lightly (5 minutes on a hot surface or in a pan) — this develops the nutty flavour and reduces any residual toxic compounds. Grind roasted seeds into a high-protein flour.
- **Key ID features:** Yellow ball-shaped flowers 5–15 mm across. Long narrow seed pods (straight to coiled, 5–15 cm). Pods dry and rattle when shaken.
- **Reliably safe species:** *Acacia aneura* (mulga) — narrow grey-green phyllodes, central/western AU. *Acacia victoriae* (elegant wattle) — prickly shrub with cream flower balls, semi-arid AU. *Acacia coriacea* (dogwood) — long curved seed pods, bark like cork, northern AU. *Acacia salicina* (cooba) — weeping willow-like foliage, near watercourses.
- **Nutrition:** ~25% protein, high carbohydrate, good fat content.

{% warning() %}
**WATTLE SEED DANGER:** Australia has over 1,000 Acacia species. Some are toxic. Acacia georginae (Georgina gidgee, found in QLD/SA/NT Channel Country) contains fluoroacetate (1080 poison) — the same toxin in pest baits. It has killed people and livestock. Other species contain cyanogenic glycosides. Do NOT eat wattle seeds unless you can POSITIVELY identify the species. Positive identification requires flowers AND seed pods AND a reliable field guide. Roasting reduces but does not eliminate all toxins.
{% end %}

### 4. Pigface / Karkalla (*Carpobrotus* spp.)

- **Where found:** Coastal and inland AU — sand dunes, rocky shores, salt flats, inland claypans. Widespread in all states.
- **Description:** Succulent ground-covering plant with fleshy, triangular-shaped leaves. Large pink/purple daisy-like flowers. Fruit is a fleshy fig-like structure.
- **Edible parts:** Leaves (succulent, salty) and fruit (dried on the plant — tastes like salty fig).
- **How to prepare:** Picked fresh leaves — eat raw (crunchy, salty, thirst-quenching). Fruit — pick when dried on the bush. The seeds inside are edible.
- **Key ID features:** Triangular fleshy leaves in cross-section. Pink/purple flowers. Grows flat along the ground in coastal and salt-affected areas.
- **Deadly look-alikes:** None — but do not confuse with coastal spurge (*Euphorbia paralias*), which has milky sap (pigface sap is clear). Milky sap = toxic.

### 5. Lilly Pilly / Riberry (*Syzygium* spp.)

- **Where found:** Rainforest and coastal scrub — east coast Qld, NSW, NT, north Qld, south-west WA
- **Description:** Shrubs or small trees with glossy, opposite leaves. Fruit is a fleshy berry, typically pink-mauve to red, containing a single large seed.
- **Edible parts:** Ripe fruit — tart, refreshing. Native varieties include *S. smithii*, *S. paniculatum* (magenta lilly pilly), *S. leuhmannii* (small-leaved lilly pilly, the "riberry").
- **How to prepare:** Eat raw. Good for jam if you have a container.
- **Key ID features:** Glossy opposite leaves, pink/red fruit with a single large seed. Check the leaf — crushed leaves smell like eucalyptus or spice (varies by species).
- **Deadly look-alikes:** None — all Syzygium fruits are edible (some are just less palatable).

### 6. Native Raspberry (*Rubus parvifolius*, *Rubus hillii*, *Rubus rosifolius*)

- **Where found:** East coast AU (QLD, NSW, VIC, TAS) — forest edges, cleared areas, along creeks
- **Description:** Prickly scrambling shrub. Fruit is an aggregate berry, red when ripe.
- **Edible parts:** Ripe berries.
- **How to prepare:** Eat raw.
- **Key ID features:** Prickly canes, compound leaves (3–5 leaflets), red aggregate fruit. Similar to European blackberry but generally smaller and sweeter.
- **Deadly look-alikes:** None — all compound-berry Rubus species in Australia are edible.

### 7. Midyim Berry (*Austromyrtus dulcis*)

- **Where found:** Sandy coastal heath — Qld, NSW (from Fraser Island to Sydney)
- **Description:** Small shrub (30–100 cm) with small oval leaves and white flowers. Fruit is a small (5–8 mm) berry, spotted when young, turning white when ripe.
- **Edible parts:** Ripe berries — sweet with a spicy, minty aftertaste.
- **How to prepare:** Eat fresh.
- **Key ID features:** Tiny white berries with a faint purple spot on each fruit when ripe. Grows in sandy coastal heath.

### 8. Davidson's Plum (*Davidsonia* spp.)

- **Where found:** Rainforest — north Qld, northern NSW
- **Description:** Small tree with large, fern-like leaves. Fruit is a large (3–5 cm) deep purple/blue plum-like fruit with a furry skin, hanging in clusters from the trunk (cauliflorous).
- **Edible parts:** Sour plum flesh — very tart.
- **How to prepare:** Not great raw for most people (extremely sour). Best stewed, or as a flavouring. High in anthocyanins (antioxidants).
- **Key ID features:** Fruit hanging from the trunk, not the branches. Furry purple skin. Very sour taste.

### 9. Finger Lime (*Citrus australasica*)

- **Where found:** Subtropical rainforest — Qld, NSW (border ranges)
- **Description:** Small tree or shrub with small thorns. Fruit is a long (5–10 cm) cylindrical citrus, green to yellow to purple-black depending on ripeness.
- **Edible parts:** The inside pulp — tiny juice vesicles that pop in the mouth (the "citrus caviar").
- **How to prepare:** Cut in half, squeeze out the vesicles. Eat raw.
- **Key ID features:** Long finger-shaped fruit. Citrus scent when cut. Small thorns on branches.

### 10. Kakadu Plum / Gubinge (*Terminalia ferdinandiana*)

- **Where found:** Top End (NT, Kimberley WA) — woodlands, open forest
- **Description:** Medium tree (5–10 m) with broad leaves. Fruit is a small (1–2 cm), greenish-yellow drupe with a single seed.
- **Edible parts:** Fruit flesh (extremely tart) and seed kernel.
- **How to prepare:** Eat raw (bitter, sour — but one fruit has the highest known concentration of vitamin C of any food). Dried into powder. Kernel can be roasted like a nut.
- **Key ID features:** High tannin content — the fruit dries your mouth like strong tea. Grows in the far north.
- **Nutrition:** Highest natural vitamin C content of any food on Earth (~3,000 mg per 100 g — 60× more than orange).

## Greens & Vegetables

### 11. Warrigal Greens / Native Spinach (*Tetragonia tetragonioides*)

- **Where found:** Coastal AU (all states), inland in salt-affected areas. Sandy soils, dunes, salt marshes.
- **Description:** Low-growing, sprawling plant with fleshy, diamond-shaped leaves. Small yellow flowers. The fruit is a hard, horned capsule.
- **Edible parts:** Young leaves and tips.
- **How to prepare:** **Must be cooked.** Contains oxalates (like true spinach). Boil for 3–5 minutes, discard water. Eat as cooked greens.
- **Key ID features:** Fleshy diamond-shaped leaves. Small yellow flowers. Fleshy, sprawling growth habit.
- **Deadly look-alikes:** None — but do not confuse with coastal spurge (milky sap test applies).

### 12. Saltbush (Various *Atriplex* spp., *Rhagodia* spp.)

- **Where found:** Arid and semi-arid AU — salt pans, claypans, inland plains. Also coastal salt marsh.
- **Description:** Hardy silvery-grey shrubs with small fleshy leaves. Grows in salty or alkaline soils.
- **Edible parts:** Leaves — salty, fleshy.
- **How to prepare:** Eat raw (the salt content makes them taste like salted greens) or lightly boiled. Traditional Aboriginal ingredient (bush salt).
- **Key ID features:** Silvery-grey leaves, grows in salty soil. Leaves are small, fleshy.

### 13. Samphire / Glasswort (*Sarcocornia* spp., *Tecticornia* spp., *Salicornia* spp.)

- **Where found:** Coastal salt marshes, inland salt lakes. All AU states.
- **Description:** Jointed, fleshy, bright green to red succulent growing in dense patches in salt-affected areas.
- **Edible parts:** Young green stems — salty, crunchy.
- **How to prepare:** Eat raw or blanch in boiling water for 2 minutes.
- **Key ID features:** Jointed succulent stems, grows in salt marsh. Tastes like salted asparagus.
- **Deadly look-alikes:** None — but do not harvest from polluted areas (salt marshes near industrial sites can absorb heavy metals).

### 14. Sea Celery / Native Celery (*Apium prostratum*)

- **Where found:** Coastal AU (all states) — rocky headlands, sand dunes, salt marsh edges
- **Description:** Low-growing perennial with small, finely divided leaves, similar in appearance to cultivated celery but smaller.
- **Edible parts:** Young leaves and stems.
- **How to prepare:** Eat raw (strong celery flavour) or lightly cooked.
- **Key ID features:** Strong celery smell when crushed. Grows only near the coast.
- ⚠️ **Look-alike warning:** Resembles hemlock (*Conium maculatum*) —**dangerous**. Hemlock has purple-spotted smooth stems and smells like mouse urine (not celery). Sea celery has ridged green stems and smells exactly like celery. If it does not smell like celery, do NOT eat it.

### 15. Water Ribbons (*Triglochin* spp.)

- **Where found:** Wetlands, creeks, shallow water — all AU states
- **Description:** Aquatic plant with long, ribbon-like leaves growing from a central crown. Found in or near fresh water.
- **Edible parts:** Young leaves, root tubers, seeds.
- **How to prepare:** Young leaves — eat raw or cooked. Root tubers — roast or boil.
- **Key ID features:** Grows in water. Long flat ribbon leaves. The seed head is a spike of small green capsules.

### 16. Cumbungi / Bulrush (*Typha* spp.)

- **Where found:** Wetlands, dams, creeks — all AU states and territories
- **Description:** Tall (1–3 m) reed growing in shallow water. Distinctive brown sausage-shaped flower head. Flat strap-like leaves.
- **Edible parts:** Roots (rhizomes — starchy, year-round), young shoots (peel outer layers, eat like asparagus), green flower spikes (boil and eat like corn), pollen (high-protein flour).
- **How to prepare:** Rhizomes — peel, crush in water, let starch settle = cumbungi flour. Or roast directly. Young shoots — peel, eat raw or boiled. Pollen — collect from the yellow flower heads, use as flour.
- **Key ID features:** Brown sausage-shaped seed head is unmistakable. Grows in standing water.
- ⚠️ **Look-alike warning:** Do NOT confuse with wild iris (toxic). Iris has a fan of flat leaves and showy flowers. Cattail has a distinctive seed head. If it has a brown seed head, it's safe. If it has a showy flower, it's iris (toxic).

## Nuts & Seeds

### 17. Macadamia (Wild) (*Macadamia integrifolia*, *Macadamia tetraphylla*)

- **Where found:** Subtropical rainforest — Qld, northern NSW (within ~300 km of the coast, from about Maryborough to the Richmond River)
- **Description:** Medium evergreen tree (5–15 m) with whorled, toothed leaves. Fruit is a hard, round nut in a green fleshy husk that splits open when ripe.
- **Edible parts:** Nut kernel.
- **How to prepare:** Crack the extremely hard shell (use two rocks or a sturdy tool). Eat raw or roast for 10 minutes in coals.
- **Key ID features:** Whorled leaves with spine-toothed edges. Very hard round shell. Green husk splits when ripe.
- **Nutrition:** ~75% fat — one of the highest fat contents of any nut. Excellent survival food (high energy density).
- ⚠️ **Warning:** Other *Macadamia* species (e.g., *M. ternifolia*) have toxic seeds. Only *M. integrifolia* and *M. tetraphylla* are edible. The toxic species have smaller, differently shaped nuts. **100% positive identification is essential.**

### 18. Bunya Nut (*Araucaria bidwillii*)

- **Where found:** Qld — natural range is Bunya Mountains and surrounding areas. Planted widely in parks and gardens across AU.
- **Description:** Very large tree (30–45 m) with distinctive symmetrical dome shape. Enormous cones (football-sized, up to 10 kg) fall whole.
- **Edible parts:** Seeds inside the cone — large, starchy, chestnut-like.
- **How to prepare:** Boil or roast seeds (like chestnuts). Eat raw but cooking improves digestibility.
- **Key ID features:** Massive cone (unmistakable). Cones fall whole — one cone contains up to 100 seeds.
- ⚠️ **Warning:** Cones falling from height can kill. Do not camp under bunya trees during fruiting (Jan–Mar).

## Herbs & Flavouring

### 19. Native Mint / River Mint (*Mentha australis*, *Mentha satureioides*)

- **Where found:** Moist areas, creek banks, swamps — all AU states (especially eastern AU)
- **Description:** Low-growing perennial with the classic mint appearance — square stems, opposite leaves, purple flowers in whorls.
- **Edible parts:** Leaves.
- **How to prepare:** Steep fresh leaves in hot water for mint tea. Add to food as flavouring.
- **Key ID features:** Square stem (mint family). Strong mint smell when crushed. Grows near water.

### 20. Lemon Myrtle (*Backhousia citriodora*)

- **Where found:** Subtropical rainforest — Qld, northern NSW
- **Description:** Tree (5–10 m) with strongly lemon-scented leaves when crushed. Creamy white fluffy flower clusters in summer.
- **Edible parts:** Leaves — steep for tea.
- **How to prepare:** Steep fresh or dried leaves in hot water for 5 minutes. One of the best bush teas in Australia.
- **Key ID features:** Overwhelming lemon scent when leaf is crushed. White fluffy flowers.
- **Nutrition:** Extremely high in citral (antimicrobial — can help preserve water).

## Regional Quick Reference

### Coastal / Salt Affected
Pigface, Samphire, Sea Celery, Warrigal Greens, Saltbush, Kakadu Plum

### Arid / Central Desert
Bush Tomato, Quandong, Wattle Seed, Saltbush, Pigface (inland variety)

### Rainforest (East Coast)
Lilly Pilly, Davidson's Plum, Finger Lime, Macadamia (wild), Bunya Nut, Lemon Myrtle, Native Raspberry

### Tropical North
Kakadu Plum, Bush Tomato (also central), Native Raspberry, Lilly Pilly, Green Plum (*Buchanania obovata*), Bush Coconut (*Terminalia* spp.)

### Temperate South
Native Mint, Warrigal Greens, Samphire, Sea Celery, Native Raspberry, Midyim Berry (south to Sydney)

### Alpine
Bogong Moths (not a plant, but an important seasonal food), Native Mint, Cumbungi
