# Water — Finding & Purifying

Water is your second priority after life-threatening injuries are controlled. The human body is roughly 60% water. You lose 2–3 litres per day through breathing, sweating, and urination — more in hot or dry conditions common across Australia, and .

---

## The Water Timeline

| Time without water | Effects |
|--------------------|--------|
| 6–12 hours | Thirst, dry mouth, reduced urine output |
| 12–24 hours | Fatigue, headache, dizziness, dark urine |
| 24–48 hours | Confusion, rapid heart rate, no urine, inability to sweat |
| 48–72 hours | Organ failure, loss of consciousness |
| 72+ hours | Death |

**In a tropical Australian, n, or southern summer (35°C+), this timeline compresses dramatically.** At 40°C, death from dehydration can occur in under 24 hours.

---

## Water Requirements

| Condition | Minimum daily water |
|-----------|---------------------|
| Rest, cool climate | 2 L per person |
| Rest, hot climate (30°C+) | 3–4 L per person |
| Active work, hot climate | 5–6 L per person |
| Breastfeeding | Add 1 L to base |

Children need proportionally more water per kilogram of body weight than adults.

---

## Water Priorities

1. **Find** — Water exists in every environment; you just need to know where to look
2. **Collect** — Container improvisation, rain catchment
3. **Purify** — All found water must be treated before drinking
4. **Store** — Build reserves for days when finding fails
5. **Conserve** — Reduce your body's water loss

## Water Hierarchy — What to Drink First

In order of safety:

1. **Rainwater** — Safest natural source if collected cleanly
2. **Flowing surface water** (streams, rivers) — treat before drinking
3. **Groundwater** (springs, seeps) — often safer than surface water, still treat
4. **Standing water** (lakes, ponds) — highest risk, must treat thoroughly
5. **Collected dew/transpiration** — low volume, relatively safe
6. **Urine** — Do not drink. Despite persistent myths, urine is a waste product. Drinking it concentrates toxins, accelerates dehydration, and provides no net water gain. The only exception is using it in a solar still where only the distilled water is consumed.

---

## Water Conservation — Reduce Your Loss

Before you find water, reduce what you lose:

- **Stay in shade** during the hottest hours (10:00–15:00)
- **Do not work during peak heat** — rest, then work at dawn and dusk
- **Cover skin** — exposed skin loses water through sweat
- **Breathe through your nose** — mouth breathing increases water loss
- **Do not eat if water is scarce** — digestion requires water
- **Do not talk unnecessarily** — talking loses moisture
- **Do not drink alcohol or caffeine** — both are diuretics
- **Cool yourself externally** — damp cloth on neck and wrists uses less body water than sweating

## 🇦🇺 Australian Water Sources & Hazards

> **Australia Note:** This page has been adapted for Australian conditions. See also [Water & Food in the Outback](01-immediate/09-outback/water-food-outback) for desert-specific strategies.

### Australian Water Sources — What to Look For

| Source | Region | How to find it | Reliability |
|--------|--------|----------------|-------------|
| **Billabongs** | Northern AU (wet season) | Oxbow lakes along river courses | Seasonal — dry up in the dry |
| **Rock holes (gnamma holes)** | Granite outcrops across AU | Weather-worn depressions in granite. Found on rocky hills, inselbergs. | Unreliable — hold water days to weeks after rain |
| **Dry creek beds** | Arid & semi-arid AU | Dig at **outside bends** — water scours deeper on the outside of curves | Good — shallow groundwater persists for weeks after rain stops |
| **Artesian bores** | Great Artesian Basin (Qld, NSW, SA, NT) | Man-made bores tapping the GAB. Many are marked on maps. | Highly reliable — flowing water year-round |
| **Tropical rainforest streams** | Wet tropics (Qld, NT) | Perennial streams in dense forest | Excellent in wet season. Dries to trickles in dry. |
| **Morning dew** | Arid zone, coastal heath | Wipe metal surfaces (vehicle, wreckage) at dawn | Small — 200-500 mL per vehicle |
| **Claypans** | Desert AU (SA, WA, NT) | Flat depressions after rain | Very brief — water lasts hours to days |
| **Kangaroo wells** | Dry creek beds (all AU) | Dig out and deepen existing animal scrapes | Good indicator of shallow groundwater |

### Australian Water Hazards — What Can Kill You

| Hazard | Where | What to Do |
|--------|-------|------------|
| **Saltwater crocodiles** | Northern waterways (north of Rockhampton) | Stay 5 m+ from water. Do NOT wade, swim, or collect water by wading. Crocodiles hunt at night. |
| **Freshwater crocodiles** | Northern inland rivers | Generally harmless but bite if provoked. Still — do not swim in their water. |
| **Blue-green algae** | Stagnant water (Murray-Darling, farm dams) | Toxic to liver and nerves. Do NOT drink. Boiling does not remove toxins — only distillation or advanced filtration. |
| **Bore water mineral content** | Great Artesian Basin, inland basins | High in dissolved solids, may be salty or metallic. Safe after boiling but tastes unpleasant. Let settle before drinking. |
| **Cattle-contaminated waterholes** | Pastoral zones (NSW, Qld, NT) | Giardia, Cryptosporidium, E. coli from cattle faeces. Must be boiled or chemically treated. |
| **Station runoff** | After rain near mining areas | Heavy metals, acid drainage. Avoid if possible. |

### Water Availability by Australian Region

| Region | Dry season | Wet season | Key strategy |
|--------|------------|------------|--------------|
| **Arid zone (Simpson, Nullarbor, Gibson)** | Essentially no surface water. Deep groundwater. | Brief flash floods — collect from claypans, rock holes. | Carry water. Dig at dry creek bends. Collect morning dew. |
| **Tropical north (Darwin, Kimberley, Cape York)** | Creeks dry up. Permanent waterholes persist. | Abundant surface water but contaminated by runoff. Croc risk everywhere. | Treat all water. Collect rain. Avoid water's edge at night. |
| **Temperate south (VIC, TAS, southern NSW)** | Reliable creeks and rivers. | Winter/spring reliable. | Standard treatment sufficient. Bushfire ash contamination risk in summer. |
| **Alpine (Snowy Mountains, TAS highlands)** | Snowmelt. Creeks abundant. | Summer storms. | Cold — risk of hypothermia from wet clothes while collecting. |
