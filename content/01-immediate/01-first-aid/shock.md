# Shock — Recognition & Management

Shock is not "feeling startled." It is a life-threatening condition where the circulatory system fails to deliver enough oxygen to vital organs. It can develop from bleeding, burns, dehydration, infection, allergic reaction, or spinal injury. **Shock kills** — and it can develop hours after the initial injury.

---

## Types of Shock

| Type | Cause | Common in survival scenarios |
|------|-------|------------------------------|
| **Hypovolemic** | Blood or fluid loss | Bleeding, burns, severe dehydration, vomiting/diarrhoea |
| **Cardiogenic** | Heart fails to pump | Heart attack, chest trauma |
| **Distributive (Septic)** | Severe infection | Infected wounds, peritonitis |
| **Distributive (Anaphylactic)** | Severe allergic reaction | Insect stings, snake bites, plant allergies |
| **Neurogenic** | Spinal cord injury | Falls, crush injuries |

The most common in survival settings is **hypovolemic shock** from blood loss and dehydration. That is this page's focus.

---

## Recognition — The Shock Spiral

Shock progresses through stages. Catch it early.

### Early (Compensated) Shock

The body is compensating — blood pressure is still normal-ish:

- **Heart rate:** Elevated (> 100 bpm in adults)
- **Respiratory rate:** Elevated, shallow
- **Skin:** Pale, cool, clammy
- **Mental state:** Anxious, restless, "something's wrong"
- **Capillary refill:** Press a fingernail — it takes > 2 seconds to turn pink again
- **Thirst:** Often intense

### Late (Decompensated) Shock

The body is losing the fight — blood pressure is falling:

- **Heart rate:** Very rapid, weak or thready
- **Respiratory rate:** Fast, shallow, may become irregular
- **Skin:** Cold, mottled, grey-blue (cyanosis)
- **Mental state:** Confused, drowsy, unresponsive
- **Capillary refill:** > 3 seconds or absent
- **Blood pressure:** Dangerously low (you won't be able to measure this in the field, but the other signs are enough)

**If you see late-stage shock, death may occur within minutes to hours without intervention.**

## Treatment — The Shock Position

### If Breathing is Normal and No Spinal Injury

{% steps() %}
Lay the person flat on their back; Elevate the legs 20–30 cm (use a pack, log, or rolled clothing); Keep the head flat or slightly lowered — do not use a pillow; Cover with blankets, emergency blanket, or clothing to prevent heat loss; Place insulation underneath the person — ground conducts heat 25 times faster than air; Loosen tight clothing around neck, chest, and waist
{% end %}

```
         Head flat
          _____
    _____/     \_____
   /                 \
  |                   |  <-- Torso flat
   \_____       _____/
         \     /
          |   |  <-- Legs elevated 20–30 cm
          |   |
         _|   |_
        |       |
```

**Why this position:** Elevating the legs returns blood from the lower body to the heart and brain (autotransfusion — roughly 500 mL of blood volume). Keeping the head flat maintains cerebral blood flow.

### If Breathing Difficulty

Sit the person upright or semi-reclining. Do not elevate the legs — this can make breathing harder.

### If Spinal Injury Suspected

Keep the person flat. Do not elevate legs if it causes movement or pain. Immobilise the spine. → [Fractures](01-immediate/01-first-aid/fractures)

### If Vomiting or Decreased Consciousness

Place in the **recovery position** (on their side) to prevent choking. → [Recovery Position](01-immediate/01-first-aid/cpr)

## Keep Them Warm — The Cocoon Principle

Shock victims cannot regulate their temperature. Hypothermia worsens shock; shock worsens hypothermia. This feedback loop kills.

{% steps() %}
Place insulation **under** the person — ground conduction is the biggest heat loss route. Use a sleeping mat, cardboard, leaves, pine needles, dry grass; Wrap the person in an emergency blanket (reflective side inward), sleeping bag, or multiple layers of clothing; Cover the head — 30–40% of heat loss is from the head; Build a fire nearby if conditions allow; Warm rocks wrapped in cloth placed near the armpits and groin (not directly on skin — burns risk); If conscious, give warm fluids. Not hot — warm. Small sips
{% end %}

---

## Do NOT

- **Do not give alcohol** — it dilates blood vessels and worsens shock.
- **Do not give caffeine** — it is a diuretic and may increase heart strain.
- **Do not let the person smoke** — nicotine constricts blood vessels.
- **Do not use direct heat (hot rocks, water bottles on skin)** — burn risk on unresponsive or confused patients.
- **Do not leave the person alone** — shock victims need monitoring and reassurance.

---

## Psychological First Aid

Shock has a psychological component. Fear and panic accelerate physiological deterioration.

- **Stay calm.** Your voice and demeanour matter. Speak slowly, clearly, reassuringly.
- **Explain what you're doing.** "I'm going to elevate your legs now. It will help your blood circulate."
- **Use their name.** Personal connection anchors them.
- **Give them a job.** "Squeeze my hand if you feel cold." Simple tasks keep the mind engaged.
- **Do not lie.** If they ask if they're going to die: "I'm doing everything I can to help you. Stay with me."
- **Touch** — holding a hand can slow heart rate and reduce panic. Only if culturally appropriate and welcome.

---

## Monitoring — What to Track

If you have paper, record:

| Time | Heart rate | Breathing rate | Mental state | Skin colour/temp | Fluids given |
|------|-----------|----------------|-------------|------------------|-------------|
| 14:00 | 110 | 24 | Anxious | Pale, cool | 100 mL water |

Check every 15 minutes. If things are getting worse (heart rate rising, mental state deteriorating), re-evaluate — you may have missed a bleeding source or the underlying condition is worsening.

---

## Special Considerations

### Children

- Children compensate longer, then crash faster. A child who suddenly "settles down" may be decompensating.
- Normal heart rates are higher in children: 80–120 for ages 1–5, 70–110 for ages 6–12.
- Keep the child with a parent or carer if possible — separation worsens psychological shock.
- Warm them more aggressively — children lose heat faster due to higher surface-area-to-volume ratio.

### Elderly

- Elderly patients may not mount a strong heart-rate response. Normal pulse does not rule out shock.
- Medications (beta blockers) can mask tachycardia.
- Be conservative with leg elevation if cardiac history is unknown.
- Elderly are more vulnerable to hypothermia — insulate thoroughly.

---

## When Shock Resolves

Signs of improvement:

- Heart rate decreasing toward normal
- Skin warming, colour returning
- Mental clarity returning
- Urine output (pale yellow)

**Do not let a recovering shock patient stand up suddenly.** Orthostatic hypotension (blood pressure drop on standing) can cause fainting and re-injury. Have them sit for 5 minutes before attempting to stand.
