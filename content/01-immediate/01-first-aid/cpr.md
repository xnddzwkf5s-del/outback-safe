# CPR — Basic Life Support

Cardiopulmonary resuscitation (CPR) is the bridge between clinical death and definitive care. In a survival scenario without access to defibrillation or advanced life support, the odds of successful resuscitation are lower — but not zero. This page covers what to do.

---

## The Chain of Survival (Simplified)

```
Early Recognition → Early CPR → Early Defibrillation → Advanced Care
```

In the field without a defibrillator, the chain ends at CPR. But CPR alone can maintain enough circulation to buy time — sometimes enough for the heart to restart spontaneously, especially in drowning or lightning strike victims.

---

## Step 1: Assess the Scene and the Person

{% steps() %}
Check that the scene is safe for you to approach — no ongoing electrical hazard, fire, unstable structure; Tap the person's shoulders firmly and shout: "Are you okay? Can you hear me?"; If no response, shout for help — get someone else to assist if possible; Check for breathing: Look for chest rise, listen for breath sounds, feel for airflow on your cheek. Do this for no more than 10 seconds
{% end %}

**Is the person breathing normally?**

- **Yes, breathing normally:** Place in recovery position (see below). Monitor continuously.
- **No breathing, or only gasping (agonal breathing):** Begin CPR immediately.
- **Gasping is not breathing.** Occasional, irregular, snoring or gurgling breaths (agonal respiration) are a sign of cardiac arrest, not life. Begin CPR.

---

## Step 2: Compression-Only CPR (For Untrained Rescuers)

If you are not trained in rescue breathing — or if you are alone and the person is not a drowning or child victim — **compression-only CPR** is the recommended approach. It is nearly as effective as conventional CPR for adult cardiac arrest and avoids the risk of disease transmission from mouth-to-mouth contact.

{% steps() %}
Kneel beside the person's chest; Place the heel of one hand in the centre of the chest on the lower half of the breastbone (sternum); Place your other hand on top and interlock fingers; Position your shoulders directly above your hands with arms straight; Push hard and fast — compress the chest at least 5 cm (one-third of chest depth); Allow full chest recoil between compressions — do not lean on the chest; Compression rate: 100–120 per minute. Count aloud: "1-and-2-and-3-and-4..." or push to the beat of "Stayin' Alive" by the Bee Gees; Continue without interruption until: the person shows signs of life, you are physically exhausted, or the situation becomes unsafe
{% end %}

### Compression Depth Guide

| Patient | Depth |
|---------|-------|
| Adult | At least 5 cm |
| Child (1–12) | About 5 cm (one-third chest depth) |
| Infant (< 1) | About 4 cm (two fingers, one-third chest depth) |

## Step 3: Conventional CPR with Rescue Breaths (If Trained)

If you are trained and willing to provide rescue breaths:

{% steps() %}
Perform 30 chest compressions; Open the airway: tilt the head back with one hand on the forehead, lift the chin with two fingers of the other hand (head-tilt/chin-lift); Pinch the nose closed; Take a normal breath and seal your mouth over the person's mouth; Blow steadily for 1 second — watch for chest rise; Give a second breath (each breath 1 second); Resume compressions immediately; Continue 30 compressions to 2 breaths
{% end %}

```
   Head-tilt / Chin-lift

   Back of head
        |
   []===|   <-- Hand on forehead tilting head back
    \   |
     \  |
      \ |   <-- Fingers lifting chin
       \|
        O   <-- Mouth open, airway straightened
```

**If the chest does not rise:**
1. Re-tilt the head and try again
2. Check for visible obstruction in the mouth (finger sweep only if you can see the object)
3. If breaths still don't go in, continue compressions — chest compressions can dislodge obstructions

### CPR for Drowning

Drowning victims need rescue breaths — the primary problem is lack of oxygen, not cardiac arrest.

{% steps() %}
Give 5 initial rescue breaths before starting compressions (drowning is a respiratory arrest first); Then continue 30:2 ratio as normal; If you are alone, perform 1 minute of CPR before leaving to get help
{% end %}

## Recovery Position

Use when the person is breathing normally but unconscious (not in cardiac arrest). This position keeps the airway open and allows fluids to drain from the mouth.

{% steps() %}
Kneel beside the person; Remove glasses and bulky items from pockets; Straighten the legs; Place the arm nearest to you at a right angle to the body, elbow bent, palm up; Bring the far arm across the chest and hold the back of their hand against the cheek nearest to you — keep holding it; With your other hand, grasp the far leg just above the knee and pull it up, keeping the foot on the ground; Pull on the far leg to roll the person toward you onto their side; Adjust the upper leg so both hip and knee are at right angles; Tilt the head back slightly to keep the airway open; Check breathing every 2 minutes
{% end %}

## When to Stop CPR

CPR is physically exhausting. In a field setting without a defibrillator:

- **Stop if the person shows signs of life** — coughing, opening eyes, breathing normally, purposeful movement.
- **Stop if you are too exhausted to continue effectively.** Ineffective compressions help no one.
- **Stop if the scene becomes unsafe.**

### How Long to Continue CPR

Continue CPR until:
- The person shows signs of life (breathing, coughing, movement)
- Emergency services arrive and take over
- You are physically unable to continue (exhaustion — swap with another person if possible)
- A defibrillator (AED) advises no shock and the person remains unresponsive (follow AED prompts)

**Do NOT stop CPR after a fixed time limit.** The "30 minute" rule is outdated. Prolonged CPR has resulted in neurologically intact survival in cases of:

- **Hypothermia** (body temperature below 32°C) — continue until rewarmed
- **Drowning in cold water** — continue for at least 1 hour
- **Snake bite/toxin-related arrest** — antivenom can reverse the cause. Continue until help arrives
- **Lightning strike** — the heart may restart spontaneously once the electrical disruption resolves

The ONLY time to consider stopping is when ALL of these apply:
- You are alone and physically exhausted beyond ability to continue
- No help is available or coming
- The person has been unresponsive for an extended period (not hypothermic/drowning/toxin)
- AND there are no signs of life for more than 20 minutes of continuous CPR

## Choking — Conscious Adult/Child

If someone is choking but still conscious and coughing:

- **Encourage coughing** — do not intervene if they are coughing effectively
- **If coughing weakens or stops:** Deliver up to 5 back blows between the shoulder blades with the heel of your hand
- **If still choking:** Perform abdominal thrusts (Heimlich manoeuvre) — stand behind, place fist above navel, grasp with other hand, pull sharply inward and upward up to 5 times
- **Alternate 5 back blows and 5 abdominal thrusts** until obstruction clears or the person becomes unconscious

**If the person becomes unconscious:**
- Lower them to the ground
- Begin CPR immediately — chest compressions can dislodge the obstruction
- Check the mouth before giving rescue breaths

### Choking — Infant (< 1 year)

{% steps() %}
Sit or kneel; Lay the infant face-down along your forearm, head lower than chest, supporting the head; Give up to 5 back blows between the shoulder blades with the heel of your hand; Turn the infant face-up along your other forearm; Give up to 5 chest thrusts using two fingers on the lower half of the breastbone (same position as infant CPR); Alternate back blows and chest thrusts; If the infant becomes unconscious, begin infant CPR
{% end %}

## After Successful Resuscitation

If the person regains consciousness and normal breathing:

1. Place in recovery position — they may vomit or lose consciousness again
2. Keep them warm — cardiac arrest survivors are at risk of hypothermia
3. Monitor breathing and pulse continuously
4. Do not give food or drink until fully alert
5. Assess for injuries — CPR often fractures ribs, especially in elderly patients. This is survivable and was necessary
6. Seek advanced medical care as soon as possible — survivors of out-of-hospital cardiac arrest need hospital evaluation

---

## Psychological Note

Performing CPR is intense. If the person does not survive, understand:

- Out-of-hospital cardiac arrest without a defibrillator has a very low survival rate — typically under 10%
- **You gave them their only chance.** Doing something is always better than doing nothing
- Rib fractures are expected and survivable — they mean you compressed deeply enough
- Talk about the experience with someone afterward. Do not bottle it up
