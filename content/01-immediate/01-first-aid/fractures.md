# Fractures — Splinting & Immobilisation

A broken bone, properly managed, is painful but survivable. Improperly managed, it can sever arteries, cause permanent disability, or kill through shock and infection.

---

## Recognising a Fracture

| Sign | Closed fracture | Open (compound) fracture |
|------|----------------|-------------------------|
| Pain | Severe, localised | Severe, localised |
| Swelling | Present | Present |
| Deformity | Possible | Likely |
| Bone visible | No | Yes — bone protrudes through skin |
| Bleeding | Internal only | External, potentially severe |

**Open fractures are life-threatening.** The exposed bone and tissue create a direct path for infection. The bleeding can be severe. Treat the bleeding first, then stabilise the bone.

---

## General Principles of Splinting

1. **Immobilise the joint above and below** the fracture site.
2. **Check circulation before and after** splinting: pulse, sensation, movement (PSM) in the extremity beyond the fracture.
3. **Pad the splint** where it contacts bony prominences (ankles, wrists, elbows).
4. **Do not attempt to straighten** a deformed limb — splint it in the position found, unless there is no pulse below the break. No pulse = emergency reduction attempt (very carefully, and only once).
5. **Splint before moving** the patient unless they are in immediate danger.

---

## Splinting Materials

{% materials() %}
Rigid splint material (sticks, tent poles, trekking poles, rolled magazines, cardboard, SAM splint if in kit); Padding (clothing, moss, grass, foam, towels); Binding material (strips of cloth, bandages, belts, paracord, tape); Sling material (triangular bandage, large cloth, shirt)
{% end %}

### Improvised Splints

Any rigid object long enough to span the joint above and below the fracture. In the bush:

- **Sticks or branches** — straight, thumb-thick, padded with moss or grass
- **Bamboo** (abundant in SEA and southern China) — split lengthwise, excellent rigidity
- **Rolled bark** from paperbark trees (Melaleuca, common in northern Australia)
- **Trekking poles** — collapse to appropriate length
- **Sleeping pad** — roll tightly around the limb, secure with cord

## Splinting by Body Region

### Forearm/Wrist

```
    [Fracture site]
         |
    ====|====  <-- Splint from elbow to beyond fingertips
         |
```

{% steps() %}
Pad the splint where it contacts the elbow and wrist; Place the padded splint under the forearm from elbow to fingertips; Secure with bindings above the fracture, below the fracture, and above the elbow; Create a sling to support the forearm against the chest; Check: can the person feel their fingers? Is the hand warm?
{% end %}

### Upper Arm (Humerus)

{% steps() %}
Pad a rigid splint from shoulder to elbow; Place the splint on the outside of the arm; Secure at the shoulder, above the fracture, and below the fracture; Sling the arm against the chest; Bind the sling to the torso with a second wrap around the chest (not too tight — must allow breathing)
{% end %}

### Lower Leg (Tibia/Fibula)

```
       [Fracture site]
            |
    ========|========  <-- Splint from above knee to below ankle
            |
```

{% steps() %}
Use two splints — one on each side of the leg; Splints must extend from above the knee to below the ankle; Pad generously at knee, ankle, and fracture site; Secure at thigh, above fracture, below fracture, and ankle; Tie the legs together for additional stability (the uninjured leg serves as a natural splint)
{% end %}

### Thigh (Femur)

A femur fracture is **serious** — the thigh muscles can spasm and cause the bone ends to override, damaging the femoral artery. Massive internal bleeding is possible.

{% steps() %}
Apply manual traction: one person holds the ankle and pulls gently but firmly in line with the leg until the limb length matches the uninjured side; Maintain traction while a second person applies the splint; The splint must extend from armpit to beyond the foot; Pad the groin, knee, and ankle; Secure at chest, waist, above fracture, below fracture, and ankle; Maintain traction until the splint is fully secured
{% end %}

### Ankle/Foot

{% steps() %}
Remove footwear carefully — check circulation in toes; Pad the ankle with soft material; Wrap with a compressive bandage (not too tight — toes must remain pink and warm); Splint with rigid material in a U-shape around the ankle; Elevate to reduce swelling
{% end %}

### Collarbone (Clavicle)

{% steps() %}
Place the arm on the injured side in a sling; Add a swathe (broad bandage) around the chest and upper arm to immobilise; The sling supports the arm; the swathe prevents shoulder movement
{% end %}

## Checking Circulation

After every splint application, check:

- **Pulse** — Can you feel a pulse below the fracture? (Radial pulse at wrist for arm, dorsalis pedis on top of foot for leg)
- **Sensation** — Can the patient feel you touch their fingers or toes?
- **Movement** — Can they wiggle their fingers or toes?

**If circulation is lost after splinting**, loosen the bindings immediately. You have cut off blood supply.

**If circulation was absent before splinting**, attempt gentle realignment to restore blood flow. This is painful but may save the limb. Pull gently in line with the bone. Stop if resistance is met.

---

## When to Move vs. When to Wait

| Situation | Action |
|-----------|--------|
| Stable fracture, safe location | Splint and stay put |
| Unstable fracture (femur, pelvis) in safe location | Splint with traction, do not move |
| Any fracture in dangerous location (fire, flood, rockfall) | Splint quickly, move immediately |
| Suspected spinal fracture | Do not move unless in immediate danger; if moving is essential, log-roll with 4+ people keeping spine aligned |

---

## Pain Management

In a field setting without pharmaceuticals:

- **Immobilisation itself reduces pain** — the bone ends stop grinding
- **Elevation** reduces swelling
- **Cold compress** (wet cloth, snow in cloth) applied for 15 minutes per hour
- **Distraction** — talking, breathing exercises
- If you have paracetamol or ibuprofen, administer per dosage instructions

**Do not give aspirin** to someone with a fracture — it impairs clotting and can worsen internal bleeding.

---

## Open Fractures — Additional Steps

1. **Control bleeding** as per [Severe Bleeding](01-immediate/01-first-aid/severe-bleeding).
2. **Do not push the bone back in.**
3. **Irrigate the wound** with clean water — the cleanest you have. Boiled and cooled is ideal.
4. **Cover with a moist sterile dressing** to prevent the bone from drying.
5. **Splint normally.**
6. **Monitor for infection** — redness, swelling, pus, fever. If infection develops and you have antibiotics, a broad-spectrum like amoxicillin-clavulanate is appropriate for open fractures. Without antibiotics, keep the wound as clean as possible and pray.
