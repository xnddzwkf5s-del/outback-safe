# Burns — Emergency Burn Care

Burns are among the most painful injuries a survivor can face. They destroy the skin's protective barrier, cause massive fluid loss, and open the door to fatal infection. Proper early care dramatically affects outcome.

---

## Types of Burns

| Type | Cause | Special considerations |
|------|-------|----------------------|
| **Thermal** | Fire, hot surfaces, steam, boiling water | Most common in survival scenarios |
| **Chemical** | Acids, alkalis, solvents | Flush with water for 20+ minutes; do not neutralise |
| **Electrical** | Lightning, power lines | Internal damage may exceed external; cardiac monitoring |
| **Radiation** | Sunburn, radiation exposure | Cumulative; prevent with covering |

This page focuses on **thermal burns** — the type you will most likely encounter.

---

## Burn Severity Assessment

### Depth

| Degree | Appearance | Sensation | Healing |
|--------|-----------|-----------|---------|
| **Superficial (1st)** | Red, dry, no blisters (sunburn) | Painful | 3–7 days |
| **Partial thickness (2nd)** | Blisters, moist, red, weeping | Very painful | 2–3 weeks |
| **Full thickness (3rd)** | White, waxy, leathery, charred | Painless (nerve endings destroyed) | Requires grafting |

### Extent — Rule of Nines

Estimate percentage of body surface area (BSA) burned:

```
 Head & neck: 9%
 Each arm: 9% (front 4.5% + back 4.5%)
 Chest: 9%
 Abdomen: 9%
 Upper back: 9%
 Lower back: 9%
 Each leg: 18% (front 9% + back 9%)
 Groin: 1%
```

**A burn is critical if:**
- Full thickness (3rd degree) > 5% BSA
- Partial thickness (2nd degree) > 15% BSA in adults
- Burns to face, hands, feet, genitals, or major joints
- Circumferential burns (wrapping around a limb or chest)
- Any burn with inhalation injury

## Immediate Treatment

### Step 1: Stop the Burning Process

- **Remove the heat source.** Get the person away from fire, remove hot/chemical-soaked clothing (unless melted to skin).
- **Remove jewellery and constricting items** before swelling begins (rings, bracelets, belts, boots).
- **If clothing is stuck to the burn**, do not pull it off. Cut around it.

### Step 2: Cool the Burn

{% steps %}
Cool the burn under cool (not ice-cold) running water for at least 20 minutes; If running water is not available, immerse in cool water or apply wet cloths, changing frequently; The 20-minute window is critical — cooling beyond 3 hours after injury provides minimal benefit; Do not use ice — it causes vasoconstriction and can deepen the burn; For chemical burns, flush with water for at least 20 minutes
{% end %}

**Why 20 minutes?** The heat continues to damage tissue even after the source is removed. Adequate cooling limits burn depth progression, reduces pain, and decreases swelling.

### Step 3: Clean and Cover

{% steps %}
Wash hands thoroughly — infection risk is extreme with burns; Clean the burn gently with clean water and mild soap if available; Do not break blisters — they are sterile, natural dressings; Apply a non-adherent dressing (paraffin gauze, cling film, or clean dry cloth); Cover loosely — burns need to breathe somewhat; For hand burns, wrap each finger separately to prevent webbing
{% end %}

**Clings film (plastic wrap)** is an excellent field dressing for burns:
- It is sterile (inside the roll)
- It does not stick to the wound
- It is transparent, so you can monitor the burn without removing the dressing
- Apply lengthwise (not wrapped around — swelling will make wrap too tight)

{% info %}
**Honey** is one of the most effective natural burn dressings available. It is antibacterial, creates a moist healing environment, and is found across AU, CN, . Apply a thin layer of raw honey to cleaned partial-thickness burns and cover loosely. Change daily.
{% end %}

### Step 4: Fluid Replacement

Burns lose massive amounts of fluid through the damaged skin. The person needs more water than normal.

- **Small burns (< 10% BSA):** Encourage oral fluids — water with a pinch of salt and sugar if available (oral rehydration).
- **Large burns (> 15% BSA):** Fluid loss can cause hypovolemic shock. → See [Shock](01-immediate/01-first-aid/shock).
- **Monitor urine output** — dark, infrequent urine means dehydration. Aim for pale yellow every 6–8 hours.

### Step 5: Pain Management

Burns are excruciating. In the field:
- Continue cooling during the acute phase
- Elevate burned limbs to reduce swelling
- Paracetamol or ibuprofen if available
- Keep the person calm — pain worsens with anxiety

## What NOT to Do

| Never do this | Why |
|---------------|-----|
| Apply ice | Deepens the burn through vasoconstriction |
| Apply butter, oil, or grease | Traps heat, increases infection risk |
| Apply toothpaste | Not sterile, does nothing, painful to remove |
| Break blisters | Opens sterile wound to infection |
| Apply egg white or other folk remedies | Extreme infection risk |
| Remove stuck clothing | Tears the wound, increases pain and infection risk |
| Apply cotton wool or fluffy dressings | Fibres stick to the wound |

## Inhalation Burns

If the person was in an enclosed fire:

**Signs of inhalation injury:**
- Burns to face, singed eyebrows/nasal hairs
- Soot in mouth or nose, carbonaceous sputum
- Hoarse voice, stridor (high-pitched breathing sound)
- Difficulty breathing

**Actions:**
- Move to fresh air immediately
- Sit the person upright to ease breathing
- Monitor airway closely — inhalation burns can cause airway swelling hours after injury
- If breathing stops, begin [CPR](01-immediate/01-first-aid/cpr)

## Wound Care Over Days

### Daily Dressing Change

1. Wash hands
2. Remove old dressing gently — soak off with clean water if stuck
3. Rinse the burn with clean (boiled and cooled) water
4. Inspect for infection: increasing redness, green/yellow pus, foul odour, fever
5. Apply fresh honey or clean dressing
6. Cover loosely

### Signs of Infection

- Increasing pain after day 2–3
- Redness spreading beyond the burn margin
- Pus or foul-smelling discharge
- Fever, chills, malaise
- Red streaks tracking from the wound

If infection develops and you have antibiotics, silver sulfadiazine cream is the gold standard for burn infections. Oral antibiotics (amoxicillin-clavulanate or cephalexin) may be needed for systemic infection.

## Sunburn

In Australian, n, and tropical environments, sunburn can be incapacitating:

- **Prevention is everything** — cover skin, seek shade 10:00–15:00
- **Treat as 1st-degree burn** — cool compresses, hydration, aloe vera if available
- **Severe sunburn with blistering** creates a real infection risk and contributes to dehydration
- **Heat stroke** can accompany severe sunburn — monitor for confusion, hot dry skin, cessation of sweating → requires immediate cooling
