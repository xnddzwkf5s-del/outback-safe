# Water & Food in the Outback

Water is the single most critical survival priority in the Australian outback. You can survive weeks without food but only **3 days without water** in extreme heat. Every outback traveller should be obsessive about water planning — carry more than you think you need, know how to find it, and never gamble on the next source.

---

## Water Requirements (Outback-Specific)

Your water needs depend heavily on temperature and activity. The outback regularly exceeds 40°C, which dramatically increases fluid loss.

### Daily Minimums

| Condition | Per Person / Day |
|---|---|
| Winter touring (mild, low activity) | 4L |
| Summer touring (hot, walking) | 8L |
| Extreme heat (40°C+, physical work) | **10L** |
| Emergency reserve (extreme + uncertainty) | +50% of expected |

### Trip Planning Table

Use this to calculate how much water to carry for your group:

| Scenario | Per Person / Day | 2 People × 3 Days | 4 People × 5 Days |
|---|---|---|---|
| Winter touring | 4L | 24L | 80L |
| Summer touring | 8L | 48L | 160L |
| Emergency reserve (summer + buffer) | 12L | 72L | 240L |

**Rule of thumb:** Round everything up. If the calculation says 72L, carry 80L. Water is heavy (1L = 1kg) but a loaded vehicle is better than dehydration.

### Never Ration Water

**Drink what you need.** Dehydration kills faster than running out of water. If you're conserving water to "make it last" while sweating in 40°C heat, you're already in trouble. The correct response to limited water is to travel at night, rest during the day, and sweat less — not drink less.

### Signs of Dehydration

| Stage | Symptoms |
|---|---|
| **1% dehydrated** | Thirst (you're already dehydrated when you feel thirsty) |
| **2-3%** | Dark urine, headache, dry mouth |
| **4-5%** | Fatigue, dizziness, irritability |
| **6-8%** | Confusion, muscle cramps, weak pulse |
| **10%+** | Severe confusion, inability to walk, organ failure |

**Monitor urine colour** — it's the simplest daily check. Pale yellow = good. Dark amber = you need water immediately. No urine output for 8+ hours is a medical emergency.

## Water Sourcing in Arid Areas

Finding water in the outback takes knowledge and effort. Don't expect clear streams — this is the driest continent on Earth. But water is there if you know where to look.

### Natural Sources

**Rocky outcrops and cliffs**
- Water pools in rock holes (gnamma holes) after rain
- Check shaded crevices and overhangs — water can persist weeks after rainfall
- Granite outcrops are the best bet; they shed water into natural basins

**Dry creek beds (creeks and rivers)**
- Dig at the **outside bend** of a dry creek — water flows fastest here and scours deepest, leaving water trapped beneath the sand
- Dig near tree roots on the outside bank — roots follow groundwater
- Dig 0.5-2m down until sand becomes damp, then wait for seepage
- The wider the creek bed, the better the chance of subsurface water

**Bird life — the best natural indicator**
- Finches: water within **5km** (they need to drink daily)
- Pigeons: water within **50km** (they can range further)
- Corellas and galahs: water within 15-20km, usually at first light
- Follow bird flight paths at dawn and dusk — they lead to water

**Vegetation indicators**
- Patches of bright green vegetation in an otherwise brown landscape
- River red gums and coolabahs — deep root systems tap groundwater
- Mulga and spinifex are not reliable water indicators (they're drought-hardy)

### Morning Dew Collection

In arid areas where temperature drops overnight, dew can yield surprising amounts of water.

- Wipe dew off vehicle body panels, tarps, and foliage with a clean cloth
- Wring the cloth into a container
- A 4WD can yield **200-500ml** on a good dewy morning
- Best collected just before sunrise (before it evaporates)
- Repeat daily — steady 500ml/day adds up

### Solar Still

A solar still uses evaporation and condensation to extract water from vegetation and soil.

- Dig a pit 1m wide × 0.5m deep
- Place a container in the centre
- Line the pit with fresh (non-toxic) vegetation
- Cover with clear plastic, seal edges with sand
- Place a small weight in the centre above the container
- Yield: **0.5-1L per day** in favourable conditions

Solar stills are labour-intensive for the yield. Only rely on them if you have no other option.

### Transpiration Bag

Simpler and more effective than a solar still.

- Tie a clear plastic bag around a leafy, healthy branch
- Seal the opening around the stem — no air leaks
- Water transpires from the leaves and condenses on the bag interior
- Yield: **0.2-0.5L per day per bag**
- Set up multiple bags on different trees
- Avoid using poisonous or sap-heavy species

### Underground Water

In dry creek beds and certain terrain, groundwater may be close to the surface.

- Look for the **greenest, tallest trees** in the area
- Dig 1-2m deep at the base of these trees
- Wait for water to seep into the hole
- Let it settle before filtering/collecting

### What NEVER to Drink

- **Radiator water** — contains toxic glycol (antifreeze). Even small amounts cause kidney failure.
- **Salt water / brackish water** — accelerates dehydration. The kidneys need more water to process the salt than you gain.
- **Milky or stagnant water without treatment** — high bacterial load. If it's your only option, treat aggressively.
- **Alcohol** — severe dehydrator. Never consume alcohol when dehydrated.
- **Soft drinks and sugary juices** — high sugar content can worsen dehydration in the short term (dilute with water if possible).

## Water Purification in the Outback

See [Water Purification](01-immediate/02-water/purification) for detailed methods. This section covers outback-specific considerations.

### Outback-Specific Water Sources

| Source | Risk | Treatment |
|---|---|---|
| **Bore water** | High mineral content (safe but tastes bad) | Usually treated already; let settle if cloudy |
| **Cattle trough water** | High bacteria, algae, animal waste | Boil or treat with purification tablets |
| **Rock hole water** | Often clean (remote from animals) | Treat anyway — murky can hide bacteria |
| **Creek seepage** | Sediment, bacteria, possibly chemical runoff | Filter then boil or treat |
| **Rainwater from tarps** | Cleanest source available | Minimal treatment needed; store well |

### Boiling

- Sea level: **1 minute** rolling boil
- Above 1,000m: **3 minutes** rolling boil (lower boiling point requires longer to kill pathogens)
- Let cool before drinking (burned mouth in remote conditions is a real problem)

### Filtering Muddy Water

If water is cloudy or silty, filter before treating:

1. Pour through a **clean cloth** (t-shirt, bandana) to remove large particles
2. Build a layered filter: fabric → sand → crushed charcoal → fabric again
3. Collect the filtered water
4. Then boil or treat with purification tablets

Charcoal from a campfire, crushed and rinsed, makes an excellent filter medium.

## Water Carrying Systems

In the outback, your vehicle is your water lifeline. How you carry and manage that water is critical.

### Vehicle Water Storage

| Container | Best For | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **Military-style jerry cans (20L)** | Primary water storage | Robust, stackable, fit in vehicle racks. Green/olive ones are best (protect from light) |
| **Water bladder tanks (40-80L)** | Bulk basecamp supply | Heavy when full; install in cargo area with a tap outlet |
| **Bottled water (1-2L)** | Daily carry, redundancy | Multiple small containers spread risk |
| **Collapsible water containers (10L)** | Backup, emergency | Cheap but puncture-prone; use for overflow only |

### Distribution — Never Put All Water in One Container

- If one container fails (crack, leak, contamination), you lose one portion — not everything
- Split water across at least 3-4 containers
- Carry daily drinking water separately from reserve supplies
- Each person should have their own day bottle so you don't dip into reserves unnecessarily

### Container Selection

| Material | Pros | Cons |
|---|---|---|
| Plastic (BPA-free) | Lightweight, cheap, transparent | UV degrades over time, can impart taste in heat |
| Stainless steel | Durable, no taste issues, long life | Heavy, expensive, dents |
| Military water jerry cans | Very strong, stackable, UV resistant | Heavy, expensive |
| Blue plastic jerry cans | Cheap, widely available | Less robust, can leak at seams |

### Marking and Labelling

- **Clearly label all water containers "WATER ONLY"**
- Never use fuel containers for water — even trace amounts of fuel residue make water undrinkable and toxic
- Use different container shapes for water vs fuel (e.g., square jerry cans for water, round for fuel)
- Colour-code with tape if needed (blue/white = water, red = fuel)

### Storage and Temperature Management

- Keep water containers **out of direct sunlight** — UV degrades plastic, heat promotes bacterial growth
- Store under the vehicle (on a roof rack shade or in the vehicle interior)
- **Evaporative cooling:** wrap containers in a wet cloth — evaporation keeps the container cooler than ambient temperature by 5-10°C
- In extreme heat, bury containers in sand at least 30cm deep for natural insulation
- Never store water next to fuel or chemicals — vapour contamination can leach through plastic

## Water Rationing (Emergency Only)

Rationing is a **last resort** — only to be used when you know exactly when rescue will arrive or how far you must travel to the next water source. In any other situation, drink what you need to stay functional.

### When to Ration

Only start rationing when:

1. You have calculated your remaining water accurately
2. You know (within reason) how long until rescue or resupply
3. The surplus calculation shows you have more water than needed for estimated time
4. You have shelter from the sun and can reduce activity

If you are lost but no one knows your location, **don't ration** — focus on signalling and water finding. Rationing assumes rescue is coming.

### How to Ration

**The formula:**

> Remaining water (L) ÷ Number of people ÷ Estimated days until rescue = Daily allowance per person

If the result is below 4L/day, you must reduce activity to minimum and stay in shade.

### Rationing Strategy

| Technique | How To |
|---|---|
| **Sip, don't gulp** | 100-200ml every hour, not 1L all at once. Your body absorbs small sips more efficiently |
| **Night drinking** | Drink larger portions in the evening when it's cool — your body retains water better after dark |
| **Temperature management** | Stay in shade, minimise movement, travel at night. Every degree of reduced body temp saves water |
| **Breath control** | Breathe through your nose, mouth closed — reduces moisture loss from breathing. This matters |
| **No talking** | Speech evaporates water from the mouth and lungs. Communicate only when necessary |
| **No smoking** | Tobacco dehydrates. You cannot afford the water loss |
| **No food (if short on water)** | Digestion consumes water. Skip meals if water is severely limited |

### Monitoring Hydration When Rationing

- **Urine colour:** dark = not enough. Clear/pale = adequate for current conditions
- **Urine output:** if you haven't urinated in 8 hours, you are dangerously dehydrated regardless of how much you're drinking
- **Skin turgor:** pinch the skin on the back of your hand — if it doesn't spring back, you need water
- **Mental state:** if you feel confused, irritable, or have a headache, drink water

### What NOT to Do

- **NEVER drink urine.** This is a myth that kills people. Urine is concentrated waste — re-drinking it forces your kidneys to process even more toxins, accelerating dehydration and kidney failure.
- **NEVER drink seawater.** The salt content will kill you faster than dehydration.
- **NEVER drink blood.** High protein and salt content requires more water to digest than it provides.
- **NEVER drink alcohol.** It's a powerful diuretic — you lose more than you gain.

## Emergency Food

**Food is not a survival priority in the short term.** You can survive 3 weeks without food if you have water. You cannot survive 3 days without water in the outback.

That said, if you have water and you're waiting for rescue, having food keeps your strength up, prevents hypothermia (body has fuel for heat), and maintains morale.

### What to Carry

When packing for outback travel, carry food that requires **no cooking, won't melt, and provides sustained energy:**

| Food | Why |
|---|---|
| Muesli/granola bars | Lightweight, shelf-stable, high energy |
| Nuts and dried fruit | Dense energy, electrolytes from dried fruit |
| Jerky/biltong | High protein, long shelf life, no prep |
| Canned tuna/spam (pull-tab) | Protein and fat, no opener needed |
| Hard cheese | Lasts days without refrigeration (wrap in cloth) |
| Instant noodles | Can be eaten dry (crunchy, salty, filling) |
| Energy bars (Clif, etc.) | Formulated for endurance |
| Crackers/dry biscuits | Lightweight, pairs with spreads |
| Electrolyte sachets | Dissolve in water, vital in sweat-heavy conditions |

### What NOT to Pack

- Chocolate (melts into a mess above 30°C)
- Fresh fruit/vegetables (rots in heat, heavy)
- Anything requiring refrigeration
- Glass jars (heavy, breakable, dangerous)
- Anything needing cooking fuel you've already accounted for separately

### Survival Foraging (If Extended Stay)

If you're stuck for more than a few days and have no food, traditional bush foods can sustain you — but only eat what you can **100% identify**. The outback has deadly look-alikes.

| Food | How to Find | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **Witchetty grubs** | Dig at base of acacia trees, especially mulga. White, fat grubs 3-8cm long | High protein, high fat. Eaten raw or cooked. Tastes like scrambled egg |
| **Bush tomato** | Small desert shrub with green-grey leaves. Fruit looks like a small, wrinkly tomato | Must be fully ripe (drops off plant). Green fruit is toxic. Strong, spicy flavour |
| **Quandong** | Small tree, bright red fruit with a central stone. Found in arid and semi-arid areas | Tart flesh eaten raw or dried. High vitamin C |
| **Wattle seed** | Acacia trees produce seed pods. Collect dry, brown pods, grind seeds | Roast then grind into flour. Nutty, coffee-like flavour |
| **Pigweed / Warrigal greens** | Broad-leafed plant found near creek beds and disturbed ground | Cook before eating (raw = toxic oxalates). Tastes like spinach |
| **Honey ants** | Mound nests near mulga trees. The "honey-pot" ants have translucent, swollen abdomens | Sweet, juicy, high sugar content. Found by digging into nest chambers |

### What NEVER to Eat

- **Any mushroom or fungus** — unless you are a trained mycologist. Deadly Amanita species grow in outback regions.
- **White berries** — the vast majority are toxic
- **Plants with milky sap** (exception: figs) — many are poisonous or cause severe skin reactions
- **Any plant you cannot identify with 100% certainty** — one mistake can be fatal or cause days of incapacity
- **Stagnant-water animals** (dead-looking frogs, insects from contaminated water) — may carry disease
- **Uncooked meat** — risk of bacteria, parasites, and worms

### Hydration & Food Interaction

- **Digesting food consumes water.** If you're severely short on water, skip meals until you find more.
- **Salty food increases thirst.** Account for this — if you eat jerky or bush tomato, you'll need more water.
- **High-protein diet requires more water** for the kidneys to process nitrogen waste.
- If you have limited water, prioritise high-carb foods (muesli bars, crackers, noodles) over high-protein foods.

## Water-Related Illness

### Hyponatremia (Water Intoxication)

**You can drink too much water.** Hyponatremia occurs when you drink large volumes of water without replacing electrolytes, diluting the sodium in your blood.

It's surprisingly common in the outback — people sweat heavily, lose salt, then drink only plain water.

**Symptoms:**

- Headache (same as dehydration, which makes it easy to misdiagnose)
- Nausea and vomiting
- Confusion and disorientation
- Muscle weakness and cramps
- Seizures (severe)
- Unconsciousness (critical)

**Prevention:**

- Eat something salty when you're sweating heavily
- Carry electrolyte sachets and use one per 2-4L of water in hot conditions
- Alternate water with electrolyte drinks
- Add a pinch of salt to meals

**Treatment:**

- Stop drinking plain water
- Consume salty food or electrolyte solution
- Small sips of a rehydration solution (see below)
- In severe cases, seek medical evacuation

### Oral Rehydration Solution

If you're dehydrated or showing early signs of hyponatremia, this simple recipe replaces both fluid and electrolytes:

> **1 litre clean water**
> **6 level teaspoons of sugar**
> **0.5 level teaspoon of salt**

Stir until dissolved. Sip slowly — don't gulp. This is the WHO-recommended formulation adapted for field use.

### Heat Stroke vs Dehydration

See [Heat Survival](01-immediate/09-outback/heat-survival) for the full guide on heat illness. The key distinction:

| Condition | Cause | Key Symptom |
|---|---|---|
| Dehydration | Water loss | Thirst, dark urine, dry mouth |
| Heat exhaustion | Heat + fluid loss | Heavy sweating, cold/clammy skin, weak pulse |
| Heat stroke | Core temp >40°C | Hot dry skin, confusion, unconsciousness |

Both conditions need water. Heat stroke needs **immediate cooling and evacuation** — see [First Aid](01-immediate/01-first-aid/).

## Summary: Water Priorities in the Outback

1. **Carry enough** — use the trip planning table, then add 50%
2. **Distribute your water** — multiple containers, never all in one
3. **Know where to find more** — dig creek beds, check rock holes, follow birds
4. **Treat everything** — even "clean" rock hole water
5. **Drink as needed** — don't ration unless you know exactly when rescue comes
6. **Replace electrolytes** — plain water alone isn't enough in extreme heat
7. **Food is secondary** — 3 weeks without food vs 3 days without water
