# Crocodile Safety

Crocodile awareness is not optional in northern Australia. Every year, travellers die or are maimed because they underestimated the risk. This page covers everything you need to know to stay safe around crocodile habitat — from identification and behaviour to camping, crossing, and emergency response.

---

## 1. Know Your Crocodiles

Australia has two species of crocodile. Only one is a legitimate threat, but both deserve respect.

| Species | Location | Size | Danger | Behaviour |
|---------|----------|------|--------|-----------|
| **Saltwater (Estuarine) Crocodile** | Coastal NT, QLD (north of Rockhampton), northern WA | 4-6 m (males), up to 1,000+ kg | EXTREMELY DANGEROUS | Aggressive, territorial, apex predator. Attacks without warning. |
| **Freshwater Crocodile** | Northern Australia inland waterways, upper river reaches, gorges, rockpools | 2-3 m, up to 100 kg | Low risk | Shy, avoids humans. Will bite if provoked or cornered but rarely fatal. |

### The Golden Rule

**Any water north of the Tropic of Capricorn, or in inland NT and QLD — assume saltwater crocodiles are present.** Even if locals say it's safe. Even if there are no warning signs. Even if you can't see anything in the water.

Freshwater crocodiles are generally harmless to adults, but they can still deliver a painful bite that becomes severely infected. Do not harass or approach them.

## 2. Where Crocodiles Are

Crocodiles are habitat generalists. They thrive in almost any permanent water in northern Australia.

| Habitat | How Dangerous | Notes |
|---------|---------------|-------|
| Rivers and creeks | HIGH | Major croc habitat. All sizes present. |
| Billabongs and lagoons | HIGH | Slow-moving water, ideal ambush spots. |
| Swamps and floodplains | HIGH | Shallow water, excellent hunting ground. |
| Coastal beaches | HIGH | Saltwater crocs travel along coastlines. |
| Estuaries and mangroves | VERY HIGH | Prime croc habitat — warm, shallow, prey-rich. |
| Inland waterholes | HIGH | Crocs can travel 100+ km inland along waterways. |
| Tiny stock dams | MODERATE | Any permanent water can hold a croc. |
| Rockpools and gorges | LOW-MODERATE | Usually freshwater crocs. Still don't swim. |
| Swimming pools | NONE | Only safe if in a fenced town swimming pool. |

Crocodiles can survive in very small waterholes. A croc can live in a dam the size of a suburban swimming pool. There is no minimum water body size that guarantees safety.

## 3. Camping Safety

| Rule | Detail |
|------|--------|
| Camp 50+ metres from water | Minimum safe distance from any permanent water. |
| Don't camp on beaches above high tide | Crocs patrol beaches at night and may haul out. |
| Rotate water access points | Using the same spot repeatedly teaches crocs your pattern. |
| Keep camp clean | Food scraps attract fish and animals, which attract crocs. |
| Don't clean fish at water's edge | Fish blood and scraps attract crocs to your camp area. |
| Don't prepare food near water | Same principle — food smells travel on water. |
| Secure food overnight | Store food in lockers or sealed containers. |

### Choosing a Campsite

In croc country, the ideal campsite is:
- On high ground, away from water
- With clear views of the water approach
- With no game trails leading to your camp
- At least 50 m from the water's edge (100 m is better)
- On the lee side (downwind) — crocs have excellent sense of smell

## 4. Water Collection Safety

Collecting water is one of the most dangerous activities in croc country because it requires crouching at the water's edge — exactly where a crocodile ambush targets.

| Practice | Safe | Unsafe |
|----------|------|--------|
| Time of day | Midday (croc resting) | Dawn, dusk, night (peak feeding) |
| Approach | Scan water for 5-10 minutes before approaching | Walk straight to water's edge |
| Position | Stand well back, use bucket on rope | Crouch or kneel at water's edge |
| Routine | Vary collection times daily | Same spot, same time, every day |
| Awareness | Watch all directions while collecting | Focus only on the water in front |
| Silhouette | Stand upright, don't crouch | Crouching at water level = perfect croc target silhouette |

If you see any sign of a croc — eyes, nostrils, ripples, bubbles, slide marks — do NOT collect water from that location. Move to another spot at least 500 m away.

## 5. Crossing Creeks & Rivers

### Before Crossing

1. **Check for crocodiles before entering water.** Observe from a safe distance (10+ m from water's edge, ideally from an elevated position).
2. **Look for:** eyes or nostrils above the water surface, slide marks on banks, bubbles or ripples, crocodile tracks in mud.
3. **Crocs can remain submerged and motionless for 2+ hours.** Seeing nothing on the surface means nothing.
4. **Never assume a water body is croc-free** just because you checked and saw nothing.

### Crossing Technique

| If | Then |
|----|------|
| Crocodile seen | DO NOT CROSS. Find an alternative or wait hours. Crocs may leave. |
| No croc seen, daylight hours | Cross QUICKLY, in a group, no splashing, watch all directions. |
| Dawn, dusk, or night | Do NOT enter the water. Wait until full daylight. |
| Estuarine/tidal crossing | Check tide and croc risk. Tidal crossings attract fish = attract crocs. |

### Group Crossing

- Cross in a tight group (safety in numbers)
- Do NOT spread out — a solitary person is the target
- Watch each other's blind spots
- If someone is seized, everyone else in the water must react immediately

## 6. What to Do If Attacked

**Prevention is the only real safety.** If a saltwater croc has seized you, the odds are heavily against survival. But there are actions that improve your chance.

### If Seized

1. **FIGHT BACK.** Target the eyes and snout. Crocodiles have extremely sensitive eyes.
2. **Gouge, poke, punch the eyes** — this is the most effective defence.
3. **Hit the snout** — the snout has sensitive nerve endings. A solid hit may cause release.
4. **The death roll**: if the croc spins, it is trying to drown and dismember you. If it releases to re-position (brief pause between rolls), that is your moment to escape.

### After Release

1. **Get out of the water and away from the edge FAST.** Crocs can move on land.
2. **Run in a straight line.** The zigzag advice is a myth. Crocs are faster than humans in a straight line on land but tire quickly. On a sprint, a motivated human can outrun a croc over 30-50 m on land.
3. **Do not go back** to help someone else until you are clear of the water.

### Medical Attention

- **Seek medical attention immediately** after any croc encounter, even if wounds seem minor.
- Croc mouths contain a massive bacterial load. Even small punctures become severely infected within hours.
- In remote areas, activate your PLB/EPIRB and request urgent medical evacuation.
- Croc attack wounds are considered highly contaminated — expect hospitalisation, IV antibiotics, and surgical debridement.

### Reporting

- Report all croc encounters (sightings and attacks) to the local National Parks or Wildlife Service office.
- In NT: contact Parks and Wildlife (08 8999 4555)
- In QLD: contact Department of Environment and Science (1300 130 372)
- In WA: contact Department of Biodiversity, Conservation and Attractions (08 9219 9000)

## 7. Swimming — DON'T

**There is no safe swimming in northern Australia except in designated, signed swimming areas.**

| Safe to Swim | NOT Safe to Swim |
|-------------|------------------|
| Town swimming pools | ANY river, creek, billabong |
| Designated swimming holes WITH croc barriers | Beaches (except patrolled beaches in major towns) |
| Elevated freshwater plunge pools above waterfalls that fish cannot reach | Gorges and rockpools |
| Hotel/resort swimming pools | Inland waterholes, stock dams |
| Sea water on the Great Barrier Reef islands (reef platform, not swimming to shore) | Mangroves, estuaries, tidal creeks |

### What About Where Locals Swim?

"If locals swim here, it must be safe" — this is the most dangerous myth in northern Australia. Locals have died swimming in "safe" spots. Even experienced croc-country residents get it wrong.

### Signs It Is NOT Safe to Swim

- Warning signs present (take them seriously — they exist because a croc IS there)
- Slide marks on muddy banks
- Crocodile tracks in sand or mud (tail drag between footprints)
- Absence of other wildlife at the water's edge (birds, wallabies, goannas avoid croc water)
- Locals telling you NOT to swim there

If you see ANY of these signs, do not enter the water. The absence of signs does NOT mean safety.

## 8. Signs You Are in Croc Country

| Sign | What It Means |
|------|---------------|
| Official warning signs | The relevant authority has confirmed a croc is present |
| Slide marks on banks | Crocodiles haul out of the water at this spot |
| Crocodile tracks | Tail drag mark between footprints — distinctive and unmistakable |
| Eyes/nostrils visible | Croc is present and observing you |
| Bubbles or ripples | Croc may be submerged and breathing |
| Absence of wildlife | Birds, wallabies, and goannas avoid croc-infested water |
| Local advice | Listen to locals when they say crocs are present. Question them when they say it is safe. |

## 9. Crocodile Behaviour to Understand

| Behaviour | What It Means For You |
|-----------|----------------------|
| Crocs are ambush predators | They strike from still water with explosive speed. You will not see them coming. |
| No warning strike | Saltwater crocs do not hiss, splash, or warn before attacking. The first sign of trouble is being seized. |
| Territorial | Resident crocs defend their stretch of water. Removing a problem croc does not make the water safe — another will move in. |
| Can travel overland | Crocs can travel up to 30 km overland to find new water. They may appear in dams and waterholes that were previously croc-free. |
| Active year-round | Crocs do not hibernate. They are active all year in northern Australia. The dry season is NOT safe season. |
| Learn patterns | Crocs can learn feeding/activity patterns of humans and livestock. They are intelligent predators. |

### Seasonal Risk

| Season | Risk Level | Notes |
|--------|-----------|-------|
| Wet season (Nov-Apr) | HIGHEST | Rivers full, crocodiles spread across floodplains |
| Dry season (May-Oct) | HIGH | Crocodiles concentrated in remaining waterholes. More encounters per swimming/camping site. |

The dry season is not safer. Crocodiles are more concentrated and hungrier as waterholes shrink and fish become more catchable.

## 10. What to Carry in Croc Country

| Item | Why |
|------|-----|
| UHF radio | Call for help. Channel 5 is monitored by some remote stations. |
| PLB / EPIRB | Essential for medical evacuation in remote areas. |
| First aid kit | Catastrophic bleeding control (tourniquet, haemostatic gauze). |
| Long stick / pole | Probe water before entering or collecting |
| Rope (15-20 m) | Pull someone clear of the water without entering |
| Satellite phone | If travelling remote Cape York or Kimberley |
