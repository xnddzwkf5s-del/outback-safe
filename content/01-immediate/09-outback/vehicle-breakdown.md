# Vehicle Breakdown: Stay or Go?

The most important decision you will make after a breakdown in the Australian outback. Make it wrong and you die. Make it right and you survive.

---

## The Golden Rule

**Stay with the vehicle unless you are certain walking is the better option.**

Vehicles are visible from the air, provide shade, contain water (radiator, tank, washer bottle), fuel for fire, components for signaling, and spare parts for repairs. A person walking in the outback becomes invisible within 1 km. Thousands of kilometres of station tracks and desert crossings are covered with the bones of people who walked.

---

## The Decision Tree

| Factor | Stay with Vehicle | Walk for Help |
|--------|------------------|---------------|
| **Vehicle location** | Visible from air (open plain, ridgeline) | Hidden in gully, dense scrub, bottom of hill |
| **Water** | You have > 5 L per person | You have < 2 L per person |
| **Route notified** | Someone knows your route and ETA | No one knows where you are |
| **Rescue timeframe** | Estimated rescue within 48 hours | No rescue expected in > 48 hours |
| **Destination known** | Unsure where help is | Help is within 20 km and you know exactly where |
| **Time of day** | Late afternoon / night | Dawn (walk in cool morning hours) |
| **Physical condition** | Uninjured or minor injuries | Fully fit, no injuries |
| **Phone signal** | Signal at vehicle | No signal at vehicle but known signal point nearby |

**Decision rule:** If 3 or more factors say STAY, stay. If 4 or more say GO, consider walking — but only at dawn.

## Step-by-Step Protocol

### Step 1: Safety First (Immediate)

1. Pull off the road as far as possible — do not block the track
2. Turn on hazard lights immediately
3. If safe, raise the bonnet (this is the universal distress signal for vehicles)
4. Check for injuries in the vehicle
5. Check for fuel leaks, electrical fire, or hot components
6. Move everyone at least 10 m from the vehicle if you smell fuel

### Step 2: Assess (First 5 Minutes)

Inventory your situation:

- **Injuries** — Anyone hurt? Apply [first aid](01-immediate/01-first-aid/) before anything else
- **Water** — How many litres do you have total? Include bottles, radiator (no coolant), esky ice
- **Food** — What do you have and how long will it last?
- **Fuel** — How much fuel? Enough for a signal fire? A tyre fire?
- **Phone signal** — Check all networks at all heights. Walk 50 m in each direction holding phone up
- **UHF radio** — Call on channel 40 (truckies), then channel 5 (emergency), then channel 10 (general call)
- **Time of day** — If it's after 14:00, you are staying overnight. Full stop
- **Who knows where you are?** — Did you log a trip plan? Did you tell anyone your route?

### Step 3: Communicate (First 30 Minutes)

1. Call **000** if you have mobile signal — ask for police
2. Try **112** — works on any network, even without a SIM card
3. Try UHF calls every 15 minutes on channel 40 (truckies), channel 5 (emergency)
4. If no contact: **activate your PLB/EPIRB** — do not wait. See [Emergency Services](01-immediate/09-outback/emergency-services)

### Step 4: Prepare for Nightfall (First 2 Hours)

Even if you expect rescue today, prepare as if you will be here for 3 days:

1. Create shade on the southern side of the vehicle (see [Extreme Heat Survival](01-immediate/09-outback/heat-survival))
2. Inventory and ration all water — 500 mL per person every 30 minutes, not chugging
3. Gather firewood before dark — at least 3 hours worth
4. Prepare signaling materials: mirror, reflective items, spare tyre for burning
5. Set up sleeping area outside the vehicle (the vehicle interior becomes an oven)
6. Tie something visible to your aerial — flag, shirt, reflective tape

### Step 5: Overnight

1. Sleep outside under the vehicle or beside it — never inside a stopped car in heat
2. Keep a watch rotation if possible (2 hours on, 2 hours off)
3. Listen for vehicles — vehicles can be heard from 5 km+ on still nights
4. If you hear a vehicle: light a fire, flash hazard lights, wave a light
5. Stay warm — outback nights can drop below 10°C even after 45°C days

### Step 6: Dawn Reassessment

At first light, repeat the decision tree:

- **Same factors as last night + daylight for walking**
- If you decide to stay: improve shade, arrange ground signals, continue UHF calls at 15-minute intervals
- If you decide to walk: leave a detailed note on the dashboard stating direction, time, number of people, and final destination

## Survival Timeline in 45°C Heat

| Time Elapsed | Without Shade | With Shade |
|--------------|---------------|------------|
| 1 hour | Heat stress begins | Manageable with water |
| 3 hours | Heat stroke imminent | Thirsty, still OK |
| 6 hours | Life-threatening | Weak, but alive |
| 12 hours | — | Severe dehydration |
| 24 hours | — | Critical without water |
| 3 days | — | Death without water |

**Key takeaway:** Shade is not optional. If you cannot create shade, you have less than 3 hours.

---

## Rationing While Waiting

| Resource | Ration Strategy |
|----------|----------------|
| **Water** | 500 mL per person every 20–30 minutes in heat. 4 L per person per day minimum. 8 L better. |
| **Food** | Eat only enough to maintain energy. Fat and protein need more water to digest — prefer simple carbs. |
| **Phone battery** | Turn off. Use only for calls. Charge from vehicle battery if engine runs. |
| **Vehicle battery** | Use hazard lights sparingly. 15 minutes on, 45 minutes off. |
| **Fuel** | Reserve at least 10 L for signal fire or emergency burn. |

---

## What to Do While Waiting

Do not just sit. Survivors stay busy:

- Create larger ground signals — stomp patterns in red sand, arrange rocks, lay out bright clothing
- Dig a sun shelter on the southern side of the vehicle
- Collect firewood and arrange it in a triangle (international distress signal)
- Check the vehicle for useful components — see [Vehicle as a Resource](01-immediate/09-outback/vehicle-as-resource)
- Walk 200 m in each direction at midday to check for signal — **mark your trail** so you can find the vehicle again
- Keep a written log of time, actions, water consumed — helps rescuers and keeps your mind focused

---

## The Walking Decision — If You Must Go

Walking in the Australian outback kills people. If you absolutely must:

- Walk at dawn and dusk only. Rest 10:00–16:00.
- Carry all your water. You will need 1 L per 5 km walked in heat.
- Leave a note on the dashboard with: direction, time, names, final destination
- Follow fence lines, power lines, or creek beds downhill
- Mark your path every 200 m — torn cloth, rock cairns, scratches in bark
- Stay on roads and tracks — do not cut cross-country
- Walk at night only if you can navigate by stars or landmarks — see [Navigation Basics](01-immediate/09-outback/navigation-basics)

### How Far Can You Walk?

| Conditions | Distance per day (estimate) |
|------------|----------------------------|
| Flat track, dawn/dusk, 30°C | 20–25 km |
| Flat track, dawn/dusk, 40°C | 10–15 km |
| Cross-country, 40°C+ | 5–10 km |
| With 10 L water load, 40°C+ | 8–12 km |
