# Vehicle as a Resource

A broken-down vehicle is not a failure — it is a survival kit on wheels. Every component, fluid, and piece of fabric can be repurposed. Modern 4WDs carry 50+ litres of water-equivalent, fuel for fire and signaling, and hundreds of parts that can be adapted for survival use.

---

## Water Sources

| Source | Approximate Volume | Notes |
|--------|-------------------|-------|
| See [Water & Food in the Outback](01-immediate/09-outback/water-food-outback) for water treatment methods.radiator: 8–12 L | **Only if no coolant.** Coolant is toxic — if it smells sweet or is coloured (green/pink/red), do not drink. In older vehicles with plain water, radiator water is safe after boiling. |
| Windscreen washer reservoir | 2–5 L | Contains alcohol/detergent. Pass through a filter of charcoal or cloth to reduce contaminants. Ingest only as a last resort. |
| Heater core (hoses) | 1–2 L | Same coolant risk as radiator. |
| Esky / cooler | Variable | Melted ice water. Safe to drink. |
| Air conditioner condensate | Drip rate | If the AC has been running, condensate water drips under the vehicle. Clean water. Collect with a container. |
| Rain water collection | Unlimited (when it rains) | Use bonnet or tarp to funnel water. See Rain Collection below. |
| Water bottle / container | Whatever you brought | Ration strictly. |

### Radiator Water — Important Warning

**Do not drink radiator water if it contains coolant.** Glycol-based coolant (antifreeze) causes kidney failure and death even in small amounts. The sweet taste is deceptive — it tastes pleasant. If in doubt, use radiator water only for external cooling (wet cloths) and save drinking water for consumption.

If the radiator contains plain water (common in older vehicles or after a repair): boil it for 5 minutes before drinking.

## Fire

| Method | What You Need | How To |
|--------|---------------|--------|
| **Battery + steel wool** | Battery (any), fine steel wool | Stretch steel wool between battery terminals — it ignites instantly. Steel wool packed in toolkits or cleaning supplies. |
| **Battery + foil gum wrapper** | Battery, gum wrapper (foil type) | Cut wrapper into a thin strip. Touch ends to battery terminals. The strip glows red and can light tinder. |
| **Cigarette lighter** | Vehicle cigarette lighter | Press and hold. Once glowing, use to light tinder. |
| **Fuel + spark** | Petrol, spark source | Soak a rag in fuel. Create a spark by striking metal on metal near the rag. **Extremely dangerous** — stand back, use small amounts, and do not pour fuel directly. |
| **Brake fluid + chlorine** | Brake fluid, pool chlorine (if carried) | Mix in small amounts — chemical reaction produces intense flame. Handle with extreme caution. |
| **Tyre fire** | Spare tyre, fuel | Stack tyre on rim, add fuel, ignite. Produces massive black smoke visible for 30 km+. See Signaling below. |

## Signaling

| Method | Effectiveness | Notes |
|--------|---------------|-------|
| **Signal mirror** | Best. Visible up to 30 km on a clear day. | Use side mirror, rear-view mirror, or any reflective surface. Flash toward aircraft or searchers. |
| **Burning spare tyre** | Excellent. Black smoke visible 30 km+. | Remove spare tyre from carrier. Stack with rim. Pour 1–2 L of fuel on it. Ignite. The smoke column is unmistakable from air. |
| **Hazard lights** | Good at night. Limited daytime range. | Keep vehicle battery alive — run hazards 15 min on, 45 min off. |
| **Bonnet up** | Universal distress signal. | Do this immediately after breakdown. |
| **Reflective sun shade** | Good. | Place on roof or bonnet. Flash in direction of aircraft. |
| **Flag on aerial** | Good. | Tie bright clothing, reflective tape, or emergency blanket to the radio aerial. Makes the vehicle visible from distance. |
| **Headlight flashing** | Night only. | Flashes can be seen from 5+ km at night. |
| **Whistle** | Short range (500 m) but carries further than shouting. | Three blasts = universal distress. |
| **Ground signals** | Very good for aerial search. | Arrange rocks, stomp patterns in sand, or lay out bright clothing in an SOS or large arrow. Make letters at least 3 m tall for visibility. |
| **Signal fire** | Good — three fires in a triangle. | Prepare three piles of wood 30 m apart in a triangle. Light when you hear/see aircraft. |

## Shelter

| Vehicle Component | Survival Use |
|-------------------|--------------|
| **Vehicle body** | Provides shade (south side) and windbreak. **Do not sleep inside** in heat — interior becomes an oven. |
| **Seats** | Remove rear seats for sleeping platform. Cushions for bedding. |
| **Floor mats** | Ground insulation. Protects from 60°C+ sand. |
| **Carpet/headlining** | Can be torn out for insulation, bedding, or tinder. |
| **Bonnet** | Remove and prop up as shade wall. |
| **Doors** | Open doors create shaded alcoves. Remove doors for larger tarps. |
| **Spare tyre cover** | Waterproof sheet. Can be used as ground sheet, rain catchment, or shade. |
| **Tool roll / bag** | Storage for collected items. |

The vehicle itself provides the best shelter — use the south side for shade, remove seats for bedding, and use floor mats for ground insulation.

## Tools and Materials

| Component | Survival Use |
|-----------|--------------|
| **Jack** | Lifting, prying, breaking. The scissor jack mechanism can be disassembled for parts. |
| **Wheel brace / wrench** | Hammer, pry bar, digging tool, striking surface for spark. |
| **Seatbelt** | Rope, tourniquet, lashing, strap for carrying. Cut with knife or melt with lighter. |
| **Upholstery** | Bandage material, padding, insulation, cloth for water filtration. |
| **Wire (from electrical system)** | Snares, bindings, stitching, fishing hooks (twisted). |
| **Hose (radiator/vent)** | Siphon tube, drinking straw, water transport. |
| **Plastic bottles** | Water containers, scoops, funnels. |
| **Fuse / wiring** | Small metal parts for hooks, fish hooks, or improvised tools. |
| **Glass (broken window)** | Cutting tool, scraper, signal reflector. Wrap one edge for safe handling. |
| **Oil** | Lubricant, fire accelerant, skin protection (for hands in cold), preserving metal tools. |
| **Duct tape / zip ties** | Repair, lashing, bandage securing, shelter building. |

## Navigation

| Resource | Use |
|----------|-----|
| **Phone GPS** | Check coordinates and save location before battery dies. Take a screenshot of your GPS coordinates. |
| **Paper maps** | Glovebox maps are common. Hema maps preferred. Learn to read them. |
| **Vehicle compass** | Many 4WDs have an internal compass. Check it works. |
| **Sun position** | In Australia, the sun tracks north — use it for rough direction. See [Navigation Basics](01-immediate/09-outback/navigation-basics). |
| **Road signs** | Note the last road name and distance marker. Write it down. |
| **Fuel receipt** | GPS coordinates are sometimes printed on fuel receipts. Check the glovebox. |

## Rain Collection

When it rains in the outback — and it does, even in deserts — collect every drop:

1. **Bonnet collection:** Angle the bonnet so it tilts downward. Water runs off the engine-side surface. Place containers at the low edge.
2. **Tarp collection:** Tie a tarp between the vehicle roof and the ground at a 30° angle. Water runs to the low point. Place container there.
3. **Vehicle roofline:** If the roof has gutters, water pools there. Sponge it up or drill/tap a small hole to drain into a container.
4. **Windscreen base:** Rain runs down the windscreen and collects at the base below the wipers. Sponge out.

All collected rain should be treated before drinking — boil for 5 minutes or use purification tablets if available.

**Expected collection:** A 2 m × 3 m tarp and 10 mm of rain yields approximately 60 L of water.
