# Water Crossings

Crossing watercourses in the Australian outback is one of the most dangerous things you can do in a 4WD. Deep channels, hidden obstacles, flash flooding, and crocodiles all conspire to make an easy crossing deadly in seconds. The good news: most crossings can be assessed and executed safely if you follow a consistent process.

---

## 1. The Golden Rule

**Walk every crossing before driving it.**

You cannot assess a water crossing from inside the vehicle. Water hides everything — depth, bottom surface, submerged objects, drop-offs, and current speed. Walking it is non-negotiable.

When wading in to assess:

| Check | What to Look For |
|-------|-----------------|
| Depth | Above knee (400 mm) = serious risk. Above thigh (600 mm) = extreme risk. |
| Bottom surface | Sand, rock, mud, or concrete. Test with a stick. |
| Current speed | Can you stand easily against it? If it pushes you, it will push the vehicle. |
| Entry/exit banks | Gentle slope both sides? Steep exit = vehicle may not climb out. |
| Width | Longer crossings mean longer exposure to current. |
| Debris | Trees, logs, branches indicate flash flooding may have occurred. |

If you cannot stand in it — do not drive it.

---

## 2. Vehicle Preparation

Before entering any water crossing:

1. **Fit a snorkel** — Raised air intake essential for deep crossings. Also critical for dust on corrugated roads. If no snorkel, water entering the air intake = engine hydro-locked = destroyed engine.
2. **Blind/bra over radiator** — Prevents the fan from bending water into the radiator core (fans act like propellers in water).
3. **Disconnect fan belt** (viscous/hub fan) — Electric fans can be turned off or disconnected. A spinning metal fan hitting water can explode and destroy the radiator.
4. **Engage low range 4WD** — Low range ensures steady, controlled speed.
5. **Windows DOWN** — If the vehicle floods and electronics die, you need to escape. Rolling down power windows won't work underwater.
6. **Seatbelts OFF** — Same reason. You need to exit fast if the vehicle floods.
7. **Only one vehicle in the water at a time** — If one vehicle stalls, the second cannot help and will also be stranded.

---

## 3. Crossing Technique

| Phase | Technique |
|-------|-----------|
| Entry | Enter slowly. No splash. Ease into the water, front wheels first. |
| Gear | Low range 2nd gear. 1st gear has too much torque and will spin wheels. |
| RPM | Steady 2,000-2,500 RPM. Maintain enough revs to keep exhaust pressure up (prevents water entering exhaust). |
| Speed | Create a bow wave in front of the vehicle. The wave pushes water away from the engine bay. Too fast = wave goes over bonnet. Too slow = water enters engine bay. |
| Mid-crossing | CONSTANT speed. Do not change gear. Do not stop. Do not accelerate suddenly. |
| Exit | Once front wheels touch the far bank, gentle acceleration to climb out. |
| Post-exit | Slow, steady drive on flat ground. Pump brakes to dry them. |

### The Bow Wave

The bow wave is your friend. A correctly sized bow wave pushes water away from the engine bay and prevents it entering the air intake. The wave should stay at bonnet height — not over the bonnet (too fast) and not behind the air intake (too slow).

## 4. Depth Guide

| Depth | Risk | Action |
|-------|------|--------|
| 300 mm (hub height) | Low | Standard crossing for any 4WD |
| 500 mm (mid-wheel) | Medium | Walk crossing. Know your vehicle's wading depth. Snorkel strongly recommended. |
| 600 mm (top of tyre) | High | Snorkel recommended. Proceed with extreme caution. This is the maximum safe depth for most stock vehicles. |
| 700 mm+ (bonnet level) | Extreme | Snorkel ESSENTIAL. Vehicle flooding likely. Only attempt with snorkel, preparation, and firm ground. |
| Above bonnet | Do NOT attempt | Vehicle will float, engine will ingest water. |

Depth measurements are at the DEEPEST point of the crossing, not the entry point. Walk the full line before driving.

**Know YOUR vehicle's factory wading depth:**
- Toyota LandCruiser 70/200 series ≈ 700 mm
- Nissan Patrol GU ≈ 700 mm
- Ford Ranger ≈ 800 mm
- Toyota HiLux ≈ 700 mm
- Mitsubishi Triton ≈ 500 mm
- Isuzu D-Max ≈ 600 mm

These are factory figures for **slow, steady crossings with a bow wave**. Add a safety margin of 200 mm — a 700 mm rated vehicle should not attempt water deeper than 500 mm without a snorkel and preparation.

## 5. River Bottom Types

| Bottom Type | Assessment | Risk |
|-------------|-----------|------|
| Sand | Test with a long stick. Firm sand = good traction. Soft = vehicle will sink. | Moderate — good if firm, dangerous if soft |
| Rock | Large rocks = risk of puncturing oil pan, diff, or tyre. Walk to ensure no sharp boulders. | Moderate — traction is good but damage risk is real |
| Mud | Very risky. Bottomless mud is common in outback creek channels. If your stick sinks easily with no firm bottom, do NOT attempt. | High |
| Concrete causeway | Easiest crossing. Still walk first — may have damage, drop-offs, or debris. | Low — but still needs inspection |
| Gravel | Good traction but loose. Exit bank may be slippery. | Low to moderate |

## 6. Floodways & Flash Flooding

Flash flooding kills more outback travellers than any other hazard. It can happen in clear skies, with rain falling 50 km upstream and water arriving without warning.

### Rules for Floodways

- **NEVER camp in a dry creek bed or floodway.** Outback storms can flood a dry crossing in minutes.
- Before crossing, check upstream for storm clouds. If the sky upstream is dark or raining, do NOT cross.
- If water is RISING while you assess — leave immediately. Do not attempt to cross.
- If you see abandoned vehicles at the crossing, check why. They may have been swept there by floodwater. Do not assume they drove in and recovered — they may have been carried by water.

### How Flash Floods Work

| Factor | Reality |
|--------|---------|
| Warning time | Often zero. Clear skies locally, but rain 30-50 km upstream sends a wall of water. |
| Speed | Floodwaters can travel at 30+ km/h. A dry crossing becomes a raging torrent in under a minute. |
| Water depth | A normally knee-deep crossing can become 2 m+ deep in a single storm event. |
| Duration | Most flash floods recede within 1-4 hours. Wait it out on high ground. |

### If You Get Caught

- If vehicle is in floodwater and you cannot drive out: **get out immediately**. A vehicle will float and be swept away with you inside.
- Move to high ground. Do not stay on the floodplain.
- If you are already across when flooding begins: stay where you are. Do not attempt to go back.
- Wait for water to fully recede before assessing the crossing again.

## 7. Salt Water / Estuarine Crossings

Cape York and the Kimberley have tidal crossings that combine crocodile risk with tide timing.

### Crocodile Danger

In northern Australia (north of the Tropic of Capricorn, and all inland NT/QLD waterways):
- **ALWAYS check for crocodiles** before entering water.
- Never wade across at dawn, dusk, or night (peak crocodile feeding times).
- Crocodiles can remain submerged for hours. Seeing nothing on the surface means nothing.
- Use a long stick to probe the water before stepping in. Crocodiles may be submerged and invisible.
- See [Crocodile Safety](01-immediate/09-outback/crocodile-safety) for comprehensive guidance.

### Tide Timing

- Estuarine crossings may be impassable at high tide (salt water fills the crossing).
- Check tide charts before planning a crossing.
- Salt water is corrosive — wash the vehicle chassis and underbody with fresh water after crossing.

## 8. Post-Crossing Checklist

After every water crossing, regardless of depth:

1. **Check air filter** — If wet, remove, shake dry, and replace. Do not run engine with wet air filter (it will restrict air and may damage engine).
2. **Check diffs and gearbox** — If the breathers were submerged, water may enter. Milky oil = water contamination. Change oil as soon as possible.
3. **Check inside vehicle** — Water on floor = potential electrical damage. Dry carpets and check connections.
4. **Pump brakes to dry** — Wet brakes have significantly reduced stopping power. Pump them while driving slowly until braking feels normal.
5. **Check radiator** — Mud, grass, and debris block airflow. Clean out any blockage.
6. **Check trailer plug** — If towing, ensure trailer plug is sealed or disconnected before crossing. Water in trailer wiring can cause short circuits.
7. **Grease fittings** — If you have grease points on driveline fittings, pump fresh grease to expel any water.
8. **Check lights** — Water may have entered light housings. Dry and reseal if needed.

---

## 9. When to Turn Back

The most important skill in water crossings is knowing when NOT to attempt. Turn back if:

- **Water is moving faster than walking pace** — You cannot stand in the current. The vehicle will be pushed sideways.
- **Depth above knee without snorkel** — You will hydro-lock the engine.
- **Bottomless mud or soft sand** — Tested with a stick, no firm bottom found.
- **Crocodiles present** (tropical north) — Do not enter the water. Wait or find an alternative.
- **Water is rising** — Leave immediately. Do not attempt a crossing.
- **No recovery vehicle present** — If you get stuck in deep water, you are alone. Stalling in a deep crossing can total the vehicle.
- **Gut feeling says no** — Listen to it. Outback travellers who survive are the ones who respect their instincts.
