# Fuel Management

Running out of fuel in the outback is not an inconvenience — it is a life-threatening emergency. With fuel stops often 300-800 km apart, and some tracks having no fuel for 1,000+ km, fuel management is the single most important planning factor for any outback trip.

---

## 1. Know Your Vehicle's Range

### Real-World Consumption

Manufacturer fuel consumption figures are measured in controlled conditions — not loaded 4WDs on corrugated roads with 100 kg of roof rack. Your real-world consumption will be higher.

| Terrain | Consumption Multiplier | Example: 12 L/100 km highway |
|---------|----------------------|------------------------------|
| Sealed highway | 1.0× | 12 L/100 km |
| Good gravel road | 1.1× | 13.2 L/100 km |
| Corrugated gravel | 1.3× | 15.6 L/100 km |
| Sandy track | 1.5× | 18 L/100 km |
| Soft sand / dunes | 2.0× | 24 L/100 km |
| Low range crawling | 2.0-3.0× | 24-36 L/100 km |

### Calculating Your Range

1. **Determine your usable fuel capacity** — Total tank capacity minus reserve (typically 5-10 L unusable in most tanks).
2. **Set a real-world baseline** — Fill up, record kms, fill up again. Do this on a loaded trip, not empty commuting.
3. **Apply the terrain multiplier** — For a mixed trip with gravel and sand, use a blended multiplier.
4. **Apply a safety margin** — Never plan to arrive at a refuel with less than 50 km of range remaining.

### Fuel Reserve Rules

| Reserve | When to Apply |
|---------|---------------|
| 100 km safety margin | Sealed roads with frequent stops |
| 100 km safety margin | Gravel roads with fuel stops 200-300 km apart |
| 200 km safety margin | Remote tracks with fuel stops 400-600 km apart |
| 250 km+ safety margin | Extreme remote tracks (Canning, Anne Beadell) |
| Minimum 50 km | ALWAYS — never arrive at a fuel pump on fumes |

## 2. Planning Fuel Stops

### Never Assume Fuel Is Available

Remote fuel stops do not guarantee fuel. Before relying on a fuel stop:

1. **Call ahead** — Check opening hours, availability, and whether they take card or cash.
2. **Check recent traveller reports** — ExplorOz, The Grey Nomads forum, or Facebook groups for current fuel availability.
3. **Check if there is a fuel strike or supply issue** — Outback roadhouses have run out of fuel on busy long weekends.
4. **Have a backup plan** — Know the next closest fuel stop and whether you can reach it.

### Outback Roadhouse Realities

| Factor | Reality |
|--------|---------|
| Hours | Limited and often erratic. Many close by 17:00 or earlier on weekends. |
| Payment | Cash or EFTPOS (if working). Some are cash-only. Carry $200-500 in cash. |
| Price | $2.50-$3.50 per litre. A full tank can cost $250-400. |
| Availability | May run out. May only have diesel. May be undelivered. |
| Sunday/public holiday | Often closed or very limited hours. |
| Ethanol-mixed fuel | Some remote stations sell E10. Check if your vehicle is compatible. |

### Fuel Stop Types

| Type | Reliability | Price | Notes |
|------|------------|-------|-------|
| Major roadhouse (BP, Caltex, Shell) | High | Moderate ($2.00-2.80/L) | Main highways, towns |
| Independent roadhouse | Moderate | High ($2.50-3.50/L) | Remote highways, limited hours |
| Remote station homestead | Low-Moderate | Very high ($3.00-4.00/L) | Call ahead. May sell from a drum. |
| Aboriginal community | Low | Varies | May not sell to visitors without permit. |
| Muster point/fuel cache | N/A | N/A | Pre-arranged fuel drop only. |

## 3. Carrying Extra Fuel

### Jerrys Cans

| Type | Pros | Cons |
|------|------|------|
| Military-style metal cans (20 L) | Durable, stackable, seal well, no vapour loss | Heavy (15 kg full), can rust |
| Plastic jerry cans (20 L) | Light, cheap, see-through level | Can crack, vapour loss over time, UV damage |
| Flat-pack fuel bladders | Save space, collapsible when empty | Expensive, puncture risk, hard to secure |
| Auxiliary fuel tank | Safest option, good capacity, no manual handling | Expensive ($1,000-2,500 installed), reduces ground clearance |

### Storage Rules

- **Outside the vehicle only** — Roof rack, rear carrier, or front bull bar mount. NEVER in the cabin (fumes are toxic and highly flammable).
- **Secure every can** — Ratchet straps rated for the load. A 20 kg jerry can in a rollover is a lethal projectile.
- **Load distribution** — Even weight side-to-side. Multiple cans distributed to avoid overloading one roof rack corner.
- **Ventilation** — Leave storage area open to airflow. Fumes accumulate.
- **Heat** — Metal cans get hot on roof racks. Allow space between cans for airflow. Plastic cans may expand/bulge in extreme heat.

### Pouring Safety

- **Fuel is heavy** — 20 L of fuel = 15 kg. Carry a good pouring spout to avoid spills.
- **Static electricity** — Petrol (especially) can ignite from static discharge. Touch the vehicle body before pouring. Use metal cans with conductive spouts.
- **Grounding** — Pour on the ground, not on the vehicle paintwork. Fuel destroys clear coat.
- **Funnels** — Carry a proper funnel. Mr Funnel or similar for pre-filtering.

### How Much Extra to Carry

| Trip Type | Rule of Thumb |
|-----------|---------------|
| Highway trip with regular stops | 1 jerry can (20 L) for peace of mind |
| Remote highway (Oodnadatta Track) | 2-3 cans (40-60 L) |
| Desert crossing (Simpson, Tanami) | 3-5 cans (60-100 L) |
| Extreme remote (Canning Stock Route) | 6-10 cans (120-200 L) + fuel drop |

**Formula:** Carry 30-50% MORE than your planned maximum leg distance.

### Specific Track Requirements

| Track | Longest Fuel Leg | Minimum Extra | Notes |
|-------|-----------------|---------------|-------|
| Simpson Desert (French Line) | ~550 km Mt Dare to Birdsville | 40-60 L | Sand doubles consumption |
| Canning Stock Route | ~850 km Wiluna to Kunawarritji | 80-120 L | Fuel drop at Well 23 |
| Anne Beadell Highway | ~770 km Coober Pedy to Ilkurlka | 60-80 L | Fuel available at Ilkurlka (call ahead) |
| Cape York | ~180 km between fuel stops | 20 L | Good fuel availability at frequent stops |
| Gibb River Road | ~250 km between fuel | 20 L | Adequate stops, but check hours |
| Tanami Track | ~580 km Tilmouth Well to Halls Creek | 60 L | Mid-point fuel at Rabbit Flat (call ahead) |
| Oodnadatta Track | ~320 km Marla to Oodnadatta | 40 L | Fuel at Oodnadatta plus remote stations |
| Strzelecki Track | ~340 km Lyndhurst to Innamincka | 40 L | Fuel at Innamincka plus Moomba |

## 4. Diesel vs Petrol

| Factor | Diesel | Petrol |
|--------|--------|--------|
| Fuel economy off-road | Better (30-40% better than equivalent petrol) | Worse |
| Safety carrying | Lower flash point (62°C vs -43°C). Much safer to carry. | Highly volatile. Fumes + heat = fire risk. |
| Availability | Widely available in remote areas (standard for stations, mines, roadhouses) | Less common. Some remote stops diesel-only. |
| Cost per litre | Similar or slightly cheaper | Similar or slightly higher |
| Octane/quality | Less sensitive to stale fuel | Stale fuel causes rough running, pinging |
| Cold starting | Needs glow plugs below 0°C | Starts easily in all outback conditions |

### For Petrol Vehicles

- **Carry octane booster** — Some remote fuel stations sell low-octane or stale fuel. Octane booster compensates.
- **Avoid ethanol-blended fuel (E10)** if possible — Ethanol absorbs water, has lower energy density, and can damage older fuel systems.
- **Petrol goes stale** — In 4-6 weeks, stored petrol degrades. A trip across the Simpson may use 2-week-old fuel from your jerries. Not ideal but acceptable.

### For Diesel Vehicles

- **Diesel is more tolerant of storage** — Lasts 6-12 months with biocide treatment.
- **Biocide additive** — Carry diesel biocide for long trips. Bacteria + water = algae in diesel = blocked filters.
- **Winter diesel** — In southern Australian winter, remote diesel may be summer-grade (waxes in cold). Not a problem in the outback where temps rarely drop below 0°C except on high ground.

## 5. Fuel Contamination

Remote fuel is not the same quality as city fuel. Contamination is common.

| Contaminant | Source | Effect |
|-------------|--------|--------|
| Water | Old underground tanks, condensation, rain ingress | Engine stalls, injector damage, corrosion |
| Dirt/sediment | Old tanks, drums, jerry cans | Blocked fuel filter, injector damage |
| Algae (diesel) | Bacteria + water in diesel tank | Black slime in fuel, blocked filters |
| Stale petrol | Old fuel (3+ months) | Rough idle, loss of power, hesitation |
| Low octane | Wrong grade supplied to station | Pinging, knock damage under load |

### Prevention

| Item | Why |
|------|-----|
| Mr Funnel or pre-filter | Filters water and debris when pouring from jerry can into vehicle |
| Spare fuel filter | Replace if engine runs rough after a remote fill |
| Fuel additive | Biocide (diesel), stabiliser (petrol), octane booster (petrol) |
| Water indicator paste | Paint on dipstick = water in bottom of tank |
| Check facility quality | Busy roadhouses = fresher fuel. Quiet ones = stale fuel. |

### Signs of Bad Fuel

1. Rough idle after filling
2. Loss of power
3. Engine stalling
4. Excessive smoke
5. Engine running hotter than normal

If you suspect bad fuel: replace the fuel filter immediately. If symptoms persist, the fuel in your tank may need to be drained.

## 6. Driving for Economy

### Speed

| Speed | Fuel Consumption (relative) | Why |
|-------|----------------------------|-----|
| 80 km/h | Baseline | Best economy for a loaded 4WD |
| 90 km/h | +5-8% | Air resistance starts to increase |
| 100 km/h | +15-20% | Significant drag increase |
| 110 km/h | +25-35% | Massive fuel penalty from air resistance |

**On corrugated roads:** slower speeds (60-70 km/h) actually improve economy because the vehicle is not bouncing (losing momentum) and the engine is not working as hard.

### Load Management

| Factor | Fuel Penalty | Mitigation |
|--------|-------------|------------|
| Roof rack (empty) | +5-10% at highway speed | Remove when not needed |
| Roof rack (loaded, 50-80 kg) | +15-25% at highway speed | Use cargo barrier + internal storage instead |
| Roof rack (heavy, 100+ kg) | +20-30% at highway speed | Reconsider — put inside or in trailer |
| Opening windows | +5-10% at highway speeds | Use air conditioning instead (surprisingly better) |
| Air conditioning | ~5% penalty | Use it in extreme heat — dehydration costs more than fuel |
| Weight (per 100 kg) | ~3-5% extra consumption | Travel as light as possible |
| Under-inflated tyres | Significant rolling resistance increase | Keep tyres at correct pressure for surface |

### Idling

- Engine idling uses approximately 1.5-2.5 L/hour
- A 30-minute lunch break sitting in the running vehicle = 1 L of fuel wasted
- Turn the engine off whenever stopped for more than 1 minute
- Exception: in extreme heat (45°C+), it may be necessary to run the AC. Balance fuel consumption against heatstroke risk.

### Terrain Management

Signal your fuel consumption will spike if you encounter unexpected terrain. On the move:
- Soft sand on an unexpected detour → drop tyre pressures, use low range, maintain momentum
- Extended corrugations → slow down (60 km/h vs 80 km/h may save 20% fuel)
- Alternative route using rougher country → calculate the fuel cost vs distance saved

## 7. Emergency Fuel Scenarios

### Running Out of Fuel

**STAY WITH THE VEHICLE.** This is the golden rule of outback survival, and it applies here too. A vehicle with no fuel is still a shelter, a signal platform, and a known search target.

### Response Protocol

1. **Assess** — How far to the nearest fuel stop? Is it reachable on foot?
2. **Conserve** — Turn off all non-essential electronics. Conserve drinking water. Create shade.
3. **Signal** — Activate hazard lights. If you have a PLB, this is the time to use it.
4. **Decision** — Use the [Vehicle Breakdown](01-immediate/09-outback/vehicle-breakdown) framework: stay or go?

### Sending One Vehicle for Fuel

If you are travelling in a convoy and one vehicle is out of fuel:

1. The vehicle out of fuel STAYS PUT (with as many people as practical)
2. One vehicle goes for fuel with enough containers for ALL vehicles
3. The recovery vehicle takes as many jerry cans as it can carry safely
4. Everyone left behind: shelter, shade, water, signals, UHF listening watch
5. Agree on a return ETA — if not back by that time, it is an emergency

### Walking for Fuel

| Factor | Verdict |
|--------|---------|
| Is walking for fuel ever the right answer? | Almost never |
| When would it be? | You KNOW exactly where fuel is, how far, and have sufficient water. Even then, probably wrong. |
| Golden rule | Stay with the vehicle. A PLB call costs $300-500. Walking costs your life. |

### Siphoning

Modern vehicles have anti-siphon devices in the fuel filler neck. Siphoning may not be possible. If you have jerry cans, use them. If you don't, you were not properly prepared.

### Rationing Fuel

If you have some fuel but not enough to reach the next stop:
- Reduce speed to 60 km/h to maximise economy
- Remove roof load if possible
- Increase tyre pressures to highway pressure (35-40 PSI) for the extra distance — the drag from under-inflated tyres costs you range
- Only run air conditioning when critically needed
- Plan the most direct route, even if it is rougher
