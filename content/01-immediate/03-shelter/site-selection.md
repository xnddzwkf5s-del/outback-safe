# Site Selection

The difference between a good shelter and a good shelter in the wrong location is the difference between safety and disaster. A perfectly built debris hut in a dry creek bed will wash away; a crude lean-to on a well-chosen site will keep you alive.

---

## The Five Fatal Site Mistakes

| Mistake | Why it kills | How to avoid |
|---------|-------------|-------------|
| **Dry creek bed / drainage line** | Flash flooding | Build on elevated ground; check for flood debris in trees |
| **Under dead trees or limbs** | Falling branches ("widowmakers") | Look up. Any dead or leaning trees within falling distance = move |
| **Ridge top** | Wind, lightning | Build below the crest on the lee side |
| **Animal trail** | Predator or large animal encounters at night | Do not build across game trails |
| **Insect nest proximity** | Wasps, ants, termites | Scan ground and trees within 20 m before building |

---

## Hazard Checklist (Pre-Build)

**Spend 2 minutes on this before you drive the first stake:**


### Look Up — Overhead Hazards
- [ ] No dead trees or dead limbs within falling distance
- [ ] No leaning trees
- [ ] No loose rock on overhanging ledges
- [ ] No bee/wasp nests in trees
- [ ] No coconut palms (if in SEA/coastal — falling coconuts kill more people than sharks)
- [ ] No power lines (post-disaster scenarios)

### Look Down — Ground Hazards
- [ ] No ant nests (bull ants in Australia, fire ants in SEA/CN)
- [ ] No snake holes
- [ ] No termite mounds
- [ ] No animal tracks running through the site
- [ ] No poisonous plants (stinging tree in Australia, poison ivy relatives in China)
- [ ] No sharp objects, broken glass, or metal debris (post-disaster)

### Look Around — Environmental Hazards
- [ ] Not in a drainage line or dry creek bed
- [ ] Not in an avalanche runout zone (snow country)
- [ ] Not in a landslide-prone area (deforested slopes, steep loose soil)
- [ ] Not under cliffs with loose rock
- [ ] Above the high-water mark (check tree trunks for flood debris lines)
- [ ] At least 50 m from water's edge in crocodile country (northern Australia, SEA)

### Check Ground Conditions
- [ ] Ground is well-drained (sandy/loamy, not clay that becomes bog)
- [ ] Press foot into ground — water does not seep up
- [ ] Ground is flat enough to sleep on (or can be levelled)
- [ ] No persistent dampness or rotting vegetation (indicates poor drainage)

## The Ideal Site — Seven Criteria


### 1. Water Proximity

| Distance | Pros | Cons |
|----------|------|------|
| **0–30 m** | Very convenient | Flood risk, mosquito density, animal encounters at water's edge |
| **30–100 m** | Convenient, safer | Requires water containers or multiple trips |
| **100+ m** | Safest | Significant effort to carry water |

- **Above the flood line** — look for debris (leaves, sticks) caught in tree branches — that is the high-water mark. Build above it.
- **Not directly at the water's edge** — animals use water sources at dawn and dusk. You do not want to be on their path.

### 2. Drainage Assessment


**Quick drainage test:**
1. Dig a small hole (15 cm deep, 15 cm wide)
2. Fill with water
3. Time how long it takes to drain

| Drain time | Soil type | Suitability |
|------------|-----------|------------|
| < 5 minutes | Sand/gravel | Excellent drainage — may be too dry (far from water) |
| 5–30 minutes | Sandy loam | Ideal — drains well, holds some moisture |
| 30–60 minutes | Clay loam | Adequate — dig drainage trench on uphill side |
| > 60 minutes | Heavy clay | Poor — site will become bog in rain; move to higher/drier ground |

**Monsoon note (SEA, northern Australia):** In monsoon conditions, drainage is everything. A shelter without drainage in a tropical downpour will flood within minutes. Dig a drainage trench at least 20 cm deep and 30 cm wide on the uphill side of the shelter.

### 3. Wind Pattern Indicators

**How to determine prevailing wind direction (no tools):**

| Method | How to do it | Reliability |
|--------|-------------|------------|
| **Wet finger** | Wet your finger and hold it up — the cold side faces the wind | Good |
| **Dust/grass toss** | Throw dry dust, grass, or leaves — wind carries them | Good |
| **Tree growth** | Branches are often denser on the leeward side (less wind stress) | Moderate |
| **Flagging trees** | Trees with branches growing only on one side indicate consistent strong wind from the opposite direction | Good (coastal/mountain) |
| **Cloud movement** | Watch low clouds — they move with the prevailing wind | Good (daytime) |
| **Sand/snow drifts** | Drifts form on the lee (downwind) side of obstacles | Good (desert/snow) |

**Regional prevailing winds:**
- **Australia:** South/southeast in most regions; northerly in tropical north during wet season
- **Southeast Asia:** Monsoonal — northeast (dry) and southwest (wet) depending on season
- **Southern China:** North/northwest in winter, south/southeast in summer
- **Northern China:** Northwest in winter, southeast in summer

**Build shelter with entrance facing away from prevailing wind.** In cold climates, also aim for east-facing to catch morning sun.

### 4. Sun Exposure

| Climate | Ideal sun exposure | Shelter orientation |
|---------|-------------------|-------------------|
| Cold | East-facing — catches morning sun, warms early | Entrance faces east |
| Temperate | East or north-east — morning warmth | Entrance faces east/northeast |
| Hot/tropical | Shaded all day — under canopy | Under dense trees, south side of hill (southern hemisphere) |
| Desert | Maximum shade — under trees, rock overhangs, east side of large features | Deepest shade available |

### 5. Natural Hazards (Detailed)


**Region-specific hazards:**

| Region | Key hazards |
|--------|------------|
| **Australian bush** | Snakes, falling limbs (river red gums notorious), goannas, bull ants |
| **SEA jungle** | Cobras, kraits, centipedes, leeches, elephant trails |
| **Chinese countryside** | Abandoned wells (covered or not), unstable slopes after rain, paddy field leeches |
| **Australian desert** | Mulga snakes (king brown), extreme temperature swings (40°C day → 5°C night) |
| **Coastal (all regions)** | Saltwater crocodiles (northern Australia, SEA), tidal surge, cliff erosion |
| **Alpine/snow** | Avalanche terrain, cornice collapse, snow-load tree fall |

**Crocodile country specific (northern Australia, parts of SEA):**
- Stay at least 50 m from water's edge
- Do not camp near estuaries, mangroves, or tidal creeks
- Crocodiles hunt at night — they can outrun a human in a short sprint
- Freshwater crocs are generally harmless (unless provoked); saltwater crocs are aggressive

### 6. Resources

Your site should be near:


| Resource | Ideal distance | Why |
|----------|---------------|-----|
| **Building materials** | 10–30 m | Close enough to carry, far enough not to deplete shelter area |
| **Firewood** | 20–50 m | Dead standing wood preferred; gather from different directions over time |
| **Water** | 30–100 m | See criterion 1 |
| **Food sources** | Identify but do not camp in them | Preserve resources; harvest from wider area |

Do not build your shelter IN the middle of your resource area — you deplete what is immediately around you and degrade your own site.

### 7. Visibility vs. Concealment

| Objective | Site choice | Signal strategy |
|-----------|------------|----------------|
| **Signalling for rescue** | High visibility — open area, near clearing, high ground | Maintain cleared signal area 50–100 m from shelter |
| **Avoiding detection** | Concealed — under canopy, behind terrain, away from skyline | Camouflage shelter; no visible smoke; minimize tracks |
| **Balanced approach** | Concealed shelter, visible signal area nearby | Best of both — safe from weather/threats, rescuable |

If you want rescue, maintain a signal area within sight but not at the shelter itself.

## Site Selection by Environment

### Australian Bush


- **Good:** Ridge saddles (below the crest), gentle slopes, near creek lines but above flood level
- **Bad:** Dry creek beds (flash floods), under river red gums (they drop limbs), near termite mounds (millions of termites, attract echidnas/goannas)
- **Bushfire escape route:** Always identify at least one escape direction — downhill toward cleared or already-burned ground

### Southeast Asian Jungle

- **Good:** Ridge lines (drier, breezier, fewer insects), near streams but 3+ m above water level
- **Bad:** Valley floors (damp, mosquito-dense, flood-prone), elephant trails, bamboo groves (rats and snakes)
- **Specific:** Leeches at ground level; centipedes with painful venomous bite; cobras and kraits — check sleeping area thoroughly

### Chinese Countryside

- **Good:** South-facing slopes (more sun, drier), near small streams, abandoned agricultural terraces (flat, well-drained)
- **Bad:** Paddy fields (wet, leeches, pesticides), landslide-prone slopes (deforested hills)
- **Specific:** Abandoned wells (covered or not), unstable slopes after rain

### Australian Desert / Outback

- **Good:** Near rock outcrops (shade, water runoff, wind protection), under desert oaks or mulga
- **Bad:** Open flats (no windbreak, full sun), dry lake beds (salt, no water)
- **Specific:** Temperature swing up to 35°C in 24 hours — prepare for both extremes

### Coastal

- **Good:** Behind dunes (windbreak), above high tide line (check debris line), near fresh water seeps
- **Bad:** On the beach (wind, sand in everything, tidal surge), below cliffs (rockfall, erosion)
- **Croc country:** See croc warning above — saltwater crocodiles in northern Australia and parts of SEA

## Quick Site Assessment (2-Minute Drill)

Before you commit to a site, spend 2 minutes checking:


{% steps() %}
Look up — 360° scan for dead trees or limbs; Look down — scan ground for insect nests, holes, tracks, droppings; Walk the perimeter — 20 m radius, check drainage, hazards, resources; Press foot into ground — does water seep up? If yes, move uphill; Check wind — which direction? Does the site have a windbreak?; Estimate distance to water — is there a visible water source within sight and sound?; Mark the high-water/debris line — look at nearby tree trunks and vegetation for flood debris; If all checks pass: build here; If any fail: move. There is always another site.
{% end %}

## Multi-Day Camp Expansion

If you stay more than one night:


| Zone | Distance from shelter | Notes |
|------|----------------------|-------|
| **Latrine** | 50+ m, downhill/downstream from water | Dig 15–20 cm deep; cover after use |
| **Food storage** | 20+ m from sleeping area | Hung from tree branch (bear/croc/animal safety) |
| **Firewood storage** | 3–5 m from shelter | Under cover if possible; not against shelter wall |
| **Tool/work area** | 5–10 m | Separate from sleeping area to keep bedding clean |
| **Signal area** | 50–100 m | Maintained clearing visible from sky/ridge |
| **Rubbish pit** | 50+ m, downhill | Bury or burn; do not attract animals to camp |

## 🇦🇺 Australian Site Selection — Essential Considerations

> **Australia Note:** This page has been adapted for Australian conditions.

### Australian Site Considerations

| Priority | What to check | Why |
|----------|---------------|-----|
| **Flood level** | Check for debris caught in tree branches — that's the high-water mark from the last flood | Wet season tropical rivers can rise **metres overnight**. A dry creek bed can turn into a raging torrent in hours. Build well above the highest debris line. |
| **Gum trees overhead** | Avoid camping under eucalypts, especially River Red Gums (*E. camaldulensis*) | Eucalypts are notorious for dropping limbs without warning — called "widow-makers." They shed branches in hot weather, windy conditions, or even calm days. |
| **Hanging dead branches** | Check within a 20 m radius overhead | Any dead branch that falls can kill or seriously injure. Clear them or move. |

### Australian Wind Patterns

| Wind | Season | Characteristics |
|------|--------|-----------------|
| **Summer northerlies** | Dec–Feb (southern AU) | Hot, dry winds from the interior. Carry dust and heat. Build shelter entrance away from the north. |
| **Winter southerlies** | Jun–Aug (southern AU) | Cold winds from the Southern Ocean. Build shelter entrance away from the south. |
| **Afternoon sea breezes** | Coastal AU (summer) | Cool wind from the ocean, arrives mid-afternoon. Predictable. Use for cooling or ventilation. |
| **Desert katabatic winds** | Arid AU (night) | Cool air flowing down from ranges at night. Can be strong and cold. Windbreak essential in desert camps. |
| **Wet season monsoonal winds** | Nov–Apr (northern AU) | Strong north-westerly winds bringing rain. Build shelter with back to the wind. |

### Australian Ground Hazards — Pre-Build Check

| Hazard | Region | What to look for |
|--------|--------|-----------------|
| **Bull ant nests** | All AU | Distinctive large mound (sometimes hidden). Do not camp near — painful bite, hours of burning pain. |
| **Funnel-web spider burrows** | NSW, Qld (east coast) | Look for silk trip-lines radiating from a burrow entrance in the ground. The Sydney funnel-web is deadly. Do not camp on ground with burrows. |
| **Snake tracks in sand** | Arid and semi-arid AU | Snakes use sandy areas to move. Look for S-shaped tracks in sand near your site. |
| **Stinging tree** (*Dendrocnide moroides*) | Rainforest Qld, northern NSW | Heart-shaped leaves with serrated edges. Fine silica hairs cause extreme pain. Check for these trees near your site. |
| **Termite mounds** | All AU | Do not camp near termite mounds. They attract echidnas, goannas, and other animals digging for termites at night. |
| **Grass tree clumps** | Coastal heath, ranges | Grass trees burn incredibly fiercely. If there is any fire risk, do not camp near dense grass tree stands. |

### Seasonal Australian Hazards by Region

| Season | Region | Specific hazard | Site selection strategy |
|--------|--------|----------------|------------------------|
| **Wet season** (Nov–Apr) | Tropical north (Qld, NT) | Flash flooding, crocs moving into new areas, monsoon rain | Camp on highest ground available. 50 m+ from water. Elevated sleeping platform. |
| **Bushfire season** (summer/autumn) | Southern AU (NSW, VIC, SA, WA, TAS) | Catastrophic fire danger days, ember attack, radiant heat | Camp near an escape route toward cleared ground, burned areas, or water. Identify two escape directions. Avoid ridgetops in fire-prone areas. |
| **Winter** (Jun–Aug) | Desert (central AU) | Extreme cold at night (0–5°C), frost, strong winds | Wind protection is priority #1. Build a low, enclosed shelter. Insulate ground bed thickly. |
| **Spring** (Sep–Nov) | All AU | Snake season — snakes emerging from hibernation, aggressive and active | Clear ground cover around camp. Check bedding and boots daily. |

### Bushfire Escape Route Planning

If camping in southern Australia during bushfire season (Oct–Mar):

{% steps() %}
Identify the prevailing wind direction — bushfires travel fastest with the wind; Identify two escape routes — ideally at 90° to the expected fire front direction (never run directly away from a fire; run at an angle to the fire front); Your escape routes should lead to: (1) already-burned ground (the safest place in a bushfire), (2) a large river crossing, (3) a wide clearing with minimal fuel; Do NOT camp in a narrow valley or box canyon — fire can trap you; Have a woollen blanket or drenching water available to cover yourself if caught.
{% end %}

### Water Proximity — Australian Crocodile-Specific Rules

In northern Australia (north of Rockhampton on the east coast, north of Broome on the west):

- **Minimum 50 m from water's edge** — saltwater crocodiles can launch from water.
- **Do not camp on sandbars or riverbanks** — crocs patrol these areas.
- **Do not camp near mangroves or tidal creeks** — prime croc habitat.
- **Nocturnal hunting** — crocs hunt at night. Stay well back from water after dark.
- **Billabong edges** — crocs in billabongs are often submerged and invisible. Assume every northern waterway has crocs.
