# Start Here — Immediate Priorities

If you are reading this **during an active emergency**, breathe. Panic kills. Focus on the next right action.

This page is your triage tool. Work through it in order. Do not skip ahead.

---

## The Survival Rule of Threes

You can survive approximately:

- **3 minutes** without air
- **3 hours** without shelter in extreme conditions
- **3 days** without water
- **3 weeks** without food

These numbers are maximums, not targets. In heat or cold, shelter becomes urgent long before the 3-hour mark. Dehydration impairs judgement within 24 hours. Let the rule guide your priorities; let common sense guide your urgency.

---

## First Hour — STOP

**STOP** is the universal immediate-action drill:

| Letter | Action | What it means |
|--------|--------|---------------|
| **S** | **Stop** | Do not move. Do not act on impulse. Take three slow breaths. |
| **T** | **Think** | What just happened? Where are you? What threats remain? |
| **O** | **Observe** | Scan 360°. Look for fire, unstable structures, rising water, injured people. Listen. Smell for gas or smoke. |
| **P** | **Plan** | Pick the highest-priority action from the checklist below. Execute it. Then pick the next. |

{% info() %}
**Why STOP first:** In every documented survival case, the survivors who paused to think — even for 30 seconds — made better decisions than those who acted on adrenaline. You have time. Use it.
{% end %}

## First-Hour Checklist

Work through these in strict order. Each item has a link to the detailed guide.

### 1. ✅ Assess and Treat Life-Threatening Injuries

Check yourself, then others. Look for:

- **Severe bleeding** — Bright red spurting (arterial) or dark steady flow. Apply direct pressure immediately. → [Severe Bleeding](01-immediate/01-first-aid/severe-bleeding)
- **Breathing difficulty** — Check airway. Clear obstructions. → [CPR & Airway](01-immediate/01-first-aid/cpr)
- **Shock** — Pale, cold, clammy skin. Rapid pulse. Confusion. → [Shock Management](01-immediate/01-first-aid/shock)
- **Spinal injury** — Do not move the person unless they are in immediate danger. → [Fractures & Immobilisation](01-immediate/01-first-aid/fractures)

**If someone is not breathing and has no pulse**, begin compression-only CPR if you are untrained. → [CPR](01-immediate/01-first-aid/cpr)

### 2. ✅ Get Out of Immediate Danger

- Fire, gas leak, unstable building, rising water, rockfall zone
- Move the minimum distance necessary to reach safety
- Take injured people with you only if you can do so safely

### 3. ✅ Find or Create Shelter

Exposure kills faster than thirst. In cold, wet, or extreme heat, shelter is your second priority after bleeding control.

- Use what exists: vehicles, buildings, caves, overhangs, dense tree cover
- If nothing exists, build a debris shelter or lean-to immediately → [Immediate Shelter](01-immediate/03-shelter/immediate-shelter)
- Site selection matters: avoid ridgelines (wind), dry creek beds (flash floods), and dead trees (falling branches) → [Site Selection](01-immediate/03-shelter/site-selection)

### 4. ✅ Start a Fire

Fire provides warmth, water purification, signalling, predator deterrent, and morale.

- Gather tinder, kindling, and fuel **before** attempting ignition → [Fire Basics](01-immediate/04-fire/fire-basics)
- Have at least two ignition methods if possible
- If you have no tools, learn friction methods → [Friction Fire](01-immediate/04-fire/friction-methods)

### 5. ✅ Find Water

You need 2–4 litres per person per day. More in heat.

- Look downhill: water flows to the lowest point
- Follow animal trails converging downward
- Green vegetation in dry areas indicates ground water → [Finding Water](01-immediate/02-water/finding-water)
- **Treat all found water before drinking.** Boiling is the gold standard → [Purification](01-immediate/02-water/purification)
- If you cannot find open water, build a solar still → [Solar Still](01-immediate/02-water/solar-still)

### 6. ✅ Signal for Help

If rescue is possible, make yourself visible.

- Three fires in a triangle (international distress signal) → [Signal Fire](01-immediate/06-signaling/signal-fire)
- Ground-to-air signals: SOS (three short, three long, three short) or large markings visible from sky → [Ground Signals](01-immediate/06-signaling/ground-signals)
- Improvised mirrors: any reflective surface, aimed at aircraft or distant searchers → [Signal Mirror](01-immediate/06-signaling/signal-mirror)

### 7. ✅ Secure Emergency Food

Food is the **lowest** first-week priority. You will not starve in 7 days. But if you have water, shelter, and fire established, begin:

- Universal edibility testing for unknown plants → [Edibility Test](01-immediate/05-food-emergency/universal-edibility-test)
- Insects are the safest wild protein → [Insects](01-immediate/05-food-emergency/insects)
- Ration what you have → [Emergency Rationing](01-immediate/05-food-emergency/emergency-rationing)

## First Day Priorities

By end of Day 1, aim to have:

| Priority | Target |
|----------|--------|
| Medical | All injuries assessed and stabilised |
| Shelter | Waterproof, windproof, insulated sleeping area |
| Water | At least 4 L per person located and purified |
| Fire | Sustained fire with fuel stockpile for 24 hours |
| Signals | At least one signalling method prepared |
| Group | Roles assigned if with others → [Group Management](01-immediate/07-group-management/) |
| Security | Perimeter assessed → [Security](01-immediate/08-security/) |

## First Week Overview

### Days 1–2: Survive
- Stabilise injuries
- Secure shelter and fire
- Find and purify water
- Signal for rescue

### Days 3–4: Consolidate
- Improve shelter (insulation, drainage)
- Build water storage capacity
- Expand fire fuel stockpile
- Begin systematic foraging
- Establish watch rotation if in a group

### Days 5–7: Expand
- Explore wider area (mark your trail)
- Identify reliable water sources
- Cache firewood and food
- Improve signalling (larger ground signals, multiple signal fires prepared)
- Document what you've learned about the area

## If You Are With a Group

Group survival is fundamentally different from solo survival. Read the [Group Management section](01-immediate/07-group-management/) thoroughly, but for now:

- **Assign a leader.** Vote if possible. Inaction kills.
- **Inventory skills.** Who has medical training? Who can start a fire? Who knows the area?
- **Keep everyone busy.** Idle hands breed panic.
- **Ration early.** Fair distribution prevents conflict later.

---

## If You Are Alone

- **Stay visible.** Solo survivors are harder to spot. Maximise your signalling.
- **Conserve energy.** Every calorie matters. Rest during the hottest part of the day.
- **Maintain routine.** A schedule — wake, water run, fire check, forage, signal, sleep — keeps the mind focused.
- **Talk to yourself aloud.** It sounds odd, but verbalising decisions reduces panic and improves decision quality.

## Equipment You Should Have Right Now

If you have a kit, prioritise these items. If you don't, each section covers improvisation.

| Item | Why |
|------|-----|
| Knife or multi-tool | Cuts, carves, pries, digs. The single most versatile tool. |
| Fire starter (lighter, ferro rod, matches) | Fire saves lives. Carry at least two methods. |
| Water container (metal preferred) | Metal can boil water. Plastic cannot. |
| First-aid kit | Stop bleeding, prevent infection. |
| Emergency blanket or tarp | Shelter, rain catch, ground insulation, signalling. |
| Whistle | Carries further than shouting. Three blasts = distress. |
| Cordage (paracord, bank line) | Shelter building, snares, repairs. |

## Mental Survival

The most dangerous thing in a survival situation is your own mind.

**Common psychological traps:**

- **Panic** — Adrenaline impulse to run or act without thinking. Counter: STOP drill.
- **Denial** — "This isn't really happening." Counter: Accept the situation. It is real. Act accordingly.
- **Despair** — "There's no point." Counter: Set one small goal. Achieve it. Set another.
- **Loneliness** — Solo survivors risk giving up. Counter: Talk aloud, keep a mental log, create small rituals.

**The will to survive** is consistently cited by rescue professionals as the single biggest factor in who lives and who dies. More important than gear. More important than skills. Cultivate it deliberately.

## Quick Reference — Instant Actions

If you've read nothing else and have seconds to act:

1. **Stop moving.** Look around. Identify the biggest threat.
2. **Stop bleeding.** Direct pressure. Anything.
3. **Get dry and warm.** Shelter first, fire second.
4. **Find water.** Downhill. Green vegetation. Animal trails.
5. **Signal.** Three of anything = distress.
