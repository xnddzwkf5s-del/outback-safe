Shields

A shield is your first line of defense — the thing that stops a weapon before it reaches your armour or body. In a survival context, a shield is much easier to make than armour and arguably more important. A well-made shield can stop arrows, deflect spear thrusts, and provide cover from thrown rocks.

Shield Covering

A wood shield needs a facing to prevent it from splitting along the grain when struck:

  • Rawhide (best) — Stretched wet over the shield face and edges, shrinks as it dries. Creates a tough, unified surface. Edge binding is especially critical.
  • Canvas / heavy linen — Glued to the face with hide glue or modern adhesive. Not as tough as rawhide but easier.
  • Leather — Thin leather glued and stitched over the face.

Edge Protection

The edge of a shield takes the most abuse — weapons are deflected here. Reinforce it:

  • Rawhide binding — A strip of wet rawhide stitched around the entire edge. Shrinks as it dries, gripping the rim tightly.
  • Metal rim — A thin iron or brass strip nailed or riveted around the edge. Heavy but very durable.
  • Leather binding — Thick leather strip stitched on. Adequate, easier to make than rawhide.

Wicker / Rattan Shield (SEA/AU)

Traditional throughout Southeast Asia and the Pacific Islands. Lightweight, surprisingly tough, and uses renewable materials.

📋 Materials
  • Rattan vines (Calamus spp.) — abundant in SEA rainforests — or flexible willow/pliable saplings for frame
  • Split rattan or thin bamboo strips for weaving
  • Wooden handle (hardwood)

A well-made rattan shield 70 cm in diameter weighs only 2–3 kg. It will turn a spear thrust (the point may penetrate the weave but the shield absorbs the energy) and stop arrows from low-poundage bows. It will not stop a crossbow bolt or a high-poundage war arrow.

ℹ️ Information
**AU Note:** Aboriginal Australian shields were traditionally made from hardwood (mulga, ironbark) carved from a single piece, or from bark (e.g., stringybark) folded and shaped. Hardwood parrying shields (*murnong*, *mulga wood*) are narrow (10–20 cm wide) and used actively to deflect spears and clubs rather than to hide behind. They are not passive shields — they are wielded dynamically.

Shield Maintenance

  • Keep dry when not in use — Wet wood warps, glued joints separate, rawhide softens
  • Re-paint annually — The paint is more than decoration; it's weatherproofing
  • Check edge binding regularly — Rewet and tighten rawhide if it stretches
  • Check grip and straps — Leather rots, rivets work loose. Check before any serious use
  • Store flat or hung by the guige — Leaning a shield on its edge causes warping

Using a Shield (Basic Principles)

Centre-Grip Round Shield

  • Don't hide behind it — The shield is an active tool. Move it to intercept attacks, not a static barrier.
  • Keep it extended — A shield held close to the body protects little. Extend it toward the threat — this closes distance the weapon must travel and exposes the opponent's hand to counter-strike.
  • Edge-forward can be offensive — The rim of a shield can strike, push, and trap an opponent's weapon.

Strap-Grip Kite/Heater Shield

  • Cover the body in a fighting stance — The shield should cover from the left shoulder to left knee (for a right-handed fighter).
  • "Present" the shield — Extend slightly forward. This protects the forward leg and creates a barrier the opponent must work around.
  • Keep it high enough to see over — The top rim should be at or just below eye level.

Against Arrows

  • Close the distance — The shield is most effective when moving. Stand still and the archer finds gaps.
  • Kneel behind a kite shield — Kneeling presents a smaller target. The bottom point of a kite shield touches the ground, covering the legs.
  • Arrow penetration test — Test your shield against your own bow at expected engagement range. Know what it will and won't stop.
