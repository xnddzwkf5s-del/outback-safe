Crossbows

The crossbow is a bow mounted on a stock with a mechanical trigger. It stores drawn energy in the locked position — unlike a hand bow, you do not need to hold the draw while aiming. This makes it far easier to use accurately, at the cost of slower reloading.

Why Crossbows?

Advantages Over Hand Bows

  • Easier to learn — A novice can shoot a crossbow competently in an afternoon
  • Holds aim indefinitely — No muscle fatigue from holding draw
  • Higher draw weight possible — Mechanical spanning allows 150–1200 lb (670–5300 N) draw weights
  • Less physical requirement — Children, elderly, and injured can use crossbows effectively
  • Steady aim — Stock and trigger like a firearm

Disadvantages

  • Slow to reload — 2–4 shots per minute vs. 10–15 for a hand bow
  • Heavier — The stock adds weight
  • More complex to manufacture — Trigger mechanism requires metalwork
  • Shorter power stroke — The prod (bow) has a shorter draw length, so energy is less efficient than a hand bow of equal draw weight

Prod (Bow) Construction

The prod is the bow portion of the crossbow. It is shorter and much stiffer than a hand bow.

Materials

Material Draw Weight Capability Manufacturing Difficulty
Wood (self-bow prod) Up to 150 lb (670 N) ★★
Wood + sinew backing Up to 200 lb (890 N) ★★★
Horn composite (horn belly, wood core, sinew back) 200–1200 lb (890–5300 N) ★★★★★
Steel (spring steel prod) 150–800 lb (670–3600 N) ★★★★
Bamboo laminate Up to 150 lb (670 N) ★★★

Wood Prod

A wooden prod is the simplest to make. Use the best bow woods (yew, osage orange, lemonwood — see Bows page). The prod should be: - Length: 60–90 cm (shorter = heavier draw for a given thickness) - Width at centre: 4–6 cm - Thickness: 2–3 cm at centre, tapering to 1 cm at tips - Shape: Deflexed (curved away from the string when unstrung)

Steel Prod (If Forging Capability Exists)

A steel prod made from spring steel (or salvaged leaf spring from a vehicle) is immensely powerful and durable.

Trigger Mechanism

The trigger holds the drawn string and releases it smoothly. This is the precision component.

Simple Rolling-Nut Trigger (Medieval European)

                │          STOCK               │
                │                              │
    STRING ─────┼───┐                          │
                │   │                          │
                │ ┌─▼───────────────────────┐  │
                │ │  ROLLING NUT (cylindrical│  │
                │ │  with notch for string) │  │
                │ └─┬───────────────────────┘  │
                │   │                          │
                │   │  Pivot pin               │
                │   │                          │
                │   │                    ┌─────▼──┐
                │   └────────────────────┤ TRIGGER│
                │                        │  BAR   │
                │                        └────┬───┘
                │                             │
                │                        ┌────▼───┐
                │                        │ TRIGGER│
                │                        │ LEVER  │
                └────────────────────────┴────────┘

The rolling nut is a cylinder (often antler, bone, or brass) with: - A notch to hold the bowstring - A sear notch on the bottom engaged by the trigger bar - A pivot pin through its centre

When the trigger is pulled, the trigger bar drops out of the sear notch, the rolling nut rotates, and the string is released.

Three interlocking bronze castings. Extremely reliable — identical parts were mass-produced and interchangeable. This is harder to make without metal casting capability but is superior to the European rolling nut design.

Stock

The stock (tiller) holds the prod, trigger mechanism, and provides the shoulder rest.

Design

        ┌────┐                              ┌──────────┐
        │    │                              │          │
        │Prod│──────────────────────────────│  Stock   │──────┐
        │    │                              │          │      │
        └────┘                              └──────────┘  Butt│
                                                           ┌──┘
        ┌────┐                              ┌──────────┐   │
        │Bow │                              │ Trigger  │   │
        │    │                              │          │───┘
        └────┘                              └──────────┘
  • Length: 60–90 cm overall
  • Material: Hardwood (walnut, beech, acacia, jarrah)
  • Prod socket: A mortise at the front to seat the prod securely. The prod is held by a wedge or binding.
  • Bolt groove: A shallow channel the length of the stock to guide the bolt
  • Trigger slot: Housing for the rolling nut and trigger mechanism

Bolts (Quarrels)

Crossbow bolts are shorter and heavier than arrows — typically 30–40 cm long (vs. 70–80 cm for arrows).

Bolt Shaft Materials

Material Diameter Notes
Hardwood dowels 8–12 mm Heavier than necessary but strong
Bamboo (thick-walled) 10–14 mm Excellent. Must straighten nodes.
Cane/reed 10–14 mm Lighter than bamboo, less durable.
Aluminium tubing (salvaged) 8–10 mm Consistent, durable.

Fletching

Crossbow bolts often use only two feathers (top and bottom, or side-side) rather than three. Some use no fletching at all (relying on weight-forward design for stability — like a dart).

Bolt Heads

Crossbow bolt heads are typically heavier than arrowheads: - Bodkin: Narrow, square-section, penetrates armour (traditional military) - Broadhead: Wide cutting edges for game - Blunt: For small game

Spanning (Drawing) the Crossbow

A crossbow of any significant draw weight cannot be drawn by hand.

Methods (From Simplest to Most Powerful)

Method Max Draw Weight Reload Time Equipment
Hand span Up to 80 lb (360 N) 6–8 sec None
Belt hook 80–200 lb (360–890 N) 8–12 sec Hook on belt, stirrup on bow
Goat's foot lever 100–300 lb (450–1300 N) 10–15 sec Lever device
Windlass 300–1200 lb (1300–5300 N) 30–60 sec Cranequin or windlass with pulleys

Belt Hook Method

    1. Place foot in stirrup (on front of prod)
    2. Hook belt hook over string
    3. Straighten body (legs and back)
    4. String rises to catch on nut/sear
    5. Remove hook, load bolt, ready to fire


    SHOOTER (standing)
        │
        ├── Belt with hook
        │   │
        │   └── Hook engages string
        │
        ├── Foot in stirrup
        │   │
        │   └── Prod/stirrup assembly
        │
        └── Hands-free to load bolt

The belt hook is the fastest practical spanning method and can be fabricated from an iron hook riveted to a leather belt.

Safety

  • Never dry-fire a crossbow (release string without bolt). The energy must go somewhere — it damages the prod.
  • Check the string regularly for fraying at the nocks.
  • Check the prod for cracks (wood) or permanent set (steel) after each session.
  • Keep fingers below the bolt groove when firing — the string travels in the groove and will take fingers with it.
  • Span to full engagement only — a partially engaged string on the nut can release unexpectedly.

Draw Weight Bolt Weight Velocity Effective Range (Hunting)

150 lb (670 N) 40 g ~55 m/s 35 m
300 lb (1300 N) 50 g ~65 m/s 50 m
600 lb (2700 N) 70 g ~75 m/s 70 m
1000 lb (4400 N) 90 g ~85 m/s 90 m
ℹ️ Information
**Accuracy note:** A 150 lb crossbow will reliably hit a rabbit-sized target at 25 m. A 300 lb crossbow extends this to 40 m. Beyond 50 m, bolt drop becomes significant and wind affects the bolt dramatically. Crossbows are short-range weapons compared to firearms or even hand bows.
