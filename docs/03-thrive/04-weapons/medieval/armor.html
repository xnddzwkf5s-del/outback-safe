Armour

Armour protects the body from weapons. In a survival context, the primary threat is not battle but accidents, animal attacks, and the occasional hostile encounter. A practical approach to armour prioritises what you can make and what protects the most vital areas.

The Armour Hierarchy

Padded Armour (Gambeson)

A gambeson is a quilted jacket stuffed with padding. It was worn as standalone armour by poorer soldiers and as padding under mail or plate by everyone else. It is surprisingly effective — a well-made gambeson will stop slashing cuts from most edged weapons.

Construction

📋 Materials
  • Outer shell: Heavy linen, canvas, or strong cotton — approximately 2 m × 1.5 m
  • Inner lining: Lighter linen or cotton
  • Padding: Raw wool, cotton batting, scrap fabric, or multiple layers of heavy cloth (15–25 layers)
  • Heavy thread for quilting

Thickness: Minimum 10 mm of compressed padding. Ideal: 15–20 mm.

A 15 mm padded gambeson will stop a slashing cut from a knife or sword. It will significantly reduce the penetration of a thrust. It will NOT stop an arrow or crossbow bolt.

Hardened Leather Armour

Raw leather offers moderate protection. Hardened leather (cuir bouilli — "boiled leather") is significantly better.

Hardening Methods

Method 1: Hot Water Hardening

Method 2: Wax Hardening

Wax-hardened leather is stiffer and more water-resistant than hot-water-hardened leather. It provides better protection but is heavier and more brittle in cold weather.

Leather Armour Pieces

Vambraces (forearm guards): - Simple tube with a gap on the inside of the forearm - Lace or buckle closure - Protects from string-slap (archery) and defensive cuts

Cuirass (chest piece): - Shaped front plate covering chest and abdomen - Shoulder straps - The hardened leather should be at least 4–5 mm thick

Greaves (shin guards): - Curved plates covering the front of the shins - Strapped behind the calf

Chainmail (Mail)

Chainmail is the most labour-intensive armour to make, but it is incredibly effective against cuts and provides flexible, comfortable protection.

Wire Making

Ring Making

Butted mail (ends just pressed together, no rivet) is much faster to make but will open under stress. A strong thrust from a spear will pop butted rings apart. Riveted mail (each ring secured with a tiny rivet) is the historical standard and dramatically stronger.

Weave Pattern

The standard European 4-in-1 pattern:

    Each ring passes through four others:

    ┌─┐ ┌─┐ ┌─┐
    │ │ │ │ │ │    ← Ring passes through four neighbouring rings
    └┬┘ └┬┘ └┬┘
     │   │   │
    ┌┼─┐┌┼─┐┌┼─┐
    ││ │││ │││ │    ← Each ring linked to 4 others
    └┼─┘└┼─┘└┼─┘
     │   │   │

This pattern hangs correctly, drapes well, and provides natural stretch around curves.

Helmet Construction

A helmet is the single most important piece of armour. Head wounds are disproportionately fatal.

Minimum Viable Helmet

Hardened leather skull-cap: - Vegetable-tanned leather, 5–6 mm thick - Hot-water hardened as a deep bowl shape - Suspension liner inside (leather straps or padded cloth) — creates air gap between head and helmet critical for impact absorption - Chin strap

A hardened leather helmet will stop a glancing blow from a club, deflect a knife cut, and protect from falling debris. It will not stop an arrow or direct blow from an axe.

Helmet Liner

The liner is as important as the shell. A helmet without a liner transfers impact directly to the skull.

Scale / Lamellar Armour

Scales or lamellae are small plates laced together into a flexible sheet. This is a good compromise between leather (light but weak) and mail (strong but labour-intensive).

Materials

  • Leather scales — Quickest to make. Punch from hardened leather.
  • Horn scales — Water buffalo horn, boiled and flattened. Traditional in Asia.
  • Bone scales — Large animal bones, cut and shaped.
  • Steel scales — Best protection. Cut from 1 mm sheet steel or salvaged metal.

Practical Priorities

For survival, acquire armour in this order:

  1. Helmet (head wounds are fatal)
  2. Gloves/gauntlets (hands are exposed in every activity)
  3. Forearm guards (defensive wounds — forearms take cuts when you raise them to protect face/body)
  4. Torso protection (chest and abdomen contain the vital organs)
  5. Shin guards (legs are hard to protect in a fight; shin strikes are common)
ℹ️ Information
**Animal defence consideration:** If your primary threat is animal attack (dingoes, feral dogs, boar), focus on forearm guards and shin guards. Predators typically go for extremities first. A thick leather vambrace can be the difference between a bad bite and a severed artery.
