Swords

The sword is a specialised weapon — exclusively for fighting other humans. It is not a hunting tool. A sword has no utility purpose — it cannot chop wood (it will break), it cannot butcher game efficiently (a knife is better), and it cannot be thrown (it will break). A sword is a sidearm for personal defense and close combat.

In a survival context, make spears, bows, and axes before attempting a sword. Once basic needs are met and you have forging capability, a sword may be worthwhile for defense and trade.

Sword Types and Priorities

Type Length Weight Difficulty Utility
Long knife / short sword (40–60 cm) Single-handed 0.5–1.0 kg ★★★ Good — multi-purpose
Machete / bolo (50–70 cm) Single-handed 0.6–1.2 kg ★★★ Excellent — tool and weapon
One-handed sword (70–90 cm) Single-handed 1.0–1.5 kg ★★★★ Poor — weapon only
Two-handed sword (110–140 cm) Two-handed 2.0–3.5 kg ★★★★★ None — dedicated weapon

For survival, the long knife / machete form is ideal — it serves as both a tool (clearing brush, light chopping) and a weapon. A full-length sword is a luxury project for when basic forging is well-established.

ℹ️ Information
**SEA Note:** The *bolo* (Philippines), *parang* (Malaysia/Indonesia), and *golok* are traditional blade forms that bridge the gap between tool and weapon. These designs have been refined over centuries for jungle use — clearing vegetation, harvesting crops, and personal defense. They are probably the single most practical blade form for survival in Southeast Asian environments.

Blade Steel

Source Carbon Content Use Notes
Leaf spring (vehicle) 0.5–0.7% C ★★★★★ Excellent Already is spring steel. Do not overheat — preserve existing temper.
Coil spring (vehicle) 0.5–0.7% C ★★★★★ Excellent Straighten while hot, then re-treat.
Old files 0.9–1.2% C ★★★★ Very good High carbon — brittle if over-hardened. Temper carefully.
Railroad spike (high-carbon) 0.3–0.4% C ★★ Borderline Not enough carbon for a good edge. Adequate for machete.
Mild steel (structural) < 0.2% C ★ Not suitable Cannot be hardened. Will not hold an edge.
Wrought iron < 0.1% C ✗ Useless Cannot be hardened at all. Not a sword material.

Handle Materials

  • Hardwood: Oak, ash, walnut, jarrah, ironbark, mulga, teak
  • Antler: Deer, elk (not available wild in AU; feral deer in some areas)
  • Bone: Large animal leg bones (cattle, buffalo)
  • Horn: Water buffalo horn (SEA) — excellent, traditional
  • Leather washers: Stacked leather discs, compressed and shaped

Tang Design

The tang is the single most important structural element. A blade that snaps at the tang during use becomes a flying piece of sharp steel.

Full tang (recommended): The tang extends the full length and width of the handle. Handle scales (wood, bone, antler) are riveted to each side. This is the strongest design.

Stick tang (traditional, adequate): A narrower tang that runs through the handle and is peened (riveted over) at the pommel. Adequate if properly sized, but more prone to breakage than a full tang in a poorly-made blade.

Forging Process

Heat Treatment Reference

Stage Temperature Colour Duration Purpose
Normalising 770–800°C Bright red Heat + air cool × 3 Stress relief, grain refinement
Hardening 770–800°C Bright red 2–3 min soak, oil quench Form martensite (hard phase)
Tempering (machete) 230–270°C Straw to purple 1 hour Toughness for chopping
Tempering (sword) 200–230°C Light straw 1–2 hours Edge retention + reasonable toughness

Guard (Crossguard)

A guard prevents your hand from sliding onto the blade when thrusting into a target.

  • Mild steel bar, slotted to fit over the tang
  • Brass or bronze — non-rusting, traditional
  • Hardwood — simplest, least durable

The guard should be at least 2 cm longer than the blade width on each side (so 4 cm total wider than the blade).

Sharpening

Edge Geometry

  • Machete / chopping blade: Convex edge, approximately 25–30° per side. Durable, resists chipping.
  • General-purpose sword: Flat grind to edge, approximately 20–25° per side. Balanced cutting and durability.
  • Razor-sharp sword (cutting competition): Approximately 15–20° per side. Cuts beautifully, dulls quickly.
