Towers

A tower is height turned into tactical advantage. It extends your line of sight, extends your effective weapons range, and provides a protected fighting position that overlooks the approaches and the walls themselves.

Why Towers?

  1. Extended visibility — From a 10 m tower, your horizon is approximately 11 km (vs. 5 km at ground level). You see threats earlier.
  2. Gravity assist — Arrows, bolts, and thrown objects gain energy when launched downward
  3. Enfilading fire — Towers projecting from walls allow defenders to shoot along the face of adjacent wall sections, catching attackers between two lines of fire
  4. Overlook your own walls — If attackers reach the top of your wall, the tower provides an elevated position to shoot down at them
  5. Communication — Visual signals (flags, smoke, mirrors) from a tower are visible for kilometres
  6. Safe storage — The upper levels of a tower are the most secure place in your compound

Corner towers: At every corner of the perimeter. These see two faces of the wall and provide converging fire.

Interval towers: Along straight wall sections, spaced so that arrows from one tower reach the base of the next tower. Maximum spacing: 40 m for bows, 60 m for crossbows, 80 m for firearms.

Gate towers: Flanking the main gate. Two towers, one on each side, connected by a wall walk above the gate. This creates the barbican/gatehouse with defenders on three sides of the gate passage.

Freestanding Towers (Keep/Dungeon)

A large central tower (keep) serves as: - Last defensive position if outer walls are breached - Secure storage for critical supplies (food, water, weapons, powder) - Command post with best visibility - Living quarters for key personnel

Materials

Timber tower (fastest, simplest):

Earth-and-timber tower (motte):

A conical mound of earth (motte) with a timber tower on top. This is the classic Norman motte-and-bailey design.

Stone tower (permanent, labour-intensive):

See the Construction section (Tier 3) for stone masonry techniques, but key points:

  • Walls batter (taper inward) as they rise — a 2 m base wall may be 1.2 m at the top
  • Corner quoins (large, well-dressed stones at corners) are essential — they tie the walls together
  • Vaulted ceilings (barrel vaults) between floors — these are fireproof and support the weight above
  • Spiral staircases in the wall thickness conserve interior space

Crenellations (Battlements)

The top of a defensive tower or wall is capped with crenellations:

    ┌───┐   ┌───┐   ┌───┐   ┌───┐
    │   │   │   │   │   │   │   │  ← MERLONS (solid sections)
    │   │   │   │   │   │   │   │     Defender stands behind
    └───┘   └───┘   └───┘   └───┘
    ┌───┐   ┌───┐   ┌───┐   ┌───┐
    │   │   │   │   │   │   │   │
    └───┘   └───┘   └───┘   └───┘
      ↑       ↑       ↑       ↑
    EMBRASURES (gaps) — defender shoots through here
  • Merlon: The solid upright section. Should be wide enough to completely conceal a person (minimum 60 cm).
  • Embrasure (crenel): The gap. Wide enough to shoot through (30–50 cm).
  • Height: Merlons should be 1.5–2.0 m tall. Embrasures should be deep enough (1.0–1.5 m from floor) that a defender has cover while shooting.

Arrow Loops (Arrow Slits)

In enclosed tower levels (below the open top), arrow loops provide protected shooting positions:

    EXTERIOR VIEW                INTERIOR VIEW

    ┌──┐                         ┌───────────┐
    │  │                         │           │
    │  │  ← Narrow slit          │  ╲     ╱  │
    │  │     (5-8 cm wide)       │   ╲   ╱   │
    │  │                         │    ╲ ╱    │
    └──┘                         │     ▼     │
                                 │  ARCHER   │
                                 └───────────┘

The exterior opening is narrow (5–8 cm) to minimise exposure. The interior opening flares wide (60–80 cm) to allow the archer to traverse (aim side to side) and to admit light.

Vertical slits allow shooting at various elevations. A horizontal cross-slit (cruciform) allows wider traverse at the cost of larger external opening.

Communication Between Towers

Towers should be able to communicate:

  • Voice — Practical between towers up to 30 m apart (yelling). Beyond that, use signals.
  • Horn/whistle — Pre-arranged signals (one blast = all clear, two = alert, three = attack on wall section)
  • Flag signals — During daylight. Coloured flags or simple position codes.
  • Signal fire / smoke — At night (fire) or day (smoke by adding green vegetation). Primary tower ignites, others relay.
  • Bell — A bell in the main tower can be heard throughout the compound.

Establish a simple signal code and ensure every tower occupant knows it.

Tower Defense

If attackers scale a wall and reach a tower:

  • The tower base door should open inward (easy to barricade from inside)
  • Spiral staircases favour right-handed defenders — Defenders retreating upward have their sword arm free; attackers climbing have their sword arm against the central pillar (assuming clockwise spiral)
  • Storage of projectiles at every level — So defenders retreating upward are not unarmed
  • The roof is the last stand — Defenders at the top can hold off attackers on the stairs (one-at-a-time bottleneck)
