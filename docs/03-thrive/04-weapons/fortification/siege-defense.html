Siege Defense

A siege is a battle of logistics and morale. The attackers do not need to breach your walls — they only need to outlast your supplies or break your will to resist. Every aspect of siege defense is about extending the time you can hold out.

The Nature of a Siege

A siege is not a battle. It is a waiting game with intermittent violence. The typical pattern:

  1. Investment — Attackers surround the position, cutting off access
  2. Bombardment/assaults — Periodic attempts to breach or scale walls
  3. Attrition — Both sides wait. Defenders' supplies dwindle. Attackers' supplies also dwindle (they must be supplied too).
  4. Resolution — Either defenders surrender, attackers breach the walls, a relief force arrives, or both sides exhaust themselves

Your job as defender: stretch phase 3 as long as possible while making phase 2 as costly as possible.

Pre-Siege Preparation

The time to prepare for a siege is before one starts. Once investment occurs, you cannot resupply.

Water Is Everything

Water plan: - Well or spring inside the walls (best) - Large cistern fed by roof rainwater collection - Multiple storage barrels/tanks, stored in cool, dark location - Water treatment capability (boiling, filtration) for if you must use suspect water

Food Preservation

Your food must last months without refrigeration (see Food Preservation section, Tier 2):

  • Grains and legumes in sealed containers (clay jars, metal bins) — rodent-proof
  • Dried meat/fish — properly dried and stored with salt
  • Hard tack / ship's biscuit — flour and water, baked hard. Lasts years.
  • Preserved vegetables — fermented (sauerkraut, kimchi), dried, or stored in sand (root vegetables)
  • Preserved fruit — dried, or in honey

Ammunition Stockpile

Calculate your ammunition needs:

  • How many arrows/bolts can your archers shoot in one assault? (50–100 per archer)
  • How many assaults might occur in a siege? (3–10 over 90 days)
  • Multiply: 3 archers × 100 arrows × 5 assaults = 1500 arrows

Make them before the siege. Arrow-making during a siege is possible but diverts labour from defense.

During The Siege

Organisation

Rationing

Begin rationing food from day 1 of a siege, not when supplies are visibly low:

Week Ration Level Daily Food (per person)
1–2 Full rations Normal intake
3–6 Three-quarter rations ~75% normal
7–10 Half rations ~50% normal
10+ Survival rations Minimum to maintain defensive capability

Announce the rationing plan at the start. People accept rationing when they know the plan and understand why. Surprise ration cuts damage morale.

Prioritise fighters on wall duty — They get full rations longest. Non-combatants, the injured, and the elderly get reduced rations first. This is brutal but necessary — if the walls fall, everyone dies.

Water Conservation

  • No bathing (wipe down with damp cloth)
  • Cooking water reused for washing
  • Rainwater collection maintained aggressively — every roof is a water collector
  • Monitor consumption daily — know your rate and remaining supply

Sanitation

Disease in a siege is usually what breaks the defense, not direct assault:

  • Dedicated latrine area — Far from water storage and food preparation
  • Lime or wood ash in latrines after each use (reduces smell, kills pathogens)
  • Hand washing — After latrine, before food. Non-negotiable.
  • Waste burial — If possible, bury or burn waste outside the walls (under cover of darkness, with armed escort)
  • Isolate the sick — Any illness spreads rapidly in the confined siege environment
  • Dispose of the dead — If casualties occur, burial or cremation must happen within 24 hours

Night Defense

Night is the most dangerous time. Attackers will attempt to:

  • Scale walls under cover of darkness
  • Place ladders against walls
  • Dig under walls (sapping)
  • Set fires

Countermeasures:

  • Illuminate the wall base — Fires, torches, or pitch-soaked bundles on the wall face create a lit zone at the base. Attackers cannot approach unseen.
  • Noise-makers on approaches — Trip lines with bells, dry branches that crack when stepped on, gravel patches
  • Night watch rotation — Never the same people on night watch two nights running (fatigue)
  • Listen — Sound carries at night. Train watch-standers to identify normal sounds vs. suspicious sounds.
  • Counter-sapping — Place vessels of water on the ground inside the walls. If the water ripples, someone is digging below.

Countering Assaults

When an assault comes:

  1. Alarm — Signal reaches all positions simultaneously
  2. Man the walls — Every able person with a weapon. Pre-positioned weapon caches at each fighting position.
  3. Concentrate fire — Focus archers/crossbows on breaching points or areas where attackers are massing
  4. Repel ladders — Use forked poles (not weapons) to push ladders away from the wall. Destroying a ladder is better than killing the man climbing it.
  5. Drop objects — Rocks, boiling water, burning material through machicolations or over the parapet
  6. Rotate fighters — Fresh people to the wall every 15–20 minutes. Fighting is exhausting. Exhausted defenders make mistakes.
  7. Reserve force — Keep a small group in reserve. If a section of wall is in danger of being overrun, the reserve reinforces it.
  8. Counter-attack — If the assault falters, a sally from a concealed gate to attack their flank or rear can rout them

Countering Fire

Attackers will try to burn your walls, gate, and buildings:

  • Fire buckets filled with sand or water — Positioned at intervals along the walls and at every tower
  • Wet hides or blankets — Cover exposed timber sections during attack. Wet them frequently.
  • Remove flammable materials from near walls — Firewood, thatch, stored timber
  • Roofs — If you have thatched roofs inside the compound, they are vulnerable to fire arrows. Cover with wet hides during attack or prepare firebreaks (clear areas between buildings).

Psychological Warfare

Attackers will try to break your morale:

  • Taunts and threats from outside the walls
  • Displaying captured or dead members of your group
  • Night-time noise to prevent sleep
  • Feigned retreats to lure defenders out

Countermeasures:

  • Do not respond to taunts — Engaging gives them information about your state of mind
  • Brief everyone on psychological tactics — Knowing it's a tactic reduces its impact
  • Maintain routine regardless of provocation — Predictable routine is reassuring
  • Rotate sleep locations if night noise is targeted at specific areas
  • Never pursue a feigned retreat unless you have overwhelming force and clear intelligence

Surrender

Surrender is a tactical decision, not a moral failing. If your water is gone and there is no relief force, surrender may save lives that a last stand would waste.

Before surrendering: - Negotiate terms from a position of remaining strength (while you still have ammunition and the walls are intact) - Demand safe passage for non-combatants - Demand that your group not be separated - Get terms witnessed by multiple people on both sides - Be aware: in a lawless post-collapse scenario, surrender terms may not be honoured

After the Siege

Whether the siege is broken by relief, the attackers withdraw, or you survive an assault:

  1. Do not pursue immediately — It may be a feigned retreat
  2. Secure the perimeter — Post guards before anything else
  3. Assess casualties — Treat wounded, count dead
  4. Assess damage — Breaches in walls, burned structures, damaged gates
  5. Repair immediately — A breached wall invites immediate re-attack. Even a temporary repair (timber and earth) is better than an open gap
  6. Replenish water — If safe to exit, send a water party under armed escort
  7. Rest the garrison — After the adrenaline fades, exhaustion hits. Rotate rest periods.
  8. Document what happened — What worked? What failed? What would you do differently? This is crucial — there may be another siege.
ℹ️ Information
**Historical note:** During the 1565 Siege of Malta, the defenders held out for nearly 4 months against overwhelming Ottoman forces. They succeeded because of: prepared positions (multiple layers of walls), secure water (cisterns), effective communication between forts, and unwavering morale (they knew a relief force was coming). The relief force arrived on day 112. The Ottomans withdrew.
