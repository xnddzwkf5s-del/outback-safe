Moats

A moat is a defensive ditch, dry or water-filled, surrounding a fortification. It is one of the oldest and most effective defensive features — a 3 m deep, 5 m wide moat stops an attacker more effectively than a 3 m stone wall, because the wall can be climbed and the moat must be crossed first.

Why a Moat?

  1. Prevents direct approach to walls — Attackers must cross the moat before they can even touch your walls
  2. Breaks momentum — A charging group must stop, descend, cross, and climb — under fire the entire time
  3. Traps attackers — Attackers in the moat are below your line of fire, but they're also stuck. You can drop things on them.
  4. Undermines siege engines — Battering rams and siege towers cannot reach your walls if a moat is in the way
  5. Provides building material — The excavated earth becomes your rampart
  6. Psychological barrier — A wide, deep ditch is intimidating even before anyone tries to cross it

Dry Moat vs. Wet Moat

Feature Dry Moat Wet Moat
Water required No Yes (continuous source)
Crossing difficulty ★★★ ★★★★★
Maintenance Low (clear debris) High (manage water level, prevent stagnation)
Mosquito risk Low High (standing water breeds mosquitoes)
Can be used as obstacle course Yes (add stakes, caltrops) No (everything underwater)
Escape route possible Yes Only with boats/prepared crossings
Construction difficulty ★★ ★★★★ (requires water management)

Dry Moat Advantages

  • Can be filled with obstacles — Sharpened stakes, caltrops, trip hazards
  • Defenders can enter it — To remove ladders, clear sappers, or fight attackers who have fallen in
  • Sally ports can open into it — Hidden exits into the moat allow counter-attacks
  • Easier to maintain — No water management
  • No breeding ground for mosquitoes

Wet Moat Advantages

  • Much harder to cross — Swimming in armour or carrying weapons is nearly impossible
  • Undermines sapping — Tunnels under a water-filled moat are extremely difficult
  • Water source — If managed, the moat water can supplement your water supply (stock water, irrigation)
  • Fish — A managed moat can be stocked with fish as a food source

Moat Design

Dimensions

Parameter Minimum Recommended Notes
Width 3 m 5–8 m Wider = harder to cross. Must exceed the length of any likely ladder.
Depth 1.5 m 2.5–3.0 m Deep enough that a person cannot simply step out.
Distance from wall 3 m 5–10 m Prevents attackers from reaching the wall with poles/ladders from the moat's far edge.
Moat wall slope 60° 70–80° (near-vertical) Steeper = harder to climb. The inner face (toward your wall) should be steeper than the outer face.

The outer face (attacker's side) is slightly less steep than the inner face — it's easier to descend. The inner face is nearly vertical (faced with timber or stone revetment) — extremely difficult to climb.

Scarp and Counterscarp

  • Scarp: The inner face of the moat (defender's side). Reinforced to prevent climbing.
  • Counterscarp: The outer face (attacker's side). Also steep, but attackers descend it to enter the moat.

Berm

The flat area between the moat and the wall. This should be at least 3 m wide — prevents attackers from reaching the wall directly from the moat edge.

Moat Obstacles

In a Dry Moat

  • Sharpened stakes — Pointed hardwood stakes driven into the moat floor, pointing upward at 45°
  • Caltrops — Four-pointed iron spikes that always land with one point up. Can be forged or improvised (nails through wood blocks)
  • Chevaux-de-frise sections — Portable spiked barriers placed at intervals
  • Punji sticks — Sharpened bamboo stakes (SEA/AU traditional). Often smeared with excrement for infection risk (effective but ethically fraught)
  • Trip hazards — Stones, logs, hidden holes

In a Wet Moat

  • Submerged stakes — Sharpened stakes just below the water surface, invisible to waders
  • Entanglement — Submerged netting, wire, or thorny branches that entangle swimmers

Water Management (Wet Moat)

Water Source

A wet moat requires continuous water input. Options:

Source Reliability Notes
Diverted stream ★★★★★ Best option. Control the inlet and outlet.
Spring ★★★★★ If on site. Constant flow.
Groundwater ★★★ If water table is high enough. Seasonal variation.
Rainwater collection ★★ Dependent on rainfall. Supplementary only.
Manual filling ★ Impractical for anything but small, symbolic moats.

Inlet and Outlet Control

    INLET ──► ┌──────────────────────┐ ──► OUTLET
              │                      │
              │    MOAT (circuit)    │
              │                      │
              └──────────────────────┘
  • Inlet: Controlled by a sluice gate. Higher than the outlet to ensure flow.
  • Outlet: Lower than inlet. Flow prevents stagnation.
  • Overflow: An emergency spillway if water level rises too high (heavy rain).
  • Drain: A way to drain the moat completely for maintenance.

Preventing Stagnation

Standing water becomes a mosquito-breeding, disease-spreading swamp:

  • Ensure flow-through — Inlet and outlet keep water moving
  • Stock with fish — Mosquito fish (Gambusia) or native fish control larvae
  • Keep edges clear — Cut vegetation that provides mosquito habitat
  • Depth — Deeper water (2+ m) stays cooler and supports fewer mosquito larvae in the surface film

Crossing Points

You need ways for your own people to cross. Every crossing point is a potential attack point.

A simple wooden bridge hinged at the gate end, raised by chains or ropes from above. When raised, it covers the gate, providing additional protection.

Construction:

Fixed Bridge with Removable Section

A simpler alternative to a drawbridge:

  • Permanent bridge from the far side to the middle of the moat
  • Gap of 2–3 m from the mid-platform to your gate
  • Planks span the gap when needed, removed for defense
  • Defenders can still cross (jump the gap) — attackers cannot

Moat Maintenance

  • Clear silt annually — Earth moats fill with eroded soil over time
  • Maintain revetment — The scarp face (timber or stone facing) must be repaired as it weathers
  • Check drainage — A moat that holds water after rain but is meant to be dry becomes a muddy, impassable swamp
  • Remove vegetation — Plants growing in or near the moat provide cover and climbing aids
  • Check obstacles — Wooden stakes rot, iron caltrops rust. Inspect and replace regularly

AU/SEA Considerations

  • Termites: Timber revetments and stakes in tropical AU/SEA will be destroyed by termites within years. Use termite-resistant species (ironbark, cypress, teak) or accept that timber components are temporary.
  • Monsoon: A dry moat in monsoon climate will flood seasonally. Design with drainage or accept it as seasonal wet moat.
  • Mosquito-borne disease: Dengue, malaria, Ross River virus. Wet moats in tropical AU/SEA are serious disease risks. If you must have a wet moat, stock it aggressively with fish and keep water flowing.
  • Saltwater: In coastal areas, a moat connected to tidal saltwater (via a controlled inlet) avoids mosquito breeding entirely. Also provides fish, crabs, and salt for preservation.
