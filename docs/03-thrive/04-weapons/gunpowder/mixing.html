Mixing and Incorporating Gunpowder

Mixing is the most dangerous step in gunpowder manufacture. A dust explosion at this stage can be fatal. Read the Safety page first. Work small, work damp, work outdoors.

The Standard Ratio

75% Potassium Nitrate (saltpetre)
15% Charcoal
10% Sulfur

All measurements by weight. Use a balance scale, not volume — densities vary dramatically.

Mixing Methods (Ranked by Safety)

Method 1: Wet Incorporation (Safest, Recommended)

This is the historical method used in all proper powder mills from the 15th century onwards. The ingredients are ground together while wet, forming a paste that is later dried and granulated.

Method 2: Wheel Mill Incorporation (Historical Commercial)

The traditional powder mill used edge-runner stones:

        │   Wooden beam    │

The damp powder is spread on the stone bed and the heavy stone wheels (1–3 tonnes each) rotate over it, grinding and incorporating for 4–8 hours. Water is added periodically. This is not practical for most survival scenarios unless you are equipping a militia.

Grain Sizes

The grain size determines burn rate. Larger grains burn more slowly and are used for larger charges.

Grain Name Sieve Size Typical Use Burn Speed
Meal < 0.5 mm (passes fine screen) Priming pans, flash powder Very fast
FFFFg (4F) 0.5–0.8 mm Priming flintlocks, small pistols Fast
FFFG (3F) 0.8–1.2 mm Pistols, small-bore rifles Medium-fast
FFG (2F) 1.2–1.7 mm Muskets, rifles, shotguns Medium
FG (1F) 1.7–2.4 mm Large muskets, blunderbusses Medium-slow
Cannon 2.4–4.0 mm Cannons, large blasting charges Slow
Large cannon 4.0–10 mm Siege cannons, mining Very slow

Sieve Construction

Build a set of graduated sieves:

Top: 4 mm mesh (coarse wire or perforated sheet)
  └── 2.4 mm mesh
       └── 1.7 mm mesh
            └── 1.2 mm mesh
                 └── 0.8 mm mesh
                      └── 0.5 mm mesh
                           └── Fine cloth (collects meal/dust)

Wooden frames, stretched brass or copper wire mesh — never iron or steel wire.

Granulation Procedure

Quality Indicators After Mixing

Observation Meaning
Uniform dark grey colour Good incorporation
Light or dark streaks Poor mixing — re-incorporate
White crystals visible Undissolved saltpetre — grind finer before mixing
Yellow spots Unmixed sulfur — grind finer
Crumbly, won't hold grain shape Too dry during granulation — mist lightly with water
Sticky, clumps into balls Too wet — let it dry more before sieving

Polishing (Optional)

Commercial powder was often "polished" by tumbling the dried grains in a rotating wooden barrel for several hours. This rounds the sharp edges, reducing dust and making the grains more resistant to breaking during transport.

For survival use, polishing is optional. It improves consistency but adds a processing step with its own safety considerations (dust generation in the tumbler).
