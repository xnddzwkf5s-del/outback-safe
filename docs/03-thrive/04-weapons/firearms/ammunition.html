Ammunition

No ammunition means your firearm is a club. Unlike modern cartridges, black powder ammunition is assembled at loading — you need a supply of lead for balls, cloth for patches, and tools for casting.

Lead

Lead is dense (11.3 g/cm³), soft (easily cast and swaged), and has a low melting point (327°C). It is near-perfect as a projectile material and cannot be substituted — no other commonly available material matches its combination of density, softness, and low melting point.

Sources

Source Quality Notes
Roof flashing ★★★★★ Nearly pure lead. Common in old buildings.
Wheel weights ★★★★ Lead-antimony alloy. Slightly harder than pure — acceptable.
Plumbing (old) ★★★★★ Pure lead pipe. Scrap yards, demolished buildings.
Car batteries ★★ DO NOT USE Battery lead contains calcium and other additives. Produces toxic fumes when melted. The plates are also coated in lead sulfate/paste which is extremely hazardous.
Fishing sinkers ★★★★ Often pure lead. Melt and re-cast.
Scrap electronics solder ★★★ Tin-lead alloy. Slightly lighter than pure lead (tin is 7.3 g/cm³). Adequate but not ideal.

Galena (Lead Ore)

If you must smelt lead from ore:

Galena (PbS — lead sulfide) is the primary lead ore, found in: - AU: Broken Hill (NSW), Mount Isa (QLD), Rosebery (TAS), many smaller deposits - CN: Extensive deposits in Yunnan, Hunan, Guangdong provinces - SEA: Minor deposits in Myanmar, Thailand, Vietnam

Smelting galena requires a furnace reaching ~1000°C. The process produces SO₂ gas (toxic — do this far from living areas). See the Metallurgy reference section for details.

Ball Casting

Equipment

📋 Materials
  • Cast iron or steel bullet mould — hinged blocks that close to form a cavity. Available in .50, .62, .69, .75 cal common sizes.
  • Lead melting pot — Cast iron or steel. Dedicated to lead only — NEVER use for food.
  • Dipper/ladle — Cast iron or steel, small size for pouring.
  • Heat source — Charcoal fire, propane, or forge. Consistent heat is important.
  • Sprue cutter — Gate shears or heavy pliers for cutting off the sprue (the lead that solidifies in the pouring channel).
  • Flux — Beeswax, tallow, or rosin. A pea-sized piece added to molten lead reduces oxidation and brings impurities to the surface.

Casting Procedure

Ball Sizing

The ball must be slightly undersized relative to the bore to allow for the patch:

Bore Ball Diameter Patch Thickness Notes
.50 cal (12.7 mm) 11.9 mm 0.4–0.5 mm Light small-game load
.62 cal (15.7 mm) 14.7 mm 0.5–0.6 mm General hunting
.69 cal (17.5 mm) 16.5 mm 0.5–0.6 mm Military standard
.75 cal (19.1 mm) 17.5 mm (or 18.0 mm) 0.5–0.8 mm Heavy military

The ball + patch combination should be a snug fit — requires firm pressure on the ramrod, but not so tight that it must be hammered down.

ℹ️ Information
**Bare ball loading** (no patch) was used historically for rapid military fire. The ball is undersized by 2–3 mm and simply dropped down the bore. Accuracy is poor beyond 30 m, but loading speed increases dramatically (4–5 shots per minute vs. 2–3 with patched ball). This is a battlefield technique, not for hunting.

Patching

The patch fills the gap between ball and bore, engages the land-and-groove (if rifled), and imparts spin.

Patch Material

Material Pros Cons
Linen (washed) Traditional. Tough, consistent thickness. Wears out faster than cotton drill.
Cotton drill/ticking Tightly woven, durable. Standard for modern muzzleloading. Can smoulder if not well-lubricated.
Cotton pillow ticking ~0.4–0.5 mm thick. Excellent patch material. —
Wool flannel Thick (~0.8 mm+). Good for very undersized balls. Compresses unevenly.
Leather (thin, oiled) Very durable. Re-usable? Difficult to get consistent thickness.
Paper (cartridge) Disposable. Used in military paper cartridges. Tears easily.
Wasp nest paper Historical field expedient. Thin, fibrous. Availability.

Patch Lubrication

A dry patch will smoulder in the barrel and potentially ignite a fresh powder charge during loading (a "cook-off"). Always lubricate patches.

Lubricant Properties Best For
Spit Traditional field lube. Always available. Short strings of shots (dries out).
Tallow + beeswax (50:50) Excellent all-rounder. Stays soft. All uses.
Bore butter / natural lube Beeswax + oil base. Hunting (can leave loaded for hours).
Olive oil / linseed oil Works. Absorbs into patch over time. Short-term use only.
Mink oil (from rendering mink fat) Traditional, excellent. Doesn't freeze. Cold weather.
Crisco / lard Cheap, works. Practice/range use.

Cutting Patches

Pre-cut patches are convenient. Cut them round, approximately 2.5× the ball diameter (so the patch wraps up around the ball when rammed). Alternatively, cut at the muzzle — lay a strip of patching material over the muzzle, place the ball on it, and cut flush with a patch knife.

Wad Materials

Type Material Use
Over-powder wad Felt (compressed wool), leather, cork, or thick card (1–3 mm) Seals gas behind shot column
Cushion wad Fibre wad (jute, sisal, hemp) or felt, 5–10 mm thick Absorbs shock, protects shot from deformation
Over-shot card Thin card (playing card weight), 0.5 mm Retains shot in barrel

Construction: A wad punch (a sharpened tube of the correct diameter) can be used to cut wads from sheet material. For felt wads, soak in melted tallow-beeswax lubricant and allow to cool.

Shot Making

Method 1: Shot Tower (Traditional)

Molten lead poured through a copper sieve at the top of a tall structure (20–30 m). Droplets form spheres during the fall due to surface tension and cool sufficiently to retain shape when they land in water at the bottom.

     ┌────────────────┐
     │  Lead pot      │
     │  (molten lead) │
     └───────┬────────┘
             │
     ┌───────▼────────┐
     │  Sieve/colander│ ← Multiple small holes
     │  (appropriate  │
     │   hole size)   │
     └───────┬────────┘
             │
             │  Lead droplets fall
             │  20–30 metres
             │
    ┌────────▼────────┐
    │  Water tank     │
    │  (catches       │
    │   shot)         │
    └─────────────────┘

Required height: Approximately 30 m for #6 to #2 shot sizes. Smaller shot requires less height — surface tension dominates. The bottom water tank should be at least 1 m deep.

Method 2: Shot Mould (Small Scale)

For small quantities, a mould that casts multiple small balls simultaneously (similar to a ball mould but with multiple cavities). The resulting shot is not perfectly spherical, but it works adequately for short-range hunting.

Method 3: Ruperts Shot

Pouring a thin stream of molten lead onto an inclined, grooved plate, then directly into water. This produces teardrop-shaped "Rupert's shot" — not spherical but functional at close range.

Paper Cartridges

Pre-made paper cartridges were the military standard for rapid loading. The soldier bit off the end, poured powder down the barrel, then rammed the ball (still in its paper wrapper) down as a combined wad and patch.

    ┌───────────────────────────────────┐
    │  Powder    │  Ball (in paper)     │
    │            │                      │
    └────────────┴──────────────────────┘
    ↑            ↑
    Tied/folded  Tied

Paper cartridges speed loading but the paper wrapper adds to barrel fouling. They are ideal for defense situations where loading speed matters more than precision accuracy.

Ammunition Storage

Store cast balls in a dry location — lead oxidises but the oxide layer does not affect performance.

Store powder separately (see Gunpowder Storage page).

Store patches lubricated in a sealed container. Pre-lubricated patches dry out over time.

Ammunition allocation: A typical hunting kit for a week-long trip: - 20 balls (expect to fire 5–10 for hunting, remainder for emergencies) - 20 pre-lubricated patches - 20 measured powder charges (in individual vials or paper cartridges) - 5 extra flints - Cleaning kit (patches, oil, vent pick, turnscrew)
