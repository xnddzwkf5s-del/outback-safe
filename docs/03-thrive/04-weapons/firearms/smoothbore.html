Smoothbore Muskets

The smoothbore musket was the standard infantry weapon worldwide from approximately 1600 to 1850. It is a deceptively simple machine: a tube, a lock, a stock. But each component must be made correctly or the result is a pipe bomb held against your face.

Barrel Making

The barrel is the most difficult component to manufacture. Medieval and early-modern gunsmiths spent years mastering barrel forging.

Method 1: Forged and Welded (Traditional)

A flat iron bar (skelp) is heated and hammered around a mandrel, with the edges forge-welded to form a tube.

Method 2: Seamless Tube (If Available)

If you have access to seamless steel tubing (salvaged from modern machinery, hydraulic cylinders, etc.), barrel making becomes dramatically easier. The tube must be:

  • Seamless (not welded pipe — welded pipe splits along the seam under pressure)
  • Bore is already smooth (or can be honed)
  • Wall thickness at least 3 mm for small-bore (under .50 cal), 4–5 mm for musket-bore
  • Material is mild steel or better (not cast iron, which shatters)

Method 3: Bamboo (Emergency/SEA Only)

Historically used in China and Southeast Asia for early firearms ("fire lances"). A thick-walled bamboo pole (at least 4 cm outer diameter, 1 cm wall thickness) can fire a few shots with reduced charges.

Construction: Select aged bamboo at least 3 years old. Remove nodes internally with a long rod. Reinforce the breech end by wrapping tightly with wet rawhide, which shrinks as it dries. Bind with copper wire. Use half-size powder charges only.

Bore Dimensions

Common historical calibres for smoothbore muskets:

Designation Bore Diameter Ball Diameter Typical Charge Use
.69 cal (French) 17.5 mm 16.5 mm 5–7 g Military musket
.75 cal (Brown Bess) 19.1 mm 17.5 mm 6–8 g British military
.62 cal (Fowler) 15.7 mm 14.7 mm 4–6 g Hunting/sporting
.50 cal (Small-bore) 12.7 mm 11.9 mm 3–5 g Small game
12-gauge 18.5 mm — 4–6 g Shotgun equivalent

The ball is always undersized relative to the bore — typically 1.0–1.5 mm smaller. The patch (cloth or paper wrapped around the ball) fills the gap and engages the bore.

Choosing Your Calibre

  • Small game (rabbits, birds): .50 cal or 12-gauge with shot
  • Medium game (deer, wallabies): .62–.69 cal
  • Large game (cattle, buffalo): .69–.75 cal
  • Defense: .69–.75 cal (psychological effect, stopping power)
  • Ammunition economy: Smaller calibre uses less lead and less powder per shot

AU Note: For typical Australian game (kangaroo, wallaby, feral pig, goat), a .62 cal smoothbore is a good all-rounder.

Wood Region Notes

Beech Europe, Asia Common military stock wood. Adequate.
Acacia (blackwood, mulga) AU Excellent dense hardwoods. Australian acacias are superb stock woods.
Jarrah AU (WA) Very hard, stable. Difficult to work but extremely durable.
Teak SEA Excellent weather resistance. Hard on tools (silica content).
Bamboo laminate SEA, CN Split, glue, and press bamboo strips into a composite.

Stock Inletting

The barrel channel and lock mortise must be cut precisely. The barrel should sit in its channel with full-length contact (no high spots that create pressure points). The breech must seat firmly against solid wood — this is where all the recoil force is transmitted.

Loading Procedure

The standard drill, unchanged for 200 years. Practise this until it is automatic — and always follow the safety checks.

Cleaning

Black powder fouling is hygroscopic and corrosive — it absorbs moisture from the air and rusts steel within hours.

After the first cleaning, check the barrel 24 hours later — a surprising amount of additional fouling will have loosened. Run an oiled patch through again.

Proof Testing

Every newly-made barrel must be proofed before anyone fires it from the shoulder.

A barrel that passes double-charge proof is safe for normal service loads. A barrel that bulges but does not burst should be scrapped — a bulge is a future burst point.

ℹ️ Information
**The "Brown Bess" British musket proof load:** The Tower of London proof house used a charge of approximately 20 g of powder behind two balls. The standard service charge for a .75 cal Bess was approximately 8 g. This 2.5× proof factor was considered adequate for a service life of thousands of rounds.
