Emergency Services

When you need help in the Australian outback, knowing who to call and what to say can mean the difference between a rescue and a recovery. Response times in remote areas are measured in hours or days, not minutes — every piece of correct information you provide accelerates your rescue.

Emergency Phone Numbers

Number Use Notes
000 National emergency — police, fire, ambulance Primary call for any emergency in Australia. Works from any fixed or mobile phone.
112 Mobile emergency (international) Works on any mobile network, even if your phone has no SIM or is locked. Try 112 if 000 fails.
106 Text-based emergency (TTY) For people with hearing or speech impairments.

Key Emergency Services

Service Role Contact
RFDS (Royal Flying Doctor Service) Remote area medical evacuation and primary health. The most important service for outback medical emergencies. Through 000. Have your GPS coordinates ready.
SES (State Emergency Service) Road crash rescue, flood response, storm damage, land search. Through 000. Each state has its own SES.
Police Coordination of land searches, missing persons, accident scenes. Through 000.
Ambulance Medical emergency response — may dispatch RFDS in remote areas. Through 000.
AMSA (Australian Maritime Safety Authority) Coordinates search and rescue for PLB/EPIRB activations. Also handles aviation and maritime. PLB/EPIRB activation automatically routes here.

UHF Radio Emergency Channels

Channel Frequency Use
Ch 5 476.525 MHz EMERGENCY ONLY — repeater-enabled. Legally restricted to emergency use. Start here.
Ch 10 476.650 MHz 4WD clubs and convoys
Ch 11 476.675 MHz CALLING channel — make contact, then move to another channel
Ch 18 476.875 MHz Caravan and camper convoys
Ch 29 477.150 MHz Pacific Highway (NSW/QLD)
Ch 40 477.400 MHz HIGHWAY — trucks, road trains, general highway use. Monitor this when driving.
Ch 35 (Deprecated) 477.275 MHz Historically the emergency channel. Some legacy repeaters may still operate, but Ch 5 is the official emergency channel. Do not rely on Ch 35.

Emergency UHF Call Format

  1. Press mic button. Wait 2 seconds before speaking.
  2. "Break, break, break — this is an emergency call"
  3. "[Your location] — nearest town or landmark, road name, approximate distance from X"
  4. "Number of people: [X]"
  5. "Nature of emergency: [vehicle breakdown / medical / fire / lost / etc.]"
  6. "Water remaining: [X litres]"
  7. "Over"

Wait 30 seconds. Repeat. If no response, move to next channel.

PLB / EPIRB Activation

When to Activate

  • Any life-threatening medical emergency in remote areas
  • Vehicle breakdown with no other means of communication and you are staying with the vehicle
  • You have decided to walk — activate before you leave the vehicle so your position is transmitted
  • Someone in your group is missing

Do not wait. The single biggest factor in survival outcomes is early activation. If you are unsure, activate.

How to Activate

  1. Remove PLB/EPIRB from its bracket/case
  2. Extend antenna if required
  3. Press and hold the activation button until the light starts flashing (usually 1–2 seconds)
  4. The signal transmits your GPS location (if GPS-enabled) or a fixed position (older models)
  5. Place the beacon upright with a clear view of the sky — do not put it under metal (vehicle roof blocks signal)
  6. Leave it ON — the beacon transmits for 24–48 hours continuously

After Activation — What Happens

  1. Beacon is detected by the COSPAS-SARSAT satellite network
  2. Signal is relayed to AMSA (Australian Maritime Safety Authority)
  3. AMSA contacts local police or RFDS
  4. Search teams are dispatched — timeline depends on:
Location Typical Response Time
Near major highways (Stuart Hwy, Hume, Bruce) 1–2 hours
Secondary sealed roads 2–4 hours
Well-known station tracks 4–12 hours
Remote station tracks 12–24 hours
Extreme remote (desert, Kimberley, Cape York) 24–72 hours (RFDS airlift)

PLB Registration

  • All PLB/EPIRBs must be registered with AMSA
  • Registration includes: owner name, emergency contacts, vehicle description, medical conditions
  • Registration is free. Update it every year.
  • An unregistered beacon significantly delays response — AMSA must verify the signal before dispatching
  • Register or update at: www.amsa.gov.au

What to Tell Emergency Services

When you get through to someone, they need this information in order:

  1. Your exact location — GPS coordinates above all else. If no GPS: road name, distance from last town, landmark description
  2. Number of people — names, ages, medical conditions
  3. Vehicle description — make, model, colour, registration plate
  4. Nature of emergency — vehicle breakdown, medical emergency, lost, fire
  5. Injuries / medical issues — what happened, who is affected, how serious
  6. Water remaining — in litres
  7. When you broke down — date and time
  8. Your planned route — where were you heading?
  9. Who knows you are missing — did you leave a trip plan? With whom?

Trip Plan — Leave One Every Time

A trip plan is a written document left with someone responsible:

  • Your planned route — specific roads and tracks
  • Estimated arrival time at destination
  • Alternative route if conditions change
  • Vehicle description and registration
  • Number of people and their medical conditions
  • Communication equipment carried (PLB, UHF, sat phone)
  • What to do if you do not arrive by X time

Leave a copy with: - A family member or friend who will notice if you are overdue - The local police station - The national park office or station homestead
