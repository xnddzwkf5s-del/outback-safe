Bushfire Safety in Remote Areas

In the Australian outback, bushfire is a sudden and catastrophic threat. Unlike urban areas with evacuation plans and fire services, remote travellers are entirely on their own when fire approaches.

The Australian Fire Danger Rating System (AFDRS) gives you warnings — but in the outback, you are the fire service. Understand the rating, know the signs, and have a plan before you need it.

Before You Travel

Item Action
Fire Danger Index Check the AFDRS rating for your route at bom.gov.au before departure and daily while on the road
Total fire ban rules Each state has different rules — know them for your route (NSW: RFS; VIC: CFA; QLD: QFES; SA: CFS; WA: DFES; NT: NT Fire Service; TAS: TFS)
Bushfire Information Line Save: 1800 240 667 (nationwide) or your state's line
Nearest fire-safe areas Identify cleared areas, riverbeds, or already-burnt ground on each leg of your route
PLB / EPIRB Confirm registration and battery status — if you're trapped this is your only way to call for help
⚠️ Warning
Never camp in long grass or heavy spinifex country during the fire season. A fire front can travel faster than a vehicle on rough tracks. Your escape route must always be on vector — you need to know which direction the fire is coming from and which way to go.

Early Warning Signs

Fire may be over the next ridge and you won't see the flames. Watch for these signs:

Sign What It Means
Smoke on the horizon Note the wind direction — smoke may be from a fire or a planned burn. If the wind is blowing toward you, move away from the smoke source
Change in wind direction or speed A sudden hot, dry wind shift is a classic fire behaviour change — the fire front may turn toward you
Smoke smell intensifying If you smell bushfire strongly but don't see flames, stop, get out, and assess
Ash falling You are close. If ash is falling, the fire front may be within 5–10 km. Act now
Animal behaviour changes Birds fleeing en masse, kangaroos/emus running in one direction — they know where the fire is. Follow the animals
Red/orange glow at night The fire front is visible at night from many kilometres. Identify its direction and your escape route immediately
Cracking or roaring sound A fire front makes a distinct continuous roar. If you hear this and cannot see the fire, it is likely over the next ridge and approaching fast

If Fire Approaches

⚠️ Warning
**Do not try to outrun a bushfire in a vehicle unless you have a clear, open escape route at right angles to the fire's path.** Fire can travel at 25 km/h in open country and much faster upslope. On a rough outback track you will do well to maintain 15 km/h. You cannot outrun it — you must outsmart it.

In a Vehicle

  1. Stop the vehicle in the most open area available — clear of grass, spinifex, and low scrub
  2. Turn the engine off — wait and watch the fire approach
  3. Stay inside the vehicle — the metal body protects you from radiant heat
  4. Cover yourself with a wool blanket — wool does not melt like synthetics. Lie below window level on the floor
  5. Close all windows and vents — recirculate cabin air
  6. After the fire front passes — danger is largely over. The vehicle will be hot but survivable
  7. Check for spot fires and embers before exiting
ℹ️ Information
The interior of a vehicle during a bushfire can reach 50°C+ from radiant heat. Synthetic clothing and seat covers will melt. Keep a wool blanket within reach of the driver's seat — not in the boot.

On Foot (vehicle abandoned or breakdown)

  1. Find the most cleared area available — already burnt ground is the safest option
  2. Lie face down — cover your head and back with a wool blanket or jacket
  3. Breathe through cloth — if possible, wet it first
  4. Protect your airways — hot inhaled air kills before flames do
  5. Do not move until the fire front has fully passed — spot fires ahead of the main front can travel up to several kilometres ahead

Fire Shelter Selection

Choose your shelter position carefully:

Good Shelter Poor Shelter
Already burnt ground (lowest risk) Gully or saddle (fire funnels through)
Large clearing (dirt, rock, sand) Heavy fuel loads (spinifex, dry grass, dead timber)
Rocky area (low fuel, low heat reflection) Base of a hill (fire moves fastest uphill)
Behind a ridge (downwind side preferred) Paddock with long, dry grass
Riverbed with no vegetation (stony creek bed) Under large trees (ember attack + falling branches)
⚠️ Warning
Never take shelter in a cave or under a rock overhang. The radiant heat from a fire passing over the opening can superheat the air inside the cave — you will be cooked by the air itself. Similarly, do not shelter in a shallow drainage channel; fire can funnel through and the heat rises directly over you.

UHF Emergency Channels

Channel Use
UHF 5 (27.100 MHz) Emergency — simplex (line-of-sight only, no repeater)
UHF 10 Road train and emergency vehicle coordination
UHF 40 Call channel — can request assistance relay
UHF 11 Community / repeater access for specific regions
ℹ️ Information
In a bushfire emergency, call on **UHF channel 5** first. If no response, try **channel 40** (call channel). If you have repeater access for your area (typically UHF 1–8 on repeater pairs), try that too. Note that fire may have taken out repeaters — but line-of-sight UHF between two vehicles may still work.

After the Fire

  • Beware of falling trees and branches — fire-damaged trees may fall without warning, especially in wind
  • Hot ground can still ignite tyres and shoes hours after the fire front passes
  • Check on other travellers — use UHF to contact anyone in range
  • Do not move until you are sure the fire has fully passed. Spot fires ahead of the main direction are common and can trap you
  • Treat burns immediately — cool with clean water (not ice) for 20 minutes. Cover with non-stick dressing. Evacuate via RFDS or closest medical centre
