Why Coordinates Matter

When you call emergency services, the single most important piece of information you can give is your exact location. In the Australian outback, where a property address or street name doesn't exist, coordinates are everything.

  • "On the Canning Stock Route near Well 26" is too vague for a helicopter pilot
  • "-24.3020, 124.4930" can put a RFDS plane directly on your position

Coordinates are universal — they work across all devices, services, and emergency responders. If you can read out decimal degrees, anyone with a GPS device anywhere in the world can find you.

Your GPS works without mobile signal. GPS satellites are completely separate from phone networks. You can get accurate coordinates with zero phone reception — hundreds of kilometres from the nearest tower.

iPhone — Getting Your Coordinates

Method 1: Compass App (built-in, no internet needed)

  1. Open the Compass app (pre-installed on all iPhones)
  2. Your coordinates appear at the bottom of the screen in degrees/minutes/seconds format
  3. Tap the coordinates to copy them to your clipboard
  4. Format looks like: 24°18'07"S 124°29'35"E

The Compass app also shows elevation and heading. On newer iPhones (iPhone 12 and later), it supports location sharing directly from the screen.

Method 2: Apple Maps

  1. Open Apple Maps
  2. Tap the blue dot (your current location)
  3. Scroll down — coordinates appear under "Marked Location"
  4. Tap to copy

Apple Maps works with downloaded offline maps if you have them saved before your trip.

Method 3: Google Maps (works offline if maps pre-downloaded)

  1. Open Google Maps
  2. Tap and hold on your current location (the blue dot)
  3. A red pin drops — coordinates appear in the search bar at the top
  4. Tap the search bar to copy the coordinates

To use offline: before your trip, zoom to your route area and tap your profile photo → Offline maps → Select Your Own Map. Download areas along your planned route.

Method 4: Emergency SOS Screen

  1. Press and hold side button + either volume button
  2. The Emergency SOS screen shows your approximate location
  3. This also automatically shares your location with emergency services

This works even when your phone is locked and is a last-resort option if you can't navigate the normal menus.

Android Phone — Getting Your Coordinates

Method 1: Google Maps (built-in)

  1. Open Google Maps
  2. Tap and hold on your current location (blue dot)
  3. Coordinates appear in the search bar at the top
  4. Tap to copy. Format: -24.3020, 124.4930

Download offline maps before your trip: Google Maps → your profile → Offline maps → Select Your Own Map.

Method 2: Dedicated GPS Apps

These free apps give you more detail than Google Maps:

  • GPS Status & Toolbox (free) — shows coordinates, altitude, speed, satellite lock count, and accuracy
  • GPS Test (free) — satellite view with signal strength bars, real-time coordinate updates
  • OsmAnd (free) — offline maps with coordinates in the dashboard, great for outback navigation
  • MAPS.ME (free) — detailed offline maps with coordinate display

Install these before your trip. You won't have reception when you need them.

Method 3: Photo Metadata (backup method)

  1. Take a photo — GPS coordinates are embedded in the EXIF data automatically
  2. Later you can view photo details: open the photo → tap the info icon (i) or menu → Location
  3. This is useful if you need to confirm where something was seen after the fact

To enable: ensure Location tagging is ON in your camera settings before you leave.

Vehicle GPS / Navigation Units

Garmin Automotive GPS (Drive, Nuvi series)

  1. From the main map screen, tap the vehicle icon
  2. Select "Where Am I?" or "Current Location"
  3. Coordinates displayed in degrees/decimal minutes (e.g., S24°18.12' E124°29.58')
  4. Write these down — most automotive units don't have a copy function

Garmin Overlander / Tread (Off-Road GPS)

  1. Tap the coordinates widget on the home screen
  2. Displays decimal degrees format by default
  3. Create a waypoint to save the coordinates for later
  4. The Overlander also displays elevation and nearest town/highway

Hema HX-1 / HX-2 Navigator

  1. From the map screen, tap and hold any location
  2. Coordinates appear in the pop-up window
  3. Save as a waypoint or share via text (if paired with a phone)
  4. Hema units include topo maps of Australia and show grid references on paper map overlays

In-Vehicle Navigation Systems

These vary wildly. Check before you leave:

  • Toyota / Lexus (most models): Navigation → Menu → Where Am I? or Current Location. Some newer Toyota systems show coordinates on the map screen by default
  • LandCruiser 300 / Prado (current gen): Tap the compass/vehicle icon in the bottom corner → coordinates shown. Touch and hold to copy
  • Ford Ranger with SYNC 3/4: Navigation → Current Location → coordinates displayed but limited format
  • Isuzu D-Max / MU-X: Check under Map → Info or Information. Some models don't expose coordinates from factory GPS
  • Mitsubishi Triton / Pajero Sport: Navigation → scroll right → Where Am I?

If your factory system doesn't display coordinates, use your phone instead. Factory GPS units are generally inferior to phone GPS for coordinate sharing. Don't waste time fighting a hidden menu — just pull out your iPhone or Android.

Smart Watches

Apple Watch (Series 2 and later)

  1. Open the Compass app (included in watchOS 9+)
  2. Scroll using the Digital Crown — coordinates appear at the bottom
  3. Also shows elevation, heading, and incline
  4. Works completely offline — GPS is built into the watch

The Apple Watch Compass app also lets you drop waypoints and backtrack along your route. This works even without your phone nearby (GPS-only mode).

Garmin Fenix / Instinct / Epix Series

  1. Press the GPS / Up button to access the GPS menu
  2. Select "Save Location" to mark your current coordinates
  3. View saved locations from the Navigate → Saved Locations menu
  4. Name waypoints the same day (e.g., "Vehicle Camp") — it's easy to forget what "Waypoint 023" was

Garmin watches can display coordinates in multiple formats: decimal degrees, DMS, MGRS, or UTM. Set this in Settings → Position Format before you leave.

Garmin inReach Devices (Explorer, Mini 2, Messenger)

  1. From the home screen, your coordinates are shown at the top
  2. Press OK to save a waypoint
  3. From your paired phone (Earthmate app), tap "Send Message" — your coordinates are automatically included
  4. The inReach can send your coordinates via satellite text without any mobile signal

The inReach is the single best emergency communication device for the outback. It pairs GPS coordinates with two-way satellite messaging. If you're going remote, this is the device to buy.

Samsung Galaxy Watch / Wear OS

  1. Open Google Maps on the watch
  2. If offline maps are downloaded, tap your location dot
  3. Coordinates are displayed — you may need to scroll down

Dedicated Handheld GPS Units

Garmin eTrex / GPSMAP Series

  1. The Satellite page shows your coordinates at the top of the screen
  2. Press Mark to save your current position as a waypoint
  3. Waypoints can be named and organised (e.g., "Lunch Spot" or "Camp Day 3")
  4. Change coordinate format under Setup → Position Format (choose DD, DMS, or MGRS)

The eTrex is simple, reliable, and runs on AA batteries you can carry spares of. A true outback standard.

Garmin Montana Series

  1. Coordinates are shown on the dashboard (customisable from the main screen)
  2. Tap the coordinates to cycle through format options
  3. Most versatile unit for outback use — supports UTM, MGRS, DMS, and DD
  4. Battery life is excellent (18+ hours) and uses rechargeable or AA batteries

Format Conversion

You may receive coordinates in one format and need to enter them in another. This table covers the common formats:

Format Example When Used
Decimal Degrees (DD) -24.3020, 124.4930 GPS devices, Google Maps, most phone apps, this guide
Degrees Minutes Seconds (DMS) 24°18'07"S 124°29'35"E Aviation, nautical charts, some paper maps
Degrees Decimal Minutes (DDM) 24°18.12'S 124°29.58'E Marine, some Garmin units, Hema maps
UTM (Universal Transverse Mercator) 52J 0444700 7311800 Military, Search and Rescue, topographic maps
MGRS (Military Grid Reference) 52J FQ 44700 11800 Military, emergency coordination, Garmin-inReach

Quick conversion trick: Open Google Maps (works offline if maps are downloaded), type coordinates in any format into the search bar, and it will place a pin at that location. This confirms your conversion was correct. For offline conversion, download GPS Coordinates Converter or UTM Converter before your trip.

Most emergency services want Decimal Degrees (DD) in WGS84 datum. This is the default on all modern GPS devices. If you're not sure what format to give, use Decimal Degrees.

Sharing Coordinates in an Emergency

Write coordinates on paper before calling. Screens die. Phones fall. Batteries die. Paper doesn't.

When you get through to emergency services:

  1. Read slowly and clearly: "S 2-4 point 3-0-2-0, E 1-2-4 point 4-9-3-0"
  2. Read each digit individually — "point" for the decimal, not "dot" or "stop"
  3. Read them back twice: the operator will confirm them. If they repeat something different, correct them
  4. State what format you're using: "Decimal degrees, WGS84 datum"
  5. Also provide: altitude (from compass app), heading/direction of travel, visible landmarks (creek, hill, track), number of people, injuries

Example call:

"My coordinates are S 2-4 point 3-0-2-0, E 1-2-4 point 4-9-3-0. That's decimal degrees WGS84. Altitude 450 metres. We're on the Gunbarrel Highway, about 15 kays west of the Docker River turnoff. Three people, one with a broken leg. I repeat: S 2-4 point 3-0-2-0..."

Also: Your PLB or EPIRB transmits coordinates automatically to AMSA. Even so, having written coordinates as a backup is critical — radio communications can fail, but paper doesn't.

Practice Before You Go

  1. Get coordinates from your car at a known intersection near home — then verify them on Google Maps
  2. Practice getting coordinates from your phone in airplane mode (GPS still works)
  3. Have every person in your vehicle practice — anyone might need to do it
  4. Write down the exact procedure for YOUR specific phone model (menus vary)
  5. Save a screenshot of this guide on your phone — screenshot, not a bookmark (no internet needed)
  6. Download offline maps for your entire planned route
  7. Check that any apps you need (Compass, GPS Status) are installed and working before you leave

No-GPS Backup Methods

If every device is dead, flat, or damaged:

  • Paper map + compass: Triangulate your position from visible landmarks. Requires a topographic map of your area
  • Distance and bearing from known features: "I'm 3 km south of the Homestead Bore, along the Old Stock Route" — less accurate but better than nothing
  • Latitude from stick shadow (noon method): With practice, you can estimate latitude from the sun. Longitude is harder without an accurate timepiece

These methods are significantly less accurate than GPS — expect errors of 1–5 km even with good technique. But in an emergency, any position is better than none.
