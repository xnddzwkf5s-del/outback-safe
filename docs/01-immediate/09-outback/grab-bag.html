Grab Bag — Emergency Evacuation

A vehicle fire doubles in size every 30 seconds. A rollover can trap occupants. A sudden flash flood can sweep a vehicle away in minutes. In any of these scenarios, you have less than 60 seconds to get out and get clear.

You will NOT have time to unload the fridge, find the first aid kit, or search for the PLB. You need one bag — always in the same place, always packed, always ready.

1. The Golden Rule

The grab bag is NEVER unpacked. It lives in the vehicle. It is replenished after every trip. It sits in the SAME position every time — passenger footwell, behind the driver's seat, or strapped to the cargo barrier within arm's reach of the driver — so you can grab it by feel in smoke or darkness.

It is NOT your general gear bag. It is NOT where you store snacks for the trip. It is for ONE scenario only: "We are abandoning this vehicle RIGHT NOW."

Every occupant should know: - Where the grab bag is - Who grabs it (designated before trip) - What's in it - How to use the PLB

2. The Bag Itself

Requirement Why
Bright colour (red, orange, yellow) Visible in smoke, bush, darkness, or if dropped
Waterproof or water-resistant Vehicle may end up in water during rollover or flood
Backpack style (not duffel) Both hands free for climbing, running, carrying children
25-35 litres Enough for essentials, not so big it slows you down
Multiple compartments Quick access to PLB without unpacking everything
Sturdy grab handle on top Single-handed grab-and-go

Recommended: Any bright-coloured hiking daypack. Avoid black — invisible at night and in smoke. A waterproof dry-bag backpack is ideal for river crossings and wet season.

3. Contents — Tiered by Priority

Tier 1 — Life Preservation (in external pockets for instant access)

These items can be accessed within 5 seconds of grabbing the bag:

Item Quantity Why
PLB (Personal Locator Beacon) 1 THE most important item. Registered with AMSA. Activates with one button. DO NOT store in the vehicle glovebox — if the dash is on fire, you can't reach it.
Satellite communicator (inReach/Zoleo) if carried 1 Two-way communication. Pre-paired with phone if applicable.
Water — 1L minimum 1 bottle Immediate hydration. The bag itself won't save you without water.
Lighter or ferro rod 1 each Fire in all conditions. Lighter for speed, ferro rod for backup.
Fixed blade knife 1 Full tang, 10-15cm blade. Not a folding knife — fixed blade cannot fail. For cutting seatbelts, preparing tinder, self-defence, general tool.

Tier 2 — Medical & Protection (in main compartment, top layer)

Item Quantity Why
Snake bite bandage (Setopress) 2 Australian specific. Pressure immobilisation. The single most important medical item.
CAT tourniquet 1 For trained users only. Severe arterial bleeding.
Israeli bandage / trauma dressing 2 Stops bleeding anywhere on body. Self-application possible.
Burn gel dressing (Burnaid) 2 Vehicle fires cause burns. Apply immediately.
SAM Splint (36") 1 Reusable, lightweight, radiolucent. Immobilises fractures.
Emergency blanket (space blanket) 2 Hypothermia from shock + night cold. Also: signaling reflector, rain shelter, ground sheet.
Medical tape (5cm wide) 1 roll Secures dressings, makeshift bandage, labels, repairs
Gloves (nitrile) 3 pairs Protect yourself from blood/fluids
Antiseptic (Betadine swabs/packets) 10 Wound cleaning
Electrolyte sachets (Hydralyte) 10 Dehydration from heat + shock. Works faster than plain water.
Pain relief (paracetamol + ibuprofen) 10 each Pain management. Also: ibuprofen reduces inflammation.
Antihistamine (Zyrtec/Telfast) 5 Allergic reactions, insect bites, anaphylaxis support
Anti-diarrhoea (Imodium) 6 Diarrhoea + dehydration = rapid deterioration

Tier 3 — Shelter, Food & Navigation (main compartment, bottom layer)

Item Quantity Why
Tarp (2.5m × 3m, lightweight) 1 Shelter, rain collection, ground sheet, signaling. Silver/reflective side preferred.
Cordage (550 paracord, 15m) 1 Shelter construction, repairs, improvised belt, boot lace, splint tie, fishing line core
Paper maps of the region 1 set Electronics fail. Paper doesn't. Hema or Westprint. Folded in waterproof bag.
Compass (Suunto or Silva) 1 Navigation without GPS. Know how to use it before you need it.
Head torch (with red light mode) 1 Hands-free. Red mode preserves night vision and is less visible from distance.
Spare batteries for head torch 1 set Store separately from torch (prevents accidental activation drain)
Emergency food 2 days High-energy, no-cook, won't melt: muesli bars × 4, nuts/dried fruit × 500g, jerky × 200g, chocolate × 200g
Water purification tablets 20 Treats 20L. Backup if water runs out and natural source found.
Heavy-duty garbage bags 3 Rain poncho, water collection, waterproofing, emergency insulation
Zip ties (300mm × 8mm wide) 20 Repairs, shelter building, makeshift handcuffs, gear attachment
Duct tape (small roll — 5m wrapped around water bottle) 1 Repairs, medical (blister prevention), gear fixes
Whistle 1 Audible signal carries much further than voice. 3 blasts = SOS.
Signal mirror 1 Visible from aircraft 30km+ on clear days.
Notebook + pencil (small) 1 Leave notes if moving. Record water consumption. Document injuries for rescuers. Pencil works when wet.
Multi-tool (Leatherman or similar) 1 Pliers, screwdrivers, knife, saw, file — all in one

Optional — Add If Space Allows

Item Why
Stainless steel water bottle (1L, single-wall) Boil water directly in it over fire. Nalgene/collapsible bottles cannot do this.
Sawyer Mini water filter Filters 100,000L. Removes bacteria and protozoa. Faster than tablets.
Ground sheet (Tyvek, lightweight) Separate from tarp. Keeps you dry from ground moisture.
Insect repellent (DEET, small) Tropical north — prevents mosquito-borne illness.
Sunscreen (SPF50, small) Australian sun burns fast. Even one day without can be debilitating.
Spare prescription glasses If you wear them. You cannot survive what you cannot see.
Fire steel (ferro rod) with striker Backup fire source. Works when wet. Lasts thousands of strikes.
Fishing kit (small: hooks, line, sinkers) Protein if stranded near water. Weighs almost nothing.
Spare phone charging cable + small power bank Phone may survive evacuation. Last chance communication.
Copy of ID and emergency contacts (laminated) In waterproof bag. Rescuers need to know who you are.

4. Where to Keep the Grab Bag

Position 1: Passenger Footwell (Recommended)

  • Easiest to grab — lean over, one hand, pull it out
  • Visible — you can see it in smoke
  • Protected from direct sun and heat
  • Disadvantage: takes up passenger legroom. Acceptable trade.

Position 2: Behind Driver's Seat

  • Driver can reach back and grab by feel
  • Out of the way in daily use
  • Disadvantage: harder to reach in a rollover where the vehicle is on its side

Position 3: Strapped to Cargo Barrier

  • At head height, visible, easy grab from driver
  • Disadvantage: in a wagon, you may need to fold seats to access — that's impossible in a fire

Position 4: On the Passenger Seat (When Travelling)

  • If driving solo, put it on the passenger seat. Instant access.
  • If carrying a passenger, it goes in the footwell or behind the seat

Where NOT to Keep It

  • ❌ In the boot/trunk — unreachable in fire, may be crushed in rollover
  • ❌ On the roof rack — inaccessible, exposed to weather and theft
  • ❌ Buried under other gear — "I'll just dig it out" doesn't work in 30 seconds
  • ❌ In a locked toolbox or drawer — keys get lost, locks jam in crashes

5. Vehicle Fire — What Happens and What to Do

Timeline

Time Event
0:00 Fire detected — smell, smoke, flames visible
0:05 Stop vehicle immediately. Engine OFF.
0:10 Everyone OUT. Do NOT gather belongings. Do NOT open the bonnet (oxygen = fireball).
0:15 Grab bag retrieved. Move 50m+ from vehicle, UPWIND.
0:30 Activate PLB.
0:60 Vehicle fully engulfed. Fuel tank may rupture.

The window between "I smell smoke" and "the car is a fireball" is typically 30–90 seconds.

Key Facts

  • A vehicle fire in the Australian bush can start a bushfire within MINUTES. Get the vehicle off the track onto bare ground if safe to do so.
  • Fuel tanks (petrol especially) can rupture and create a flowing fire that spreads downhill
  • Tyres explode violently when heated — shrapnel travels 50m+
  • Burning vehicles produce toxic smoke (plastics, upholstery, battery acid) — stay UPWIND
  • Magnesium components (some steering wheels, gearbox casings) burn at 3,100°C and cannot be extinguished with water — they burn UNDERWATER

6. Other Scenarios Requiring the Grab Bag

Rollover

  • Vehicle on its side or roof. Occupants may be disoriented.
  • Engine may still be running — turn it OFF if you can reach the key
  • Fuel may be leaking — smell for it, move UPHILL of any fuel smell
  • Grab bag may be pinned or thrown — know WHERE it started so you can search the cabin
  • If the vehicle is upside down: cut seatbelts with the fixed blade (carried on your person, not in the bag — see below)

Flash Flood / Water Crossing Gone Wrong

  • Vehicle swept off crossing or caught in rising water
  • Seconds to get out before water pressure prevents doors opening
  • If submerged: windows DOWN immediately (electrics fail within seconds). Doors WON'T open against water pressure until cabin is flooded — window is your only exit
  • Grab bag: if accessible, grab it. If not, GO WITHOUT IT. Your life is more important than any bag.
  • The PLB lanyard should be accessible SEPARATELY from the bag (see Section 7)

Bushfire Approaching

  • Vehicle caught in bushfire zone with no escape route
  • Decision time is seconds, not minutes
  • If escape not possible: shelter IN the vehicle (last resort). Lie on the floor below window level. Cover with woollen blanket. The vehicle will protect you from radiant heat for 1-2 minutes as the fire front passes. The fuel tank is unlikely to explode (modern vehicles have vapour recovery systems). After the front passes, exit with grab bag.
  • Better option if available: clear a patch of bare ground, lie face down, cover yourself with the emergency blanket (reflective side out). The fire will pass over in 30-60 seconds.

7. The PLB Lanyard — Separate from the Bag

This is critical. If you cannot reach the grab bag, YOU STILL NEED THE PLB.

Wear the PLB on a lanyard around your neck when driving in remote areas. It weighs 130g. It is waterproof. It does not need batteries. It will save your life.

If you are thrown from the vehicle in a rollover with no bag, no water, no supplies — you can still activate a PLB around your neck.

If the vehicle is on fire and the grab bag is burning in the footwell — you can still activate the PLB around your neck.

The PLB should be considered personal safety equipment, not bag equipment. Carry a SECOND PLB in the grab bag if budget allows. The one on your body is the one that matters.

8. Maintenance Checklist

Before every trip: - [ ] Check PLB battery expiry date (10-year life from manufacture) - [ ] Check PLB registration is current with AMSA - [ ] Rotate water (replace if older than 6 months) - [ ] Rotate food (replace annually or before expiry) - [ ] Check torch batteries - [ ] Check all sealed packaging intact (burn gel, antiseptic swabs) - [ ] Test lighter - [ ] Verify paper maps are current and for the region you're visiting - [ ] Sharp check on fixed blade knife

After every trip: - [ ] Replenish any items used - [ ] Dry out bag if damp - [ ] Repack in correct tier order - [ ] Return bag to designated position
