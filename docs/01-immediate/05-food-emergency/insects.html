Insects as Food — Safe Protein

Insects are the most reliable wild protein source. They are everywhere, abundant, require no tools to harvest, and — if you can get past the psychological barrier — many are genuinely good food. Across Southeast Asia, China, and Indigenous Australia, entomophagy (insect eating) has been practiced for millennia.

Why Insects?

Advantage Detail
Abundant There are approximately 1.4 billion insects per human on Earth
Protein-rich Most edible insects are 40–70% protein (dry weight)
Easy to catch No tools required — hand collection works for most
Safe (if rules followed) Fewer toxic species than plants
Nutrient-dense High in iron, zinc, B12, and essential fats
Energy-efficient harvest You burn fewer calories collecting insects than hunting mammals

The Golden Rules of Insect Eating

  1. Cook everything. Heat kills parasites, bacteria, and deactivates some defensive toxins.
  2. Avoid brightly coloured insects — red, orange, yellow combined with black usually means toxic.
  3. Avoid hairy caterpillars — urticating hairs cause severe skin reactions, and some are toxic internally.
  4. Avoid insects that feed on toxic plants — they accumulate plant toxins. (E.g., caterpillars on oleander.)
  5. Avoid insects with strong chemical odours — stink bugs, some beetles.
  6. Avoid insects found dead — you do not know what killed them. Only collect live insects.
  7. Worms and grubs are your best bet — they are soft-bodied, easy to cook, high in fat and protein, and generally safe.

Safe Insects — The Top 15

Grubs & Larvae

1. Witchetty Grub (Endoxyla leucomochla)

  • Range: Central and northern Australia
  • What it is: Large white wood-boring moth larva (up to 7 cm)
  • Taste: Described as "nutty scrambled eggs" when cooked; "almond paste" when raw
  • Nutrition: High in protein and fat (~38% protein, ~40% fat)
  • Preparation: Eat raw (traditional Aboriginal method) or roast in coals for 1–2 minutes. Remove head before eating raw (it bites).
  • Harvest: Find in the roots and trunks of witchetty bushes and some eucalypts. Look for bore holes at base of plants.

2. Bamboo Grub / Bamboo Worm (Omphisa fuscidentalis)

  • Range: Throughout SEA, southern China
  • What it is: Moth larva that lives inside bamboo internodes
  • Taste: Creamy, slightly sweet
  • Nutrition: High in protein (~26%) and fat (~51%)
  • Preparation: Traditional method: skewer and roast over fire. Also fried or added to soups. Staple food in northern Thailand and Laos.
  • Harvest: Cut bamboo culms that show entry holes. Split open to extract grubs.

3. Palm Weevil Larva (Rhynchophorus spp.)

  • Range: Tropical SEA, northern Australia, Pacific Islands
  • What it is: Large white/cream beetle larva found in rotting palm trunks
  • Taste: "Bacon-flavoured" when fried; creamy when raw. Considered a delicacy across SEA.
  • Nutrition: Extremely high in fat (~60%), good protein (~20%)
  • Preparation: Skewer and roast, or pan-fry in their own fat
  • Harvest: Find fallen or rotting palm trunks. Split open to find larvae. A single palm trunk can yield hundreds.

4. Bogong Moth (adult) (Agrotis infusa)

  • Range: Southeastern Australia (migratory)
  • What it is: Moth that aestivates (summer-hibernates) in large numbers in the Australian Alps
  • Taste: Nutty, slightly smoky when roasted
  • Nutrition: Very high in fat (~60%)
  • Preparation: Traditionally roasted in hot ashes, then winnowed to remove wings and legs. Staple Aboriginal food for millennia.
  • Note: During drought years, Bogong moths may accumulate arsenic from agricultural areas. Use with caution if near farmland.

Crickets & Grasshoppers

5. Field Cricket (Teleogryllus spp.)

  • Range: Worldwide — all AU, CN, SEA regions
  • Taste: Nutty, savoury
  • Nutrition: ~60% protein, good iron and B12
  • Preparation: Remove wings and legs (the spurs on legs can irritate the throat). Roast or boil. Boiled crickets make a good soup base.
  • Harvest: Look under rocks, logs, and in tall grass. Most active at night.

6. Grasshopper / Locust (Acrididae)

  • Range: Worldwide — all regions. Locust swarms occur in inland AU and northern CN.
  • Taste: Smoky, nutty
  • Nutrition: ~50% protein
  • Preparation: Remove wings, legs, and head. Roast until crispy. Can be ground into a protein powder/flour for adding to other foods.
  • Warning: Some grasshopper species accumulate toxins from the plants they eat (especially brightly coloured species). Stick to brown/green grasshoppers.

Ants & Termites

7. Green Tree Ant / Weaver Ant (Oecophylla smaragdina)

  • Range: Northern Australia, SEA, southern China
  • What it is: Arboreal ant that weaves leaf nests in trees
  • Taste: Tangy, citrus-like — their abdomens contain formic acid with a lime/lemon flavour
  • Uses: Eat the abdomen for a vitamin C boost. Crushed ants in water make a tangy, nutritious beverage. Aboriginal Australians use them for colds and sore throats.
  • Preparation: Grab the whole nest and shake ants into a container. Crush or eat whole abdomens. Can be eaten raw (traditional use).

8. Termites (Isoptera)

  • Range: Throughout AU (especially), SEA, southern CN
  • Taste: Nutty, slightly woody
  • Nutrition: High in protein (~38%) and fat (~45%)
  • Preparation: Roast in a pan or on hot coals. Can be ground into flour. The queen termite is a massive protein/fat source and considered a delicacy.
  • Harvest: Break open termite mounds or rotting wood. Use a stick inserted into a tunnel — termites will bite and cling to it when withdrawn.

9. Honeypot Ant (Camponotus inflatus / Melophorus spp.)

  • Range: Arid and semi-arid Australia
  • What it is: Specialised worker ants that store nectar in their hugely distended abdomens, acting as living food storage for the colony
  • Taste: The abdomen is a small sphere of sweet nectar — a natural sugar source in the desert
  • Nutrition: Sugar (carbohydrate), small amount of protein
  • Preparation: Gently pull the ant from its underground chamber. Bite off the abdomen. The rest is discarded.
  • Harvest: Dig carefully around the nest entrance. Honeypot chambers can be up to 2 m deep.

Other Insects & Arthropods

10. Mealworms / Darkling Beetle Larvae (Tenebrio molitor)

  • Range: Worldwide (stored product pest — find in grain stores, flour, abandoned pantries)
  • Taste: Mild, nutty
  • Nutrition: ~50% protein, ~30% fat
  • Preparation: Roast in a dry pan. Excellent addition to foraged greens for a complete meal.

11. Silkworm Pupae (Bombyx mori)

  • Range: China, parts of SEA (near mulberry trees)
  • What it is: The pupa left after silk is extracted from the cocoon
  • Taste: Creamy, savoury
  • Nutrition: High in protein and essential amino acids
  • Preparation: Boiled or stir-fried. A traditional byproduct food in Chinese silk-producing regions. If you find mulberry trees, you may find silkworms.

12. Cicadas

  • Range: Throughout Australia, SEA, China (seasonal — summer)
  • Taste: Asparagus-like
  • Nutrition: Good protein
  • Preparation: Remove wings. Roast until crispy. The emerging teneral (newly moulted) cicadas are soft and white — these are the best eating. Catch them at dawn during emergence.
  • Harvest: Listen for the loud buzzing. Look on tree trunks at dawn for emerging nymphs (climbing up from underground).

13. Giant Water Bug (Lethocerus indicus)

  • Range: Freshwater habitats throughout SEA, southern CN
  • Taste: Intense, aromatic. Used as a flavouring in Thai and Vietnamese cuisine (nam phrik).
  • Preparation: Remove the scent gland. Roast or grind into paste. The aroma is very strong — used more as a seasoning than a main food.

14. Sago Grub (Rhynchophorus ferrugineus)

  • Range: SEA, Pacific Islands (in sago palms)
  • What it is: Weevil larva found in sago palm trunks
  • Taste: Rich, fatty, bacon-like when fried
  • Nutrition: Very high in fat
  • Harvest: Fell a sago palm and leave it for 1–2 months. The larvae will infest the pith.

15. Earthworms

  • Range: Worldwide in moist soil
  • Taste: Earthy
  • Nutrition: ~60–70% protein (dry weight)
  • Preparation: Purge worms by keeping them in clean water for a few hours (they expel soil from their gut). Then boil or roast. Can be dried and ground into protein powder.
  • Harvest: Dig in rich, moist soil. Look after rain. Alternatively, drive a stake into the ground and vibrate it — worms surface.

Insects to AVOID

Insect Why avoid
Hairy caterpillars (processionary, tussock moths, etc.) Urticating hairs cause severe skin/eye/respiratory reactions. Some are internally toxic.
Brightly coloured insects (red, orange, yellow + black) Aposematic colouration = toxic or foul-tasting
Stink bugs (Pentatomidae) Intense, foul chemical taste. Some are mildly toxic.
Blister beetles (Meloidae) Contain cantharidin — causes severe skin blistering and internal bleeding
Ladybirds/ladybugs (Coccinellidae) Secrete foul-tasting, mildly toxic alkaloids
Insects found on toxic plants (e.g., caterpillars on oleander) Accumulate plant toxins
Flies and mosquitoes Disease vectors; not worth the negligible nutrition
Ticks Disease vectors; not food
Slugs and snails Can carry rat lungworm (Angiostrongylus — causes meningitis). If eating snails, purge for several days and cook thoroughly.
Cane toads (technically an amphibian, not insect) Toxin in skin and glands. Common Toxics

Insects consistently outperform conventional meat in iron and often in protein density. For a survivor in calorie deficit, insects provide exceptional nutritional return for the effort invested.

Psychological Barrier

If you have never eaten insects, the idea is viscerally off-putting. This is cultural, not biological. Two billion people across Asia, Africa, and Latin America eat insects regularly.

Tips for first-timers: - Start with roasted crickets — they are nutty, crunchy, and familiar-textured - Grind them and mix with other food so you do not see the form - Focus on the taste, not the source - In a genuine survival situation, hunger overrides disgust faster than you think
