Drowning — Water Rescue & Aftercare

Drowning is the third leading cause of unintentional injury death worldwide. In the Asia-Pacific region — with its extensive coastlines, rivers, monsoon flooding, and tropical storms — water rescue is an essential survival skill.

Scene Safety — Do Not Become a Victim

The most common cause of multiple drowning fatalities is a would-be rescuer entering the water and drowning themselves.

Before entering the water:

  • Reach — Extend a pole, branch, oar, or throw a rope. Stay on solid ground.
  • Throw — Throw a buoyant object (life ring, empty container, cooler, log). Aim upwind/upstream.
  • Row — Use a boat if available. Approach from downwind/downstream.
  • Go — Enter the water only as a last resort, and only if you are a strong swimmer. Bring a flotation aid.

The Drowning Sequence

 Panic → Breath-holding → Involuntary inhalation of water
 → Laryngospasm (throat closes) → Hypoxia → Cardiac arrest

Key points: - Drowning takes 20–60 seconds from submersion to unconsciousness in an adult, 10–30 seconds in a child - Only a small amount of water enters the lungs (laryngospasm prevents most) - The primary problem is lack of oxygen, not water in the lungs - This is why rescue breaths matter in drowning CPR

Resuscitation After Water Rescue

If the Person is Conscious and Breathing

{% steps %} Remove wet clothing; Dry and warm the person — wrap in blankets, clothing, emergency blanket; Place in recovery position if nauseous or weak; Give warm (not hot) fluids if fully alert; Monitor for at least 4 hours — secondary drowning can occur {% end %}

If the Person is Unconscious but Breathing

{% steps %} Check airway — clear visible debris from mouth; Place in recovery position; Keep warm — wrap in blankets or emergency blanket; Monitor breathing continuously — breath every 5–10 seconds is normal; Be prepared to start CPR if breathing stops; Monitor for vomiting — drowning victims often vomit swallowed water {% end %}

If the Person is Not Breathing

{% steps %} Shout for help; Open the airway — head-tilt/chin-lift; Give 5 initial rescue breaths — drowning is respiratory arrest, and these early breaths are critical; If still no signs of life, begin CPR: 30 compressions : 2 breaths; Continue until signs of life, help arrives, or you are exhausted {% end %}

→ For detailed CPR technique, see CPR & Basic Life Support.

{% info %} Spinal injury precautions: If the drowning involved diving into shallow water, surfing, or a fall from height, suspect spinal injury. Open the airway using a jaw thrust (hands on either side of the head, push the jaw forward without tilting the neck) instead of head-tilt/chin-lift. If the jaw thrust does not open the airway, use head-tilt/chin-lift anyway — life over limb. {% end %}

Hypothermia After Immersion

Water conducts heat 25 times faster than air. In water below 20°C, hypothermia develops rapidly.

Water temperature Survival time (approximate)
20–25°C 2–12 hours
15–20°C 1–6 hours
10–15°C 30 min – 2 hours
5–10°C 15–90 minutes
< 5°C < 15–45 minutes

Cold water is protective for the brain, even though it threatens the body. There are documented cases of full neurological recovery after 60+ minutes of submersion in cold water. Never assume a cold-water drowning victim is beyond saving. Begin CPR and continue.

Hypothermia Signs After Water Rescue

Stage Signs
Mild (35–32°C) Shivering, cold hands/feet, fatigue, mild confusion
Moderate (32–28°C) Violent shivering stops, confusion, slurred speech, poor coordination
Severe (< 28°C) Unconscious, rigid, barely breathing, may appear dead

Rewarming

{% steps %} Move the person to shelter — out of wind and onto insulation (mat, leaves, clothing); Remove all wet clothing — cut it off if necessary; Dry the person thoroughly — pat dry, do not rub (rubbing damages cold skin); Wrap in warm, dry layers — emergency blanket, sleeping bag, multiple layers of clothing; Cover the head — a hat or improvised head covering; Apply warm (not hot) objects to armpits, groin, and neck — wrapped warm rocks, water bottles if available; If conscious and alert, give warm, sweet drinks — no alcohol, no caffeine; Do not rub or massage extremities — this can push cold blood to the heart and cause cardiac arrest; Handle gently — the cold heart is irritable and susceptible to arrhythmia {% end %}

Secondary Drowning (Delayed Drowning)

A person can appear to recover from a near-drowning, then deteriorate and die hours later. This is caused by:

  • Laryngospasm that partially resolves but recurs
  • Pulmonary oedema — fluid leaking into lungs from damaged tissue
  • Aspiration pneumonia — infection from contaminated water in lungs

Warning Signs (First 24 Hours)

  • Persistent cough
  • Shortness of breath or rapid breathing
  • Chest pain
  • Extreme fatigue or sleepiness
  • Behavioural changes (irritability, confusion — especially in children)
  • Bluish lips or nail beds
  • Frothy sputum

If these develop, the person needs advanced medical care. In the field, keep the person resting, warm, and sitting upright. Monitor breathing continuously.

Water Rescue in Flood Conditions

Monsoons across , cyclones in northern Australia, and typhoons in southern create flood conditions regularly.

Flood-specific dangers: - Debris — submerged objects, broken glass, sewage - Contaminated water — floodwater carries cholera, leptospirosis, typhoid - Currents — even 15 cm of moving water can knock an adult off their feet - Drains and culverts — invisible from the surface, create deadly suction

{% steps %} Do not enter floodwater unless absolutely necessary; If you must wade, use a pole to probe ahead for holes and debris; Wear footwear — cuts in contaminated water lead to serious infection; After any floodwater contact, wash thoroughly with clean water and soap; Treat any cuts from floodwater debris as potentially infected — clean with antiseptic {% end %}

Emptying Water from the Lungs

Do not attempt to drain water from the lungs. The old technique of abdominal compressions or hanging someone upside down is outdated and dangerous:

  • Only a small amount of water enters the lungs
  • Attempting to "drain" water wastes time that should be spent on resuscitation
  • It increases the risk of vomiting and aspiration

Just begin CPR. Chest compressions will move any water in the lungs out through the airway.

Aftercare for the Rescuer

Water rescue is physically and emotionally intense. After the event:

  • You will be cold and exhausted — get dry and warm
  • You may have swallowed water — monitor yourself for secondary drowning symptoms
  • Adrenaline crash can cause shaking, nausea, emotional release — this is normal
  • Talk to someone about the experience — do not process it alone
  • If the person did not survive: you gave them a chance they did not have without you
