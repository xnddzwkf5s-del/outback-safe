Fire Basics — Tinder, Kindling & Fuel

Most failed fires fail before they begin — because the builder did not prepare properly. The most common mistake is attempting to light kindling before tinder has caught. Master the preparation sequence and you master fire.

Materials Checklist

Before striking a single spark, gather everything:

📋 Materials
  • **Tinder:** enough to fill both cupped hands — dry, fine, fluffy material that catches from a spark or ember
  • **Stage 1 kindling:** pencil-lead thin twigs (1–2 mm), fistful
  • **Stage 2 kindling:** pencil-thick twigs (5–8 mm), fistful
  • **Stage 3 kindling:** finger-thick sticks (1–2 cm), armload
  • **Stage 4 kindling:** wrist-thick branches (3–5 cm), multiple pieces
  • **Fuel wood:** stack of wrist-thick and larger wood, enough for at least 2 hours of burn time
  • **Fire site:** cleared area 2 m radius, ring of rocks (not river rocks), water or sand nearby to extinguish
  • **Ignition source:** lighter, ferro rod, friction set, or solar lens — whatever method you are using

Phase 1: Understanding Fire

The Fire Triangle

Every fire needs three elements. Remove any one and the fire dies:

  • Heat — ignition source (spark, friction, focused sunlight)
  • Fuel — material that burns (tinder, kindling, fuel wood)
  • Oxygen — air flow (a smothered fire dies; a well-ventilated fire thrives)

The Fire Preparation Rule

Gather everything before you strike a single spark.

If you are fumbling for kindling while your tinder burns, you have already lost.

Phase 2: Tinder — What Catches the Spark

Tinder is material that ignites from a spark, ember, or brief flame. It must be:

  • Dry — bone dry. Any moisture and it won't catch.
  • Fine — separated fibres, fluffed up, air between strands
  • Abundant — you need more than you think

Best Natural Tinders (AU/CN/SEA)

Making Char Cloth

Char cloth is fabric that has been heated without oxygen — it becomes a carbon-rich material that catches the faintest spark.

📋 Materials
  • 100% cotton fabric (t-shirt, denim, bandana — synthetics do not work)
  • Small metal tin with a tight lid (shoe polish tin, mint tin)
  • Nail or awl to punch a hole in the lid

Phase 3: Kindling — What Carries the Flame

Kindling bridges the gap between tinder (fast-burning) and fuel (slow-burning). Without proper kindling, your tinder will burn out before the fuel catches.

Kindling progression — must be gathered in this sequence:

Stage Thickness Quantity needed Burn time
Stage 1 Pencil-lead thin (1–2 mm) Fistful 5–10 seconds
Stage 2 Pencil-thick (5–8 mm) Fistful 30–60 seconds
Stage 3 Finger-thick (1–2 cm) Armload 1–3 minutes
Stage 4 Wrist-thick (3–5 cm) Multiple pieces 5–15 minutes

Every stage must be dry. Test by snapping a twig — if it bends without snapping, it is too wet. If it snaps cleanly, it is dry enough.

Gathering Kindling in Wet Conditions

  • Dead standing wood — branches still attached to trees but dead. They stay drier than ground wood.
  • Split open thicker dead wood — the interior is often dry even in rain.
  • Under dense tree canopies — the ground may be drier directly against the trunk.
  • Bamboo — split lengthwise; the inside is usually dry.

Phase 4: Fuel — What Keeps It Burning

Once the fire is established with kindling, fuel wood sustains it.

Fuel wood types:

Type Characteristics Best for
Softwoods (pine, cypress) Burns fast, hot, sparks Quick heat, starting fires
Hardwoods (eucalypt, oak, acacia) Burns slow, hot, coals well Sustained fire, cooking, overnight banking
Green wood Does not burn well — sizzles, smokes NOT for fire. For construction, tool handles.

AU/CN/SEA fuel woods:

Wood Region Quality
Ironbark (Eucalyptus) Eastern Australia Excellent — burns very hot, long-lasting coals
Red gum (E. camaldulensis) Australia-wide Excellent — dense, coals well
Acacia / wattle Australia-wide Good — coals well, some species are smoky
Bamboo (dry) SEA, southern China Fair — burns fast, loud popping (trapped air pockets explode). Split to reduce popping.
Mango wood (dry) SEA, northern Australia Good — burns relatively clean for a fruitwood
Pine China, introduced AU Fair — burns fast, resin creates soot. Good for starting; poor for sustained fire.
Oak China Excellent — dense, slow-burning, excellent coals

Phase 5: Fire Lays — Configuring Your Fire

How you stack the wood determines how the fire burns. Choose the lay that matches your purpose.

Teepee Fire

Best for: Starting fires, quick heat, cooking on a pot hung from a tripod above.

Log Cabin Fire

Best for: Cooking (flat top), sustained fire, wet conditions (the structure self-feeds as logs burn and fall inward).

Star Fire

Best for: Conserving fuel, all-night fire, solo survival. You can sleep while the fire feeds itself slowly.

Dakota Fire Hole

Best for: High-wind conditions, concealment, fuel conservation, windy Australian outback or plains.

Reflector Fire

Best for: Shelter heating. Redirects 30–50% more heat toward you compared to an open fire.

Phase 6: Fire in Wet Conditions

Tropical downpours, monsoons, and persistent drizzle are common across SEA, northern Australia, and southern China. Fire is harder — but still possible.

Troubleshooting

Problem Likely cause Fix
Tinder won't catch Too damp, not fine enough, or spark missing Dry tinder against body heat for 10 minutes. Fluff it more. Check spark placement.
Tinder catches but dies No kindling ready, or kindling too thick Have kindling staged BEFORE lighting. Start with pencil-lead thin.
Kindling catches but won't spread Packed too tight, no airflow Loosen the lay. Leave gaps. Fire needs oxygen.
Fire smokes heavily, weak flame Wet fuel, green wood, or overloaded Remove wet/green pieces. Reduce fuel load. Give it air.
Fire dies when adding fuel Thermal shock — cold, large log on small fire Feed progressively. Warm the log near the fire first.
Fire won't stay lit in wind No windbreak Build a windbreak from logs, rocks, or dig a small pit. Light from the sheltered side.
One big log kills the fire Crushes flame, blocks airflow, absorbs heat Feed progressively. Thumb-sized additions until the fire is established.

Safety Checkpoints

Fire safety in Australian, Chinese, and SEA conditions:

ℹ️ Information
**⛔ Before lighting — stop and check:** - [ ] 2 m radius cleared to bare earth? - [ ] Fire ring in place (NOT river rocks)? - [ ] Water, sand, or shovel within arm's reach? - [ ] No overhanging branches? - [ ] Wind not carrying sparks toward dry vegetation? - [ ] Checked local fire danger — during Australian bushfire season, a total fire ban may be in place? **⛔ Extinguishing — the cold-hand test:** 1. Douse with water — pour until hissing stops 2. Stir ashes and coals with a stick 3. Douse again 4. Hold back of hand 5 cm above ashes 5. If you feel ANY warmth, repeat from step 1 6. Cold to the touch = fire is out

Common Mistakes

  • Not enough preparation — gathering fuel WHILE the fire burns = dead fire. Gather everything first.
  • Kindling too thick — going from tinder directly to pencil-thick kindling. You need the pencil-lead stage.
  • Packed too tight — fire needs airflow. A tightly packed pile of kindling smothers itself.
  • Wet fuel — "it looks dry" is not the same as "it is dry." If it doesn't snap cleanly, find drier wood.
  • One big log on a small fire — crushes the flame, blocks airflow, and the thermal mass sucks heat away. Feed progressively.
  • Building too large too soon — start smaller than you think necessary. A fist-sized fire that is burning is infinitely better than an armload of wood that is not.

Success Indicators

A well-built fire shows these signs:

  • Clean, bright flame — not smoky, not struggling. Yellow-orange, not dull red.
  • Steady crackle — not popping (wet wood) or silent with smoke (smothered).
  • Hot enough to feel at arm's length — a proper fire radiates noticeable warmth at 1 m.
  • Coals forming within 30 minutes — a good bed of glowing coals develops naturally.
  • Easy to feed — adding fuel doesn't threaten the fire; it grows steadily.

Australian Fire Woods — What Burns Best

Wood Region Burn quality Best for
Gum leaves (Eucalyptus spp.) Everywhere in AU Burn very fast, high heat, short duration. Good tinder. Starting the fire, getting kindling hot. Not for sustained burn.
Paperbark (Melaleuca spp.) Northern and eastern AU Excellent fire starter. Peels in thin oily sheets. Catches from a spark. Burns fast. Tinder and fire-starter material. The best Australian tinder.
Mulga (Acacia aneura) Central and western AU Extremely dense (hardwood). Burns very hot and long. Excellent coals. Sustained fire, cooking, overnight banking. One of Australia's best fuel woods.
Spinifex (Triodia spp.) Arid interior AU Burns fiercely and fast — intense short flame. Fire starter. POOR for cooking — too fast, no sustained heat.
Wattle / Acacia (various spp.) Throughout AU Burns well, good coals. Some species are smoky. Good general-purpose fuel.
Casuarina / She-oak (Casuarina spp.) Coastal and inland Excellent — burns hot, long-lasting coals, little smoke. Cooking fire, sustained heat.
Ironbark (Eucalyptus spp.) Eastern AU Among the hottest-burning Australian woods. Very dense. Sustained fire, coals for cooking.
Red Gum (E. camaldulensis) Along watercourses everywhere Excellent — dense, clean burn, great coals. Cooking and sustained fire.
Stringybark (Eucalyptus spp.) Eastern and southern AU Good — fibrous bark is excellent tinder. Wood itself is good fuel. Tinder from bark. Fuel from wood.
Grass tree (Xanthorrhoea spp.) Coastal heath, ranges (east coast, south-west WA) Flower spike is excellent friction fire material. Whole plant burns incredibly fiercely. Fire starter in emergencies. Respect bushfire risk — grass tree fires burn extremely hot.

Fire in Extreme Australian Conditions

Total Fire Ban Days

Australia has formal fire danger ratings. Do NOT light fires on Total Fire Ban (TOBAN) days.

Fire Danger Rating What it means Can I light a survival fire?
Moderate Fire can be controlled Proceed with caution, cleared site
High Fire can be difficult to control Only if essential, very careful
Extreme Fire will be very dangerous Only if life-threatening emergency
Catastrophic Fire will be uncontrollable No. Even a survival fire can escalate to a bushfire. Use cold camp. If you MUST have fire (life-threatening cold), choose a beach, riverbed, or previously burnt ground.

Check the local fire danger rating at any roadside fire danger sign, online, or ask at a visitor centre. Ignorance is not an excuse — lighting a fire during a total fire ban can result in fines of $27,500+ and/or imprisonment under Australian law.

Wet Season Tropics (Northern Australia)

Everything is soaked for months. Wood is saturated. Fire-making is genuinely difficult:

Desert — Scarce Fuel

In the Australian desert, fuel is widely scattered and often thorny:

  • Plan ahead — collect firewood as you travel during the day, don't wait until dark.
  • Dead mulga is gold — it burns long and hot. Carry a few pieces when you find them.
  • Spinifex clumps — the dead outer leaves burn fiercely. Good for starting, poor for cooking.
  • Animal dung — kangaroo and camel dung burn well when dry. Gather it.
  • Night fire — in desert, fire is as important for psychological comfort as warmth. Collect enough during the day.
  • Wind — desert nights often have strong winds. Build a Dakota fire hole or use a windbreak.
