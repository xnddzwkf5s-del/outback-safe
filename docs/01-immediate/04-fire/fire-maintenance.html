Fire Maintenance

Starting a fire is one skill. Keeping it alive through the night, through rain, and across days is another. A well-maintained fire is a constant companion — it requires attention but repays it with warmth, safety, and signalling. Letting a fire die can mean hours of work to restart it.

Materials Checklist

📋 Materials
  • **Fire tools:** long green stick (fire poker), two green sticks for tongs, hollow bamboo or reed (bellows), curved bark piece (ash scoop)
  • **Overnight materials:** large log 20–30 cm diameter for all-night burn, ash from previous fires for banking, long logs for star fire
  • **Transport materials:** punk wood or bracket fungus for slow match, metal tin or folded bark for fire carrier, green branch and bark for torch
  • **Rain protection:** tarp, plastic sheet, or large bark sheets, green logs for elevated platform

The Fire Tender Mindset

Fire is alive. It breathes, eats, and needs attention. If you treat it as a task to complete, it will die when you are not looking. If you treat it as a camp member that needs regular feeding and occasional adjustment, it will serve you reliably.

Rule: never walk past the fire without checking it. Add a stick if needed. Adjust a log. Every pass-by is an opportunity to maintain.

The Lifecycle of a Fire

Phase What it needs What not to do
Ignition Tinder, steady flame, wind protection Do not pile on large fuel — it crushes the flame
Growth Progressive kindling, good airflow Do not let it run out of kindling before fuel catches
Sustained burn Regular fuel additions, air management Do not overload — smothers the fire
Coal bed Let large logs burn down; good for cooking Do not stir vigorously — scatters the coals
Banking Cover coals with ash/dirt, restrict airflow Do not seal completely — fire needs minimal oxygen
Re-ignition Gently uncover coals, add tinder, blow Do not dump fuel on — start small again

Phase 4: Fuel Conservation

Fuel gathering consumes calories. Efficient fire management reduces the fuel burden.

Technique Fuel saving Method
Small fire is a hot fire 50% A knee-high fire is more efficient than a waist-high bonfire. Sit closer.
Star fire 50% Push logs in as ends burn. Full control over burn rate.
Cooking over coals 30% Do not cook over flames — cook over the coal bed. More even heat, no flame scorching, uses less wood.
Dakota fire hole 40% Underground fire concentrates heat; burns less fuel for same cooking heat
Put the fire out when not needed 100% If it is midday and 35°C, you do not need a fire. Extinguish it.
Use the right wood Variable Dense hardwoods burn longer than softwoods. One ironbark log = 3 pine logs.

Phase 5: Extinguishing Safely

Whether to keep a fire burning while you sleep depends on several factors:

Condition Keep fire? Reason
Cold night, below 5°C Keep (banked or star fire) Hypothermia risk
Warm night, above 15°C Extinguish No heating benefit; fire risk if unattended
Wildfire risk (dry, windy) Extinguish Sparks can start bushfire while you sleep
In a shelter with fire reflector Keep (banked) Maintains shelter temperature
Unsure of safety (animals, people) Keep Fire = deterrent

Fire Tools

Improvised tools make fire management easier:

Tool How to improvise
Fire poker Long green stick — adjust logs without burning yourself
Coal tongs Two green sticks used like chopsticks — move hot coals
Bellows Hollow bamboo or reed — blow through it to direct air at the fire base without putting your face in the smoke
Ash scoop Large curved piece of bark or folded bark — move ash for banking

Common Mistakes

  • Leaving the fire unattended: Fires die fast without attention. Never assume it will be fine for "just a few minutes."
  • Overloading: Adding too much fuel at once smothers the fire. Feed progressively.
  • Under-banking: Banked coals with too little ash covering burn out in 1–2 hours instead of lasting the night.
  • Green wood for the all-night log: Green wood sizzles and smokes. Use seasoned hardwood instead — green wood as the main log produces more smoke than heat.
  • No coal bed before banking: Banking a fire that hasn't produced a proper coal bed = banking ash. Let the fire burn down to glowing coals first.
  • Slow match too tightly wrapped: No oxygen = the ember dies. The wrap should be snug but not airtight.

Success Indicators

  • Coal bed is deep and glowing: At least 5 cm of hot coals before banking or placing the all-night log
  • Banked coals are still warm after 8 hours: Uncover in the morning and find a red glow — or at minimum, warm ash that re-ignites with gentle blowing
  • Slow match smokes steadily: Not flaming, not cold. Gentle wisp of smoke every few seconds.
  • Fire re-ignites from banked coals in under 2 minutes: With proper tinder and gentle blowing, the fire comes back quickly
  • No burns: Using improvised tools to handle logs and coals — your hands stay unburned
