Lean-To

The lean-to is the fastest enclosed shelter you can build. If you have a tarp, emergency blanket, poncho, or large piece of plastic, you can have a waterproof shelter in 15 minutes. Without synthetic materials, a natural-material lean-to takes about an hour.

Quick-Reference Decision Matrix

Situation Choose lean-to Choose debris hut
You have a tarp or plastic sheet ✅ Lean-to
It is raining and you need shelter NOW ✅ Lean-to
You have limited time before dark ✅ Lean-to
You are in a group (needs to fit multiple people) ✅ Lean-to
It is very cold and you have no sleeping bag ✅ Debris hut (warmer)
You have no tarp ✅ Debris hut (more waterproof)
You'll be in the same spot for multiple days ✅ Debris hut (more durable)

Tarp Lean-To Variations

Variation 1: A-Frame Lean-To (Fastest)

{% materials %} Tarp, emergency blanket, or plastic sheet; Ridge line (cord, paracord, rope, or strong vine); Two anchor points (trees, poles, or forked sticks at each end); 4–6 ground stakes or heavy rocks; Additional cord for tie-downs if windy {% end %}

{% steps %} Tie the ridge line tightly between two trees at approximately 1.2–1.5 m height; Drape the tarp over the ridge line so it forms an A-frame (equal lengths on both sides); Pull the tarp edges out to form the shelter walls; Stake down or weigh down the side edges with rocks; If you have a groundsheet, place it inside before staking the walls; The tarp should be taut but not drum-tight — allow some give for wind; Angle the closed end toward the wind; The open ends face away from wind {% end %}

Aspect Rating
Time to build 10–15 minutes
Waterproofing Excellent (with intact tarp)
Wind protection Good — closed on both sides
Space 1–2 people comfortably
Weight capacity Moderate — snow will slide off steep sides
Fire compatibility Poor — closed ends block radiant heat

Variation 2: Single-Slope Lean-To (Fire-Friendly)

{% steps %} Tie the ridge line between two trees at approximately 1.5 m height (high side); Pull the tarp from the ridge line down to the ground at a 45° angle; Stake the bottom edge to the ground; The back (high side) faces the wind — wind hits the tarp and goes over; The open front faces away from wind; This design is better for a fire — the open front lets radiant heat in {% end %}

Aspect Rating
Time to build 10–15 minutes
Waterproofing Good — one-directional coverage
Wind protection Adequate — open front
Space 1–3 people
Fire compatibility Excellent — open front captures radiant heat
Best for Cold, dry conditions with a fire

{% info %} With a fire: A single-slope lean-to with the open face toward a fire and a reflector wall behind the fire is one of the coziest survival setups. The tarp reflects radiant heat back down onto you. {% end %}

Variation 3: Plow-Point (Wind-Directional)

{% steps %} Tie one corner of the tarp to a tree at approximately 1.5 m height; Pull the opposite corner straight back along the ground and stake it; Stake the two remaining corners to the ground, creating a wedge shape; The narrow opening faces away from wind; The wedge shape sheds wind aerodynamically; Tighten all edges — the tarp should be taut {% end %}

Aspect Rating
Time to build 5–10 minutes (fastest tarp shelter)
Waterproofing Good
Wind protection Excellent — aerodynamic wedge shape
Space 1 person
Fire compatibility Adequate — fire can be placed near the opening
Best for High-wind conditions, solo survival

Natural Material Lean-To

If you have no tarp, you can build an effective lean-to from natural materials in about an hour.

Ridge Pole Lean-To

{% materials %} Strong ridge pole (3–4 m, wrist-thick); Two support poles with forked tops (or use a tree fork); 8–12 rafter poles (thumb-thick, 2 m long); Thatching material (leaves, grass, bark, palm fronds); Cordage for lashing (vines, bark strips, paracord); Bedding material {% end %}

{% steps %} Set two forked support poles into the ground at the desired shelter length — about 1.2–1.5 m high; Alternatively, lash the ridge pole to a tree at one end and use one support at the other; Lay the ridge pole across the fork supports and lash it in place; Lean rafter poles against the ridge pole at 45–60° angle, spacing 15–20 cm apart; The rafter poles extend from the ridge pole down to the ground; Begin thatching from the bottom up — overlapping layers like roof shingles; Thatch thickness: 30+ cm for rain protection; Cover the ridge line thoroughly — this is where most leaks occur; Optional: build side walls by adding shorter rafters at each end {% end %}

Aspect Rating
Time to build 45–90 minutes
Waterproofing Good (30+ cm thatch)
Wind protection Adequate (open front)
Space 1–3 people
Durability 1–4 weeks depending on thatch material

Thatching Materials (AU/CN/)

Material How to prepare Durability
Palm fronds Fold over rafter so frond hangs on both sides; overlap like shingles 2–4 weeks
Paperbark sheets Peel large sheets; lay flat across rafters; weigh down with poles 2–3 months
Banana leaves Lay flat; overlap; become brittle quickly 3–5 days
Split bamboo Split lengthwise; remove nodes; alternate concave-up/concave-down like roof tiles 1–3 months
Grass bundles Tie grass into handful-sized bundles; tie to rafters from bottom up 2–4 weeks
Bark sheets (eucalyptus) Peel from dead trees; lay flat; heavy — needs strong rafters 1–3 months

Reflector Wall Addition

A lean-to becomes dramatically more effective when combined with a fire and reflector wall:

Key dimensions: - Fire: 1.5–2 m from the lean-to opening - Reflector wall: 1 m behind the fire - Wall height: 60–90 cm - Wall width: as wide as the lean-to opening

The reflector wall bounces heat back into the shelter. Without it, 90% of the fire's radiant heat is lost to the open air.

{% steps %} Build a wall of logs (green wood, so it does not catch fire) or rocks; Stack to 60–90 cm height; Slightly concave toward the shelter — it focuses reflected heat; Alternatively: drive four stakes into the ground and stack logs between them; Ensure the wall is stable — collapsing logs into the fire is dangerous {% end %}

{% info %} Temperature boost: A reflector wall can increase perceived warmth inside the shelter by 5–10°C when a fire is burning. This is the difference between shivering and comfortable sleep. {% end %}

Waterproofing Checks

Before you settle in for the night:

  1. Lie inside and look up — can you see sky through any gaps? Patch them.
  2. Pour a cup of water on the ridge line — does it run off or seep through? If it seeps, add more thatch at the ridge.
  3. Check the ground slope — if water runs under the shelter, dig a drainage trench on the uphill side.

Converting a Lean-To to a Debris Hut

If you have time and the weather is cold, convert your lean-to:

{% steps %} Thatch more heavily — build up to 60+ cm; Close off one or both ends with additional poles and thatch; Create a door plug for the entrance; The lean-to becomes an A-frame debris hut with better weatherproofing; This conversion takes an additional 1–2 hours but significantly improves warmth {% end %}
