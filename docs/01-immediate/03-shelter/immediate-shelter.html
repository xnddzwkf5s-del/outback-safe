Immediate Shelter

When exposure is imminent — you are wet, cold, and night is falling — you need shelter in minutes, not hours. This page covers what to use when you have no time to build.

Quick-Reference Decision Matrix

What you have Best immediate shelter Time needed Expected comfort
Vehicle nearby Sleep in vehicle 2 minutes Good
Tarp/plastic sheet Wrap yourself or rig basic lean-to 5–15 minutes Adequate
Emergency blanket (mylar) Wear as poncho, stuff with leaves 3 minutes Adequate
Cave/rock overhang visible Occupy cave or overhang 5 minutes (check first) Good to excellent
Dense leaf litter 10-minute debris pile 10–15 minutes Poor but survivable
Nothing at all Curl under largest tree, insulate clothing with leaves Immediately Survival only

The Improvised Shelter Hierarchy

In order of speed and quality:

  1. Existing structures — buildings, vehicles, shipping containers, bridges
  2. Natural cavities — caves, rock overhangs, large hollow trees
  3. Dense vegetation — thickets, under large trees, bamboo groves
  4. Terrain features — ditches, gullies, against embankments
  5. Your clothing — the shelter you are always wearing
  6. Quick debris pile — 10-minute insulation cocoon

Existing Structures

Buildings

In post-disaster scenarios, buildings may be available but compromised:

Option Pros Cons
Interior rooms (no windows) Best temperature retention, most structural protection May be dark, poor ventilation
Upper floors Safe from flooding Structural collapse risk higher
Bathrooms Strongest framing (smallest room), often has water Limited space
Under sturdy table/desk Secondary roof-collapse protection Claustrophobic, limited insulation

Vehicles

A vehicle is an outstanding emergency shelter:

Feature Why it matters
Windproof & waterproof Already built — zero construction time
Insulated cabin Traps body heat even without engine running
Elevated off ground No ground conduction heat loss
Reclining seats Keeps you off the cold floor
ℹ️ Information
**Time estimate:** 2 minutes — get in, close doors, settle. Fastest effective shelter available.

Shipping Containers & Trailers

Abandoned shipping containers (common at ports, industrial areas across AU/CN/SEA) provide instant shelter:

Pros Cons
Fully enclosed, pest-proof, windproof Ventilation is the main problem — doors must be cracked open
Structurally strong Metal = extreme temperatures (oven in sun, freezer at night)
Large interior space May have carried toxic cargo — check for chemical residue
ℹ️ Information
**Time estimate:** 5 minutes to inspect and occupy.

Natural Cavities

Caves & Rock Overhangs

Ideal if available — nature's fully-built shelters.

Pre-occupancy hazard checklist:

Hazard How to check Pass/Fail
Collapse risk Fresh rockfall debris at entrance? Cracks in ceiling? Move if yes
Flooding Water marks on walls? Is cave in a drainage line? Move if yes
Animals Tracks, droppings, bones? Scan with light. Move if occupied by predators or venomous snakes
Bats Guano (bat droppings) present? Avoid — histoplasmosis risk (lung infection)
CO₂ / heavy gases Feel short of breath, dizzy? Flame burns strangely? Leave immediately if yes
ℹ️ Information
**Time estimate:** 5–10 minutes — includes safety inspection.

Australian cave warning: Many Australian caves are in sandstone escarpments (Blue Mountains, Kimberley, Grampians). Some are Aboriginal rock art sites — do not damage or disturb them. Northern Australian caves may contain freshwater crocodiles.

SEA cave warning: Limestone caves in Thailand, Vietnam, and southern China may flood rapidly during monsoon rains. Check weather patterns before occupying.

Hollow Trees

Large trees with hollow bases (common in old-growth eucalypts in Australia, banyan trees in SEA and southern China) can shelter 1–2 people:

Pros Cons
Thick wood = excellent natural insulation Limited space (1–2 people max)
Already standing — zero construction Must clear debris and check for inhabitants
Rain protection from canopy Do NOT light a fire inside — hollow tree = chimney
ℹ️ Information
**Time estimate:** 10 minutes to inspect and clear.

Dense Vegetation

Under Large Trees

The canopy of a large tree reduces rainfall by 30–50% and blocks wind:

Tree type Region Shelter quality
Banyan SEA, southern China Excellent — enormous canopies, aerial roots form natural "rooms"
Moreton Bay fig Eastern Australia Excellent — massive canopy spread
River red gum Australia-wide Good — broad canopies, always near water
ℹ️ Information
**Time estimate:** 15–20 minutes for basic setup with ground clearing.

Bamboo Groves

Bamboo is self-spacing — between clumps, there is often clear ground sheltered by the tall culms:

  • Bend and tie tops of adjacent culms together to form an A-frame
  • Thatch with bamboo leaves
  • Bamboo groves are warmer than open ground — the dense stalks trap air
ℹ️ Information
**Time estimate:** 20–30 minutes for an A-frame shelter.

Swamp/Mangrove — AVOID

Do not sleep in mangroves or swamps: - Tidal flooding risk - Crocodile habitat (northern Australia, SEA) - Mosquito density is extreme - Mud is insulating but immersion in wet ground kills faster than exposure

Terrain Features

Ditches, Gullies, Embankments

Natural depressions provide wind protection and can be improved quickly:

ℹ️ Information
**Time estimate:** 10–15 minutes.

Warning: Do not shelter in dry creek beds or drainage lines if rain is forecast. Flash floods kill.

Against Rock Faces or Embankments

A solid wall on one side blocks 50% of wind exposure:

  • Face the wall toward the prevailing wind
  • Build a small windbreak on the opposite side with branches or rocks
  • The wall radiates stored heat back at night (especially dark rock that has been in sun all day)
ℹ️ Information
**Time estimate:** 5–10 minutes to set up.

Clothing as Shelter

Your clothing is your first and most portable shelter. Maximise it:

The Layer System

Layer Function Materials
Base layer Wicks sweat from skin Synthetic or merino wool — never cotton against skin in cold
Mid layer Insulation — traps warm air Fleece, wool, down
Outer layer Wind and water protection Waterproof-breathable shell; emergency poncho; plastic bag with holes cut

Improvised Insulation Techniques

Technique How to do it Time to apply
Stuff clothing with leaves/grass Crush dry material first so it traps more air; stuff between layers 5 minutes
Newspaper insulation Stuff between clothing layers (common post-disaster) 3 minutes
Plastic bag vapour barrier Wear plastic bag against skin under other layers — prevents evaporative heat loss 2 minutes
Foot insulation Stuff dry grass/leaves inside socks; replace when compressed 2 minutes

10-Minute Debris Pile

If you have nothing and need shelter NOW:

Aspect Rating
Time to build 10–15 minutes
Waterproofing Poor — under tree canopy helps
Wind protection Good — debris absorbs wind
Insulation Good — 60 cm of dry debris traps significant body heat
Comfort Poor — but you will be alive
Temperature rating Survivable down to ~5°C with adequate debris

When You Have Nothing — Mental Survival

If you are truly caught with nothing — no tools, no shelter materials, extreme conditions:

  • Do not lie down directly on the ground. Sit on your pack, your shoes, a pile of leaves — anything.
  • Curl into a ball — reduces exposed surface area by 30%.
  • Cover your head and neck.
  • Do not sleep if dangerously cold — shivering is your body's heater. If you fall asleep, shivering may stop (paradoxical undressing risk).
  • Move — light exercise (squats, arm swings) generates heat. Do not sweat.
  • Wait for dawn. The sun will rise. Temperatures will improve. Hold on.
