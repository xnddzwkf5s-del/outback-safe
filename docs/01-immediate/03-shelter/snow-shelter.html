Snow Shelter

Snow is one of the best insulating materials on Earth. The air trapped between snow crystals gives it an R-value comparable to fibreglass insulation. A properly built snow shelter can maintain an interior temperature around 0°C even when outside temperatures drop to -40°C — a life-saving 40°C difference.

ℹ️ Information
**Regional relevance:** This page applies to northern China (Heilongjiang, Inner Mongolia, Xinjiang, Tibetan plateau), the Australian Alps (Snowy Mountains, Tasmanian highlands — winter only), and high-altitude SEA (northern Vietnam, northern Thailand peaks, Myanmar highlands). If you are in tropical SEA or northern Australia, skip to [Debris Hut](debris-hut.html).

Why Snow?

Property Explanation
Insulation Fresh snow is 90–95% trapped air — excellent insulator
Windproof Snow is impenetrable to wind
Sound barrier Snow shelters are quiet — psychological benefit
Availability In snow country, material is everywhere, free, and unlimited
Temperature Interior stays near 0°C regardless of outside temperature

Snow Trench — Fastest Emergency Shelter

When you have minutes, not hours:

Aspect Rating
Time to build 20–30 minutes
Tools needed Shovel or improvised digging tool (snowshoe, pot lid)
Best for Emergency overnight, blizzard conditions
Comfort Minimal — cramped but survivable
Temperature Interior ~0°C regardless of outside temperature

Snow Cave

A natural snowdrift on the lee side of a ridge is ideal. Snow caves are faster to build than quinzhees if a suitable drift exists.

Aspect Rating
Time to build 1–2 hours
Tools needed Shovel, snow saw helpful but not essential
Best for Existing snowdrift on lee side of ridge; multi-night use
Comfort Good — spacious compared to trench
Temperature Interior ~0°C; can reach +5°C with body heat and a candle

Quinzhee — The Tool-Free Snow Shelter

A quinzhee (also spelled quin-zhee or quinzee) is a hollowed-out mound of piled snow. It requires no cutting tools and no existing snowdrift — just snow, a shovel (or your hands), and time.

Construction Rules for All Snow Shelters

Ventilation is Life and Death

Warning sign What it means Action
Headache CO₂ build-up Enlarge ventilation hole
Nausea CO₂ build-up (worsening) Exit shelter, clear vent, let air exchange
Shortness of breath Severe CO₂ / low O₂ Exit immediately
Candle flame dims or changes colour Low oxygen Enlarge ventilation hole

Condensation Management

  • Smooth interior walls — rough walls collect water droplets that then drip on you
  • Create drainage channels — a small groove around the perimeter of the sleeping platform directs meltwater away
  • Minimise moisture inside — brush snow off clothing before entering; do not cook inside
  • Keep a small gap at the entrance — promotes airflow and moisture escape

Structural Safety

  • Dome shape — arches distribute weight. Flat ceilings collapse.
  • Minimum 30 cm wall thickness — thinner walls are weaker and less insulating
  • Do not light a fire or stove inside — the heat weakens the structure AND creates CO
  • Heavy snow load on the roof — a quinzhee can collapse under wet, heavy snow. Knock accumulated snow off the outside periodically.

Sleeping in a Snow Shelter

  • Lie on insulation — evergreen boughs, sleeping mat, pack, or a thick layer of dry grass packed in
  • Sleep in dry clothing — change out of damp clothing before entering
  • Keep boots inside with you (in a stuff sack or at your feet) — frozen boots in the morning are useless
  • A candle provides surprising warmth and light — a single candle can raise interior temperature by 5–10°C. Ensure ventilation.
  • Pee before sleeping — your body wastes energy keeping urine warm
  • Keep a digging tool inside — if the entrance collapses, you need to dig out

Avalanche Awareness

Snow shelters are built in avalanche terrain. Do not become a victim.

Avalanche terrain signs — AVOID building shelters here:

  • Slopes between 30° and 45° (the prime avalanche angle)
  • Lee slopes after heavy snowfall or wind loading
  • Convex rollovers (where the slope bulges outward)
  • Terrain traps: gullies, creek beds — avalanches funnel into these
  • Recent avalanche debris — it happened once, it can happen again

Where to build:

  • Ridge tops (windward side sheds snow; lee side accumulates it — build on windward side)
  • Valley floors away from avalanche runout zones
  • Dense tree stands (trees anchor the snowpack)
  • Low-angle terrain (< 25°)
