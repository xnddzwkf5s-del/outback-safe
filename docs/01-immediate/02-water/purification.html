Water Purification

All water found in the wild is contaminated until proven otherwise. Even crystal-clear mountain streams carry Giardia, Cryptosporidium, bacteria, and viruses from animal waste upstream. In post-disaster environments, add chemical contamination, sewage, and heavy metals to the list.

What You Are Fighting

Contaminant Examples Size Removed by
Sediment/turbidity Dirt, sand, organic matter Visible Filtration, settling
Bacteria E. coli, Salmonella, Cholera, Leptospira 0.2–5 microns Boiling, chemical, UV, fine filtration
Protozoa Giardia, Cryptosporidium 5–15 microns Boiling, filtration (1 micron), UV
Viruses Hepatitis A, Rotavirus, Norovirus 0.02–0.2 microns Boiling, chemical (chlorine), UV
Chemicals Pesticides, industrial runoff Molecular Activated carbon, distillation
Heavy metals Lead, mercury, arsenic Ionic Distillation only

Method 1: Boiling (The Gold Standard)

Boiling — the gold standard for pathogen removal. Bring to a rolling boil and it's safe. Image: Wikimedia Commons (CC BY-SA).

Effectiveness

  • Removes: ALL pathogens — bacteria, viruses, protozoa
  • Does NOT remove: Chemicals, heavy metals, sediment, salt

Materials

📋 Materials
  • Metal container (pot, billy can, canteen cup)
  • Heat source (fire, stove, camp burner)
  • Cloth for pre-filtering (if water is cloudy)

The Truth About Boiling Time

The myth of "boil for 10 minutes" wastes fuel and water. Water is pasteurised at temperatures well below boiling:

Temperature Time to pasteurise
55°C 60 minutes
60°C 30 minutes
65°C 3 minutes
70°C 10 seconds
100°C (boiling) Instant

In practice: Bring water to a rolling boil. It is now safe — let it cool and drink. At altitudes above 2000 m (western China, parts of SEA), boil for 1 additional minute (water boils at a lower temperature at altitude).

Step 1: Pre-Filter (If Cloudy)

Pour turbid water through a clean cloth into the boiling container. This removes sediment that can harbour pathogens and improves taste.

Step 2: Bring to a Rolling Boil

Place the container on your heat source. A rolling boil means large bubbles breaking the surface continuously — not just small bubbles on the bottom.

Step 3: Cool and Aerate

Remove from heat and let cool. Boiled water tastes flat because dissolved oxygen has been driven off. Pour between two clean containers several times to re-aerate.

Time Required

  • Setup: 2 minutes
  • Processing: 5–15 minutes (depending on volume and fire intensity)
  • Yield: Whatever your container holds (typically 1–5 L per batch)

Fuel Cost

Roughly 1 kg of dry wood to boil 5 L of water. Plan your firewood accordingly.

Method 2: Solar Disinfection (SODIS)

SODIS — UV radiation and heat from sunlight kill pathogens in clear PET bottles. Works best in high-UV regions like Australia. Image: Wikimedia Commons (CC BY-SA).

Effectiveness

  • Removes: Bacteria, viruses, most protozoa
  • Does NOT remove: Chemicals, heavy metals, sediment. Less effective against Cryptosporidium cysts.

Materials

📋 Materials
  • Clear PET plastic bottles (water/soft drink type — not coloured, not PVC, not glass)
  • Direct sunlight for 6+ hours
  • Pre-filtered (clear) water

How It Works

UVA radiation and heat synergistically kill pathogens. At water temperatures above 50°C, the process is complete in 1 hour. SODIS works best in Australia (high UV), tropical SEA, high-altitude China, and anywhere between 35°N and 35°S latitude.

Step 1: Prepare Bottles

Use clear PET bottles only. Remove labels so UV can penetrate. Wash bottles if dirty — mud or residue blocks UV.

Step 2: Fill and Shake

Fill bottles ¾ full with pre-filtered (clear) water. Leave air space. Shake vigorously for 20 seconds — oxygenation accelerates UV disinfection.

Step 3: Place in Sun

Lay bottles horizontally in direct sunlight. A corrugated iron roof is ideal — the metal reflects additional UV and conducts heat. If no metal surface, use any sun-exposed surface.

Step 4: Wait

  • Sunny day: 6 hours minimum
  • Overcast: 2 full days
  • Water temperature above 50°C: 1 hour

Water is now safe to drink.

Time Required

  • Setup: 5 minutes
  • Processing: 6 hours (sunny) to 48 hours (overcast)
  • Yield: Whatever your bottles hold (typically 0.5–1.5 L per bottle)

Limitations

  • Water must be clear (turbidity blocks UV)
  • Requires PET bottles (scavenge in post-disaster areas)
  • Does not work in heavy cloud cover
  • Not effective for chemical contamination

Method 3: Cloth Filtration

Effectiveness

  • Removes: Sediment, large protozoan cysts (Giardia, 5–15 microns), visible debris
  • Does NOT remove: Bacteria, viruses, chemicals, heavy metals. This is a pre-treatment, not a standalone method.

Materials

📋 Materials
  • Clean, tightly woven cloth (cotton t-shirt, bandana, coffee filter, muslin)
  • Two containers (one for dirty water, one for filtered)
  • Cord or elastic band (optional, to secure cloth)

What Cloth Filtration Actually Does

Cloth filtration reduces turbidity and removes larger pathogens like Giardia cysts (5–15 microns). It is the bare-minimum first step before boiling or chemical treatment. Never drink cloth-filtered water without additional treatment.

Step 1: Secure the Cloth

Stretch the cloth over the mouth of the clean collection container. Secure with cord or hold in place. The cloth should be taut but not stretched thin — multiple layers are better than one.

Step 2: Pour Slowly

Pour the turbid water slowly through the cloth. Do not overfill — let it drain before adding more. If the cloth clogs, replace or rinse.

Step 3: Treat the Filtered Water

The filtered water is clearer but NOT safe. Boil, chemically treat, or SODIS before drinking.

Time Required

  • Setup: 1 minute
  • Processing: 1–2 minutes per litre
  • Yield: As much as you filter

Method 4: Sand Filtration (Improvised Charcoal-Sand Filter)

Sand and charcoal filter — removes sediment, some pathogens, and improves taste. Always follow with boiling or chemical treatment. Image: Wikimedia Commons (CC BY-SA).

Effectiveness

  • Removes: Sediment, some bacteria (mechanical trapping), some chemicals (charcoal adsorption), improves taste
  • Does NOT remove: Viruses, most bacteria, protozoan cysts reliably. This is not a standalone method — always follow with boiling or chemical treatment.

Materials

📋 Materials
  • Plastic bottle (cut off bottom) or bucket with a small hole
  • Clean cloth or coffee filter
  • Fine sand (20 cm)
  • Crushed charcoal (15 cm)
  • Coarse sand (10 cm)
  • Fine gravel (5 cm)
  • Collection container
    Charcoal-Sand Filter — Cross Section

    ===========================
    |  Water in               |
    |                         |
    |  ~~~~  Fine gravel   ~~~|  (5 cm — traps large particles)
    |  ~~~~  Coarse sand    ~~|  (10 cm — traps medium particles)
    |  ####  Crushed charcoal #|  (15 cm — adsorbs chemicals, odours)
    |  ....  Fine sand     ...|  (20 cm — traps fine particles)
    |----- Cloth filter ------|
    |         |  Drip         |
            / \
           /   \
          /     \
    Filtered water (treat before drinking)

Step 1: Prepare the Container

Cut the bottom off a plastic bottle. Place cloth over the bottle opening and secure with cord or rubber band. The bottle neck faces down into your collection container.

Step 2: Layer the Filter Media

Add layers from bottom to top (fine to coarse). Each layer must be level and gently tamped:

  1. Fine sand (20 cm) — goes in first, sits against the cloth
  2. Crushed charcoal (15 cm) — the workhorse for chemical and taste removal
  3. Coarse sand (10 cm) — mechanical barrier
  4. Fine gravel (5 cm) — top layer, catches the largest debris

Step 3: Pour and Collect

Pour turbid water slowly into the top. Do not pour hard enough to disturb the layers. Collect the filtrate below. The water will emerge clearer but still requires treatment.

Step 4: Treat the Output

The filtered water is clearer and has fewer pathogens and chemicals, but it is NOT safe to drink. Boil, chemically treat, or SODIS before consumption.

Time Required

  • Setup: 15 minutes (building the filter)
  • Processing: 5–10 minutes per litre (slow drip)
  • Yield: Continuous as long as you feed water in

Filter Maintenance

  • Replace charcoal layer after filtering ~20 L (it becomes saturated)
  • If flow slows, the fine sand layer has clogged — replace it
  • Do not let the filter dry out completely between uses (cracks form)

Method 5: Chemical Treatment

Chlorine Bleach (Sodium Hypochlorite)

Effectiveness

  • Removes: Bacteria, viruses, most protozoa
  • Does NOT remove: Chemicals, heavy metals, Cryptosporidium (resistant), sediment

Materials

📋 Materials
  • Unscented household bleach (sodium hypochlorite, no additives, no thickeners, no fragrances)
  • Dropper or measuring device
  • Container for treated water
  • Timer (watch, phone, or count)

Step 1: Identify Your Bleach Concentration

Check the label. Standard household bleach is 4–6% sodium hypochlorite.

Bleach concentration Drops per litre
1% 10 drops
4–6% (standard household) 2 drops
8% 1 drop

Step 2: Add Bleach and Mix

Add the appropriate number of drops per litre. Shake or stir thoroughly. If water is cloudy, double the dose.

Step 3: Wait 30 Minutes

Let the water stand for 30 minutes. After 30 minutes, smell the water — it should have a faint chlorine odour.

ℹ️ Information
If there is **no** chlorine smell after 30 minutes: repeat the dose and wait another 15 minutes. If the smell is **too strong**: pour between containers several times to dissipate excess chlorine.

Time Required

  • Setup: 1 minute
  • Processing: 30 minutes (contact time)
  • Yield: As many litres as you have bleach for

Iodine

Effectiveness

  • Removes: Bacteria, viruses
  • Does NOT remove: Chemicals, heavy metals, sediment. NOT effective against Cryptosporidium.

Materials

📋 Materials
  • Iodine tincture (2%) OR iodine tablets (tetraglycine hydroperiodide)
  • Dropper
  • Timer

Step 1: Measure Dose

Form Clear water dose Cloudy water dose
2% tincture 5 drops / L 10 drops / L
Tablets 1 tablet / L 2 tablets / L

Step 2: Mix and Wait

Add iodine, shake or stir, and wait 30 minutes. Water should be at least 20°C — cold water needs longer contact time (60 minutes if below 10°C).

To improve taste: Add a small amount of vitamin C (ascorbic acid) AFTER the 30-minute treatment time. This neutralises the iodine taste. Do not add vitamin C before treatment is complete — it deactivates iodine.

Limitations

  • Not effective against Cryptosporidium
  • Do not use for more than a few weeks (thyroid toxicity risk)
  • Pregnant women, people with thyroid conditions, and those with shellfish allergies should not use iodine
  • Water tastes unpleasant

Time Required

  • Setup: 1 minute
  • Processing: 30 minutes (60 minutes if cold)
  • Yield: As many litres as you have iodine for

Potassium Permanganate (Condy's Crystals)

If you have a first-aid kit with potassium permanganate:

Step 1: Add Crystals

Add crystals gradually until water turns light pink — roughly 3–4 crystals per litre.

Step 2: Wait and Check

Wait 30 minutes. The water should retain a light pink tint.

ℹ️ Information
- **Light pink** = correctly treated - **Clear/colourless** = too little — add more - **Brown or purple** = overdose — discard and start again

Method 6: Distillation

Distillation — the only method that removes everything: pathogens, chemicals, heavy metals, and salt. Image: Wikimedia Commons (CC BY-SA).

Distillation is the only method that removes everything: pathogens, chemicals, heavy metals, and salt. It is the only way to make seawater drinkable.

→ Full step-by-step instructions: Solar Still

Effectiveness

  • Removes: ALL pathogens, ALL chemicals, ALL heavy metals, salt
  • Does NOT remove: Nothing — this is the gold standard for purity

Time Required

  • Setup: 15–30 minutes (building the still)
  • Processing: 4–8 hours of sunlight
  • Yield: 500–1,500 mL per still per day

Water Quality Decision Matrix

Condition Recommended method
Clear stream water, have fire Boil — 1 minute at rolling boil
Clear water, no fire, sunny SODIS — 6+ hours
Cloudy water, any situation Cloth filter → Boil or Sand filter → Boil
Chemical smell, industrial runoff Distillation only
Seawater Distillation only
Floodwater (high contamination) Cloth filter → Boil → Chemical (double-treat)
Post-disaster tap water Boil (treatment plants may be offline)

Improving Taste

Boiled and chemically treated water tastes flat or unpleasant. To improve:

  • Aerate — pour between two containers several times
  • Add charcoal — a small piece of clean charcoal in the water container absorbs off-tastes
  • Pine needles — a few clean pine needles steeped in boiled water add vitamin C and a mild flavour (safe with most pine species; avoid yew which is toxic)
  • Lemon myrtle or tea tree leaves (Australia) — steep for a mild, antiseptic tea
  • Wild mint — found near water across Eurasia and Australia, improves taste

🇦🇺 Australian Purification Methods & Context

Australia Note: This page has been adapted for Australian conditions.

Boiling in Australia — Fuel Considerations

In Australia, the fuel you use varies dramatically by region. Plan your boiling method around available firewood:

Fuel Type Burn Characteristic Best for Found in
Gum leaves (Eucalyptus) Burn very fast, high heat, short duration Getting water to a quick boil if you have plenty of leaves Everywhere in AU
Spinifex (Triodia) Burns fiercely and fast — a flash fire Fire starter only. POOR for sustained boiling. Arid interior (central AU)
Mulga wood (Acacia aneura) Burns hot and long, excellent coal bed Sustained boiling — one of Australia's best fuels Central and western AU
Paperbark (Melaleuca) Burns fast with oily smoke Fire starter. Not a sustained fuel. Northern and eastern AU
Dead mallee roots Dense, burns for hours underground Long, slow boil. Excellent fuel. Mallee regions (VIC, SA, NSW)

Fuel estimate: 1 kg of dry mulga or ironbark will boil approximately 5 L of water. 1 kg of gum leaves will boil about 2 L before being consumed.

Solar Disinfection (SODIS) in Australia

Australia has among the highest UV index levels on Earth. SODIS works extremely well here.

  • Clear sky desert (central AU): 4 hours full sun is sufficient — UV is intense enough to kill pathogens faster than standard SODIS guidelines.
  • Coastal (Sydney, Brisbane, Perth): 6 hours full sun standard.
  • Overcast (anywhere): 2 full days minimum.
  • Tropics (Darwin, Cairns): Even cloudy wet season days have high UV. 8 hours in partial cloud works.
  • Water temperature tracking: On a 40°C outback day, SODIS bottles easily reach 55–65°C inside. At 50°C+ water temperature, disinfection completes in under 1 hour.

Materials note: Clear PET water bottles are abundant on Australian roadsides. Scavenge from rest areas, tourist sites, and 4WD tracks.

Filtration Through Sand — Australian Notes

Sand type Use Problem
River sand (inland creeks, rivers) Excellent for improvised filters None — wash thoroughly before use
Beach sand (coastal) Do NOT use Salt contamination. Even washed beach sand retains salt crystals. Your filtered water will be brackish.
Desert sand (central AU) Poor Too fine. Clogs filters quickly. Use charcoal + gravel layers instead.

Australian Bore Water

The Great Artesian Basin underlies 22% of Australia. Many pastoral stations and public bores tap it:

  • Appearance: Clear when it comes up, may turn brown/red on exposure to air (iron oxidising).
  • Taste: Often warm (30–70°C at the bore head), mineral-rich (salty, metallic, or sulphurous).
  • Safety: The water is generally safe microbiologically (deep aquifer, ancient water) but mineral content may cause diarrhoea if you have a sensitive stomach. Still boil it — bore heads can be contaminated by animals.
  • Cooling: Let bore water cool before drinking. Hot bore water can burn.

Billabong Water

Billabongs are oxbow lakes common in northern Australia:

  • Threats: High bacterial load, algae, crocodiles (saltwater and freshwater).
  • Collection: Do NOT wade in to collect. Use a container on a rope, thrown from the bank. The mud at the bottom is thick, and a croc could be submerged.
  • Treatment: Filter through cloth (removes algae), then boil. If the water is green with algae, pre-filter through multiple cloth layers or let settle overnight.

Water from Rock Holes (Gnamma Holes)

Natural depressions in granite outcrops:

  • Source: Rainwater collected in weather-worn holes on granite.
  • Quality: Often surprisingly clean — the first rain flushes the rock surface. However, animals also use these holes. Treat before drinking.
  • Finding them: Look for dark water staining on granite outcrops, especially on the lee side of prevailing wind where water pools.

Tropical Water Purification — Northern Australia

In tropical northern Australia (Top End, Cape York, Kimberley, Gulf Country), water purification requires different strategies than the arid south.

Why tropical water is different: - Temperature. Water in tropical billabongs and creeks is warm (25-35°C). Bacteria multiply much faster in warm water — recontamination can occur within hours of purification. - Organic load. Tropical water contains more decomposing plant material, algae, and microorganisms. This increases the pathogen load AND reduces the effectiveness of some purification methods. - Wildlife. Watering points are shared with crocodiles, buffalo, pigs, and cattle — all adding faecal contamination. - Turbidity. Many tropical water sources are cloudy with suspended sediment, which protects pathogens from UV and chemical treatment.

Recommended tropical purification protocol: 1. PRE-FILTER through cloth or sand to remove suspended solids (turbidity blocks UV and chemical treatment) 2. BOIL — rolling boil for 3 minutes (compensates for altitude in some areas and higher pathogen load) 3. STORE in a CLEAN, COVERED container — recontamination from flies and dust is rapid in tropical conditions 4. CONSUME within 24 hours of treatment — do not store treated water for multiple days in tropical heat 5. If using SODIS in tropical conditions: the water MUST be clear. Full sun exposure minimum 8 hours (not 6 — UV has to penetrate through higher humidity). Consume immediately after treatment.

What NOT to do in tropical areas: - Do NOT use chemical treatment (iodine/chlorine) in turbid water without pre-filtering — the organic matter neutralises the chemical - Do NOT collect water from billabongs at dawn or dusk (peak crocodile and buffalo activity) - Do NOT assume clear running water is safe — giardia and cryptosporidium are common in tropical streams with no visible contamination - Do NOT store treated water in direct sunlight — warm water + light = algae growth in containers

Tropical water sources — safety ranking: 1. Rainwater (best): Collected directly from clean tarp/roof. Naturally distilled. Treat anyway (bird droppings on collection surface). 2. Sand-filtered creek water: Dig a hole 1-2m from the creek edge. Let it fill with water filtered through sand. Much clearer than surface water. 3. Rock pool water: Small pools in sandstone/granite outcrops. Often clean but treat — animals drink from these. 4. Billabong water (worst): Highest pathogen load, highest croc risk. Last resort. Pre-filter, boil for AT LEAST 5 minutes, consume immediately.
