Finding Water

Water exists in every terrestrial environment on Earth — even deserts. The trick is knowing where to look.

The Golden Rule of Water Location

Water flows downhill. Always.

If you are lost and thirsty, the single most reliable strategy is to follow the terrain downward. Streams join creeks, creeks join rivers. The lowest point in any valley or ravine will eventually hold water.

Terrain Reading

Drainage Patterns

    Ridge
     /\
    /  \        ← No water on ridges — they shed water
   /    \
  /      \_____  ← Look for darker vegetation along drainage lines
 /  GREEN  \         (indicates moisture near surface)
/____________\___ Water collects at the valley floor

What to look for:

  • Drainage lines — Even dry creek beds in Australia's outback may hold water just below the surface. Dig at the outside bend of a dry creek bed — water scours deeper there.
  • Converging valleys — Where two valleys meet, the water table is shallowest. Dig at the junction.
  • Rock outcrops — Water seeps from cracks and fissures at the base of cliffs and rock faces. In Australian sandstone escarpments (Blue Mountains, Kimberley), water pools in rock hollows (gnammas) for days after rain.
  • Limestone karst — Common in southern China (Guilin, Yunnan) and parts of SEA. Caves and sinkholes may contain underground streams. Enter with extreme caution — flash flooding risk.

Green Lines in Dry Country

In arid Australia (the "Red Centre"), Southeast Asian dry season, or northern Chinese steppe:

  • Lines of green vegetation snaking through brown terrain = watercourse. Even if the surface is dry, water is near the surface.
  • River red gums (Eucalyptus camaldulensis) in Australia always indicate permanent or semi-permanent water — their roots reach the water table.
  • Pandanus palms (coastal northern Australia and SEA) grow only where fresh water is near the surface.
  • Bamboo thickets (throughout SEA and southern China) indicate high soil moisture — often near streams.

Animal Signs

Animals need water daily. They know where it is. Learn to read their behaviour.

Birds

Bird type What to watch for Reliability
Grain-eating birds (pigeons, finches, sparrows) Fly to water at dawn and dusk. Follow their flight lines. High
Flesh-eating birds (raptors, crows) Get moisture from prey. Not reliable water indicators. Low
Waterbirds (ducks, herons, egrets) Where they are, water is nearby. Very High
Large flocks wheeling at dawn or dusk Heading to water. Note the direction. High

Mammals

  • Game trails converging downhill — follow them. The wider and more worn the trail, the closer you are to water.
  • Kangaroos and wallabies (Australia) dig shallow wells in dry creek beds — called "kangaroo wells." These can be deepened for human use.
  • Wild pigs (Australia, SEA, southern China) wallow near water sources. Their wallows may contain water but it will be heavily contaminated.
  • Bats emerging from caves at dusk in large numbers — often near water.

Insects

  • Bees — A bee line (direct flight path) in the morning or evening leads to water. Bees rarely travel more than 1 km from water. Follow them.
  • Mosquitoes — Annoying, but they indicate standing water nearby.
  • Ants — Columns of ants climbing trees can indicate water stored in tree hollows above.

Devil's Advocate — False Water Indicators

  • Mango trees and coconut palms are often planted near water, but they can survive far from it in SEA. Do not rely on them.
  • Cacti — The "barrel cactus full of water" myth is largely Hollywood. Most cactus fluid is alkaline, bitter, and causes diarrhoea — which worsens dehydration. In real emergencies, prickly pear fruit (Australia) is a moisture source, but the pads are not.

Seasonal Considerations

Australia

Season Region Water availability
Wet (Nov–Apr) Northern Australia (Darwin, Kimberley, Cape York) Abundant surface water. Flooding risk — water is everywhere but contaminated by runoff. Treat thoroughly.
Dry (May–Oct) Northern Australia Creeks dry up. Dig at creek bends. Rely on permanent waterholes, rock hollows, and kangaroo wells.
Summer (Dec–Feb) Southern Australia Hot and dry. Surface water scarce. Dig in drainage lines. Morning dew is reliable near coast.
Winter (Jun–Aug) Southern Australia Cooler. Creeks flow in higher country (Great Dividing Range). Frost melt provides small amounts in alpine areas.

Southeast Asia

Season Region Water availability
Wet/Southwest Monsoon (May–Oct) Thailand, Myanmar, Laos, Cambodia, Vietnam, Philippines Torrential rain. Rain catchment primary. Surface water abundant but turbid and contaminated. Bamboo water available.
Dry/Northeast Monsoon (Nov–Apr) Most of SEA Surface water reduces. Dig in dry riverbeds. Bamboo water less reliable. Mountain streams still flow in highland areas (northern Thailand, Laos, Vietnam).
Year-round Equatorial (Malaysia, Indonesia, southern Philippines) Consistent rainfall. Water sources steady but always treat — high pathogen load in tropical water.

China

Season Region Water availability
Summer (Jun–Aug) Southern China (Guangdong, Guangxi, Yunnan) Heavy monsoon rain. Abundant surface water. Flood risk. Treat for turbidity.
Summer (Jun–Aug) Northern China (Inner Mongolia, Gansu, Xinjiang) Hot and arid. Limited surface water. Groundwater deep. Dew collection at dawn. Dig in dry riverbeds.
Winter (Dec–Feb) Northern China Snow and ice are water sources but must be melted (eating snow directly lowers core temperature — dangerous). Frozen rivers require breaking through ice.
Spring/Autumn Nationwide Transitional. Southern water stable. Northern water scarce until snowmelt (spring) or before freeze (autumn).

Dew Collection

In coastal or humid environments, dew can provide useful quantities of water.

📋 Materials
  • Absorbent cloth (t-shirt, towel, bandana)
  • A container for wringing

Tree Transpiration Bags

📋 Materials
  • Large clear plastic bag
  • Cord or tape

Expected yield: 200–500 mL per bag per sunny day.

Water in Tropical Environments (SEA, Northern Australia, Southern China)

These regions receive heavy seasonal rainfall but can be surprisingly dry in the dry season.

Monsoon season (roughly May–October): - Water is abundant but contaminated by flood runoff — treatment essential - Bamboo: look for older stems with brown colouring near nodes. Tap them — if water sloshes inside, cut a hole and drink. Bamboo water is generally clean enough to drink without treatment. - Banana/plantain stumps: cut the trunk about 30 cm above ground. Scoop out a bowl-shaped depression in the stump. It will fill with water within hours. - Vines: cut a vine as high as you can reach, then cut near the bottom. Water flows from the bottom if the vine contains it. Test vine sap on skin before drinking — some vines are toxic.

Water in Arid Environments (Outback Australia, Northern China)

  • Kangaroo/rock wallaby wells — dig out and deepen existing animal wells in dry creek beds
  • Rock hollows (gnammas) — still water in rock depressions after rain. Must be treated.
  • Clay pans — flat depressions in arid Australia that hold water briefly after rain. Dig at the lowest point.
  • Morning dew on metal — any metal object (vehicle wreckage, sign) collects heavy dew at dawn. Wipe with cloth and wring.

Emergency Sources — Last Resort

Source Notes
Car radiator Contains toxic antifreeze unless you know it is pure water. Do not drink from an unknown vehicle radiator.
Toilet cistern (not bowl) The tank water is clean — it is the same as tap water. In a post-disaster building, this is a good emergency source.
Hot water system In abandoned houses, the hot water tank holds 100–400 L of potable water. Drain from the valve at the bottom.
Canned food liquid The water in tinned vegetables or fruit is safe and contains some calories.

🇦🇺 Australian Water Finding — Landscape Reading & Animal Signs

Australia Note: This page has been adapted for Australian conditions.

Following Australian Birds to Water

Australian birds are excellent water indicators. Learn their patterns:

Species What to look for Reliability
Zebra finch Flocks flying in straight line at dawn/dusk. Water within 5 km. High — needs water daily in arid zones
Budgerigar Large flocks heading in one direction at dawn/dusk High — wild budgies need daily water
Pigeons & doves (crested pigeon, spinifex pigeon) Fast, direct flight at dawn/dusk heading to water High — grain-eaters, must drink daily
Galah Flocks return to same water source daily High — widespread, reliable indicator
Major Mitchell's cockatoo Follow in arid zone — needs water daily High
Honeyeaters Active near flowering trees but also need water Moderate — get some moisture from nectar
Birds of prey (eagles, hawks) NOT reliable — get moisture from prey Low — do not follow them for water

Technique: At dawn, climb a high point and watch the horizon. Birds flying in a straight, purposeful line at low altitude are heading to water. Note the direction. Follow it.

Reading the Australian Landscape for Water

Landscape feature What to look for How to access water
Driest green patch in arid zone The greenest trees in the landscape indicate water — but it may be deep. Mulga roots go 20 m+ to reach groundwater. Dig at the base or look for shallow seeps along creek lines.
Dry creek bed with sand Dig at outside bends — water erodes deeper there, and the sand holds moisture. Dig 30–50 cm in the sand at the outside curve. Wait 20 min for seepage.
Rocky outcrop with water staining Dark vertical streaks on rock faces = water runs here after rain. Check for rock holes (gnammas) at the base of the staining. Often holds water for weeks.
Claypan Flat, hard-baked depression with no vegetation in the centre. Water sits on the surface briefly after rain. Dig at the lowest point for shallow groundwater.
Sand dune swales Between parallel dunes in the Simpson and other deserts. Dig in the swale (low area between dunes). Water table may be 1–3 m deep.
Gorge / creek line in ranges Following a dry creek into a rocky gorge Waterholes often persist in gorges long after surrounding country is dry.

Plant Where it grows What it indicates
River Red Gum (Eucalyptus camaldulensis) Along permanent watercourses across AU Permanent groundwater within 10 m. Always follow River Red Gums.
Paperbark / Melaleuca Swamps, creek lines northern and eastern AU Swampy ground or seasonal water near surface

Australian Water Indicators — Other Clues

Indicator What it means
Frogs calling (especially after rain) Standing water nearby — check creek beds, rock holes
Mosquitoes Still water within 100 m. Could be a small pool, hoof print, or dam.
Dragonflies Clean water nearby — they breed in unpolluted water
Bee flight lines Bees rarely travel more than 1 km from water. Follow their direct flight path.
Ant trails going to tree hollows Water stored in tree hollows above
Fly density High fly density near creek lines = water present or recent

Rainwater Collection in Australia

Region Season Reliability Method
Tropical north (Darwin, Kimberley, Cape York) Nov–Apr (wet season) High — reliable monsoonal rains Build sheets, tarps, large containers. Plenty of water.
Arid zone (central AU) Unpredictable Very low — don't count on it Occasional thunderstorms produce brief runoff. Collect from rock surfaces, tarps.

Morning Dew Collection — Desert Technique

In the Australian desert, morning dew can provide useful water:

📋 Materials
  • Cloth (t-shirt, towel, bandana)
  • Container for wringing
  • Vehicle with metal surfaces (ideal)

Dew forms reliably in desert mornings when the night is clear and calm. The temperature drop causes condensation on any metal or glass surface.
