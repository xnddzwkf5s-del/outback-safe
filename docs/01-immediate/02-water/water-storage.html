Water Storage

Water is heavy — 1 L = 1 kg. You cannot carry more than a few days' supply. But at your base camp or shelter site, stored water is the difference between stability and a daily crisis.

Storage Priorities

  1. Store what you find/collect — do not let water run away. Every litre collected is one less you need to find tomorrow.
  2. Protect from contamination — mosquitoes, dust, animals, sunlight
  3. Treat for storage — prevent bacterial and algal growth during extended storage
  4. Rotate — stored water degrades over time and needs refreshing

Container Types

Best to Worst

Container Pros Cons
Metal pot/billy Can boil directly in it; durable; no chemical leaching Heavy; limited capacity; can rust over time
Glass bottles Chemically inert; easy to clean; no taste transfer Heavy; fragile; breakage risk in rough conditions
PET plastic bottles (water/soft drink) Light; abundant as post-disaster litter; free UV degradation makes them brittle over months; cannot boil; may leach after years
HDPE buckets/containers (food-grade) Large capacity; durable; stackable Scarce in wilderness; bulky to carry
Ceramic/clay pots Natural evaporative cooling; traditional in SEA/CN Heavy; fragile; porous (slow seepage)
Plastic bags Extremely light; scavengeable anywhere Puncture easily; no UV resistance; short lifespan
Improvised (bamboo, bark, large leaves) Available everywhere at no cost Leak; rot within weeks; limited capacity

Scavenging Containers Post-Disaster

In urban or semi-urban collapse scenarios, check these locations:

  • Kitchen cupboards — bottles, containers, pots with lids
  • Rubbish bins and recycling — PET bottles are everywhere in the modern world
  • Vehicles — water bottles, coolant containers (flush thoroughly if used for coolant — toxic residue)
  • Bathrooms — buckets, basins, bathtub (can hold 150+ L)
  • Construction sites — large buckets, drums (verify they did not contain chemicals)
  • Office kitchens — water cooler bottles (15–20 L, ideal)
  • Restaurants — large food-grade plastic buckets (mayonnaise, oil — wash thoroughly)

Container Preparation

Before using any found container for water storage:

Step 1: Scrub

Remove all visible dirt, residue, and debris. Use clean water and, if available, a small amount of unscented bleach or soap. Pay attention to threads on bottle caps — contaminants hide there.

Step 2: Sanitise

Step 3: Air Dry

If possible, let containers dry completely in the sun before filling. UV light provides additional sanitisation. This step can be skipped in an emergency but reduces the risk of stored water going bad.

Treatment for Long-Term Storage

Water stored for more than 3–5 days in warm conditions needs preservation treatment. Otherwise, bacteria and algae will grow.

Chlorine Treatment for Storage

Storage duration Treatment (4–6% unscented bleach)
< 1 week No treatment needed if kept cool and dark
1–4 weeks 2 drops per litre
1–6 months 4 drops per litre

Boiled Storage (No Chemicals)

If chlorine is not available, water can be boiled, cooled, and sealed while still warm (essentially sterile filling). This works for 1–2 weeks if the seal is not broken. Once opened, treat like any open water source.

Silver (Traditional Method)

A silver coin or piece of silver jewellery placed in stored water has a mild antibacterial effect — silver ions inhibit bacterial growth. This practice was used by Australian settlers, traditional Chinese households, and ancient civilisations.

ℹ️ Information
Silver helps but is **not a substitute for proper treatment**. It extends storage life marginally — do not rely on it alone for more than a few days of storage in warm conditions.

Mosquito Prevention

Standing water breeds mosquitoes. In tropical SEA and northern Australia, this is not just an annoyance — it is a disease vector (dengue, malaria, Ross River virus, Japanese encephalitis).

Method How it works Best for
Tight-fitting lid Physical barrier — mosquitoes cannot access water surface All containers. Always the first choice.
Fine mesh or cloth cover Allows air but blocks insects Open-top containers (buckets, pots)
Oil film Thin layer of cooking oil on water surface prevents larvae from breathing Emergency use, short-term open containers
Frequent draining Empty and refill every 3 days — breaks the 7+ day mosquito life cycle Any container that cannot be sealed

Storage Location

Requirement Why How
Cool Below 20°C dramatically slows bacterial growth Bury containers underground; place in deepest shade; wrap in wet cloth for evaporative cooling
Dark Sunlight promotes algae growth in clear containers Wrap clear bottles in cloth; bury; use opaque containers; store in caves or under shelters
Away from latrines At least 30 m from any contamination source Position your water store upwind and uphill from latrines, carcasses, and rubbish pits
Elevated or sealed Animals investigate water containers Raise off ground on a platform or hang from a branch; seal all openings
Marked clearly You need to know which is treated vs. untreated Charcoal on lid; scratch marks into plastic; different coloured containers; position-based system

Rotation Schedule

Water does not "expire" the way food does, but stored water degrades over time. Follow a rotation schedule:

Storage Life by Method

Storage condition Safe drinking window
Sealed, chemically treated, cool/dark 6 months
Sealed, boiled, cool/dark 2–4 weeks
Open container, cool/dark 1 week
Open container, warm (25°C+) 3–5 days

Rotation Rules

Signs Your Stored Water Has Gone Bad

Sign Likely cause Action
Cloudiness or discolouration (was clear, now not) Bacterial growth Re-boil vigorously for 2 minutes; if still cloudy, discard
Green tint or visible algae Algae from light exposure Discard — algal toxins may survive boiling
White film on surface Bacterial or fungal biofilm Skim off, re-boil; if it returns, discard
Rotten egg smell Anaerobic bacteria (sulphur) Discard — hydrogen sulphide is a warning of serious contamination
Earthy/musty smell Algae or fungi Boil thoroughly; if odour persists, distill or discard
Sour or metallic taste Chemical leaching from container or bacterial acids Do not drink — source is uncertain; distill or discard
ℹ️ Information
**When in doubt, throw it out.** Dehydration is dangerous, but waterborne illness in a survival situation is potentially fatal. The risk of drinking questionable water outweighs the benefit of saving it.

Emergency Storage Improvisation

When you have no manufactured containers:

Bamboo Water Containers

Bark Containers

  • Paperbark (Melaleuca) — common across northern Australia. Peel large sheets from the tree trunk. Fold corners up and tie with cord or vine. Line with a plastic bag if available. Lasts several days.
  • Birch bark (northern China) — traditional container material for centuries. Fold rectangular piece, fold corners, peg with small sticks. Waterproof and surprisingly durable.

Clay-Lined Pits

Natural Rock Hollows

In Australian sandstone country, natural rock hollows (gnammas) hold water for weeks after rain. They were used by Aboriginal Australians for millennia.

ℹ️ Information
Rock hollow water develops a surface film and may contain organic matter (leaves, insects, animal droppings). Always filter and treat before drinking. Scoop from the surface gently — avoid stirring up sediment at the bottom.

Contamination Prevention Checklist

Daily storage inspection:

  • [ ] All lids and seals intact — no gaps, cracks, or loose covers
  • [ ] No mosquito larvae visible (tiny wriggling worms at the water surface)
  • [ ] No algae growth (green film or tint)
  • [ ] No unusual odour when opening
  • [ ] Water is clear (or as clear as when stored — some natural water has colour)
  • [ ] No animal sign around containers (tracks, droppings, chew marks)
  • [ ] Storage area is shaded and cool
  • [ ] No standing water within 10 m of storage area

Quantity Targets

Scenario Recommended storage Notes
1 person, 3 days 10 L minimum 2 L drinking + 1 L hygiene/cooking per day
1 person, 7 days 25 L minimum Add 5 L buffer for hot days
4 people, 3 days 40 L minimum Children count as full person for water planning
4 people, 7 days 100 L minimum This is a serious storage effort — plan container strategy

Build toward these targets over your first week. You will not achieve them on Day 1. Aim to have at least a 3-day reserve by Day 3, and a 7-day reserve by the end of the first week.
