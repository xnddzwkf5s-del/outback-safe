Solar Still

A solar still uses the sun's heat to evaporate water from soil or vegetation (or contaminated water), then condenses the vapour on a plastic sheet where it runs into a collection container. The resulting water is pure distilled water — free of pathogens, salt, chemicals, and heavy metals.

It is the only method capable of purifying: - Seawater - Chemically contaminated water (pesticides, industrial runoff) - Urine (for emergency use only — the water produced is pure, not the original urine) - Water of unknown quality

How a Solar Still Works

Solar still cross-section — sunlight evaporates moisture, which condenses on the plastic and drips into the collection container. Image: Wikimedia Commons (CC BY-SA).

    Sunlight
      |   |   |   |
      v   v   v   v
    =======================  ← Clear plastic sheet
    \                     /
     \     💧 droplet    /
      \   (condensed)   /
       \      v        /
        \     O       /
         \          /
          \   🪣  /         ← Collection container
           \     /
            =====            ← Ground (moist soil/vegetation/contaminated water)

The process:

  1. Sunlight passes through the clear plastic and heats the ground/water below
  2. Moisture in the soil (or water placed in the still) evaporates
  3. Water vapour rises and condenses on the cooler underside of the plastic
  4. Droplets run down the sloped plastic and drip into the collection container

Construction — Pit Still (Standard Ground Still)

Materials

📋 Materials
  • Clear plastic sheet (at least 1.5 m × 1.5 m — larger is better)
  • Collection container (cup, can, cut bottle, bowl — preferably not metal, which can leach into distilled water)
  • Drinking tube (optional — allows drinking without dismantling the still)
  • Rocks or soil to hold plastic edges down
  • A small stone to weight the centre of the plastic
  • Digging tool
  • Green vegetation (optional — increases yield dramatically)
  • Contaminated water source (optional — pour into soil, NOT directly into container)

Step 1: Select Location and Dig

Choose a location with full sun all day. Avoid shade from trees, rocks, or buildings. Dig a bowl-shaped hole approximately 1 m wide and 40–60 cm deep. The wider the hole, the more collection area — and more yield.

Step 2: Place Collection Container

Place the collection container in the centre of the hole. The container must sit level and stable. If it sits above the hole floor, build a small platform of flat stones or packed soil.

Step 3: Add Moisture Source

If available, pack green vegetation, leaves, or cactus pieces around and under the container. Vegetation's own water content will be distilled, dramatically increasing yield.

If using contaminated water (seawater, urine, grey water), pour it into the soil around the container — do not pour directly into the collection container. The soil absorbs it, and the still distills the vapour.

Step 4: Cover and Seal

Drape the plastic sheet over the hole. Seal the edges with the excavated soil, sand, or rocks — the seal must be airtight. Any gap allows vapour to escape instead of condensing.

Step 5: Weight the Centre

Place a small stone in the centre of the plastic, directly above the collection container, to create a 30–45° slope. The slope guides condensed droplets toward the centre, where they drip into the container.

Step 6: Add Drinking Tube (Optional)

Run a drinking tube from the collection container, under the plastic edge, and seal around it with soil. This lets you drink from the still without dismantling it — preserving the vapour seal.

Step 7: Wait

Production begins within 1–2 hours in strong sun. Check in the late afternoon for your water. Distilled water tastes flat — this is normal.

Time Required

  • Setup: 15–30 minutes
  • Processing: 4–8 hours of sunlight (daytime only)
  • Yield: 500–1,500 mL per still per day (see yield table below)

Effectiveness

  • Removes: ALL pathogens, ALL chemicals, ALL heavy metals, salt
  • Does NOT remove: Nothing — this is the purest water you can produce

Variation: Vegetation Bag Still (Transpiration Still)

A simpler, faster still using a living plant. Lower yield but no digging required.

Materials

📋 Materials
  • Large clear plastic bag
  • Cord or tape
  • Small rock

Step 1: Select a Branch

Choose a large, leafy branch on the sunny side of a non-toxic tree. Avoid eucalyptus (oils can leach into water). Good choices: most broadleaf trees, fruit trees, shrubs.

Step 2: Enclose and Seal

Insert the branch end into the bag, enclosing as many leaves as possible. Seal the bag opening tightly around the branch with cord or tape. The seal must be airtight.

Step 3: Weight and Angle

Place the small rock in the bottom corner of the bag. Angle the bag so the weighted corner is the lowest point — this is where water will collect.

Step 4: Collect

Leave for 4–8 hours. Water collects in the weighted corner. To extract: carefully untie the branch, pour water into a container. Do not drink directly from the bag — the plastic exterior may be contaminated.

Time Required

  • Setup: 5 minutes
  • Processing: 4–8 hours
  • Yield: 200–500 mL per bag per sunny day

Effectiveness

Same as pit still — pure distilled water, but lower volume.

Expected Yield — All Methods

Still type Conditions Yield per day
Pit still (bare soil) Sunny, 30°C+ 500–800 mL
Pit still (with vegetation) Sunny, 30°C+ 800–1,200 mL
Pit still (with contaminated water added) Sunny, 30°C+ 1,000–1,500 mL
Vegetation bag Sunny, 25°C+ 200–500 mL per bag

In optimal Australian or SEA conditions (35°C+, full sun), yields can exceed 1.5 L per day from a single large pit still.

Yield Calculation Formula

For a pit still:

Yield ≈ Still area (m²) × Solar radiation × Soil moisture × 0.3 (efficiency)

In practical terms: - A 1.5 m × 1.5 m still (2.25 m²) on moist soil in full sun = approximately 1 L/day - Double the area ≈ double the yield - Adding vegetation ≈ +50% yield - Adding liquid water to soil ≈ +100% yield

Realistic Expectations

A single solar still will not meet your full daily water needs. At 1.5 L maximum per day, it provides roughly half the minimum for one person. Strategy:

  • Build 2–3 stills operating simultaneously for 3–4+ L per day
  • Use solar stills as a supplement to other water sources
  • Rely on stills for seawater distillation or contaminated water purification, not as primary supply

Maximising Yield

  • Add green vegetation — leaves, grass, even prickly pear cactus pads (Australia). The vegetation's own water content is distilled.
  • Add contaminated water — pour seawater, urine (last resort), or questionable water into the soil. The still will distill it.
  • Larger plastic = more still area — yield scales with surface area. A 2 m × 2 m sheet produces roughly double a 1.5 m × 1.5 m one.
  • Steeper slope — increases droplet runoff speed, reducing re-evaporation of condensed droplets.
  • Dark soil — dark-coloured soil absorbs more solar radiation. If your soil is light-coloured, place dark rocks or charred wood inside the still.
  • Multiple stills — 3–4 stills operating simultaneously can produce 4+ L per day.

Environmental Performance

Tropical (SEA, Northern Australia Summer)

  • High humidity reduces evaporation differential — lower yield than expected from temperature alone
  • BUT abundant vegetation compensates — pack the still full of fresh leaves
  • Multiple vegetation bag stills may be more productive than pit stills in humid conditions
  • Cover still from sudden tropical downpours — rain can collapse the plastic or contaminate the distilled water

Arid (Outback Australia, Northern China)

  • Low humidity = high evaporation differential = excellent yield
  • BUT limited vegetation — add contaminated water or urine to the soil to boost moisture
  • The desert is where solar stills perform best (high sun, low humidity, large temperature differential)
  • Night-time temperature drop creates strong condensation on the plastic — collect dew in addition to still output

Temperate (Southern China, Southern Australia)

  • Variable sun; stills work best in summer (December–February AU, June–August CN)
  • Winter production drops 60–80%. Supplement with other methods.
  • Spring and autumn: moderate yield. Works, but don't rely on it as sole source.

Improvised Plastic Alternatives

If you have no plastic sheet:

Alternative Effectiveness Notes
Space blanket (mylar) Good Reflective side down — reflects heat back to ground, increasing evaporation
Rain poncho Good If clear/translucent. Opaque ponchos reduce light transmission — lower yield.
Windscreen sunshade (reflective) Good Reflective surface works well; common in vehicle wreckage
Large leaves (banana, taro) Poor Overlap many leaves shingle-style; high vapour loss through gaps
Metal sheet Moderate Condenses water but loses droplets through re-evaporation; collect quickly

Night-Time Dew Bonus

Solar stills do not produce distilled water at night (no sun), BUT:

  • The plastic sheet will still collect dew overnight — especially in humid environments
  • At dawn, carefully lift one edge and wipe the underside of the plastic with a cloth — wring into a container
  • A second still can be set up overnight in a different location to maximise collection area
  • In coastal or tropical environments, dew collection from still plastic can add 100–300 mL overnight

Maintenance

  • Check daily — animals (ants, rodents) may try to access the moisture inside
  • Clean the plastic — dust, bird droppings, and condensation residue reduce light transmission. Wipe the outside with a damp cloth.
  • Replace vegetation — spent vegetation (brown, dry) stops producing moisture. Replace with fresh green material every 2–3 days.
  • Repair holes immediately — a single puncture in the plastic dramatically reduces yield. Patch with tape, a small piece of plastic, or even a leaf stuck on with mud.
  • Re-seal edges — wind and animals can disturb the edge seal. Check and re-seal daily.

Troubleshooting

Problem Likely cause Fix
No water after 4+ hours in sun Plastic not sealed (vapour escaping) Check all edge seals; look for holes
No water after 4+ hours in sun Soil too dry Add vegetation or pour water into soil
Very little water Plastic slope too shallow (droplets not running) Increase weight stone size for steeper angle
Very little water Still in partial shade Relocate to full sun
Water tastes/smells bad Contaminated soil chemicals (fuel, pesticides) Relocate still; do not use that location
Insects inside still Edge seal compromised Re-seal; add mesh filter to drinking tube
