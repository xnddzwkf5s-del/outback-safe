Perimeter — Sentry Rotation & Early Warning

A perimeter watch provides early warning of approaching threats — animal or human — and allows the rest of the group to sleep or work without constant vigilance.

When to Set a Perimeter Watch

Situation Watch needed?
Group of 3+, anywhere Yes — at minimum a rotating watch at night
Solo survivor Unrealistic — you need sleep. Rely on noise traps and fire.
Known predator activity in area Yes — 24-hour watch in rotation
Post-disaster urban area Yes — other survivors may be unpredictable
Remote wilderness, no large predators Night watch only
Storm/blizzard conditions No — stay sheltered. Threats are not approaching in a storm.

Sentry Rotation Schedule

The watch schedule must be sustainable. An exhausted sentry sees nothing.

4-Person Rotation (Ideal)

Shift Time Sentry
1 20:00–22:00 Person A
2 22:00–00:00 Person B
3 00:00–02:00 Person C
4 02:00–04:00 Person D
5 04:00–06:00 Person A (second rotation)

2-hour shifts allow each person ~6 hours of sleep (interrupted, but sufficient).

2-Person Rotation (Minimum Workable)

Shift Time Sentry
1 20:00–23:00 Person A
2 23:00–02:00 Person B
3 02:00–05:00 Person A
4 05:00–07:00 Person B

Harder shifts but manageable short-term.

Sentry rules: - Stay awake — no exceptions. A sleeping sentry endangers everyone. - No fire-gazing — staring at flames destroys night vision. Look AT the darkness, not the fire. - Change position every 15 minutes — a stationary sentry is predictable and can be approached from a blind spot - Carry a whistle and a weapon (improvised — stout stick, rock, knife) - Wake the next sentry 5 minutes before their shift - Report anything unusual to the next sentry during handover

Early Warning Systems

Noise Traps

Visual Markers

  • Trip lines with flags — light-coloured cloth strips tied to trip cord. Movement = visible signal.
  • Clear sight lines — remove vegetation and obstacles in key approach directions so you can see movement
  • Reflective markers — if you have reflective material, place it on trees. Your firelight or moonlight will reflect off it, and intruders crossing the line will block the reflection.

Natural Alarms

Animal What they signal
Birds Sudden flight or alarm calls = something is moving (animal or human)
Kangaroos/wallabies (AU) Thumping the ground = alarm. Something is approaching.
Monkeys/macaques (SEA, CN) Alarm barks = predator or human approaching
Crickets/frogs Sudden silence = something large is moving nearby

Listen to the animals. They have better senses than you do.

Approach Management

If You Are Concealing the Camp

  • No straight trails leading to camp — approach paths should curve or have false branches
  • No visible smoke from camp (Dakota fire hole or concealment) — smoke is visible for kilometres
  • Minimise noise — no shouting, no breaking sticks unnecessarily
  • Cover light — the fire should not be visible from a distance. Use natural barriers or reflector walls.

If You Are Signalling for Rescue

  • Maintain clear approaches from logical directions — rescuers come from roads, trails, open terrain
  • Signal area is separate from camp — do not lead rescuers directly into your camp. Meet them at the signal area.
  • Keep the signal area clean and obvious

Sentry Equipment

Item Purpose
Whistle Alert the group — 3 blasts = threat
Stout stick/club Improvised weapon — more useful than a knife for sentry (reach advantage)
Torch (if battery available) Light for identification. Use sparingly — light gives away YOUR position.
Watch/clock Track shift times
Water Staying awake is dehydrating

🇦🇺 Australian Camp Perimeter Context

Food Storage — Deterring Wildlife

In the Australian outback, food storage is your primary perimeter task. Food attracts wildlife in ways that simple sentry watches cannot prevent.

Australian-specific food storage rules:

  • Never store food in your tent — this is the #1 rule for Australian camping. Food in tents attracts dingoes, goannas, rodents, and insects directly to where you sleep.
  • Seal containers — use sealed plastic tubs, sealable bags, or metal bins. In the outback, even a tightly closed esky (cooler) is not enough — goannas and dingoes can open unlatched lids.
  • Store 50+ m from camp — hang from a tree branch (3+ m high) or seal in a hard-sided vehicle at night
  • In croc country: store food away from water. A dead fish smell or food scraps can attract crocodiles.
  • Clean up immediately — food scraps, dirty dishes, and spills attract ants, goannas, and dingoes within minutes. Do not leave dirty dishes overnight.

Reduce Dingo and Goanna Attraction

Animal Attracted by Prevention
Dingo Food smells, garbage, unattended cooking, small children Seal food, clean up, keep children close at dawn/dusk, never feed them
Goanna Food smells, eggs, fish scraps, pet food Seal food in hard containers. Goannas can climb cars and open soft-sided eskies.
Feral pig Food smells, garbage, vegetable scraps Store in sealed bins or in a locked vehicle. Pigs will root around camp.

Fires Keep Animals Away

Most Australian animals avoid fire and smoke. A maintained campfire at night:

  • Deters dingoes from approaching
  • Discourages feral pigs and wild dogs
  • Keeps snakes away from the camp perimeter (snakes avoid heat and movement)
  • Reduces mosquito activity near camp (smoke repels them)

Fire perimeter safety: The fire itself must be safe — see Safe Camp for firebreak requirements.

Personal Perimeter — Australian Specific Checks

Check your shoes before putting them on. Every Australian camper learns this:

  • Spiders — redback spiders (common across Australia) love dark, sheltered spots like boots. Funnel-web spiders (eastern Australia) like damp, cool places. Both deliver painful bites.
  • Scorpions — common in arid regions. Check boots, clothes left on the ground, and bedding.
  • Snakes — brown snakes (highly venomous) and pythons have been found curled in boots left outside tents overnight.

Shake out your sleeping bag before bed:

  • In the desert, scorpions and small snakes may seek warmth inside bedding
  • In the tropics, redbacks and centipedes frequent tent interiors
  • In the bush, even a small skink or gecko can startle a sleeper and cause a panic

Check under the toilet seat in long-drop toilets:

  • Redback spiders are notorious for building webs under toilet seats in outback long-drop toilets. This is a well-known hazard at remote rest stops and national park facilities.
  • Always check before sitting. Use a torch at night.

Natural Alarms in Australia

Australian animal What it signals
Birds (magpies, butcherbirds, wattlebirds) Sudden alarm calls = something is moving (dingo, snake, human)
Kangaroos / wallabies Thumping the ground with their hind feet = alarm. Something is approaching.
Emus Nervous pacing, alarm calls = disturbance near nest or territory
Cicadas / crickets Sudden silence = something large is moving nearby
Frogs Sudden silence = predator approaching a water source
