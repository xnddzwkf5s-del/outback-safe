Skill Assessment — Who Can Do What

A group's survival depends not on its strongest member but on its collective skills. Knowing who can do what — and who cannot — prevents task assignment failures, builds redundancy, and identifies critical gaps.

The Skill Inventory (First Hour)

Ask every person these questions. Record answers if you have something to write on.

Medical

  • [ ] Any first-aid training? (Level?)
  • [ ] CPR certified?
  • [ ] Any experience treating wounds, fractures, burns?
  • [ ] Any knowledge of medicinal plants?
  • [ ] Any medical conditions we should know about? (Diabetes, heart condition, allergies, medications needed)

Fire

  • [ ] Can you start a fire without matches or a lighter?
  • [ ] Have you ever used a ferro rod?
  • [ ] Have you ever made a friction fire (bow drill, hand drill)?
  • [ ] Do you know how to build different fire lays (teepee, log cabin, star)?

Shelter

  • [ ] Have you ever built a wilderness shelter?
  • [ ] Can you tie knots? (Which ones?)
  • [ ] Construction or carpentry experience?

Water

  • [ ] Do you know how to find water in this kind of terrain?
  • [ ] Do you know how to purify water without equipment?
  • [ ] Can you build a solar still?

Navigation

  • [ ] Can you navigate with a map and compass?
  • [ ] Can you determine cardinal directions without a compass?
  • [ ] Do you know this area? (If yes: how well? Trails? Water sources?)

Food

  • [ ] Can you identify any wild edible plants in this region?
  • [ ] Fishing experience?
  • [ ] Hunting or trapping experience?
  • [ ] Any food allergies?

Physical

  • [ ] Any injuries right now? (Pain, limited mobility, recent surgery)
  • [ ] Any physical limitations? (Cannot lift heavy, cannot run, etc.)
  • [ ] Can you swim?

Task Assignment Matrix

After inventorying skills, create a responsibility matrix:

Role Primary person Backup person Tasks
Medical [most trained] [next most] Triage, wound care, monitoring
Fire [best fire-starter] [next best] Fire starting, maintenance, banking
Water [best at finding/treating] [next best] Finding, collecting, purifying
Shelter [builder/engineer] [helper] Construction, repair
Food [forager/hunter] [helper] Gathering, fishing, preparation
Signaling [rotated] [rotated] Watch, signal maintenance
Navigation [best navigator] [next best] If moving: route planning
Leadership [designated leader] [designated #2] Overall coordination

Every critical role must have a backup. If only one person can start a fire and they are injured, the group is in trouble.

Cross-Training

Once basic survival is established, cross-train immediately:

  • The fire-maker teaches one other person to use the ferro rod
  • The medic teaches basic bleeding control to everyone
  • The navigator teaches sun/stick direction-finding to everyone

Goal: Within 3 days, every critical skill has at least two people who can perform it.

Covering Skill Gaps

When no one has a needed skill:

Gap How to cover
No one can start a fire Rely on this wiki. Practice during downtime. Use easier methods first (ferro rod before friction).
No one has medical training Read the First Aid section aloud. Focus on bleeding control — the most learnable life-saving skill.
No one can navigate Stay put. Do not try to self-rescue if you cannot navigate. Signal and wait.
No one can identify edible plants Use only the Universal Edibility Test as a last resort. Rely on insects instead — safer.

Age and Physical Considerations

Person Best roles Avoid
Children (4–12) Gathering small items, simple food prep, carrying messages, morale Heavy labour, dangerous tasks, night watch
Teens (13–17) Most tasks with supervision. Can do night watch if paired. Solo dangerous tasks
Elderly Knowledge (plant ID, stories, morale), light tasks, food prep, childcare Heavy labour, long-distance travel
Injured/sick Rest and recovery. Light tasks when able (sorting, mending). Any task that delays healing
Pregnant Food prep, organisation, childcare, record-keeping Heavy lifting, long travel, exposure to smoke/chemicals

The "Dead Weight" Problem

Someone who is able-bodied but refuses to contribute:

ℹ️ Information
**Distinguish between cannot and will not.** Someone with depression or trauma may APPEAR to be refusing to work when they are actually psychologically incapable. A private, compassionate conversation clarifies which it is. → [Morale](morale.html)

🇦🇺 Australian Convoy Skill Assessment Context

Pre-Trip Skill Assessment

Before departing on an extended outback trip, the convoy should conduct a full skill inventory of every person:

Key Skills for Outback Travel

Skill Why it matters How to assess
Mechanical Vehicle breakdowns are the most common outback emergency. Can someone diagnose driveline issues, fix a flat, bypass a dead alternator? "Have you ever changed a tyre on a loaded 4WD? Can you patch an air line? Have you removed a broken shock absorber mid-track?"
Medical Remote area first aid — hours or days from help "Do you have first-aid training? Remote area first aid? Do you carry a first-aid kit?"
Navigation GPS, paper maps, compass. Knowing when the Sat Nav is wrong. "Can you read a HEMA map? Plot GPS coordinates? Navigate without phone signal?"
Track experience Has the person done THIS track before? "Have you done the Canning before? How about the Oodnadatta? Simpson Desert?"
Radio / comms UHF radio etiquette and emergency procedures "Do you know how to use a UHF radio? Channels 5 and 40? Emergency call format?"
Recovery Winching, snatch straps, sand recovery "Have you used a snatch strap? Know how to set up a winch? Dug a vehicle out of sand?"
Water/food management Rationing, purification, sourcing "Can you purify water from a creek? How many litres does your vehicle carry?"
Satellite comms PLB, sat phone, satellite messenger "Have you ever activated a PLB? Used a sat phone? Sent an SOS via InReach?"

Assigning Roles

Based on the assessment, assign roles for the trip:

Role Responsibility
Lead driver Most track experience + navigation skills
Tail driver Second most experience + mechanical skills
Navigator GPS, map reading, route planning
Medic First aid, medical supplies management
Mechanic Vehicle inspections, repairs, spare parts management
Comms officer UHF radio discipline, PLB/sat phone management, check-in schedule
Recovery specialist Winching, snatch strap, sand tracks, shovel work
Quartermaster Food/water inventory, cooking roster, fuel tracking

Cross-training during the trip:

  • Day 1: Everyone practices UHF radio use and emergency call format
  • Day 2: Everyone practices changing a tyre on their vehicle
  • Day 3: Everyone practices using the PLB/satellite device (simulation, not actual activation)
  • Day 4: Navigator teaches basic map+compass navigation to one other person

Goal: By the end of the trip, every critical skill has at least two people who can perform it. If the lead driver is injured, someone else can navigate.

Physical Considerations for Outback Travel

Consideration Impact on role assignment
Age / fitness Long days of driving, setting up camp, vehicle recovery — physically demanding
Medical conditions Diabetes, heart conditions, allergies — inform the medic before departure
Pregnancy Extreme corrugations are not recommended. Rough tracks should be avoided.
Children Entertaining children on a 10-hour driving day is a job. Assign a dedicated helper.
Heat tolerance Some people handle 45°C better than others. Rotate driving shifts.
