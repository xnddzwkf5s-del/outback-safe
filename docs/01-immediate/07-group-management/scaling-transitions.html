Scaling Transitions — Convoy Splitting

🇦🇺 Australia Note: This page has been adapted for Australian outback travel. The original covered post-collapse group growth. This version covers when and how to split a large convoy.

When to Split a Large Convoy

In Australian outback travel, "scaling" means splitting a large convoy into smaller groups — not growing from 20 to 200 people. Large convoys (8+ vehicles) are inherently less efficient than small convoys (2–5 vehicles). There are valid reasons to split.

Reason 1: Different Experience Levels

When a convoy has a mix of experienced and novice 4WD drivers, everyone is frustrated:

  • Experienced drivers want to maintain momentum on tricky terrain
  • Novice drivers need more time, more guidance, and more patience
  • The convoy is only as fast as the slowest driver — and the gap between the best and worst driver can be enormous

When to split: If the novice drivers are holding up the experienced group at every obstacle, split into two sub-groups:

  1. A-group (experienced) — continues at pace. Makes camp ahead.
  2. B-group (novices) — travels with an experienced tail driver. Takes the time needed.

Meeting point: Both groups agree on the next camp (ideally a named campsite, track junction, or roadhouse). A-group scouts the camp and radios B-group with the location.

Reason 2: Different Vehicle Capabilities

Some vehicles are simply not capable on certain tracks:

Vehicle type Suitable for Not suitable for
Modified 4WD (lift, tyres, lockers, winch) Extreme tracks: Canning, Simpson, CREB —
Standard 4WD (e.g., stock Pajero, Prado) Outback highways, moderate tracks: Oodnadatta, Birdsville, Gibb (dry) Deep sand, extreme corrugations, deep river crossings
AWD / soft-roader (e.g., Forester, RAV4, CX-5) Paved roads, graded gravel, main outback highways Corrugations, sand, river crossings, rocky tracks
Camper trailer / caravan (towing) Highways, graded roads Narrow tracks, soft sand, steep passes

When to split: If the convoy contains vehicles from different categories and the planned track exceeds the capability of some vehicles, the convoy should split.

Example: 3 modified vehicles want to do the CREB Track. 2 stock Prados with camper trailers want to take the PDR (Peninsula Development Road) to the tip. They split: the modified group does the CREB (2 days), the stock group does the PDR (same route to the tip). They reunite at Seisia or at the tip.

Reason 3: Conflicting Schedules

Scenario Example Resolution
Time pressure Some people need to be back by a certain date; others have unlimited time Fast group pushes harder; slow group takes more time. Meet at scheduled points.
Different destinations Some want to go to Kakadu via the Arnhem Highway; others want the Gibb River Road Split is inevitable. Exchange contact details and next travel plans.
Side trips Part of the group wants to do a 3-day side trip (e.g., the Bungle Bungles) Main convoy camps and waits, or splits and reunites later.

Reason 4: Resource Constraints

  • Fuel range — not all vehicles have the same range. If a 500 km stretch requires a long-range tank (e.g., Canning Stock Route 400–600 km between fuel drops), vehicles without the range cannot complete the section.
  • Water capacity — if some vehicles carry 40 L and others carry 120 L, the water-limited vehicles determine the group's range.
  • Food — if part of the group has enough supplies and others do not, do not force the under-supplied group to push on. Either resupply at the nearest town or split.

How to Split a Convoy Safely

If you decide to split, follow these rules:

Before the Split

  1. Agree on the split before departure — ideally during trip planning, not mid-track
  2. Ensure each sub-group has minimum 2 vehicles — no solo vehicle safety risk
  3. Each sub-group needs its own PLB or sat phone — if the group had one shared device, it goes with the less experienced sub-group
  4. Exchange GPS coordinates of the meeting point — use named locations, not "somewhere on the track"
  5. Agree on radio check-in schedule — e.g., every evening at 6 PM on Channel 40, or a specific UHF channel
  6. Share fuel and water status — each group knows what the other has

At the Split Point

  1. All vehicles stop — final pre-split briefing
  2. Confirm radio channels — both groups must be able to reach each other via UHF if within range
  3. Confirm meeting point and time — specific date, specific location
  4. Emergency plan — what happens if one group does not arrive at the meeting point? (e.g., after 48 hours, trigger a PLB)
  5. Exchange spare parts and supplies — if someone has a spare air filter the other group might need, share it now

After the Split

  1. First night check-in — both groups confirm safe arrival at camp via radio or sat phone
  2. Daily check-in — brief message: "Fast group arrived Derby. We will be here 2 days then head toward Broome. Meet at Cable Beach 30th. Over."
  3. If no check-in for 48 hours — activate PLB. The group is in trouble.

When NOT to Split

Situation Why you should NOT split Alternative
Extreme remote track (Canning, Anne Beadell) Safety in numbers. A single 2-vehicle group on these tracks is too thin. Don't split unless you have to. Stick together.
Injured or ill person Medical care may be needed Send the most capable vehicle to fetch help while the rest stay with the injured person.
Bad weather imminent (cyclone, flood) Splitting complicates evacuation and rescue Stay together, find safe camp, wait it out.
Unknown route If neither group knows the track, splitting doubles the risk Stay together. Two pairs of eyes = safer navigation.

Summary — Splitting Decision Flow

    Is the convoy 8+ vehicles?
       |
       +--> Yes --> Are all vehicles similar capability?
       |              |
       |              +--> Yes --> Are all drivers similar experience?
       |              |              |
       |              |              +--> Yes --> Are schedules the same?
       |              |              |              |
       |              |              |              +--> Yes --> Stay together
       |              |              |              +--> No  --> Split by schedule
       |              |              |
       |              |              +--> No --> Consider split by experience
       |              |
       |              +--> No --> Consider split by capability
       |
       +--> No --> Stay together (2–5 vehicles is ideal)

Remember: Splitting is a practical decision, not a breakup. You are still travelling the same part of the world, just at different paces or on different routes. Exchange details, stay in touch, and plan a reunion drink.
