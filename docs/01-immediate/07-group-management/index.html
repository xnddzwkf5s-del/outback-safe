Group Management — Leadership & Survival

Surviving in a group is fundamentally different from surviving alone. A group can share labour, pool skills, and provide emotional support. But groups also create conflict, leadership vacuums, resource disputes, and morale contagion. More survival situations unravel from group dysfunction than from environmental factors.

The First Hour — Establish Order

The first decisions a group makes set the pattern for everything that follows. Do not delay.

Step 2: Establish Leadership

A group without a leader is a group in chaos. The leader does not need to be the smartest, strongest, or most charismatic — they need to be calm, decisive, and willing.

Leadership selection methods (in order of preference):

Method When to use
Existing authority If a clear authority figure is present (pilot, guide, parent, employer), they assume leadership.
Self-nomination + group consent Someone says "I will lead if you will follow." Group agrees. Fastest democratic method.
Skill-based selection The person with the most relevant experience (military, medical, outdoor) leads.
Rotation In groups of equals, rotate leadership daily. Prevents burnout and power consolidation.
ℹ️ Information
**Do not vote in the first 30 minutes.** Shocked, panicked people make poor electoral decisions. Use one of the faster methods above. A vote can come later when people are calmer.

Step 3: Skill Assessment

Inventory what everyone can do. Ask each person:

  • What is your name?
  • Do you have any medical training?
  • Can you start a fire without matches?
  • Do you know this area?
  • What practical skills do you have? (building, cooking, navigation, languages)
  • Do you have any injuries or medical conditions we should know about?

Record this. Assign tasks based on skill, not enthusiasm.

Group Size Dynamics

Group size Dynamics Management challenges
2–3 Intimate, fast decisions, high trust or high conflict Binary dynamics — if two people conflict, there is no tiebreaker
4–8 Manageable, diverse skills, sub-groups form Cliques can form; some members may feel left out
9–20 Significant labour capacity, needs formal structure Requires sub-leaders; communication overhead; factional risk
20+ Mini-society — needs hierarchy, rules, formal rationing Governance becomes a full-time job; law and order questions arise

The Most Dangerous Group Member

The person who is: - Able-bodied but refuses to work - Taking more than their share - Undermining leadership decisions - Spreading panic or despair

One disruptive person can destroy an entire group's survival prospects. Address the behaviour early. Private conversation first. If it continues, public accountability. As a last resort, separation (they leave, or the group leaves them). This is a hard decision but sometimes necessary.

🇦🇺 Australian Outback Convoy Context

Outback Convoy Management

Travel in the Australian outback is almost always done in convoys — multiple vehicles travelling together for safety, resource sharing, and mutual support. This is fundamentally different from post-disaster survival group management.

Convoy principle: Safety in numbers. A convoy of 2–5 vehicles on a remote track dramatically reduces risk. If one vehicle breaks down, the others provide transport and support. If someone is injured, help is immediate.

How Convoys Work

Role Vehicle Responsibility
Lead vehicle Most experienced driver Sets pace, calls hazards on UHF, navigates, manages stops
Intermediate vehicles Other travellers Follow lead, maintain distance, relay messages in large convoys
Tail vehicle Second most experienced Ensures nobody left behind, watches for mechanical issues, last through gates

Convoy rules:

  • UHF channel agreed before departure — standard is Channel 40 (highway) or a nominated channel for private groups
  • Lead vehicle calls everything — upcoming turns, oncoming traffic, hazards (animals, washouts, soft sand), oncoming convoys
  • Distance between vehicles: 5–10 seconds on gravel/paved roads, 50–100 m on corrugations (to reduce dust cloud for following vehicles)
  • If you cannot see the vehicle behind you in your mirror — slow down the tail vehicle may have stopped
  • Every vehicle carries a UHF radio — no radio = no convoy position
  • The convoy ALWAYS stops at major turns — wait for all vehicles to arrive before proceeding
  • No-one overtakes the lead vehicle without prior arrangement

Types of Outback Travel Groups

Group type Typical size Best for
Self-drive convoy (friends/family) 2–5 vehicles Remote tracks, extreme terrain
Tag-along tour (commercial guide) 10–15 vehicles Fraser Island, Cape York, Canning Stock Route
Club trip (4WD club) 6–20 vehicles Weekend trips, training runs
Solo with support 1 vehicle + backup vehicle Extreme solo challenges (rare, not recommended)
