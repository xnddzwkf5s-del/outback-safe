Ground Signals — Messages Visible from the Air

Ground-to-air signals are large symbols constructed on the ground that convey specific messages to aircraft. They are passive — they work even when you are asleep, foraging, or unable to actively signal.

International Ground-to-Air Symbols

Each symbol should be at least 3 m wide and 10 m long (larger is better). Aircraft at 1,500 m altitude need large symbols to discern them.

Symbol Meaning When to use
V Require assistance General distress
X Require medical assistance Injury or illness that needs evacuation
N No (negative) Answering "no" to a question
Y Yes (affirmative) Answering "yes" to a question
↑ (arrow) Proceeding this direction You have moved on — rescuers know which way

SOS — The Universal Distress Signal

SOS (... --- ... / three short, three long, three short) can be constructed as ground symbols:

SOS Ground Symbol:

    =====     ===     ===   (can be spelled out for absolute clarity)
    =====     ===     ===
    =====     ===     ===

    Each letter at least 3 m wide, 5 m tall

Spell out SOS rather than using Morse dots and dashes on the ground — many modern pilots are not trained to recognise Morse-based ground symbols. The letters "SOS" or "HELP" are universally understood.

Construction Materials

The symbol must contrast strongly with the background:

Background Material for symbol
Snow/light sand Dark rocks, dark vegetation, charred wood, dark clothing
Grass/vegetation Bright cloth, aluminium foil, light-coloured rocks, strip vegetation to bare earth
Dark earth/soil Light rocks, sand, clothing, paper, aluminium foil
Forest floor Stripped bark (light inner surface), clothing, light rocks
Beach/sand Seaweed, driftwood, dark rocks, arrange to cast shadows

Shadow is your friend. A symbol that casts a shadow is visible from much greater distance. Build raised symbols where possible — pile rocks, stack logs, dig trenches. At low sun angles (morning/evening), shadows make symbols pop.

Contrast and Size

The rule of contrast: If you cannot clearly see the symbol from 50 m away on the ground, an aircraft at 500 m altitude cannot see it at all.

Minimum sizes:

Aircraft altitude Minimum symbol size
300 m (helicopter search) 2 m wide, 6 m long
1,000 m (light aircraft) 4 m wide, 12 m long
3,000 m (commercial aircraft — unlikely to be searching) 10 m wide, 30 m long

Build bigger than the minimum if you have time and materials. Oversized symbols catch the eye; undersized symbols are invisible.

Construction Tips

Body Signals to Aircraft

If an aircraft is close enough to see you (low helicopter pass, light plane circling), use body signals:

Signal Meaning
Both arms raised (Y shape) "Yes" or "I need help — land here"
One arm raised, one arm down (diagonal) "No" or "Do not land here"
Both arms waving above head "I need medical assistance"
Squatting with arms wrapped around body "I am cold / need shelter" (less standardised)
Lying flat with arms out (like a cross) "Cannot proceed" or "Injured" (less standardised)
ℹ️ Information
**Stand still** when an aircraft passes. Moving figures are harder to identify as human distress cases. A stationary figure waving deliberately is clearly a signal. A jogging figure is ambiguous.

Multiple Locations

If you are moving (self-rescue attempt), leave ground signals at each camp:

  • Arrow pointing in your direction of travel, with the date scratched in the ground or spelled with materials
  • This allows rescuers to track your movement and estimate your position
  • Update symbols daily if possible

Destroying Signals

Destroy old signals if you leave them and rescuers should NOT follow that direction. An outdated arrow leading searchers the wrong way can cost lives. Destroy or clearly mark as "OLD."

🇦🇺 Australian Ground Signal Context

Minimum 3 m Tall Letters for Aerial Spotting

Australian search aircraft typically fly at 500–1 500 m altitude. Ground signals must be at least 3 m tall and 1.5 m wide to be visible from the air.

Aircraft Search altitude Minimum letter size
Helicopter (Police / RFDS / SES) 200–500 m 2 m tall
Fixed-wing light aircraft (Police Air Wing) 500–1 500 m 3 m tall, ideally 5 m
Specialised SAR aircraft (AMSA Challenger) 1 000–3 000 m 5 m+ tall

Build them bigger than the minimum if you have time and materials. A 6 m SOS that fills a clearing is impossible to miss.

Best Material Choices for Australian Terrain

Terrain Best contrast material
Red outback dirt (most of central/western AU) White rocks, light-coloured sand from dry creek beds, bright clothing (orange/yellow), green branches
Black soil plains (NT, QLD) White rocks, red rocks, bright fabric
White/pale sand (beaches, Simpson Desert) Dark branches, seaweed, dark clothing, charred logs
Spinifex plains (central AU) Burn a controlled clearing — the dark fire scar contrasts against pale spinifex
Green bush (coastal, ranges) Light rocks, stripped bark (inner white surface), bright fabric, trampled vegetation to bare earth

Fire Scars on Spinifex Plains

On the vast spinifex plains of central Australia, a controlled burn creates a dark fire scar that is:

  • Visible from aircraft for 20+ km
  • Contrasts sharply against pale-green / yellow spinifex
  • Can be shaped into SOS, X, or arrow symbols
ℹ️ Information
**Fire safety:** Only use controlled burns in a clearing or when there is NO wind. Check the Fire Danger Rating. Never light a fire on a Total Fire Ban day. A wildfire will kill you faster than any rescue delay.

Standard Ground-to-Air Signals in Australia

Symbol Meaning Australian context
SOS General distress — I need help Spelled out (not Morse dots) — modern pilots are trained for text
X Require medical assistance Common — injuries from falls, dehydration, vehicle rollovers
Arrow (→ or ↑) Proceeding in this direction Critical for self-rescue groups — tells searchers your route
N No / Negative Responding to aircraft queries
Y Yes / Affirmative Responding to aircraft queries
LL All well / Everything is OK (less common) Non-emergency signal

Spell out SOS or HELP rather than using abstract symbols. The letters are more universally understood than classic ground-to-air codes.

Who Conducts Aerial Search in Australia

Organisation Role
Royal Flying Doctor Service (RFDS) Medical evacuation. Aircraft are equipped to find and extract patients from remote airstrips.
Australian Maritime Safety Authority (AMSA) Coordinates ALL Australian SAR — land and sea. Manages PLB/EPIRB response. Operates Challenger jet for wide-area search.
State Police Air Wing Helicopters and fixed-wing for state-based SAR. NSW, QLD, WA have dedicated units.
State Emergency Service (SES) Co-ordinates ground search teams.
Bush Search and Rescue (BSAR) Volunteer ground teams (NSW, VIC, TAS). Works with Police.
Australian Defence Force Rare — only for large-scale incidents. Assets include C-130 Hercules, Navy helicopters.

Primary aerial search in Australia is conducted by AMSA and State Police Air Wings. Your ground signals must be visible from aircraft flying at altitude.
