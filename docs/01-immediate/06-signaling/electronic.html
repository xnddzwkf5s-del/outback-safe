Electronic Signaling — Phone, Radio & PLB

Electronic devices are the most effective signaling tools — when they work. In a post-disaster or remote survival scenario, battery life, signal coverage, and equipment damage are your enemies. This page covers maximising what you have.

Mobile Phone

Getting a Signal When There Is None

Emergency Calls Without a SIM

  • SIM not required for emergency calls in most countries. Even a phone with no SIM card can dial 000 (AU), 112 (international GSM standard), 911 (redirects in most countries).
  • Emergency calls may connect on ANY available network, not just your carrier. Your phone will roam for emergency calls.

Maximising Battery Life

Action Battery saving
Airplane mode when not actively signalling Maximum — disable all radios
Turn off Bluetooth, WiFi, GPS Significant
Dim screen to minimum Moderate
Close all apps Moderate (stops background activity)
Power saving mode 10–20% extension
Turn phone OFF between scheduled check-ins Maximum — phone only on for 5 minutes per hour
Keep battery warm Cold drains batteries. Keep phone against body heat inside clothing.

Schedule: Turn the phone ON for 5 minutes at the top of each hour. Attempt call/text. Turn OFF immediately. This extends battery life from hours to days.

Improvised Charging

If your phone dies but you have a charging cable:

Method Feasibility
Vehicle battery (if vehicle present) Best — USB car charger or direct wire to 12V
Solar charger (if in kit) Good — set up in direct sun
Hand-crank charger (if in kit) Poor — 10 minutes of cranking = 1 minute of call time
Multiple AA batteries wired in series Requires technical skill; 8 AA batteries = ~12V, can charge a phone via a voltage regulator

Personal Locator Beacon (PLB) / EPIRB

If you have a PLB (Personal Locator Beacon) or EPIRB (Emergency Position Indicating Radio Beacon):

PLBs work almost everywhere on Earth — they use the Cospas-Sarsat satellite system, which covers the entire globe. They do not require mobile coverage. They DO require battery life and a clear sky view.

ℹ️ Information
**Register your PLB.** In Australia, register with AMSA (Australian Maritime Safety Authority). In China, with the CNSA rescue coordination. Registered PLBs with owner contact details dramatically speed up the rescue process — authorities can call your emergency contacts to confirm you are actually missing.

Two-Way Radio (UHF/VHF)

Radio Protocols

If you have a two-way radio (UHF CB in Australia, PMR446 in parts of Asia, marine VHF):

  • Channel: Emergency channel varies by country
  • AU: UHF Channel 5 (emergency repeater), Channel 35 (emergency input)
  • Marine VHF: Channel 16 (international distress)
  • Transmit: "MAYDAY MAYDAY MAYDAY. This is [your name/call sign]. My position is [GPS or description]. I require immediate assistance. [Nature of emergency]. Over."
  • Repeat every 30 seconds if no response
  • If you hear a MAYDAY, do not transmit — write down the details. Only respond if no one else does for 1 minute.

Range

Radio type Range (flat terrain) Range (mountainous)
Handheld UHF (5W) 3–8 km 1–3 km
Vehicle UHF (5W, external antenna) 10–20 km 5–10 km
Handheld VHF (marine) 5–15 km over water N/A
HF radio (if available) 100s–1000s km Varies with frequency and conditions

Satellite Messenger (Garmin InReach, SPOT, etc.)

If you have a satellite messenger:

  • Send a preset SOS message — these devices send your GPS coordinates via satellite to a commercial monitoring centre
  • Two-way models (InReach) allow text communication with rescuers — provide details (number of people, injuries, supplies)
  • One-way models (SPOT) send only your position — rescuers know where you are but not your condition
  • Battery life: 3–14 days depending on model and tracking interval. Set tracking to maximum interval (4+ hours) to conserve battery.

Aircraft Radio (If Near a Crash Site)

If you are near a downed aircraft:

  • Do NOT touch the controls unless you are trained. However, if you can access the radio:
  • Set transponder to 7700 (emergency) — this alerts ATC
  • Radio frequency 121.5 MHz (international air distress) — transmit "MAYDAY" as per protocol
  • The aircraft's ELT (Emergency Locator Transmitter) may already be transmitting — check for a blinking light

🇦🇺 Australian Electronic Signaling Context

PLB/EPIRB Registration with AMSA — FREE

Register your Personal Locator Beacon (PLB) or EPIRB with AMSA before every trip. It is FREE and takes 5 minutes.

  • Register at: beacons.amsa.gov.au
  • Completely free — no cost, no annual fee
  • Required by law for EPIRBs (Class 1 and 2); strongly recommended for PLBs
  • Registration links your beacon to your emergency contacts, vehicle registration, and trip plan
  • Update your trip details before departure — departure date, route, expected return, vehicle description
  • If you do not register and your beacon activates, AMSA has no way of confirming who you are, what you look like, or where you were going

What happens when your PLB activates:

  1. 406 MHz signal transmits your unique registration ID + GPS coordinates to the Cospas-Sarsat satellite system
  2. Signal relayed to AMSA Rescue Coordination Centre (RCC) in Canberra
  3. AMSA contacts your emergency contacts (from registration) to confirm you are actually missing
  4. Search initiated within 1–3 hours — nearest available SAR asset dispatched
  5. Typical response time: 2–6 hours (remote areas), 30–90 minutes (populated areas)
ℹ️ Information
**A registered PLB is the single most important safety device for remote Australian travel.** It costs $250–$500 AUD. It will save your life when no other signal works. Do not travel remote Australia without one.

Satellite Phones — Iridium Coverage

The Iridium satellite network covers ALL of Australia. No black spots.

Aspect Details
Coverage 100% of Australia — including the middle of the Simpson, the Canning Stock Route, the Great Sandy Desert
Voice Works everywhere. May be patchy in deep gorges but generally reliable on open ground.
SMS Works better than voice in marginal conditions. SMS messages queue and send automatically when signal improves.
Data Very slow (2.4 kbps). Only use for SMS or short text emails.
Alternatives Globalstar has gaps in central Australia. Thuraya covers populated areas but not deep outback. Iridium is the only network covering all of Australia.

Rental: Satellite phones can be rented from most capital cities ($5–$15 AUD/day). Essential for solo travel on extreme tracks (Canning, Simpson, Anne Beadell).

PLB tips for Australian travel:

  • Test your PLB before each trip (most have a self-test mode)
  • Store PLB on your person, not in your pack
  • Replace battery before expiry date (typically 5–7 years)
  • Keep PLB accessible — not buried in luggage
  • Activate and leave it on — once activated, DO NOT turn it off
  • Place in a clear sky view location and leave it

Mobile Phone Coverage in Remote Australia

Do not rely on mobile phone coverage outside of towns and major highways.

  • Telstra has the best coverage (they are contracted to provide the Emergency Alert System)
  • Optus and Vodafone are significantly worse in remote areas
  • Even Telstra's "4G coverage map" is optimistic — coverage means you MIGHT get a signal, not that you WILL
  • On desert tracks, expect zero mobile coverage for 95%+ of the trip

If you have mobile signal (rare in remote areas):

  • 000 — Emergency (Police, Fire, Ambulance)
  • 112 — International emergency number (works on any network, even without a SIM)
  • 106 — Text-based emergency number (for hearing/speech impaired — also works via SMS relay)
  • SMS to 000 — Supported in most of Australia (text your location and emergency to 000)
