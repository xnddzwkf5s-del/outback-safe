UHF CB Radio Guide

The definitive guide to UHF CB radio for Australian 4WD drivers, truckers, caravaners, and remote travellers.

1. What is UHF CB?

UHF CB (Citizen Band) operates on 476.4250–477.4125 MHz. It is the standard short-range communication system for Australian 4WD drivers, truckers, caravaners, and remote travellers.

  • No licence required — anyone can use UHF CB in Australia
  • 80 channels — expanded from 40 channels in 2011
  • Maximum power: 5 watts (handheld or vehicle-mounted)
  • Typical range: 5–15 km vehicle-to-vehicle (line of sight), up to 30 km with elevated antenna
  • Standard in every Australian 4WD — built into bullbars, handheld units in gloveboxes
  • ACMA regulated — fines for misuse (swearing, music, deliberate interference)

3. Emergency Channels — Know These

Channel 5 — EMERGENCY REPEATER (CRITICAL)

  • EMERGENCY USE ONLY — legally restricted
  • Repeater-enabled: your signal is retransmitted at higher power from elevated repeater towers, extending range to 50–80 km
  • Monitored by: volunteer emergency services, some police stations, RFDS bases in remote areas
  • When to use: life-threatening emergencies only. Vehicle breakdown is not an emergency unless someone's life is in danger.
  • How to use: "MAYDAY MAYDAY MAYDAY — This is [vehicle description] at [location]. We have [emergency]. [Number] people. Require immediate assistance. Over."
  • Strategy: Transmit your emergency call. Wait 30 seconds. Repeat. Someone may respond. Continue every 2 minutes.

Channel 35 — EMERGENCY REPEATER INPUT

  • Do not transmit on Ch 35 for simplex conversation — it activates the Ch 5 repeater
  • Only Ch 5 should be used for emergency calling

Channel 40 — HIGHWAY CHANNEL (MONITOR THIS)

  • Used by trucks, road trains, and highway traffic nationwide
  • In remote areas: MONITOR Ch 40 at all times. Truck drivers know the road ahead, upcoming hazards, and may have sat phone access.
  • If you hear a truck within range, you are within 10–30 km of the highway
  • Calling on Ch 40: "Channel 40 — this is [vehicle] at [location]. Does anyone copy? Over."

Channel 11 — CALLING CHANNEL

  • Used to establish contact, then move to another channel
  • DO NOT hold long conversations on Ch 11 — make contact, agree on a channel, move there

Channel 10 — 4WD CONVOY CHANNEL

  • Standard channel for 4WD clubs and groups nationally
  • If you're in a convoy, agree on Ch 10 or another specific channel
  • Scan Ch 10 when remote — you may find other 4WD groups within range

4. Practical Outback UHF Use

Outback UHF Etiquette

Before transmitting: - Listen for 5 seconds — is the channel in use? - Know what you're going to say before pressing the button - Keep transmissions under 60 seconds

Convoy communication: - Lead vehicle calls hazards: "Hole left," "Oncoming vehicle," "Dust ahead" - Tail vehicle confirms: "Copy that" - Regular check-ins: every 30 minutes or at turns — "Vehicle 3 checking in, all good" - If someone doesn't check in: convoy stops. Lead vehicle counts. Tail vehicle confirms all accounted for. - UHF channel agreed before starting — write it on a sticker on the dashboard

Channel selection for convoys: - Agree on a PRIMARY channel (e.g. Ch 10, 15, or 20) and a BACKUP channel - If primary channel is busy with other traffic: switch to backup - Scan Ch 40 in the background (dual-watch feature on modern UHFs) — highway traffic provides road condition updates

Calling for help on UHF: 1. Select Channel 5 for emergencies, Channel 40 for non-emergency assistance 2. "MAYDAY MAYDAY MAYDAY" — for life-threatening emergency 3. "PAN PAN PAN" — for urgent but not life-threatening (mechanical breakdown, lost, medical but stable) 4. Provide: location (GPS coordinates if possible, nearest landmark, track name, distance from known roadhouse), number of people, nature of emergency, what you need 5. Repeat every 2 minutes. Someone may be scanning and hear you on the second or third call.

UHF relay (repeat what you hear): - If you hear a distress call but are out of range to help directly: RELAY the call - "Relay, relay, relay — I am receiving a MAYDAY from [location]. [Repeat the emergency details]. Can anyone closer respond? Over." - If you reach a truck on Ch 40: ask them to relay via their fleet communications or phone when in range

UHF limitations in the outback: - Range is line-of-sight only — blocked by hills, dunes, and dense vegetation - In sand dunes (Simpson Desert): range may be only 2–5 km (dunes block signal) - In flat open country (Nullarbor, Channel Country): range can reach 20–30 km - In mountain ranges (Flinders, MacDonnell): extremely variable - UHF CANNOT replace a satellite phone or PLB — it is short-range only - If you can't raise anyone on Ch 5, 40, or 11: you are beyond UHF range of help. Activate your PLB.

Weather monitoring on UHF: - Some UHF channels are used by weather stations in remote areas - Bureau of Meteorology broadcasts on 476.700 MHz and other frequencies in some regions - Scan for weather updates in storm-prone areas (tropical north during build-up)

5. UHF Radio Setup

Choosing a UHF Radio

Vehicle-mounted UHF (recommended): - 5 watts — maximum legal power - External antenna on bullbar or roof — dramatically better range than handheld - Features to look for: - 80-channel (narrowband) compatibility - Dual-watch / scan function (monitor Ch 40 + your convoy channel) - IP67 waterproof rating - CTCSS and DCS (privacy codes — useful in busy areas) - Remote head unit (mount body under dash, head unit where you can see it)

Handheld UHF (backup): - 2–5 watts. 5W preferred. - Carry as backup for spotter, walking reconnaissance, or if vehicle radio fails - Keep CHARGED — flat battery = useless

Recommended brands: - GME (Australian, best reputation, excellent after-sales support) - Uniden - Oricom

Antenna Setup

The antenna is more important than the radio. A cheap radio with a good antenna outperforms an expensive radio with a poor one.

Antenna mounting: - Bullbar mount (most common): Good ground plane, clear line of sight. Slightly directional (better forward). At risk of damage from branches and wildlife strikes. - Roof/gutter mount (best for range): Highest mounting point = best line of sight. The centre of the roof provides the best ground plane. Disadvantage: low branches and carparks. - Glass-mount (through-window): No drilling, no hole. Slightly reduced performance. Good for vehicles you don't want to drill. - Magnetic base: Temporary. Good for hire vehicles. Can be knocked off by branches.

Antenna length: - 3 dB (short, ~30 cm): Good in hilly terrain. Wider vertical spread. Less range on flat ground. - 6.5 dB (medium, ~60 cm): Best all-rounder. Good range on flat and moderate terrain. - 9 dB (long, ~1.2 m): Best for flat open country (Nullarbor, Channel Country). Very narrow vertical spread — poor in hills. Whips around on corrugations.

For outback use: A 6.5 dB antenna is the best compromise. Carry a 3 dB spare for hilly terrain.

Antenna cable: - Keep cable runs as short as practical - Do not coil excess cable tightly — this creates an inductor that degrades signal. Loop loosely in a figure-8. - Check connectors regularly — water ingress from river crossings corrodes connections

Interference Issues

Common UHF interference sources: - LED driving lights (cheap ones radiate RF noise — switch them off to test) - USB chargers (cheap 12V-to-USB converters are notorious RF noise generators) - Dash cams - Aftermarket engine management systems

Diagnosing interference: Turn everything off except the radio. Turn devices on one at a time. You'll hear the interference start when you find the culprit.

Fixing: Replace the offending device. Ferrite chokes (clip-on) on power cables can suppress RF noise from chargers and LEDs.

6. Legal Requirements

UHF CB Legal Requirements (ACMA)

  • No licence required for UHF CB use
  • 5 watts maximum transmission power (vehicle or handheld)
  • No music, sound effects, or deliberate interference — fines apply
  • No swearing or offensive language — children and families monitor these channels
  • Emergency channel 5 and 35: legally restricted to emergency use ONLY. Penalties for misuse.
  • Data on Ch 22 and 23: No voice permitted. For telemetry and GPS only.
  • Repeater input channels (31–38, 71–78): Do not use for simplex (direct radio-to-radio). These are for repeater towers only. Using them for simplex conversations blocks repeaters for everyone in range.

Privacy Codes (CTCSS / DCS)

CTCSS (Continuous Tone-Coded Squelch System) and DCS (Digital-Coded Squelch) are not privacy — they are selective hearing.

  • They do not encrypt your conversation
  • Anyone without a privacy code can still hear you
  • They only prevent you from hearing other people on the same channel
  • Useful in busy areas (caravan parks, popular camps) to filter out other conversations
  • DO NOT use privacy codes in emergencies — you won't hear responses from people without your code

Selcall (Selective Calling)

Some commercial UHF radios support Selcall — a 5-tone paging system. This lets you ring a specific radio in your group. Almost never used by recreational 4WDers but common in commercial fleets.
