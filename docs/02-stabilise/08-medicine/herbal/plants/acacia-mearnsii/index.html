Acacia mearnsii

Acacia mearnsii — Black Wattle. Bark used as wound dressing and bandage. Image: Wikimedia Commons (CC BY-SA).

Common Names

  • English: Black wattle, green wattle, late black wattle
  • Chinese: 黑荆树 (hēi jīng shù)
  • Aboriginal: Warrarakk (Wurundjeri), various names across southeastern groups

Identification

  • Habitat: Open forest and woodland, common in southeastern Australia from southeastern SA through Victoria to NSW and southern Queensland. Prefers well-drained soils, hillsides, and disturbed areas. Often forms dense stands after fire. Considered invasive in parts of South Africa and elsewhere.
  • Leaves: Bipinnate (feathery, divided twice), dark green, 8–15 cm long with numerous small leaflets (1.5–4 mm long). Young foliage tips are golden-green and softly hairy.
  • Flowers: Pale yellow to cream, small globular heads 5–8 mm diameter, clustered in axillary racemes. Strongly and sweetly scented. Appear in spring to early summer (September–December in Australia).
  • Bark: Smooth when young, becoming dark brown to black, hard, and fissured with age. The bark is the primary medicinal part — very high in tannins (30–45%).
  • Roots: Extensive root system with nitrogen-fixing nodules. Not medicinally used.
  • Look-alikes: Many Acacia species look similar. A. mearnsii is distinguished by: dark bark, pale yellow (not bright golden) flowers, pubescent (hairy) young stems and leaf stalks, and glands along the leaf axis between pairs of pinnae. Do not confuse with A. dealbata (silver wattle) which has silvery foliage and brighter yellow flowers.

Distribution

Native to southeastern Australia: SA, Victoria, NSW, southern Queensland, Tasmania. Widely introduced globally for tannin production — naturalised in parts of China (Yunnan, Sichuan) and scattered locations in SEA.

Medicinal Uses

  • Primary: Astringent. The high tannin content makes black wattle bark a powerful astringent for treating diarrhoea, wound cleansing, and tightening tissues. Tannins precipitate proteins and form a protective layer on damaged tissue.
  • Secondary: Gum exudate as emergency food and soothing demulcent for sore throats. Anthelmintic (anti-worm) properties from tannins. Mouthwash for gum inflammation and mouth ulcers.
  • Traditional: Aboriginal peoples used wattle bark decoctions for diarrhoea and dysentery. Bark was also used in healing baths for skin conditions. Gum was eaten as a snack and used to soothe sore throats. Smoke from burning bark was used for coughs (external inhalation only — not directly inhaled).

Preparation

  • Decoction: 1–2 teaspoons dried bark per 250 mL water. Simmer gently for 15–20 minutes. Strain thoroughly. For diarrhoea: 1/2 cup 3× daily. For mouthwash/wound wash: cool and use externally.
  • Poultice: Powder dried bark and mix with small amount of water to form a paste. Apply to wounds (promotes clotting, forms protective barrier) or haemorrhoids. Leave 30 minutes, rinse.
  • Gum: Harvest fresh gum (golden to dark amber nodules on trunk). Chew slowly for sore throat. Can be dissolved in warm water as a soothing drink. Note: nutritional value is limited — mostly complex carbohydrates.
  • Tincture: Dried bark in 50% alcohol, steep 4 weeks. Dose: 2–3 mL in water, 3× daily for diarrhoea.

Safety

  • Contraindications: Do not use long-term (more than 7 days) — tannins can interfere with nutrient absorption and damage the liver with prolonged high doses. Avoid in pregnancy. Avoid if constipated. Do not use in iron-deficiency anaemia (tannins bind iron).
  • Side effects: Constipation (ironic, as it's used for diarrhoea — dose is key). Nausea if taken on empty stomach. Stomach irritation with high doses. Darkened stools (normal — tannin effect).
  • Interactions: Reduces absorption of iron, calcium, and other minerals — take between meals. May reduce absorption of some pharmaceuticals.
  • Toxicity: Generally safe in standard doses. High tannin intake over long periods is associated with oesophageal cancer in some populations (betel nut chewers) — does not apply to short-term therapeutic use.

Cultivation

  • Propagation: Seed requires pre-treatment — pour boiling water over seeds, soak for 12–24 hours until seeds swell. Sow swollen seeds, discard non-swollen ones. Germinates in 1–3 weeks.
  • Growing conditions: Full sun, well-drained soil, very drought-tolerant. Fast-growing (1–3 m per year). Short-lived (10–20 years). Tolerates frost once established. Nitrogen-fixing — improves soil.
  • Harvest: Bark harvested from pruned branches or coppiced trees (not from main trunk if you want the tree to survive). Harvest in spring when sap is flowing — bark is easier to strip. Dry bark in sun or shade. Gum exudate collected year-round from naturally occurring wounds.
