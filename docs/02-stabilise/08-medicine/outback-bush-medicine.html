Outback Bush Medicine

When you are days from a pharmacy, the land around you is your medicine cabinet. But only if you know what to look for — and what to avoid.

This page covers plants that are actually found in remote Australian outback areas (arid and tropical). It is a curated selection, not the full database — for the complete 30-plant reference, see the Natural Medicine Database.

For general safety principles including plant identification techniques, see plant entries below for detailed identification guidance.

General Rules for Outback Bush Medicine

  1. Do not experiment on an emergency. Test new plants on healthy skin before using them for treatment.
  2. Patch test for allergies. Apply a small amount of the preparation to the inside of your wrist. Wait 24 hours. If no reaction, it is likely safe to use.
  3. Start with the smallest possible dose. A plant that is helpful in small amounts can be toxic in large amounts.
  4. Do not mix remedies. Use one plant at a time. If you combine plants and have a bad reaction, you will not know which one caused it.
  5. Know the look-alikes. See the deadly look-alike table below — these have killed people.
  6. Season matters. Many plants are not available in the dry season (no leaves, no flowers). Collect and dry them when they are abundant.

Arid Outback Plants (Simpson Desert, Central Australia, Nullarbor, Channel Country)

These are plants found in the true desert and semi-arid zones. They are hardy, drought-resistant, and can be found across vast areas.

Duboisia hopwoodii (Pituri) Pain relief ⚠️ TOXIC in high doses. Traditional use only — controlled chewing mixed with ash. Do not experiment. Sandy deserts, stony plains
Dodonaea viscosa (Hop Bush, Broad-leaved Hop Bush) Wound healing, toothache Crushed leaf poultice for wounds. Chew a leaf for toothache (spit out, do not swallow). Widespread across all Australia
Eucalyptus camaldulensis (River Red Gum) Antiseptic wash, burns Leaf infusion for washing wounds. Gum (kino) for sealing burns. Watercourses, permanent creeks. Follow dry creek beds.
Melaleuca alternifolia (Tea Tree) Antiseptic, antifungal Crush fresh leaves for poultice. Steam distillation for oil (impractical in field — use crushed leaves directly). (East coast only — NE NSW / SE QLD. Widely available as commercial oil — carry with you. Not found in arid/tropical outback.)
Sarcostemma australe (Caustic Vine, Bush Milk) Skin conditions Milky sap used on warts and skin lesions. Do not get in eyes. Arid inland, stony plains
Senna artemisioides (Silver Cassia, Desert Cassia) Laxative, skin infections Leaf infusion as mild laxative. Leaf poultice for skin conditions. Widespread arid, Mulga country
Pterocaulon sphacelatum (Apple Bush, Bush Tea) Respiratory, fever Leaf and stem tea. Used traditionally for colds, flu, fever. Northern and central Australia, desert
Eremophila duttonii (Budda, Fuchsia Bush) Antiseptic wash, respiratory steam inhalation External wash, poultice, or steam inhalation only. ⚠️ Do NOT drink Eremophila decoctions — can cause liver damage (hepatotoxic). Central Australia, all desert regions

What to Avoid — 5 Deadly Outback Look-Alikes

These are plants that look harmless or even medicinal but can kill, blind, or cause excruciating pain. Learn them before you touch anything.

Dangerous Plant Photo Looks Like Toxic Part Danger Region
Duboisia myoporoides (Corkwood) Harmless shrub with small white tubular flowers All parts — tropane alkaloids (similar to deadly nightshade) Hallucinations, coma, respiratory failure Eastern Australia, QLD, NSW
Euphorbia species (Spurges — many species) Various — some look like small succulents or weeds Milky sap — skin contact causes painful blistering and dermatitis Skin damage, eye contact causes blindness if not flushed immediately All states and territories
Ricinus communis (Castor Oil Plant) Attractive large broad-leaf shrub with spiky seed pods Seeds — contain ricin, one of the most toxic naturally occurring substances 1–2 seeds can kill a child. 3–5 seeds can kill an adult. Ricin causes severe gastrointestinal bleeding and organ failure. Widespread weed across all Australia
Datura species (Thornapple, Angel's Trumpet) Attractive large trumpet-shaped flowers (white or purple), seed pods covered in spikes All parts — tropane alkaloids (scopolamine, hyoscyamine, atropine) Hallucinations (violent), hyperthermia, seizures, coma, death. This plant kills people every year who try it recreationally. All states, common on disturbed ground and roadsides
Dendrocnide moroides (Gympie Gympie, Stinging Tree) Large soft green heart-shaped leaves with serrated edges Silica-tipped hairs on leaves, stems, and fruit — inject neurotoxin on contact Excruciating pain lasting months. Pain described as "being burned with acid and electrocuted simultaneously." Hairs remain active for years. Sitting under one during rain can cause severe reaction. QLD rainforest edges (Atherton Tablelands, Daintree)

Look-Alike ID Tips

Safe Plant Not to Be Mistaken With Key Difference
Mulga (Acacia aneura) — gum for sealing wounds Corkwood (Duboisia myoporoides) Mulga has narrow grey-green phyllodes (modified leaf stalks), not true leaves. Corkwood has small soft true leaves. Mulga gum is clear to red-brown; Corkwood sap is milky.
Eremophila leaves for tea Duboisia species Eremophila leaves are sticky, aromatic when crushed, with a strong smell. Duboisia leaves have no strong odour and are thin/soft. Crush first — smell is part of identification.
Acacia gum (edible/medicinal) Euphorbia sap (toxic) Acacia gum oozes from bark as a solid to semi-solid clear/amber lump. Euphorbia sap is white, milky liquid immediately upon cutting. Never ingest white sap without 100% certainty.

Toxin from Animals — Bush Remedies

These are not plants but are encountered in the same environments and require specific first aid that may involve natural materials.

Stinging Tree (Gympie Gympie) — Pain Management

  • DO NOT rub — this embeds more hairs
  • Use wax strips or sticky tape to remove hairs. Strip repeatedly over the affected area.
  • Dilute hydrochloric acid has been used historically to neutralise the toxin (hospital treatment). Not practical in the field.
  • Hot wax (melt candle wax, apply, let cool, peel off) can remove embedded hairs.
  • Antihistamines may help reduce the histamine component of the reaction.
  • Pain lasts months but is not fatal. Hospitalisation for pain management is common.

Stonefish Sting

  • HOT water immersion — as hot as the victim can tolerate (45°C or higher) without causing burns
  • Immerse the affected area for 30–90 minutes, keeping water hot by adding more hot water
  • Heat denatures the protein-based venom
  • Apply hot compresses to the affected area if immersion is impractical
  • Evacuate — antivenom is available but supportive treatment (tetanus, wound care) is needed

Box Jellyfish (Chironex fleckeri) — Northern Waters, November–May

  • Pour vinegar over tentacles — this stops undischarged nematocysts from firing
  • DO NOT rub the area — this triggers more nematocysts to discharge
  • DO NOT use fresh water — this triggers nematocyst discharge
  • DO NOT use alcohol, urine, or methylated spirits — these trigger discharge
  • Remove tentacles with tweezers or gloved hands after vinegar has been applied
  • Cardiopulmonary resuscitation may be needed — box jellyfish venom can cause cardiac arrest within minutes
  • Antivenom is available at coastal hospitals in northern Australia

Blue-Ringed Octopus — Rock Pools Australia-Wide

  • Immediate CPR. Venom (tetrodotoxin) causes paralysis within minutes.
  • Victim remains fully conscious but cannot move or breathe — they can hear you. Tell them what is happening.
  • No antivenom exists. Supportive treatment only (mechanical ventilation at hospital).
  • Full recovery is possible with prolonged CPR and ventilation. Toxin clears over 24 hours.

Redback Spider

  • Ice pack on the bite site — reduces pain
  • Do NOT apply PIB. Pressure immobilisation is not indicated for redback bites
  • Pain may be severe (latrodectism) — this is from neurotoxin, not a medical emergency
  • Antivenom at hospital if pain is severe or systemic symptoms develop

Funnel-Web Spider — NSW, QLD

  • Apply PIB technique (same as snake bite — one of the few non-snake uses of pressure immobilisation)
  • Venom contains delta-atracotoxin — particularly dangerous to primates
  • Antivenom is highly effective — no deaths from funnel-web since antivenom became widely available (1981)
  • Found in moist, sheltered areas — rock crevices, piles of timber, suburban garden pools

Collecting, Drying and Storing Bush Medicine

Step Method
Collect leaves Pick healthy leaves, not damaged or insect-eaten. Collect in the morning after dew has evaporated but before midday heat.
Dry Air dry in shade with good airflow. Spread on a clean surface (tarp, flyscreen, fine mesh). Turn daily.
Test for dryness Leaf should crumble when crushed, not bend.
Store Keep in sealed containers in a cool dark place. Avoid plastic bags (traps moisture). Use paper bags, cloth bags, or glass jars.
Storage life Dried leaves: 1–2 years. Bark: 2–3 years. Oils: 6 months–1 year (go rancid).
What not to collect Roadside plants (exhaust, spray drift). Plants near contaminated water. Plants with obvious disease (fungus, insect infestation). Protected/rare species.
